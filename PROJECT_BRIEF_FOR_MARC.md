# Vietnam Stories Website Project Brief

## What This Project Is

This project brings back the dialogue portion of the original `Re: Vietnam: Stories Since the War` website in a form that people can actually read, navigate, and use today.

It is not mainly about nostalgia or technical preservation for its own sake. It is about making a historically meaningful public conversation available again.

## Why It Matters

The archive appears to be important on several levels at once:

- It documents a very early experiment in connecting a television broadcast to public online discussion.
- It captures a moment when people were still working through the meaning of the Vietnam War in deeply personal ways.
- It preserves a style of online conversation that feels notably different from what dominates today: slower, more reflective, more vulnerable, and less performative.

For researchers, journalists, students, veterans, and general readers, that combination makes the archive more than a recovered website. It is a public record of how people tried to listen, argue, remember, and heal online at a very early stage of the web.

## What The New Site Should Do

The rebuilt site should do three simple things well:

- explain what the archive is and why it matters
- let people browse and read the dialogue comfortably
- preserve the historical character of the material without trapping visitors inside a 1990s user experience

For now, this work should be understood as draft development rather than a rushed public launch. The immediate goal is to get a strong local working version in place so we can review and refine it.

## Proposed Visitor Experience

Someone arriving at the site should first encounter a strong introductory page, most likely framed as a `Letter from the Producer`.

That page should:

- place the project in historical context
- explain why the dialogue was unusual and important
- prepare readers for the tone of the conversation
- point them toward a few especially meaningful threads or exchanges

From there, visitors should be able to enter the archive itself through a clean topic index and then read full thread pages.

## Key Editorial Principles

- Keep the archive readable.
- Keep the archive authentic.
- Modernize the presentation, not the voices.
- Preserve names in most cases.
- Do not force blanket email redaction if doing so would be uneven or unreliable.
- Leave room for rare exceptions if a post later needs to be edited for safety or privacy reasons.

## Design Direction

The new site should borrow some of the visual feel of the original, especially its dark presentation and sense of seriousness, while improving:

- typography
- readability
- mobile usability
- accessibility
- navigation

The point is not to reproduce the old interface exactly. The point is to preserve its atmosphere while making the archive genuinely usable now. For the first draft, we should avoid rebuilding old site chrome and instead focus on clean reading pages.

## What Will Likely Be Included First

The first public version should probably include:

- a landing page with context and framing
- a browsable list of discussion threads in activity order
- an alternate original-order view, if enabled
- individual thread pages
- links back and forth between those pages
- simple inline expansion for long posts
- early topic-based guides generated through an AI-assisted first pass
- CSV-based testing input so the build can work from normalized data as soon as it is available

Possible additions after launch:

- downloadable research data
- alternate archival views
- topic-based guides into the material
- outreach to institutional archives

## Draft Workflow

The simplest immediate step is not public hosting. It is generating a static site tree on disk that can be opened and reviewed locally.

In other words:

- first create a strong draft edition
- then refine it
- then publish it
- then pursue a broader preservation strategy

## Current Working Recommendation

Move forward with a focused public archive edition first.

That version should emphasize clarity, context, and readability. If it succeeds at those three things, it will already be a meaningful recovery of the project and a strong foundation for any later archival partnership.
