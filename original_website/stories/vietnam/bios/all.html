Introduction: Who are we?

Our thanks to Tony Heriza of Rutgers University, who organized and moderated this forum.

TONY HERIZA

I was born in 1952 and raised in small rural town in Oregon, where I was the youngest child in a large, rather poor, Roman Catholic family. I spent 10 years in Catholic school

where I first heard of Vietnam in stories of the missionary work of Father Tom Dooley. I was a patriotic kid; wrote essays like "What Freedom Means To Me" for the American Legion contests.

When I was a sophmore in high school, an older sister who was in college took me to a draft counselor in Eugene. She said I needed to think about the Vietnam War. I only vaguely understood, and I didn't think too much more about it until my senior year.

By then I'd begun leaning toward the counterculture and subscribed to RAMPARTS magazine. (My mother was sure I was going to get myself on a blacklist.) Because of what I read there, I began to oppose the war. I saw myself as a pacifist and filed for Conscientious Objector (CO) status, although my parish priest explained to me that the conflict in Vietnam was a "just war."

In the fall of 1969, I joined the ten or so students who wore black armbands to school on the day of the Moratorium. It took an intervention by the principal to keep the football team from beating us all to a pulp. I had to stand up an defend my position in math class, and was verbally torn apart by several kids who had brothers in Vietnam. The next spring, after the invasion of Cambodia and the college shut-downs, my sister came home and I helped her get signatures on a petition for the Hatfield/McGovern amendment [query: was this to budget deal?] (to end funding for the war). We sat in front of the bank on Main Street and didn't get too many signatures, but we did get lots of angry remarks. Later my sister debated the war with the town's top lawyer on the local radio station. My father couldn't go to his local bar for months.

The war caused a deep rift in my extended family. Two of my cousins did tours of duty in Vietnam, one as a Marine (he came home badly wounded) and the other in the Navy. The anti-war activism of my sister and myself led to years of stony silence at family reunions. It was seen as an almost unforgivable betrayal but, at the time, it couldn't be talked about. Years passed before it ever was.

In 1970, I went away to Antioch College in Ohio--without a clue of the kind of place to which I was headed. I'd chosen Antioch for its legendary work-study program, unprepared for what I found when I got there; the place was a hotbed of sex, drugs and politics. I soon found out I wasn't poor, I was "working class." I read Marx. I began to identify even more with the anti-war movement and saw it as a part of a larger struggle for justice in the country and in the world.

For several of my work-study terms, I worked for the American Friends Service Committee and later for the Indochina Peace Campaign. Most of what I did was organize speaking engagements for anti-war speakers in churches, schools, libraries, and whomever would have us. During this time, I learned much more about the Vietnam and about the war. Over the course of several days I escorted a young Vietnamese woman, Le Anh Thieu, to such gatherings. I was deeply moved by her description of Vietnam's 2000 years of struggle for independence.

I was also very affected by a slide show we used, called "The Automated Air-war", about smart bombs and other anti-personnel weapons. I came to see the US as a technologically superior bully--killing men, women and children indiscriminately--while trying to beat a less sophisticated but more determined adversary into submission. I believed the US was wrong. I believed it was my moral obligation to try to do something about it. For better or for worse, I spent much more time doing political work than I did working in class.

My anti-war experience colored much of what happened after the US withdrawal from Vietnam. I began working in documentary film and video because I believed it was an effective way to help bring about social change. I was later involved in other activism-- anti-nuclear, El Salvador, housing, other issues.
It was a film project that led to the expansion of my my views about the Vietnam War. On location in Wyoming, I was introduced to a group of Vietnam vets who were working with a Sioux medicine man to help heal other vets with post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD). Together they had created a ritual they called the "Day of the Howling Dog" that was based on the cleansing, purifying and reintegration ceremonies that Sioux warriors would go through when they returned from battle. For these men, the ritual provided the welcome back to the world that many vets had been denied when they returned from Vietnam. It was powerful medicine.

The film was never made, but while doing this research I heard many disturbing, personal stories of what it was like to be in 'Nam and what it was like to come home. I had never villified the soldiers who fought, but I had never empathized much, either. These working-class guys generously opened my eyes to a whole other way of seeing the war. It hit me hard, just how privileged I had been to have gotten a college deferment.

My political activism has quieted in recent years. I have lost some of the certainty of youth. I think even my faith in the possibility of change has been shaken. Like many other Americans, I am sad to say, by the time the Gulf War came I simply watched it on the evening news--captivated, horrified, resigned.

My views about the Vietnam War have gradually changed; my youthful sense of having the truth in my pocket eventually gave way to my later struggle to understand something so complex. I am troubled lately by the sense that the war is being remembered and taught in school in a totally flattened way. In particular, I am sorry to see the anti-war movement reduced to images of "protesters" mistreating Vietnam veterans. (This is to take nothing away from incidents that surely happened.) For all its warts, arrogance, and occasional foolishness, I think that the anti-war movement was, at its essence, a proud moment in American history, when average people became politically aware and forced their government to stop doing something that they believed to be wrong.

Tony Heriza

Rutgers University / Office of TV and Radio

ARTHUR VARANELLI

Hello! I am Arthur Varanelli.

I was born on December 6, 1946, in Yonkers, New York, to a very dysfunctional family (which is the topic of another story). My father was a World War II vet who fought in the Pacific theater--a fact that means a lot more to me now than it ever did when I was a boy.

Growing up in the mid-to-late 1950s, I can remember first learning about the existence of a place called Vietnam in my geography class. I recall my reactions of horror when I saw the LIFE magazine photos and stories of monks burning themselves alive in the streets of Saigon. I was 13 or so at the time.

At that time, we were stationed at Maxwell Air Force Base in Montgomery, Alabama, and I saw--but little understood--the civil rights activities that were swirling around us. I clearly remember the social prohibitions against interacting with "colored people", who were made to sit in the back of the bus and in segregated cars at the back of a train. I remember the "colored" drinking fountains, bathrooms and all the rest of it. As a Yankee from New York, I was horrified; I could not understand these customs. To this day, the civil rights activities of the monks in Saigon and the civil rights movement in Alabama remain stuck in my mind in very indellible ways. It is strange, but in some way I felt myself already inexorably linked to the path of that small "backwater" country in Asia, very far away, but much in my mind.

The twists and turns of things found me graduating high school with the 35-person Class of 1964 in Dixfield, Maine! Yes, 35 people! I put myself through school at the University of Maine in Orono and graduated in 1968 with a BA in Physics. That was the first best move I made in my life. At the U of M, I shed some of the yoke of my family situation, and began my own life. But that life led me right into the hell of Vietnam.

Most of us who were in school at the time really grappled with the issues presented by the Vietnam War, and the great fear of the great unknown. The protest movement was beginning. The burning monks of Saigon were still in the back of my mind. Many of us took the "delayed entry program" route, which consisted of a two-year obligation if you dropped out of the Officer Candidate School (OCS). So we enlisted and started our slow spiral down to the bottom.

My Vietnam experience was truly surreal, like a Salvador Dali painting of oozing clocks and watches on a bizarre landscape, punctuated with madman events that evoke some expressionist image of a Transylvanian butcher exacting that last bit of horror from his bloodied victims.

I arrived in Vietnam in May, 1969, and left in June, 1970. There I served with the 1st and 25th Infantry Divisions in an area of operation bounded by the outskirts of Saigon on a westerly line to the Parrots Beak/Angels Wing at the Cambodian border, north along the Cambodian border to where it runs east, over to Highway 13 south of An Loc, and south to Lai Khe, south southeast along the Song Be river, skirting Ben Hoa, down to Bear Cat and over to Xuan Loc, and down and around the Vung Tau peninsula.

I worked in the 1/7 and 2/77 artillery units, in which I was assigned to BN FDC Tactical Operations Centers in Di An, Lai Khe, Bear Cat, Cu Chi, and several Fire Support Bases by the Cambodian border, Tay Ninh, and Nui Ba Den--those names are only guesses to me now. I belonged to teams responsible for direct artillery support for infantry unit combat operations. I worked the night shift.

Working the night shift was my choice, a "medication," it seemed, for the depression that was creeping up my body and would soon envelope my life for a very long time. Intellectually, my head did not know what was going on, but my increasingly deadened heart did.

To me, Vietnam was a country of the night, defined by things coming at me from out of the dark--tracers from miniguns, artillery illumination, screams over open mikes, the pounding of artillery, small arms fire. Twelve hours of nerve-wrenching stress under a darkened sky. Our tribe in the hole was all we had. Daytime sleep was fitful, often interrupted by the enemy's incoming rockets and mortars or the anxiety of expectation. I slipped into a waking sleep of mechanistic existence, and buried it all in recesses of my mind. I am just now discovering this.

Within 48 hours of being plucked from 'Nam I entered grad school unprepared for the level of discrimination, hate, and abuse that awaited me. Funny, it was so like the stuff I saw back when I was 13 in Alabama. I now knew in my soul what it was like to "ride in the back of the bus." I began to drink to deaden a pain I did not understand, but the alcohol only encouraged my depression to deepen and behavior problems to blossom. I withdrew from school and started down the path to an occupation in which my Vietnam legacy could be constantly replayed, satisfying something deep, protective, and very frightened.

My ideas about the Vietnam War have changed over time, mostly due to my having initially internalized and narrowly focussed them, and then later broadened them in order to gain a better understanding of my experience. As time has passed, I have grown increasingly angry and troubled by the war. The paradoxical conduct of the war and the abandonment of the Vietnam veteran are broad topics about which I continue to be very distressed.

Skipping to the present, a series of events has led to my awareness of post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), that I have it, and that I must deal with it. That is where I am at right now. My actual experiences in Vietnam you will not hear from me here. Those must be reserved for the safe and structured situations that have become a vital part of my life.

I feel it is an honor to participate in these explorations. I am very glad to meet and interact with all of you.

Arthur

LAURA HANSMANN

My experience with Vietnam is second-hand, but as with second-hand smoke, it has the potential of being deadly. I am married to a Vietnam vet who has just begun to receive treatment for the wounds inflicted on him by that war.

I was born and reared in rural Iowa. From 1965-69, I attended a small, Baptist, liberal arts college in Ohio. It was there that I met my husband. The only thought I had of Vietnam during those years was that the boys who stayed in college wouldn't have to be drafted.

After almost two years of college, my husband dropped out and we were married in the summer of 1968. It wasn't until February, 1969, that his draft board caught up with him and he went first to Fort Polk, then Fort McClellan for advanced infantry training (AIT), and then decided to go to non-commissioned officer (NCO) training at Fort Benning. After my graduation ceremonies, I went to live in Columbus, Georgia, during his training so we could be together.

From Fort Benning he went to back to Fort Polk for OJT and once again I followed him. I had always had hopes that he would be the one who wouldn't have to go to Vietnam. That wish didn't come true; he left for the war in January of 1970.

I was pregnant with our first child when he left. He was an army NCO Sgt. E5 in the 101st Airborne Division and was a mortar specialist. He was sent to lead a platoon of men in the Ashau Valley. In order to save money, I moved in with my parents and worked as a substitute teacher in my hometown. Later that year, I signed a teaching contract and began my teaching career.

I watched the 6 o'clock news every night, praying that my husband wasn't in the body bags I saw on the television, and that the bombs exploding on the screen were falling far from him. I agonized over the fear that I might never see him again. When I got the telegram in May that he had been wounded in a mortar fight over a firebase, it felt as if my world had caved in. He was sent to Cam Rahn Bay to recuperate and then sent back out into the field to lead the men once again.

I went into labor and delivered our son three weeks early; the doctors thought it was due to stress. My baby nearly died in his first 24 hours of life due to aspiritic pneumonia, so my doctor contacted the Red Cross, asking them to bring my husband home. Though the method by which he was told of the situation is a horror story in itself, by the time he arrived, our baby was doing okay and we were home from the hospital.

When I saw him, I barely knew him. My husband is 6'5"; he now weighed only 150 pounds. Our government was starving him to death and causing him to endure unspeakable living conditons. He was made to stay in the jungle (on a snake team) for 90 days without a bath or change of clothing.

When his 30-day leave was up, I sent him back to Vietnam. We met in Hawaii in August of that year but, once again, he had to return to Vietnam. He finally came home to stay in November to help my father with the harvest.

Interestly enough, as was the case with many, I wanted to forget the nightmare of the war, so I didn't talk about it. He didn't talk about it, either. One day he was in the jungle fighting for his life; two days later he was on a tractor in Iowa, picking corn.

Because we buried the experience, our marriage has suffered greatly. He came back a haunted, totally different man than the one who left. In the 11 months that he was gone, he had visibly aged 10 years. Recently, after 28 years of marriage, we separated. But with the help of a wonderful counselor we found in Minneapolis, my husband has shared with me for the first time some personal information about what he went through in the war.

How have my views changed? I am more aware of the war today than I was in 1970 when my husband was fighting in it. Why? Because the long-term effects have had widespread consequences for our family. Our two children have had to walk on eggshells around their dad who rarely, if ever, spoke of his experiences.

There's so much more to tell....but I need to wrap this up. I am still a teacher--high school language arts (mostly writing courses) and speech. I find that most of my students know very little about the Vietnam War, so I make sure they explore it through books and the Internet.

The aftermath? This war changed the entire course of the lives of me and my family. I often wonder who we would be if it had never happened.

Laura Hansmann

TOM ADAMS

Well, now that we are posting, a little about me. I served from 1983 to 1994 with the US Navy. From 1986 to 1992 I served with a Rapid Deployment team as a combat medic. I was deployed in 1986 to the Persian Gulf during the Iran/Iraq war on a mine-hunting mission, and stayed there into the next year From 1987-88 I was deployed to the Gulf where our team was split up after the action started and I was assigned to a medevac team where I spent the rest of the war flying around picking up the dead and wounded.

My father served in the Marine Corps from 1957 to 1966, first as infantry, then as reconnaissance (Recon). He never talked about it, still doesn't. I never realized he was in 'Nam until I joined the service. One day after boot camp I was looking at the ribbon/medal chart in the exchange and realized that he had all the ribbons from 'Nam. Mom always told me he had "gone away with the Marines" before I was born. And that when he came back, he'd spent some time in the hospital. That's all I really knew about it. My cousin told me more. (He was a Marine helicopter mechanic in 'Nam and flew missions a lot as crew member.) He let on a little about what my father did.

That's about it. And that's how I came to be here. Athough I got into the discussions on Vietnam more to learn about my father than to ruminate on my own experience in the service, I see that the government hasn't learned a lot from 'Nam.

But that's another story.

HOAITHI NGUYEN

My name is Hoaithi P. Nguyen, or "Y.T." for short. It's an honor to be asked to be a part of this group, especially since I feel that my experience is so different from that of many of you.

I am a Vietnamese-American woman. I was born in Saigon, Vietnam, in 1968. My mother was an administrator the US Agency for International Development (USAID) and my father was a captain in the Vietnamese Republic Air Force*. I consider it ironic that the Vietnam war became a reality to me personally only after supposed peace reigned over our country. My family was scheduled to be evacuated out of Saigon before that fateful day in April, 1975 when the war officially ended, but I fell sick with the chicken pox and my parents decided to stay behind for my safety.

After the fall of Saigon, my family's life turned upside down. My father was imprisoned in "re-education camp" (euphemism for a combination of Communist indoctrination and labor camp). We bought his freedom in 1980 after he spent five years there watching many of his friends die slowly of the subsistence conditions in camps. He survived the war, but I believe that the imprisonment experience and his personal regrets about losing the war broke his spirit more than any other tragedy in his life. Like many Vietnam veterans, he does not talk about his war experience much, his imprisonment even less. Yet it is so clear to me that it continues to haunt him to this day.

After three attempts to flee the country by boat (the first resulted in capture by the Vietnamese government, the second in fraud perpetrated by the organizers), our family finally succeeded in 1981. We spent four nights and five days on the sea before we reached a little resort town in Malaysia. We were transferred three times to other refugee camps before we finally set foot on American soil around Thanksgiving of 1981. The whole ordeal spanned two years and was set in various detainment camps along the South Vietnam coast when we were captured, and in refugee camps in Malaysia and the Philippines. Fayetteville, North Carolina, was to have been our final settlement. Why Fayetteville? I never knew the official answer. My understanding of US immigration policy and the underpinning of this country's social policies suggests that the goal was to divert new Southeast Asian refugees, who were beginning to arrive in droves, away from places like California--where we might have actually found some small comfort and support from whatever small refugee communities existed at the time! I am sorry; I am editoralizing.

However, I still believe that it was pursuant to that objective that we were sent to Fayetteville, North Carolina, where we were one of five Vietnamese families. None of them had been there for more than three years. My parents struggled to keep us off public assistance, working at service and assembly jobs, while I faced the ridicule, name-calling, and general harassment every day in school for being poor and different. Once the initial shock--the shock of having to cope and assimilate quickly to what I clearly remember as a hostile environment--subsided, a sense of loss and displacement set in. That, I suppose, was one of the reasons why my parents decided to move to California in 1984.

We were in search of a place that resemble the home we so reluctantly left in Vietnam, a place where we might feel a sense of belonging. Thus, Orange County, California--the mecca for Vietnamese in exile--was where I went to high school and college. I still cannot get myself to call it home. I am still waiting for this feeling of displacement and alienation to subside, but I am not holding my breath. I have lived in the States for the last fifteen years.

I graduated from University of California/Irvine in 1991 with a BA in political science and women's studies. Since college, I have worked with a number of non-profit organizations around issues of sexual assault and community empowerment. I am currently in my last year of law school at UCLA and am looking forward to beginning a judicial clerkship in Honolulu after I take the bar exam this August.

My interest in the Vietnam War is purely personal. Like many of you, my life is profoundly affected by the legacy of the war. From my parents, who lost their families, their livelihood, their property, national identity, and social status as the result of the war, I've inherited a lot of bitterness. My parents continue to hang onto old mistrusts and hatreds-- of everything and everyone. They are bitter of their fate as Vietnamese in exile. They are silently resentful of the fact that despite a US citizenship that is well-earned, their allegiance and their contributions to this country are constantly challenged and under suspicion.

Again, I am editoralizing. My apologies. I hope that I am not getting too personal and emotional in this introduction. I don't have any academic perspective that I can share. I've never pursued this topic directly, in part because of the emotional baggage I am afraid I still carry. All I have to offer in this discussion is what I know of the personal and intangible losses to those who were involved, and the repercussions that will be felt for generations to come. I have been back to Vietnam twice, in 1991 and 1994. In my travels throughout the country, it is very clear to me that it is written in the landscape and in the minds of a lot of Vietnamese people: "The wound heals, but the scar still lingers."

I am very honored to be among such diverse and interesting group of people. I am looking forward to the opportunity to explore this topic with you all and hope that we can build a supportive and safe environment in which I can finally wrestle some of my own demons. Yikes!

Until then, aloha.

Hoaithi

TOM DIER

I was drafted into the US Army--received my long-awaited draft notice on Christmas Eve, 1968. After basic training and advanced infantry training (AIT), I went to an non-commisioned officers (NCO) School. After serving eight total months in the military, I became a sergeant E5.

When I arrived in Vietnam, I was assigned to an infantry company. My first job was as a squad leader in an 81mm mortar platoon. Eventually I assumed command as platoon sergeant of the entire platoon of seven men. We may have had an officer in charge of our platoon for a total of one week. For the rest of the time the platoon was led by a platoon sergeant.

My unit was C Company, 1/52 198th Light Infantry Brigade, Americal Division. Our area of operations was west of Highway 1, southwest of Chu Lai, in southern I Corps.

My opinion of the war has remained constant since January 1970, when I spent my first night I spent in the field as a scared 21-year-old kid: The war was a mistake.

What troubles me most?

1) The way veterans of Vietnam have been stereotyped as pathetic, anti-social, loser, time-bombs.

2) The attitude held by many returning vets that if one hasn't served in Vietnam, one is not entitled to voice an opinion about the war.

3) The terrible human loss suffered on all sides of the war. TheVietnamese endured very much; their country is still in ruins.

Tom Dier

EARL MARTIN

My name's Earl. Now living in Shenandoah Valley, Virginia. Grew up on small Mennonite farm in Lancaster, Pennsylvania. Hence I was a conscientious objector (CO) by upbringing. But when the draft came for Vietnam, I chose to drop out of college and volunteer to go to Vietnam as civilian aid worker in 1966. At that time, I stayed three years at that time in Quang Ngai (I Corps, south of DaNang). Main work was with refugees in the camps, distributing food, blankets and setting up vocational training classes for farmer youth so they could get jobs in the towns. Married Pat Hostetter, another Mennonite worker in Quang Ngai, a month after the Tet Offensive, just a day after the My Lai massacre occurred in our province.

Spent next three years in US doing East Asia studies in California. Back toVietnam in 1973-75, again to Quang Ngai. This time assignment was to research the problem of what to do about unexploded munitions in farmers' fields as they were returning to their homes. We rigged up an armored tractor to "plow up swords" in the fields.

When the communists took over in 1975, I stayed in Quang Ngai. Was treated well for some days, then asked by communist officials to hitchhike to Saigon. Hairy trip, since Saigon was still under old government. Detained a couple weeks. Then stayed three months in Saigon after take-over. Wrote all that up in a book called REACHING THE OTHER SIDE.

Have visited Vietnam three times since, most recently from January through July of 1993. Found Vietnamese very accepting of Americans today. Seems they've moved on beyond the war.

What's still most troubling for me? One thing: the lack of appreciation of American society for the deep-souled wisdom of the Vietnam vet. I believe the Vietnam vet serves, in a profound way, as the most poignant conscience of American society. Given all the shit and sacred trauma the Vet has experienced he/she has become (collectively and personally) a sort of a "seer" in American society. If there is any hope of salvation for our society, I believe the vet will have an important role in defining and shaping that vision.

Perhaps enough for now...thanks for inviting me into the group.

In peace,

Earl Martin

Harrisonburg, VA

RENNY CHRISTOPHER

My name is Renny Christopher, and I'm one of the women participating on

this list.

I grew up in a small rural community in California. As a teenager, I passively supported the war, until I became involved with a veteran who had recently returned from Vietnam. His stories of the war completely changed my politics--from right to left--and my outlook on my country and the world.

However changed my outlook was, my perspective was one of outrage at how Americans had been used in the war--until I began teaching at San Jose State University, where there is a large population of Vietnamese immigrants. I had a number of Vietnamese students there; they taught me that the war was, indeed, not just "about" America, but rather a Vietnamese war in which America participated.

Shortly after my teaching experience at San Jose State I went to graduate school to get a PhD, and wrote my dissertation on Vietnam War literature. It is a comparative study of works by Euro-American writers and Vietnamese exile writers. I continue to read and study works by Vietnamese exiles publishing in English, and the many newly available works translated from Vietnamese.

My personal life has also been very affected by having been married twice to Vietnam veterans. (And divorced twice.)

My concerns about the use of the war in the American imagination center on the ethnocentrism and imperialism of that cultural memory.

And I'm very glad we have a Gulf War vet in this discussion--the idea that in the Gulf "we" kicked the "Vietnam Syndrome" (whatever that is) is one that I find very important and disturbing.

Renny Christopher

California State University, Stanislaus

MIKE TOLIVER

I was grew up in Albuquerque, New Mexico, where I was born. Hated high school, was kind of a wimp, so when I graduated in '67, thought I'd "prove I was a man" and join the Marines.

Joined in December '67, boot camp in early '68 and then on to radio school, where I learned to be a radioman in an infantry battalion. After that, it was staging and on to 'Nam 25 Jun '68. Went to the 3rd battalion/1st Marines.

When I first joined the unit, we were right by Force Logistic Command (FLC), just northwest of DaNang. Stayed there about two months, where I got to see FLC get rocketed fairly regularly - a few hit within a hundred meters or so of our wire. Also went on my first patrols around here, sweeping mines from the road north. As we went through the villes, I got the distinct impression nobody was going to be throwing roses at my feet, like the French did for GI's in WWII. We moved south of DaNang after that, where I finished out my tour.

As a radioman, I didn't pull the trigger much, unless we got into a tight scrape. (Happened a couple of times.) Got out 19 July '69, had a brief leave at home and then went onto the Santa Ana helicopter base in California to finish out my enlistment. (At that time, the Crotch had 2-year enlistments available). Got out of the Marines 10 December 69 and started school at University of New Mexico January 1970.

In May of 1970, all hell broke loose on college campuses (Kent State, Jackson State). Eleven students bayoneted by the National Guard, plus your odd stabbing here and there. A real head trip for a combat vet.

Graduated in 1973 in biology, then on to graduate school at University of Illinois. Got my PhD in 1979, got a teaching job at Eureka College [in town, state TK] (Ronnie Reagan's alma mater), got married, had a daughter and now have a dog. I'm head of the science/math division at the college.

How have my views changed? Hell, I don't know for sure. I think I decided the war was a big mistake the first time I went on patrol and saw that the people I was supposed to be helping were either scared of me or hated my guts. It's a real bad feeling getting dry-sniped as you walk through a ville, looking for booby traps and mines. I went to a few peace rallies when I got back in the World, but I couldn't get fired up to join any sort of group. Considered the Vietnam Veterans Against the War, but never did join. I think Vietnam was a horrendous mistake, but one that was made for understandable reasons. Thinking that helps me sleep at night.

What lessons have we failed to learn? Well, that our leaders are human and are bound to make mistakes, but we're not very forgiving. Imagine a politician getting up and admitting that the policy he's been pursuing was completely mistaken. No re-election for that dude. JOHN C. RATLIFF

I am honored to be in this group. Here's a bit of my bio concerning the Vietnam War:

I was a US Air Force (USAF) Pararescueman, stationed at DaNang from October 1970 to July 1971. I extended my enlistment to go to DaNang, then got an early out off the extention to go to Oregon State University (OSU).

As a pararescueman, I flew HH-53 helicopter troop transports that were known as Super Jolly Green Giants. Our mission was combat rescue, mostly for aircrew members in Laos, Cambodia or North Vietnam (the Army guys got most of the rescues in South Vietnam during that time period). I flew many orbits, but actually had only one rescue mission--to the North. We picked up two pilots some sixty miles east of Hanoi. It was tense, but we went in through weather and so weren't seen. (Our high bird had to turn back because of anti-aircraft fire over Bat Lake; there was an opening in the clouds just after we went through.) Because we went out over the sea, we were able to get in and out without firing a shot.

Once, I did almost shoot down one of our own Sandy (A1-E) propeller planes while testing a minigun; he flew under our plane just 10 seconds after I fired. I was on the base during several rocket attacks, and did help the South Vietnamese with casualties one time. After I left for school, several people I knew in the unit were lost in combat rescue attempts, including the infamous "Bat 22" missions. I stayed in the USAF Reserves as a pararescueman intil 1977, and still have friends in the unit (the 304th ARRSq in Portland, Oregon).

I received two degrees, one in zoology and one in health, at OSU. I've been a college instructor, a training officer.

In 1977 I married a Chinese girl I met at OSU who was from Hong Kong. I've visited Hong Kong several times, and we have close family ties there. We have two sons, ages 15 and 17. Chris is a hospital pharmacist. I was employed by an insurance company for 18 years and one month until I was terminated last year in July.

I'm not sure that my ideas have changed that much over time. When I got to DaNang, I read two books about our involvement in Vietnam, OUR OWN WORST ENEMY and THE LOST CRUSADE; they both showed that we had problems a long time before I got there. I think I'm much more aware of how much this war has affected us now than I was in the past. I have no problems with my participation in the war, because my mission and my unit's mission--to save lives--coincided.

What is still most troubling to you about the war and its aftermath?

One of the lessons I learned as a PJ (pararescueman--that was our flight code) was that attention to detail was imperative. One of my superiors, MSgt Gorney, was instrumental in instilling this attitude. Get the details right, and the mission (plan, project, team, etc.) will come along. But miss one of the details, and your parachute might not open. We've lost that attitude in America as a whole, and we lost it before the Vietnam War. We want simple fixes to complex problems, and we want them--no we demand them--to conclude quickly. That's why we see so much absurd posturing by our polititians. Until we learn this lesson, we will continue to have problems. We need to return to our pioneering roots to help us in our modern, very complex, world.

John C. Ratliff, CSP

Roseburg, OR

PAUL HENRY ROSENBERG

I first became aware of the Vietnam War in 1962 at age 12 or 13 when I read a book by Father Tom Dooley. It was a great piece of propaganda, and had me thoroughly convinced that the North Vietnamese were evil incarnate.

Then a few months later the Buddhist monks began sacrificing themselves. It only took one to let me know that I'd been a little hasty in coming to a conclusion, to say the least. I was opposed to the war from that moment on.

Over time I have changed my understanding in many ways. But it was obvious to me from the moment that first monk went up in flames that our stated intentions did not match our national ideals on the one hand, or the reality of our actions on the other. Without integrity, it seemed to me then--and it still does--nothing else really matters. Everyone believes in their heart that they are doing right. To have integrity, one must confront the contradictions that force us to rethink what we are doing, so that we actually have a chance to correct our errors. We will always make mistakes; that goes with the territory of being alive. But to believe that we cannot be wrong, that is to ensure that we will never be right--except, like a broken clock, purely by accident. We will never learn from our mistakes, much less from the mistakes of others.

I was involved in anti-war protests for many years. At first I began to register as a consciencious objector, but then decided that I could not in good conscience do that. I was 1-A delinquent for years*, always expecting to be picked up, and to serve time in prison, but this never happened. I still have no idea why.

In the late '60s and early '70s I hitch-hiked across country a number of times. I met dozens and dozens of vets who picked me up--maybe a hundred or more. Most of them picked me up because they wanted someone to talk to, and they figured they could talk to me. Mostly I just listened. It was obvious to me that the rejection most vets felt came from their own communities, from people who had turned AWAY from the war more than against it, who just did not want to confront it. There was nothing personal in the way in which the vets were treated--which made it all the more painful in a way.

(By the way, of all the vets who picked me, exactly ONE still supported the war. But even he did not feel he could talk to people in his hometown that he had known all his life.)

As I've come to be more and more concerned about how the war in Vietnam fits into the patterns of our own history--which is mostly about our defenses against learning from our history--my ideas about the war continue to change

Early on I became aware that Vietnam had been fighting China for its independence almost since the time of Christ, so the idea that we were fighting to contain Chinese communism was clearly a story concocted for patsies. (This includes a lot of people at the highest levels of government.) Somehow, Americans seem to always confuse their inclination to be played as patsies with their perceived idealistic innocence of heart. I think of carnivals and how their organizers set up games to drain money out of frontier towns where nothing much happens all year round until the carnival arrives. We've all been patsises at one time or another in our lives. It's not a bad thing. It's far worse to be so cynical that no one could take advantage of us. But are those our only two choices? I've never believed in an either/or world. I'm not about to start now.

GERALD ALAN NEY

Bio: Gerald Alan Ney, born February 18, 1945, in Milwaukee, Wisconsin, while father was stationed at radar installation near Victorville, California, in the Mojave Desert. Educated by the School Sisters of Notre Dame for all 12 years of grade school and high school. Was boy scout, altar boy, paperboy (5 years, 10 months). Played accordian from the second thru eighth grades, and French horn in high school band and university orchestra.

In November '62, Kennedy called up the Wisconsin National Guard for the Berlin crisis. Because I was fast approaching draft age, this served as a wake-up call for me. By the time I entered the University of Wisconsin-Milwaukee (UWM), I figured we were going to get into a war someplace soon (probably Germany or Cuba), and that it would be better to go in as an officer than a private. When the recruiters from the Reserve Officers Training Corps (ROTC) made their pitch to the freshman males, I signed up. At the beginning of my junior year I signed the contract by which, in exchange for a stipend for books, Uncle Sam owned my bod. Dropping out of school after that would have meant immediate induction into the Army as a private.

Meanwhile, I joined the A Phi O National Service Fraternity, the Newman Student Association, International Club, Pershing Rifles (a military fraternity fielding drill teams), Scabbard & Blade Honorary Military Fraternity, and the Gammma Theta Upsilon Honorary Geography Fraternity. My dad had a way of putting this all in perspective when he'd ask: "Can the Vice-Chancellor spare two hours to cut the grass this Saturday?"

Attended ROTC summer camp at Fort Riley, Kansas, in July and August 1966. I was a bit flabby and overweight, and was cursed with a tendency to try see all the angles in tactical situations that demanded quick decisions. In a class of 297, I ranked 296th; no. 297 was sent home. I graduated the camp mainly on the strength of taking whatever they dished out and coming back for more.

In April '67, if my memory serves me, I was part of the honor guard for the first UWM grad to be killed in Vietnam. We couldn't help but be moved by the young widow; after that, the six of us made a pact never to get married till after we went to Vietnam. As far as I know, we all kept the pact. In May '67, the presentation of the flag to that same widow at the Chancellor's Review was used as the signal to start the first major anti-war demonstration at UWM. Because I was the Student Election Commisioner, I was one of about a dozen ROTC cadets singled out to have daisies placed in our rifles. Unlike later demonstrations of later years, there was no trouble.

On June 4, 1967, I received my commission as a Second Lieutenant in Army Military Intelligence. I began active duty at Fort Benning, Georgia, on November 26, 1967, for Infantry Officer's Basic Training ("to get an appreciation of the problems of the infantry officer"), followed by an Aerial Surveillance Officer's Course that began on February 15, 1968, at Fort Holabird in Baltimore. Five months later, after a 30-day leave, I arrived at Cam Ranh Bay in Vietnam and was assigned the aerial imagery interpretation section of the 172nd Military Intelligence Company, 173rd Airborne Brigade, at LZ English, Binh Dinh Province. Spent the next year in that job, making trips to Saigon and Qui Nhon for classes, and one to Bao Lac on detached duty for 19 days in February of 1969. Spent much of "off-duty" time on court-martial duty. Flew 39 hand-held photo missions, and was fortunate to have only been shot at once by the enemy on these--versus twice by our own 105mm artillery. Of course there were the routine dozen mortar shellings we received at night, and the occasional perimeter probes. Still I didn't have too bad a year compared to many others.

After Vietnam, I was stationed at Fort Carson, Colorado, from August '69 to July '71. Met my wife in Colorado Springs and got married six weeks before I separated from the service. I quickly found that both my BS in geography and having been an officer in the Army meant zero on the job market. Because I'd spent some time as a supply officer at Fort Carson, I eventually landed a job with the Navy Aviation Supply Office in Philadelphia, Pennsylvania, where I've been employed since October '72 as an inventory manager. Except for the nearly two years I spent as a field rep at the naval air station in Alameda, California, I've been in Philly ever since I left the Army.

Didn't become active in veterans affairs till 1985. Wasn't in the woodwork like many, but was just bound up in keeping my family's heads above water. Am currently a member of Vietnam Veterans Association (VVA), Catholic War Veterans (CWV), VHCMA [full name TK] and the Association of the 173rd Airborne Brigade. Am president, a state council delegate and education chairman of my VVA chapter; also Philadelphia United Veterans Council delegate and member of Philadelphia Vietnam Veterans' Memorial Advisory Committee.

Hope this is neither too much or too little.

LOUIS E. GRIVETTI

[Editor's note: Louis Grivetti of Univeristy of California/Davis aided Tony Heriza in moderating this forum.]

I am a professor of nutrition at the University of California/Davis (UCD). I am the coordinator of the UCD-Vietnam Cooperative Agreement that exists between our campus, the University of Ha Noi, and the Vietnamese Ministry of Agriculture. I have visited Vietnam three times during the past four years to work on nutrition-related, health-related projects.

Last fall, I was invited to teach an honors-level class at the university to first- and second year students that would explore a range of issues regarding Vietnam. The course, now in its seventh week is entitled "Viet Nam... The Way We Were... The Way We Are."

Speakers, readings, and e-mail correspondents have included a broad range of participants and perspectives, including those of geographers, historians, and social and medical scientists; physicians and nurses; veterans both for and against the war; Vietnamese-Americans, and colleagues in Ha Noi.

The primary seminar question throughout the quarter has been: "Why Does the Viet Nam - American War Continue to Haunt Americans?"

During the course of this quarter, I have had the pleasure of developing dialog through e-mail with several of you, and I look forward to participating in our discussions.

Biographical background and related issues:

I was born in 1938, finished high school in 1956, and entered the University of California/Berkeley (A.B. in 1960; M.A. in 1962). I essentially knew nothing about the war in Vietnam during the time I was in college. As a California state requirement, I was required to participate in the Reserve Officers Training Corps (ROTC), and gladly did so. (I remember getting a "B" on a military history term paper. I recall the close-order drill, disassembling and reassembling my M-1, and seeing class-mates receive the notorious "M-1 thumb." I learned quickly how to use 5-day deodorant pads for that mirror finish on my shoes that was required for passing inspection, and I polished brass many evenings in my dorm.

I served my country, but not in Viet Nam. I registered for the draft at 18, but had a II-S college deferment. It never occurred to me to give it up and enlist; I figured that when I finished school I would be a U.S. Marine, since I wanted to fly helicopters. I didn't know much about graduate school, but in my senior year when I found out how important it was, so I applied, was accepted, and my II-S was extended. I had already taken my pre-induction physical. Then my eyesight went haywire and I began to wear glasses, and that shot my chance at being a pilot.

During my last semester at Berkeley in 1961-62, I found that the USPHS [full title TK], a commissioned corps, needed scientists, so I applied and was accepted. After I got my M.A., I was sent to Cincinnati, Ohio, where I ran a laboratory that examined the quality of drinking water in the United States.

At this time I still knew nothing about the war in Vietnam, but I knew a lot about dynamics of the conflicts in Israel-Palestine, and that led to my next job.

I was attached as a civilian to the US Navy NAMRU-3 [meaning of acronym TK] hospital in Cairo, Egypt, and it was in Egypt that I learned about the war in Vietnam. There were medical corpsmen and Navy physicians who rotated through the hospital, and they talked about how tough it was being out in the field and/or working in the field hospitals. I worked in Egypt from 1964-1967. During this time, the Egyptian government, led by Gamal abd al-Nasser, allowed the importation of tons of Soviet and Chinese magazines that contained articles about Vietnam--how the Americans had lost more than 1,200 helicopters, thousands of soldiers, and had committed atrocities, etc. I passed everything off as propaganda, and continued my interest in Israel/Palestinian politics.

After the Israeli/Egyptian Six-Day War, my wife and I returned to the United States in August of 1967. For the first time, we saw the Vietnam depicted, full-blast, on television.

I remember the television in the evening, Chet Huntley/David Brinkley, Walter Cronkite..... Tet 1968....the attack on the US Embassy in Saigon, the execution of the Viet Cong, the fire-fight at Hue, and a broad range of other images. I was working overseas in Africa in 1973-75 when the war concluded.

Frankly, I don't remember ever seeing television news accounts of veterans returning home from Vietnam, only Pulitzer Prize-winning photos taken of one veteran and his family returning to Travis Airforce Base in Fairfield, California.

Throughout it all, I was part of the silent majority that clearly supported Presidents Kennedy, Johnson and Nixon in their Vietnam war efforts. I couldn't understand how the Viet Cong had done so much damage to Sai Gon and Hue during the Tet Offensive since we had been told that "the light at the end of the tunnel" had been the comparative weakness of these forces.

It is not difficult to understand my position. I grew up remembering World War II. I attended high school in the early '50s in Santa Paula, California, during the escalation of what we then called the Cold War. The "Red Scare" was upon us. Influential politicians such as Senator Joseph McCarthy suggested that there could be a "Red under every bed." If we didn't heed the World War II lessons of Munich, then we were doomed to repeat history and would need to embark upon a much worse war--one with nuclear consequences.

I learned the so-called "lessons of Munich" in high school. This view held that Western appeasement of the mustachioed German dictator--the surrender of Czechoslovakia to Nazi control--had led to greater fascist designs on more and more territory. That was the lesson of Munich: stop totalitarianism cold--no appeasement. This view led to: stop Communism cold, then peace would follow.

I also learned the so-called "domino theory" in high school. We were told that if the freedom-loving states of the West did not stop the advance of Communism--specifically the Chinese form of communism--then the states of southeast Asia would topple one by one like a row of dominoes: As China went, then would go Vietnam, Laos, Cambodia, Thailand, Malaysia, and Indonesia--all the way down and south into Australia and New Zealand and all the way west from Thailand to Burma, to Pakistan, to India. And, well, India was just a hop-step-and-jump away from the oil-rich states of the Middle East, and those national dominoes could topple, as well.

Ironically, it was also in high school that I learned the so-called "Asia war principle": one should never become involved in a land war in Asia. Memories of the Korean War remained in our minds, fresh with images of the human-wave tactics whereby hundreds of Chinese communists would rush UN- and American-held defensive positions. The Chinese troops would rush into steady machine-gun fire, rush into certain death, and they would keep coming, keep coming, until the UN and American troops had to retreat for lack of ammunition or because the gun barrels of the machine guns became overheated and the ammunition jammed. All of us in high school at the time saw newsreels of such tactics; I spoke with American veterans who had defended their positions from such tactics. It was real, not fiction.

Still, I supported the war effort of our government and soldiers in Vietnam because I believed strongly in the domino theory and the lessons of Munich.

In 1976, I became a college professor in 1976 in the departments of geography and nutrition. The war was over; people didn't talk about it much. Further, I was a conservative Republican and out of sorts with many on campus, so I also basically kept my ideas and feelings inside when it came to Vietnam. I basically ignored Vietnam, and turned my attention to making a living.

It was not until 1985 that I read my first historical book about the Vietnam War and then another and another...It was about 1990 that I had a change of mind regarding the war: I had slowly grown to believe that it was the American government that lost the war, that the government was the problem, not the American combat soldiers who were in Vietnam. The soldiers were there because, at one time, they believed that to be there was right, or they had to go, or a combination of both. I also learned, quickly, that many veterans who once thought their service in Vietnam to be right had a change of mind after being in Vietnam for only several months.

In 1992 the American government lifted the travel ban on American citizens traveling to Vietnam. I knew then that I wanted to go, so I went in1993, 1994, and 1995. I will return again this summer with my graduate students.

By reading this far, you surely realize that I am rather verbose; I'll try to be more concise in the days to come. I am delighted and honored to participate in this dialogue, and look forward to working with Tony as co-moderator. I only hope that I can meet your collective expectations.

Regards and best wishes to all of you.

L. Grivetti

JOSEPH T. MILLER

Well, folks, as I read the many introductory messages this morning, I feel compelled to join in. I explained to Tony that I might not be able to participate very often in this group, due to a rather hectic teaching and advising schedule this semester. Let me at least give you some info about my connection with the Vietnam war.

I was born in 1942 in Chicago to a working-class family on the then-all-white West side. Went to nine years of Catholic school (including kindergarten), then four years in a public high school. Raised to believe in God, country, motherhood, and apple pie, truth, justice, the "American way" and, certainly, anti-communism.

Enlisted in the Navy in April 1961, nearly one year after my high school graduation. While at Great Lakes Naval Training Center, I was classified to work as a Communications Technician (CT). I had no idea what the hell that was at the time, but, after my first service school in Bainbridge, Maryland, I began to get sort of a clue that we had something to do with the National Security Agency (NSA), though didn't know what the hell that was either. Sent to language school in Monterey, California, to be trained in Chinese-Mandarin. Learned more about what work I might be doing, passed the advancement exam for E-4 [definition TK] while there. Studied Chinese for 18 months, six hours a day, five days a week.

Sent to Taiwan in June 1963 to work with the Naval Security Group Detachment at Linkou Air Station, fifteen miles outside of Taipei. During this period I learned about the various naval patrols that were sent on intelligence-gathering missions off the coast of China and northern Vietnam. My basic duty was to attempt the breaking of Chinese (Chicom) codes, then write up daily reports to DIRNSA (Director, National Security Agency). Because this sort of work did not allow me to use my Chinese language facility very well, I became interested in developing friendships with the local people, something that was frowned upon for anyone working with a Top Secret Crypto clearance, as I was.

One friendship I had with a Taiwanese woman who worked on base became very serious. We spent all of our free time together, traveling around the island, meeting with her family, etc. When our relationship was found out by my superiors, I was given a choice: give her up and be sent to another duty station immediately, or, give up my clearance and security work and be sent out to the "regular" Navy. (That meant sea duty.) I chose to marry her and thus ended up on the attack aircraft carrier USS Ticonderoga in June1964. I was put to work in the Weapons Department Office as a Yeoman (clerk typist) trainee.

The exitement began on August 2, 1964, when our ship received word that a US destroyer, the Maddox, was under attack by North Vietnamese PT boats in the Tonkin Gulf. Planes from our ship were the first to go to the Maddox's defense. Then, a couple of days later, there were messages about another attack on two of our ships, and the president got on TV back home to tell the American people that "we weren't doin' nuthin';" those evil commies from North Vietnam had attacked us for no reason. In reprisal, the US began its first air attacks on northern Vietnam. Then the president signed the Tonkin Gulf Resolution...

A day or two after all this hoopla, our ship had occasion to refuel the USS Maddox, and my job then was to time the process. This required me to be above decks, which meant I was visible to folks on the Maddox across perhaps 40 to 60 feet of water. As I was standing there, I heard my name being shouted out from the deck of the Maddox. I looked across and could see a few of my CT buddies from Taiwan. His presence there was signficant; it suddenly dawned on me that the President had lied to the American people about what the Maddox was doing out there. The ship was on a DESOTO patrol, an intelligence gathering mission, which was meant to provoke a response from the target of the mission, whether from northern Vietnam or the Chinese island of Hainan, or the southeastern Chinese coast. Generally, the desired response was not military action by the target nation, but, when one considers the OPLAN-34A [definition of acronym TK] missions by US and South Vietnamese forces on the territory of northern Vietnam, such action should not be surprising. The thing that got to me was that the President had lied to the American people about what had gone on.

This experience caused me to ask further questions about our involvement in Indochina, and I began to study the history of that involvement with whatever material I could get hold of on board ship. Bernard Fall's books were among the first I read, and this is where I learned that Vietnam was never actually divided into two separate countries, but that the US had set up an opposition regime in the South in the 1950s. The more I learned, the more pissed off I got. Of course, I didn't know what to do about it, so I just talked with shipmates about the real history of our involvement as I had come to understand it. By the time I was transferred from that ship in February 1966, I had become an anti-war sailor.

I had two more years to serve, due to extensions from language training and one extension I took while still with the NSG [definition TK]. I spent two years at a helicopter training squadron in Pensacola, Florida; this time my wife and our baby daughter were with me. I continued to talk openly about my feelings concerning the war, and I looked forward to the time when I could leave the service and jump right into the anti-war movement. Because I did not trust the "official" press stories about what was happening in Vietnam and at home, I was reading every underground newspaper I could get hold of . I also joined the American Serviceman's Union and the American Civil Liberties Union, just in case I should come to need them.

I received my honorable discharge on February 3, 1968, and by April of 1968, I took part in my first anti-war demonstration in Chicago, marching with folks from Veterans for Peace. (No one ever told us there were groups like this.) Soon, I joined the Chicago chapter of Vietnam Veterans Against the War, and I have been with that organization ever since. I currently function as one of the four National Coordinators. This May 16-17 we will be celebrating our 30th Anniversary in Chicago.

That's more than enough for now, I guess...

Peace With Justice,

Joe
