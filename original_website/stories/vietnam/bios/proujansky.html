JED PROUJANSKY

Glad to be a part of this group. I am honored.

I was born in 1949, and grew up in the '60s. In 1964, when I was a high school sophomore, I was inspired by the work of people then committing themselves to social change in the civil rights movement and started working with that movement. Through my efforts at social change, I felt empowered, propelled as I was by the notion of social justice. That experience started me looking critically at the policies of the US government and those impacted by them.

From that place I developed a (left radical) perspective that saw the main problem as being the corporate interests and their control over the policies and inclinations of the US government, and the effect that had on the people of the US. To my mind, Vietnam was a war conducted to protect US corporate interests; the spread of communism threatened US economic domination of the global economy. (Boy, is that a mouthful.) I saw myself as a communist/anarchist and joined Students for a Democratic Society (SDS)--and some of its more extreme factions--to "fight against US Imperialism."

As a regional organizer for SDS in New England, I was arrested in anti-war protests and did other assorted activities to take a stand the war. I also worked with the organizers of an anti-war coffee house that was set up for military dissidents at Fort Dix in New Jersey. In 1970, I went to Cuba to protest the US policy of embargo. There I met several soldiers from the NLF* and saw their perspective on the war.

In retrospect I think that I was right about many of the opinions I took, but was as arrogant and closed to discussion as any other judgmental, closed-minded extremist. I believe that my work then was important and helped to save lives. But my tactics were, at best, arrogant and, at worst, very destructive.

Enough said. I'll talk more later. I feel like I could go on for hours but I'm at work and sooner or later someone will expect me to do something. Got to go.

Jed
