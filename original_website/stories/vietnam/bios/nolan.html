PAT NOLAN

My name is Pat Nolan. It was in late '64 or early '65 that I first recognized that Vietnam was different from other conflicts our country had been involved in.

My father was in the Air Force. He told me the US was gearing up for a ten-year war. All I could think was, how can that be? My parents were both World War II vets. Modern wars, we had learned, didn't last ten years. Nine years later I was wishing, praying, and writing letters to the diplomats who took part in the Paris peace talks: just get our POWs home, I begged, so we can be complete again and start this country healing.

My brother had gone from saying, "if my Dad goes, I will," to chanting, "hell, no, I won't go." In the interim, I had married a student who avoided the draft as long as he could and whose Officer Candidate School (OCS) class was the first NOT to get orders for Vietnam. My husband finished his Army stint a mere hundred miles from where he'd enlisted. He began to study journalism. That's when we began to see the flaws in the US story, as we began to view things from a journalistic perspective. I started to look at the trees (as opposed to the forest). I saw the individual lives that were scarred, the marriages of returning soldiers that broke up violently, the sons who left for Canada scorned by their fathers. My family was intact (on the surface, at least), but how was I to bring up my daughter in a country that was falling apart? Now I want to understand what happened so I can pass the story on to my granddaughter.
