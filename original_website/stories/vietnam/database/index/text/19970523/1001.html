In Jan 69 I joined the Marines to escape an abusive household. I served in Nam with the 3d Engineer Battalion at the Rockpile located in the corner of the DMZ and Laos. I left the Marines in 74 and joined the Army until 1977, when I moved to Australia until 1991.

The biggest effect it had on me was maturity and being willing to seek and accept responsibility. The most negative memories I have of the period was the African merican Marines, who for the most part would not perform and dities, claiming that "You're just picking on me cause I'm black".

When I movved to Australia, I did all I could to forget about my military experiences, particularly in the Nam, but when I came back to the US, I ran into a friend I went to school with who was married to a vet. They got me involved in a couple of veterans organizations and the yearly trip to Kokomo, IN for the Vietnam Vets Reunion.

Suddenly all the memories and pain I had been suppressing for years came back, and my wife of 24 years learned things about me and saw a side of me she had never seen before.

The support and love from her, as well as from the other vets I have met have gone a long way to healing, but I know that there is a long way yo go. Both my children had operations at 5 weeks for a heredity disease which has never been in either side of the family - and now my granddaughter had the same operation. The only thing I can put it down to is Agent Orange exposure, but, of course, this cannot be proven.

With the help and support of other vets, I'm getting better at accepting my past, but I don't thiknk I'll ever forget or forgive those "countrymen" who spit on me for doing a job that the Government decided needed doing.

Ray Fleckles
1951
