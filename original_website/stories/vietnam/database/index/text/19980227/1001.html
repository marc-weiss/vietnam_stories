I grew up with Vietnam. The fighting may have ended before I was old enough to remember, but my father, Dean, carried the war with him as if he had never come home. He served in Vietnam in the Army Signal Corps from 1969 to 1970. He married my mother in 1971, and the next year they had me and then my three younger sisters. Growing up with a Vietnam Vet as my father was really difficult. I think we knew from an early age that things were screwed up, and that for Dad, Vietnam was the cause. He would drink too much and space out so that he didn't even hear us talking to him. Or he would go crazy over the littlest things and come after us. It was always unpredictable and I never knew if he was going to be in a good mood or if he was going to lose it from day to day. The saddest thing was that he was probably one of the sweetest, most gentle people in the world when he wasn't drunk or belligerent. He took us for long hikes in the woods and told us the funniest stories that would have us all shaking with laughter. He had the craziest sense of humor and I couldn't believe some of the stuff he thought of. Sometimes he would tell stories of Vietnam. Never the morbid or gory parts, just little anecdotes about his buddies, the music they listened to, trouble they got into there...it was as if, even 20 years later, Vietnam was still on his mind every day. Sometimes I think he had flashbacks. He never really spoke of them directly, but he would remark after a bad thunderstorm, "I was on the floor after that big blast." I knew that a thunderclap was really a bomb to him. Eventually, my mother left him. I know that he was withdrawn and basically unapproachable, but I guess I still blame her for doing that. He just didn't feel like he had anything to live for after she was gone. He was always so conscientious about working, he had the same job for 25 years and always supported his daughters, but he would say that he had "nothing". In the years following their breakup, he drank more heavily than ever. He tried to kill himself once by shoving a hunting knife into his neck. He ended up in the hospital for a month and barely survived, but after he got out, he promised he wouldn't drink anymore or ever do that again. Unfortunately, he just didn't have any support. There was nothing that we could do for him. It was like Vietnam had ruined a part of him that nobody could fix. He was so tortured inside, so sad. By mid-1995, things weren't improving, and I began to have premonitions of his death. I knew how depressed he was, but nobody seemed to be able to help him, or he wouldn't let them. In October, 1995, without leaving a note or giving specific warning, he went to a resevoir near his work, slit his jugular vein with a hunting knife, and dived into the lake, where he drowned. The most difficult, and shameful, thing for me to realize was that I didn't even know him until after he was gone and I met his coworkers and friends at his funeral. I guess I was so caught up in how screwed up he was that I was ashamed of him and never appreciated how smart and gifted he was. I feel really badly about that. I also just miss him. Although I know his life was hell, I selfishly wish I could have him back. During the two years since his death, I've come to terms with the fact that he's gone, but I'm still angry about what Vietnam stole from me, and especially, from my father.

Christine Anderson
1972
Albuquerque, NM, USA
