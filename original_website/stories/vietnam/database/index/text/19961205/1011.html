The war made me grow up. It caused many family conflicts, as my thoughts varied considerably from those of my WWII vet father. Living in Detroit, and with a draft lottery number of 42, I gave considerable though to driving across the Detroit River into Canada. I applied for Conscientous Objector status, suffered through a mind-blowing hearing in front of my local draft board, and eventually received the deferrment. This started my accidental career in Health Care, as I served 2 years alternative service in a hospital (with my hair tucked into a K-mart short hair wig!). It effected the way I approached problems within the hospital, leaning towards helping people beat the system and the insurance industry whenever possible. My lack of any other marketable skills kept bringing me back to healthcare, eventually resulting in a Masters Degree and good positions. It also caused me to constantly ramble on about my anti-government, anti-military prejudices to my children, while, at the same time, encouraging them to make up their own minds and form their own opionions. Consequently, they both joined the Air Force! That reminded me that my anger was always more directed at the politicians who made the decisons, and sent the young men off to war, rather than the soldiers themselves, who had little or no choice in the matter. It made me angry when, in sistuations like Central and South America, Bosnia, and othes, people forgot that "Military Advisor" status still gets people killed. Having a child killed in a "non-war" is as bad, possibly worse than a "real" military action. I just want people to remember the pain, and to learn from past mistakes. Our protets and marches served a useful purpose. Sadly, it only opened the public's eyes a little bit. Skepticism of Government is good, but participation in the process is still required, or things get left in the hands of those who have no real stake in things.
