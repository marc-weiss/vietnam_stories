I was a parent to two young boys & newly divorced at age 25. I was then (at age 25) drafted into the US Army. This was September, 1968.

My most vivid memory of the war is the sense of powerlessness which was with me constantly as a combat infantryman in the 199th Infantry.

That experience meant the ultimate in loss of control of my life and safety. It changed my life...My soul died in Vietnam, my hollow spiritless body returned home.

I was a disposable soldier in Vietnam, I came home and was called baby killer, noone welcomed me home. I didn't speak of my experience or the fact that I had been in Vietnam.

I became self-destructive, I wanted to die like those who had died in-country ( felt they got the better deal). I was not allowed to see my children (because they viewed me as crazy). My mother died not knowing what I did in Vietnam. I was apathetic to politics (THEY were going to do what they wanted to do). I came home to a job in the trades. No one at work even asked me about what had happened in the 2 years since they had seen me. I was told by a VA Doctor in 1990, that I was no longer able to work due to the symptoms of my diagnosis (PTSD). I often find myself thinking "Who is better off, those on the Wall (whose struggle with this war & this life is over) or me ( who's war continues each & every day & night) ? I am oftened visited by those comrades whose names appear on the Wall. This is not meant as a self-pity trip; just the reality (the facts) of my daily life since my body returned from Vietnam.

PLEASE SPEAK THE NAME(S)OUTLOUD OF THOSE WHO YOU REMEMBER AND WHOSE NAMES ARE ON THE WALL. THEY CAN BE ALIVE AGAIN; AT LEAST FOR THE MOMENT THEIR NAME IS SPOKEN OUTLOUD.

They answered America's call & that call silenced their voices. I will not forget them. Do you remember or do you forget?

Many other's voices have been being silenced by death after the war yet by the war still. I remember, please remember with me. Alone is such pain, together there is shared pain which is pain lessened by this sharing & support of each other's memories.

I have no lingering anger or resentment towards those citizens of Vietnam. I do have continuing hurt & distrust of my own government in these United States of America.

I finally was welcomed home in 1984 by a fellow soldier. We were at the Wall; I cried for the 1st time since the War. He said, "Welcome Home, Brother". He wore a 199th Infantry Brigade Patch.

I continue to visit the Wall. I go to visit the spirits of my fallen comrades and find a spiritual communion occurs with us. I continue to be absorbed by & into the Wall.

I speak the name of "Ross" kia plain of reeds 1969. I speak the name of "Ross", OUTLOUD !

James M. Spilman, Sr
1943
Cincinnati, Ohio, USA
