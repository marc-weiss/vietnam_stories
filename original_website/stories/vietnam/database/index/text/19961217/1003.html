My Brother, Ralph "Bill" William Hoffman died in July 1987 in the University of Pittsburgh medical research hospital. He was chronically ill with aplastic anemia secondary to chemical contamination. His death was the most tragic event that I had ever seen. It was a death by inches with a monumental struggle by Bill to live.

>From 1967 to 1970, Bill enlisted in the United States Navy as a Seabee. He was responsible for defoliation of landing strips in Vietnam. Bill used and was exposed to Agent Orange for extended periods as a routine task. This chemical herbicide so damaged the stem cells in his skeleton that Bill was not able to produce red blood cells. He was not able to resist infections and died at the age of 39. He mercifully, left no heirs nor was he acknowledged for his sacrifice to the United States and the people of your country.

No one in his command, among his shipmates nor among the manufacturers of this poison had the awareness or courage to warn him about the consequences. To my knowledge, no one among his work mates has been spared the grief and stress that they may die horribly, well before their time. I am bitter and can not resolve my grief over Bill's death.

You see, Bill did not have to go to Vietnam. He enlisted as a volunteer. He was decorated with a Purple Heart for a wound that he received while under attack. Bill did not believe that the military of that era was justified in their actions. He served anyway and died for his loyalty and service. His name is not on the Wall. Until now you, reader, are among the few who know what he gave and did; and, have a sense of the futility of his life. He was never the same after Vietnam.

You also know that many other once young men and women are living time bombs waiting to implode. Young men and women with damaged genes who may bear this legacy for many generations so that a few sick people could make a lot of money. I am so very sad and sorry if you are one of the affected people who is reading this message. May your God or any God spare you the slow, futile death that is the ending for anyone with this kind of illness.

If anyone remembers Bill, please leave me a message. I would appreciate any pictures or stories. Bill would not talk about the war, the helicopter dives, the drug running and the killing. He did me one huge favour. Bill threatened to break my legs if I ever signed on for any tour of Vietnam. Thank you dear Brother and rest in peace.

Duane Hoffman
