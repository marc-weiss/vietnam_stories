My dad went to Vietnam in the winter of 1969. He'd been gone at something called the Air War College for several months prior to that. I don't even like to think about what they taught him there. I turned thirteen while he was at the college. My mom and I watched the riots at the Chicago Democratic Convention and I knew something was terribly wrong. Martin Luther King, Jr., Bobby Kennedy, and then the riots. I hoped against hope that Dick Gregory would win the presidency on the Peace and Freedom Party ticket, but knew better. When Nixon won, I feared the worst. And we got the worst.

Dad came back from Nam in February 1970. We met him at the airport with balloons and hugs and kisses and a new sister. The second or third day back he criticized my clothing, stating that only hippie fags in San Francisco wore bellbottoms. Long hair was out of the question for me. A few days later, the Chicago Seven were convicted and a friend and I snuck down to DC for The Day After demonstrations. We left for Germany and my dad's new assignment in Frankfurt a week or so later.

Then, Cambodia was invaded and students murdered at Kent State. Lots of us students at the Junior High I attended were pissed off. And scared. What follows is a remembrance somewhat enhanced by time.

There must have been 150 of us. We stood in the school parking lot milling around nervously. The principal, a domineering sort who emphasized discipline over learning, repeated his message to return to class over his bullhorn. Some students, sensing his growing anger, wavered and headed hesitantly towards the building doors. Don't go, whispered those nearest the wavering ones. Most stayed, encouraged by a renewed sense of solidarity. The showdown continued for another five minutes or so. After all, if National Guard troops were killing our sisters and brothers in Ohio because they opposed the war, the least we could do is refuse to go to class. At least that was how the more politically aware of our classmates saw it. So, on May 6, 1970, a bunch of us wore black armbands and refused to go to home room when the bell sounded. The administration knew something was up. Hand drawn posters had appeared on school walls the afternoon of the massacre in Kent. Of course, they had been removed as quickly as they appeared. Some of the teachers were sympathetic, putting aside time in their classes to discuss the world situation. Others weren't. One shop teacher physically threatened one boy after he overheard the student call Nixon a fascist pig in a lunchroom conversation. It was that incident which sparked the protest. The forces of reaction had sparked a response. By the end of school on May 5th, every student knew the scenario for the following morning. Wear an armband to school and refuse to go to class. The antiwar students prepared themselves for their show of conviction and the reactionaries prepared themselves for a fight. The administration, meanwhile, informed the military police since the school was, after all, on a military base. In fact, the school had served as a Nazi prison for women in an earlier incarnation during the Third Reich. Like most of the U.S. military buildings in Frankfurt am Main, it had been saved from the relentless bombing by the allies which destroyed most of the rest of the city. Rumor had it that Eisenhower wanted the military edifices saved for the occupation forces. Which they were. So, after the Nazis were defeated, the Americans moved in. Just like they were doing on that spring day in 1970. In a metaphor so obvious we couldn't ignore it, we stood in the cool morning opposing a military attack by American soldiers in Kent, Ohio on their fellow citizens. When the MP's jeeps rolled into the walled-in compound and the soldiers jumped out, we stood our ground a few more seconds and dispersed. Later in the day, the principal threatened to suspend all the participants. No one was ever suspended, though.
