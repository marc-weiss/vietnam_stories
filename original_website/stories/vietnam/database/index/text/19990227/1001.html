Iserved in the Air Force from 72-76. The moral was low and most of the guys I knew including me did any drug we could find everyday.The war was over and now I guess everyone was ticked about the outcome. Nobody cared about doing a good job.The attitudes were bad and I remember at my first permanent duty station alot of former E-3,& E-4s that had been to Thailand had been busted for drugs and were awaiting general discharges. As they were older than me, I looked up to them and adopted their attitudes. This followed me through till 1976 when I was Hon. discharged. When I got out I had a sense of relief and started to work and not utill about 1984 did I have anything to do with Veteran's organizations. I had a drinking problem which I acquired while in the service and also after being in the Phillipines, I picked up a bad idea that women were mostly for sex and so my marriage to a white woman failed. Not untill then did I start to feel guilty about missing the war after talking to veterans at the service clubs. I felt I really missed out on the big adventure of being called a Vietnam vet. I could never be in their club. But just recently I had the opportunity to read a book about my friends experience in the Marines . He is black, and he said that at seventeen years old he enlisted and was sent ot Vietnam in 1965 to DaNang. so after reading his book and his experiences, I decided that to have any guilt for not being in the Nam is totally absurd. If I had been their, no doubt I would have been killed as I was pretty naive at 18. He told me that the only ones who have no guilt are the names on the wall. I have in the past mourned the deaths of those who went but really they might be the most fortunate. That is if you believe in what Jesus said about the dead, all the dead soldiers and civilians alike. HE said at John 5:28-29 that all those in the memorial tombs will hear his voice and come out. So we do mourn our dead but do not give up the hope of seeing all those on the wall alive again. Many fellow friends and veterans of all the wars have now realized the condition of the dead and their hope for future life. Revelation 21:3,4 expresses GOD'S promise of a new world free from war and death. So now that I am in my mid 40's I do read alot about the Nam vet experiences.Their are personal stories on the web, photos, reunions, vets looking for vets, etc.It's almost like we are trying to recapture our youth by going back.Unfortunately the memmories are not always pleasant but I sense alot of pride from some and they deserve respect for the decisions that they made so long ago.

Andrew W. McGovern
1953
