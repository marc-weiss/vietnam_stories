Halesite

"I'd like to hang that guy," I said to Brett the night before we had to register for the Vietnam war. We were walking along the seawall toward the statue of Nathan Hale that stood and watched over Halesite. It stood near the beach in honor of the Revolutionary War hero who immortalized the words, "I regret that I have but one life to lose for my country." The statue's eyes stared like a sentry. Year after year Nathan Hale stood there, staring out into the bay.

"I'm not going," said Brett. His hair hung over his shoulders. His eyes were black like his hair. Every weekend he listened to psychedelic music in his bedroom, where posters of peace rallies hung on the wall. He said he wished he had gone to Woodstock.

I walked past the statue over to the playground and sat on one of the swings. I sat between its chains that were not nearly as long as I remembered. Brett sat on the swing next to me. His knees stuck up too far, so he pushed away and tucked his legs under the seat to swing back and forth.

"Do you remember hurricane Irene?" I said. The road by the beach had flooded so deep that Brett and I made rafts out of the floating boardwalks.

"Yeah, we didn't know hurricanes had an eye. We should've drowned."

"Nathan Hale saved us," I said. "We clung to him for dear life when the storm swept us over. I could've sworn he said something in the rain that day."

"He called us damn fools, is what he said."

Whistling came from the street beyond the grass. Bottles were clinking and laughter stirred the air. A silhouette of a gang marched toward us.

"Are you guys going to the induction center tomorrow?"

"Yeah, we're going," I said. "Got to. It's the law."

A guy twisted the cap off a whiskey bottle. "We're gonna kick some ass." Beer cans fizzled open. "No one beats Americans." They poured out shots and cursed the Vietnamese.

Brett walked over to the statue and sat on its base. I followed him over and sat down next to him. We talked about the time a mousetrap snapped in my hands, and how Brett pulled the metal hoop off my finger while two eyes stared down at us from the rear view mirror of a Cadillac.

"What are you doing?" said his mom, who took us out shopping with her.

"Nothing. We're just playing," said Brett, who put a finger over his lips.

After we got to Brett's house a police car pulled in the driveway. A cop rang the bell and spoke to his mom who ran upstairs. She grabbed the mousetraps and dragged us downstairs with an apology to the officer. She swore we'd get the punishment we deserved.

"See you do that, maa'm," said the officer. "I'd hate to see them youngsters get the wrong idea about gettin' away with somethin', if you know what I mean. Someone's got to be watchin', ya' know, to make sure they do the right thing."

Homes in Halesite looked down over sloping lawns manicured for the summer. The boys who lived there got out of the draft during the last war - so the stories went - and Halesite would

never lose one of her sons in another war. Everyone swore to that. That's why my parents bought a home on Long Island not far from Brett's house. My dad said war was hell and I could tell he was right from all the movies about Guadalcanal on our big t.v. with the little screen.

"Do you want to go for a swim?" I asked Brett who was leaning on the statue of Nathan Hale. We used to swim around the caves near the seawall. We would sit in the caves until the tide reached our feet, then swim up into the surf like Jonah from the whale's belly.

"The water's the same over there," said Brett.

"But there's no summer like here," I said. From out in the bay we could see white cliffs that we named Dover Beach. On top of Dover Beach Brett and I used to pretend we were Lewis and Clark. We would climb up Dover Beach just to stand on the ridge and look out.

"There's no winter like here either," said Brett. Every winter the coast froze into foam that stretched over the caves like a shroud, and every winter Nathan Hale shivered under his skin of ice. We used to throw stones at the statue - to shatter the ice into wedges that cascaded down and stuck in the snow like daggers.

"Them peace-nicks ought to be shot," said one of the gang in the playground.

"If I had a gun I'd shoot him right now."

"Hippy bum."

"Traitor."

Brett wasn't paying any attention. He stood up and clung to the statue. Fog rolled in from the sea and wrapped around them as they stared out into the bay, arm in arm.

"Let's play army," said one of the gang to the others.

"Maybe we ought to go," I said to Brett.

"I'm not going," he said. The statue leaned forward in the fog.

I backed away, away from the statue and the approaching gang.

"Fight for the red, white, and blue, fellas."

"Yes, sir," they said.

One slapped Nathan Hale. "Is this your buddy, hippy boy?" The statue turned in the mist.

"He don't hear you."

"Hey, hippy boy, ain't you gonna fight?"

"If you know what's right, you'll come and fight, unless you're chicken."

"Are you chicken, hippy boy?" The statue's arms moved in the mist.

"He don't hear nothin'."

"Maybe he's dead."

"Are you stupid, or what?"

"Can't you say nothin', hippy boy?"

Brett spoke Nathan Hale's immortal words.

"Get him to shut up, boys."

"You will if ya' know what's good for ya'."

"You damn fool."

"Teach him a lesson, boys."

A wave of fog rolled over the soldiers who were hollering curses at the hippy boy and his statue. One soldier threw sand at Nathan Hale. Another whipped him with his belt while others urinated on him. Nathan's arms held Brett and Brett held Nathan. The two men melted into one iron body. Only Brett's face rose from under the statue's embrace as the gang surrounded them in a sea of mist. A whiskey bottle floated between their hands, around and around like in a relay race. It flew against the statue and broke into fragments.

From the seawall I yelled, "Run for it, Brett." He turned to me. His face was pale and his eyes glowed behind strands of drooping hair. He yelled that he wasn't going. Another bottle whizzed like a rocket and hit Brett's head. Glass splintered on the face of Nathan Hale. Blood streamed down the uniform of the big statue. His big arms lost their grip, and Brett fell beside the old hero's boots.

A siren screamed in the distance. The gang froze. The wail grew louder as red and blue lights reflected off the statue's metal bulk.

"Run for it!"

I ran over to Brett and carried him to the seawall where I jumped onto the caves and sat him down. Waves splashed against us.

"It's time for that swim after all," he said.

"Tomorrow we'll climb Dover Beach," I said. "We'll stand on the ridge and look out. Then we'll see what Nathan Hale sees. We'll see that we're not going. We'll see that we belong here. We'll see it, won't we, Brett?" Two flashlights combed around the statue. The twin arcs circled further and further out from Nathan Hale. I ducked behind the seawall.

"Brett?" I dug into the dark water around my legs. I fanned out further and further. Deeper and deeper into the surf I waded. All along the line of caves I splashed about. Back and forth I traced my watery steps, scraping the bottom with my fingers. Still I felt nothing, just the seaweed growing like hair over the rocks.
