My father was in the Vietnam War and he's told me many stories that have impacted my life and the way I look up to and admire him. He wrote me this letter:

"Dear Scott, In July 1968 I was attending a University summer session when I received notice from the U.S. Navy reserve Unit in Fort Wayne, Indiana that our division was being called to active duty. I reported to Treasure Island Naval Base in San Francisco for active duty in September. At that time I received my orders for Navy Special Forces deployment to Viet Nam. I was 21 years old and one of the older sailors in my unit. As a Naval Reservist I was classified as a Personnelman which meant I did Basically administrative tasks in an office. Once I was on active duty status in San Francisco I was reassigned, or as we like to call it in the Navy- "volunteered", to a Navy Special Forces combat unit. I was assigned to the River Patrol Boat Unit as a Gunnersmate and promoted to 3rd Class Petty Officer. Basically my job was to be trained and have expertise in all hand held and mounted gunnery weapons used in combat in Viet Nam. I was deployed as a Rear Gunner on the River Patrol Boats in the Mekong Delta Region of South Viet Nam. We patrolled rivers and canals along the Cambodian border trying to stop the Viet Cong and North Vietnamese from advancing throughout South Viet Nam during the day. At night we would set up ambush sites along the rivers and canals to stop troop and supply movements coming from North Viet Nam. Most of our combat that we faced was at night. It was very difficult to see, maneuver the boat and deal with the dense jungle-like foliage all around us. Most of the canals we patrolled were no wider than a two-lane road with dense foliage on both sides. I manned a mounted 50-caliber machine gun and grenade launcher in the rear part of the patrol boat. The boats were 31 feet long and made of fiberglass much like a large pleasure boat. Additionally, we had a forward gunner, an enginman and coxswain on my boat. My boat crew went on 105 combat missions with about 60 of those receiving enemy fire of one from or another......."

This explains my father's job in Viet Nam. He also wrote me another article about one of the times he was wounded.

"November 12th, 1969 was a rather uneventful day at a Vietnam Army Outpost. I was stationed with a squad of 9 U.S. Army advisors at the outpost with my primary duties as Radio Operator. Because I spoke Vietnamese, I was the communications link between the Patrol Boats manned by the Vietnamese Navy and U.S. ground and air support. I had heard through the Vietnamese intelligence officer at the outpost for several days that we were going to be attacked by the Viet Cong Guerilla "Sappers." "Sappers" are somewhat like Kamikazes as they carry explosives in satchel and sometimes blow themselves up trying to kill their enemies. Night brought on heavy activity on the river as 6 of the Patrol Boats had engaged in heavy firefights. I was coordinating ground and air support at 3 different battle locations. Suddenly the fighting ended and I got up from my desk and walked to the kitchen area of the team house to get a drink. Just as I sat down a group of sappers attacked our team house throwing satchel charges in the back door by the desk where I was sitting a minute earlier. I was blown about 15 to 20 feet across the room and knew I was wounded. As I lay on the floor my only thought was to get out of there before they came in to finish us off. I was wounded in the knee, leg, hand, and foot but not badly. I grabbed my rifle and ammo belts and worked my way outside to head for our semi-underground bunker. It was the longest 50 yards I ever had to run. I was met at the opening of the bunker by one of the Army guys who came out to help me. Just as he grabbed me the Viet Cong opened fir on us. He went limp and fell to the ground tripping me also. I crawled the last 15 feet to the bunker dragging him behind me. We finally got inside the bunker and the Army guy woke up screaming about his head. He didn't have a mark as the bullet went through his helmet and ricocheted off his helmet liner, basically giving him a concussion only. The doc patched me up while I was on the radio to Vietnamese Army Outpost leaders. Strangely, no one answered. About 30 minutes later the Medi-vac chopper took me out to the base hospital. As I was carried through the outpost not a Vietnamese Army soldier was in sight. I found out later that they knew the attack was coming and they deserted the outpost and us several hours before the attack. That night was the second time I had been wounded. I was cared not so much of dying but being captured. One of the things I took away from my Navy Special Forces Training was that I did not want to be a P.O.W. It was so dark that night and really hot. The darkness compiled with the smoke from the explosions enabled me to get out of the team house unseen, which probably saved my life. I was angry and frustrated. I was there risking my life for them but they wouldn't ever tell us the attack was coming or help us fight back. As I said earlier, I was not seriously hurt and returned to my post a few days later. Unfortunately I lost all respect and trust for the Vietnamese Army stationed at the outpost. They never even came close to our area and would walk away when they saw us coming. I still to this day will never understand why they deserted us that night."

My father received two purple hearts and i'm so proud of him for that. God bless you Dad!

Scott Hamilton
1983
Ohio

A son's hero- His Father
