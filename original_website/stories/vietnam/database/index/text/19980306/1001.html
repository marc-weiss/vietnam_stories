I was 17 in 1965, a senior in high school; trying to figure out what to do with my life. A Marine recruiter came to our town and school, dressed in his "dress blues"; and telling our class about the war in a small country in Southeast Asia. He told us that we could enlist through the buddy system and join with our best friends. Frank and I did.

By the time recruit training completed that fall; newspapers and radio were talking about the atrocities being performed on American Marines killed in action. We were all pumped up; ready to ship out and show the communists that they couldn't desicrate our fellow warriors that way. After all,we were there to help a people that wanted our protection.Weren't we?

Flew into DaNang, picked up our gear; and joined a convoy headed North for DongHa. My first awakening came the second day in country when I learned how little the people we were their to "protect", felt about the importance of life. An old mama son was walking along a train track with a small child in tow. A train was coming down the track and the people scattered. the train only moving 5-10 mph. The child tripped, and when the old woman couldn't get the child to stand...she just walked away and left the child on the tracks. We watched from the highway as the train failed to brake, struck the child, kept going and the old woman went on her way. For more than 20 months in country, I wondered every day what we were trying to protect a people from who seemed to have such little regard for their own life.

During that 20 months, a high school classmate died in combat, my cousin Mike was killed in a VietCong ambush and I collected pieces of metal with my body. My life was changed forever.

We came home to a country which was profoundly partioned and changed. we left as warriors to perform our duty with honor as our country and it's leaders asked; and we returned, betrayed by our people, our National leaders and even our military leadership. I still wonder what we were tryng to protect a people from.............but as an American, I would serve my county and do my duty again.

B.J. Wright
1948
