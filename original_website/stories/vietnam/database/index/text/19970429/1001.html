I have a wbesite devoted to the MIA issue. It gets a lot of attention and from time to time people will ask me questions about serving in the Marine Corps during the Vietnam Era. Recently I received a letter from a young man who was doing a paper about the Vietnam Era for his history class... I wrote back to him and CC'd a young friend who'd asked the same question many times but never received an answer from me. It is she who suggested I share the story... Here is my response to Nick...

Nick: I'm flattered that you ask...Truth is I very rarely discuss Vietnam...since my insignificant part in that war isn't worth much space...and I know too many guys who have much more to tell... My page was a sort of therapy for me...my feelings about the war would be of little use to anyone...

My most distressing memory however, is of something that took place shortly after reporting to my first permanent duty station after Vietnam. I was assigned to the Marine Barracks in Brooklyn, New York, and was able to commute from my home in New Jersey. On my way home one night, I decided to stop in a bar that I had frequented before enlisting in the Marines in 1968. I had a Marine Corps haircut and I was wearing a windbreaker that said "USMC" across the back and it had "Vietnam 69-71" on the front. I took a seat at the end of the bar and ordered a beer and a sandwich. It was a typical college hangout with its share of hippies but most of my friends looked like hippies and they were all okay. I was by myself and I just felt like watching people having fun...just like I used to do a couple of years earlier.

I was half way through a great sandwich, enjoying the music and thinking about getting one of their famous 18" hoagies to take home for my kid brother...(he was about 9 at the time and I was his hero...) I felt good about being home, being a Marine, being able to eat a real sandwich instead of mess hall chow... Then when the music stopped, this guy about 20 walks up to the bar, orders a drink and tells the bartender "Give one to the Baby Killer too...and ask him to drink it somewhere else!"

I felt some anger right then but what I felt more was hurt. It was a complete shock...I'd been sitting there just enjoying myself, not even talking to anyone and this guy (who I probably could have made mince meat out of) comes over and insults me in a voice everyone in the place heard...I didn't know how to react (I don't even remember wanting to hurt him...I always wanted to get along with everybody).

I just turned and looked at him in surprise...then he said: "What are you gonna do, hit me? Would that make you feel like a man?"

It was completely uncalled for...I was alone with a mouth full of roast beef on rye and suddenly there's a crowd around us egging him on, waiting for me to do something...Then this greasy haired, unwashed girl in a peasant blouse and bell bottoms comes up and puts an arm around him and says to me: "Why don't you just leave...we don't want your kind around here!"

What could I do besides wreck the place, break a few heads and teach them a lesson? No...I picked up my change and walked out...and I listened to them laughing as I went through the door...

I got in my car and drove home...I hurt so bad inside I wanted to cry...

26 years later I still hear them laughing...I can recall every detail right down to the colors of their eyes...I can hear the girl's nasal tone and I can see the spot of mustard that the guy had on his chin...I can tell you how many pickle slices and potato chips were left on my plate...I can hear the cash register ringing and I can see the bartender standing there trying to ignore what was happening...he was wearing a paisley shirt with a high-roll collar and white jeans with a wide belt. He had facial tic.

I can remember how my hands started to shake and my voice caught in my throat... And I can still feel the pain...I can remember not being able to tell anyone because I suddenly didn't trust anyone and I didn't believe anyone would understand how I felt...

I still don't know why it happened...and I don't know why it still hurts so much... I wonder if the other people who were in the bar that night ever think about it... I wonder if that guy and his girlfriend have any idea how much their exercise of free speech affected me for the rest of my life...even till today... I wonder if they have any idea how the recurring pain of that brief moment in time came back to me over and over and over...how it made me feel like crying...how it affected my sense of self-worth...

I'd like to see them all again someday...I just want to ask them why they did that to a stranger who never did anything except stop in his old hangout and have a sandwich...

Well, Nick, I'm sorry I can't tell you what it was like being in the Marines during Vietnam...truth is, that one incident has colored every good memory I have ...

I'm sure you'd never consider doing something like that...I can tell from the tone of your letter...but if you have to give an oral report on the war, be sure to tell your classmates that American Servicemen...soldiers, sailors, Marines and airmen, are proud warriors who were once high school students just like them...They heard the call and answered it because they feel it's the right and honorable thing to do.

Tell them that it feels good to wear the uniform and to do that special job and to be proud of the military tradition that protects the country and our way of life.

And tell them that with all the sense of pride and honor and loyalty, it still hurts to be held up to ridicule by those same people they are sworn to give their lives for.

Take care Nick, Gunny

George "Gunny" Fallon
1947
New Jersey
