Purple, Heart

The late-night flight to D.C. had been a spur of the moment thing, though I'd promised myself for a long time of the need to go. To pay a debt, I said, as it that could in some small way tip the balance for all those who served and never came back. That afternoon I stopped at a pawn shop to buy the medal. They guaranteed it was genuine. Someone had shed their blood to earn it, though there was no way of knowing for sure. I paid a few dollars and turned it over in my palm, an insignificant token. But it had to be done.

Crossing Constitution Avenue: I walked down the grassy slope facing the Washington Monument. It was not quite sunrise but others were already gathered there, the daily witnessing. And before them, stretched toward the brightening sky, were those black granite slabs in two long walls that angled out of the ground as if this were one of the earth's corners.

In that early light I imagined those one-hundred and forty slabs as television screens, neatly lined up and nearly blank, but with something etched across them, a blurred image difficult to make out like late at night after stations sign off and there's only static to stare at. They were the way I saw that war, those screens. The only way that most of us ever saw it. So how could we really know? What it was like, I mean, from so little that we could see?

Someone was leaning against the wall, trying to decipher it. I edged myself up to a slab, not wanting to get too close at first, thinking it didn't belong to me. I could see them then, the names, the dozens of chiseled names, their small hollows that I could focus on at once. But nearby there were hundreds more. And beyond them, stretching away into the dawn, thousands and tens of thousands of shadowed pits, battered targets on a firing range. Their precisely straight or curved letters splayed out in disciplined rows, a vertical landscape of fifty-eight thousand one hundred and ninety-one names, compelling us to touch, to read them with our fingers as if by a sort of braille the oblation of it would finally soak into our being.

Blindly, I reached for one, James A. McMurdo, and, for a moment, wondered if anyone had ever come searching for him - a friend, wife, mother, son - a name special to someone that I'd found while not really looking for anyone.

Slipping across the stone, my fingers probed other shadows: Billy Lee Tobias, Jessie L. Johnson, David A. Land, Jose H. Velasquez, Edward S. Yamashiro, Timothy R., Joy L., Augustus, Thomas, Kenneth, John, the names and names of those who died those days, those weeks, those months, perhaps, as we sat at home and watched T.V. or mowed our lawns and drank cold beer.

There were mementos: flowers, old photos, a toy truck, a baseball,, shrines to remembering. Between two slabs a note was jammed in the crack, "Bob, I bring a message to you from Sandy. You'll always be her love." Another, with a photo, "Who would have known that I would grow up to look like someone I never even knew?" And nearby, "Damn you brother for not coming back."

I was suddenly aware of a dreadful weight, those black granite slabs, like a jolt of gravity; and that travail, we who never died, is to stand and remember so that the names would be etched forever into our skin. This , the medal we must wear and never take off.

It was for those burdened with remembering that I had brought the Purple Hearts. This was, it seemed, all that I had come to do. I pinned it to one of the notes, which one it didn't matter, then turned and walked away.
