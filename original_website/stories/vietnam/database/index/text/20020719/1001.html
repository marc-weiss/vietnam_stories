My most vivid memory of the Vietnam War is a jacket I received for my fourth birthday in 1968. Though I don’t remember receiving it, I later remember wearing it as a child. I continued to do so until my growth began to tear its seams, much like the death of the brother who gave it to me was torn from my memory. I struggled to learn of my brother Joe throughout the years and through all that time I kept that jacket. That and letters he wrote from Vietnam are the only tangible evidence of a memory stolen from me by war. For reasons I still can’t explain, I joined the Marines and went to boot camp just prior to the dedication of the Vietnam Memorial Wall. At the time, I didn’t even know it was being erected! I wouldn’t make it there until 1996. Perhaps I was more of a man by this time, but as I stared at the Wall, I still felt like the little brother that received the jacket so many years before. Shortly after that visit to the Wall, I embarked on the final leg of my lifelong journey. I determined to tell my brother’s story and learn all I could about him. I met my brother’s friends, talked with my family and through time I began to know the man that endured hell, but took the time to remember a brother waiting for him at home. I traveled halfway around the world in the hopes of seeing the land where my brother died. I never made it to Vietnam, but in trying, I met my wife, learned the true meaning of friendship and realized that it was time to carry on with my life; pursuing the happiness that my brother and many others fought and died for. I completed my brother’s story and when it was finished, I realized I also told my story. Joe and I fought our separate wars; his with a gun and mine, now with a pen. It was important to tell the world how the death of one man in one war affected many lives. As for the jacket, it now adorns the cover of the recently published story written as result of my brother’s death in Vietnam. War affects more than just those on the battlefield and as I sit and watch the battles around the world, I hope with all my heart that one day our world will know peace.

William Roble
1964
Costa Mesa, CA. USA
