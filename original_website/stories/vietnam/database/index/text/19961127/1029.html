I am not a Vietnam vet. I was a child during the Vietnam War. My next door neighbor went to Vietnam when he was 20 years old. I was 10 years old at the time. He and I used to play catch-baseball in his driveway a lot. One day he told me he was going away. He never said where. I found out later that day that he was going to Vietnam. As a ten year old, the only thing I knew about Vietnam was that my father watched this war on TV news every night.

When my neighbor, Phillip, showed up at our door one summer day in his military uniform, I remember wishing I had a uniform just like he did. He came to say goodbye to me and my dad. My dad was a WWII vet. I remember him waving to us from his mother's car as he left for the airport.

When Phillip returned from Vietnam over a year later, he was not the Phillip I remembered playing catch with. He was sad looking, and uncommunicative. I asked him if he saw many dead bodies over there. He nodded yes, but never said where or when he saw them. I was 11 years old when I asked this question.

One day he and I were playing catch, and I asked him if he had been shot over there. He said that the only injury he received was when he scraped his arm against a barb-wire fence where he had been playing football with his buddies. In my 11 year old's mind, I knew his injury was more than a scraped arm. His dark eyes said more than that to me. I felt sad just looking at him.

I know that Phillip works for the Post Office today. He's married, and has children. This is not much a story to an adult audience, but to me as an 11 year old, playing catch-baseball with Phillip was a bid deal. And when he returned from Vietnam with all his sadness, I knew I had lost a buddy too.
