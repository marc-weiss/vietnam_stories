The following is an excerpt chapter from a little booklet I wrote in 1998 giving my perspective on the Vietnam War. Chapters vary from "Adminstrative Duties" to "Loneliness" to "The Wall." I will provde more information later if desired. Also, I will address your question on how I am today.

I served as a PFC in Troop A's infantry platoon in the Central Highlands of South Vietnam in 1970. The mission of an Air Cavalry unit was to conduct aerial reconnaissance for enemy activity such as the infiltration of North Vietnamese Army (NVA) soldiers into South Vietnam, their stockpiling of food and weapons supplies, and hostile actions against US forces. Each troop has one infantry platoon called an aero rifle platoon which was dependent upon the helicopter for mobility rather than the typical infantry ground pounding. Its main function was to conduct standard infantry ground operations once the troop's light observation helicopters (LOHs) had either made contact with the enemy, discovered enemy stockpiles of supplies, or located friendly units which were disabled or downed by enemy fire or mechanical failure. The platoon would be inserted onto the ground to search and destroy, recover friendly KIAs or survivors of downed aircraft, and/or reconnoiter.

Typical Day1 A typical day in the aero rifle platoon's activities included the following:

6 - 7 AM Breakfast at the Camp Holloway Troop A mess hall.

7 - 8 AM Load onto the Huey helicopters for flight to a standby area near the area of operations (AO). The standby area might be an opening in the jungle which had room for the helicopters to maneuver, at another base camp LZ, at a fire base, or at an abandoned landing strip such at Dak To. The AO was whatever geographical area had been assigned to the unit to scout for enemy activity. (See Appendix A.)

9 - 4 PM Standby with the Huey helicopters and crews and wait for action. During this time the infantry soldiers would sit around sharpening knives, discussing various matters, playing touch football, reading newspapers, etc. Frequently the newest personnel, who were called newbees, would receive training such as rappelling from a helicopter, the basics of a mini-gun on a LOH, how to fasten a nylon webbed strip to a downed helicopter for salvage operations, or how to load rockets onto the Cobra gunships. Once the radio call was received from the command helicopter flying above the AO, we would each quickly put on our shirts, gear, and check our weapons and jump onto our helicopters for an insertion into the AO for action. Basically it was one squad of 5 - 6 men per helicopter. Most insertions were into cold landing zones which meant that enemy fire was not anticipated, but regardless we always had a high amount of adrenaline flowing prior to an insertion. You never knew what to expect, and you usually expected a hot LZ with hostile fire.

Noon C-ration lunch.

4 - 6 PM Return to Camp Holloway.

6 - 7 PM Eat supper at the Camp Holloway mess hall.

7 PM - 7 AM Pull guard duty on the bunker line at Camp Holloway.

Aero Rifle Platoon

Without a doubt, I was fortunate to have served in an aero rifle platoon rather than an regular infantry ground company which spent weeks in the field patrolling on foot for the enemy. Their tactics were certainly higher risk and under much more deplorable conditions. However, I believe that man for man, in action, the aero rifle platoon was more productive than the regular ground units for at least two good reasons: we were well rested and we were equipment intensive.

When we were inserted into a combat situation, we were relatively more rested than an infantry ground unit that had been patrolling for weeks without a break and we always had our own equipment. I am positive that an aero rifle platoon had more equipment per soldier than a regular infantry unit. For example, below is a list of the Troop A helicopters:

Type of helicopters Number Purpose Light Observation Helicopters 10 Aerial reconnaissance (LOH)

Cobra Gunships 9 Weapons

Huey Helicopters 8 Transport of infantry.

Not only did the LOHs provide us with excellent visual reconnaissance from 100 to 200 feet height, but with their 7.65 mm mini-guns and 2,100 rounds, they could cover an area the size of a football field with machine gun fire. In addition to the pilot, each LOH had an aerial observer, i.e. scout, who frequently had been an infantryman. The scout carried a M-60 machine gun, but could carry a M-16 if he wanted, and would shoot from the side of the LOH as he leaned out observing for enemy activity. The Cobras had mini-guns with 4,000 round, 350 40mm grenades, and 52 2.75" rockets to support us on the ground.2

As an additional safeguard, we operated within range of an artillery firebase (this was probably standard operating practice for all infantry units in Vietnam), and we also required a company sized "reactionary force" to be available to provide us with relief in the event we needed it.3 Since an aero rifle platoon was well rested, had plenty of firepower, and was supported by a reactionary force, we were well motivated and confident that we could handle any combat situation. We were a cohesive unit with a high amount of camaraderie.

In each 5 to 6 man infantry squad, there was one M60 machine gunner, one M79 grenade launcher, one radio telephone operator (RTO), and the remainder would be M16 riflemen. For each 18 to 24 man aero rifle platoon there was also a medic, usually referred to as "Doc". Frequently we also had a Kit Carson Scout 4 and a German shepherd dog with handler.

Each soldier carried two water canteens (sometimes we would carry quart water bags which I described in one of my tape recordings to my wife, Nancy, as reminding me of our baby son when I cradled the bag in my arms), two hand grenades, and a belt of M60 machine gun ammo. The M16 riflemen also carried two bandoleers of M16 ammo which amounted to approximately 300 rounds. The RTO also carried 4 or 5 smoke grenades which were fastened to the 20 pound radio that was carried on the back. The M79 gunner carried a 45 caliber handgun. Each soldier carried a compress pad in the event he was wounded and needed it to stop the bleeding.

As the helicopters approached the landing zone (LZ), we would prepare to disembark as quickly as possible. This was accomplished by standing on the skid of the helicopter and jumping to the ground just as the helicopter hovered 3 to 4 feet above the ground. I never saw the helicopter skids touch the ground either in LZs or pickup zones (PZ). There was no hesitation, because the helicopter could not hover too long unloading the soldiers or it would risk being shot down. The entire episode took only a matter of seconds, but it felt like an eternity.

Once we were on the ground, each squad had a certain area of the LZ to cover. We would each be on the edge of the LZ and spaced 20 to 30 feet apart looking outward into the jungle. There would be one sergeant in the LZ directing the helicopters in for their insertion of infantrymen. The helicopter pilots had to be careful and ensure that the helicopter blades did not hit the tall jungle trees. Once all four squads were on the ground, we would organize into a patrol unit with the squads lined up one after the other -- one squad would be point squad, two would be in the middle, and one would bring up the end. Spacing between the individual soldiers would vary but it would be 10 to 30 feet depending on the terrain and the density of the undergrowth. Each soldier's weapon would be pointed outward into the jungle with every other soldier pointing his weapon in a different direction.

Patrolling Below are a few of my personal observations and sensations felt during patrolling operations:

* I did not worry much about being shot or injured, especially when I was a newbee, because I thought that if I were wounded then I might end up back in the States with my wife and son. At the same time, I had a sense of security because of the ammunition bandoleers which I carried across my chest, my steel pot on my head, and the radio on my back. I knew that I could take either a facial shot or an abdominal shot which hopefully would be fatal, but at least I would not be in Vietnam. I was not too worried about my legs and arms. Today I look back on this and think how foolish I was then. Of course, when you see dead people around you, then you do understand fear, but it is more an appreciation for being alive than fearing death.

* When covering a LZ, PZ, or patrolling, each infantryman was responsible for the area immediately in front of him or on either side. He needed to "stay home" and watch those areas and not be distracted by what might be occurring in front of him or to the rear of him. There might be firing somewhere else, but you must cover your area of responsibility as a member of the team. You did not run up to the front or to the source of the firing unless ordered to do so -- or unless common sense told you that was the best course of action. Each of us depended on the other to perform his job. I was not worried about my back, because I knew that other solders were performing their job. I was protecting them and they were protecting me.

* When patrolling across an open area such as a rice paddy (even in the Central Highlands in the middle of nowhere on the edge of the jungle, there might be rice paddies), I felt exposed and vulnerable to enemy fire. I frequently imagined the enemy looking at us from the cover of the jungle and lining us up in their rifle sights. As I walked through the foot deep water, I knew that if we did take fire, there was no cover. Fortunately we never took fire in such a situation, and I attribute that to our LOHs which were hovering around us watching for potential ambushes or snipers.

* When patrolling on a jungle trail, you might see punja sticks poking up out of the ground. These sticks were about 9" to 12" long and were usually covered with animal or human excrement which would cause severe infections. You knew that if there were an ambush or we took fire that there would be no place to take cover except directly on the trail because of the punja sticks.

* When taking fire (i.e., being shot at by the enemy), I would quickly drop down and lay prone on the ground. I was always amazed how an infantryman found the lowest and best spot on the ground for cover from the hostile fire.5

* If the NVA had been operating in an area, and especially if they had been bivouacked in a camp for a period of time, you could actually smell their presence. I think it might have been a type of melon which they ate that left a certain odor, but after a few encounters, you learned the smell. My first experience with this was when the Troop A LOHs discovered a small NVA camp about 5-6 miles from Camp Holloway. This discovery was surprising because Camp Holloway and Pleiku are basically located on a plateau in the Central Highlands, and you would not anticipate finding a major enemy camp so close to major US forces and in scattered jungled terrain. Normally the enemy was in the hills or closer to the Cambodian border moving along the Ho Chi Minh Trail. Well, in this particular insertion, the NVA had departed once they were discovered and all we found was an empty camp, half eaten melons, and signs of very recent occupation. And the odor of the enemy was very strong.

Once an operation was completed there was always the relief and satisfaction of completing the mission, but more importantly the feeling of being alive. The only sensation I can compare it to is the feeling a person experiences in a near miss automobile accident. The difference may be that the adrenaline flow occurred earlier in the combat mission during the initial insertion from the helicopter, followed by a lull in a cold LZ (i.e., no hostile fire) or by another flow of adrenaline if you take fire, and then a lull in the PZ when you are lifting off in the helicopter and the breeze from the helicopter's blades cool you down. In a near miss automobile accident, you experience one significant flow of adrenaline and then recover relatively quickly. I was fortunate that since I was in an aero-rifle platoon, I only experienced these adrenaline flows over a 2 - 6 hour period depending on the length of the insertion. I can only imagine what the US Marine defenders of Khe Sanh must have felt in 1968 when they were surrounded and bombarded by artillery for over a month.
