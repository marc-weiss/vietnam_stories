VIETNAM 1968

F-100 PILOT. I was a career Air Force officer from 1960 to 1990. Much of that 30 years was spent as a political science professor at the AF Academy in Colorado, the Defense Intelligence College in Washington, and the National War College, also in Washington. But during the 1960's, I was a fighter pilot and flew F-100's out of Bien Hoa Air Base in 1968 where I celebrated my 30th birthday (and out of Takhli AB, Thailand in 1963 where I celebrated my 25th). Almost all of my combat missions were in South Vietnam, mostly II, III and IV Corps. Often it was, as they called it, TIC, or "Troops In Contact." There were a number of wings of F-100's in Vietnam and the F- 100 Supersabre flew more strike missions, by a wide margin, than any other aircraft in the war. Virtually all in-country. Although we took a lot of small arms fire in the south, we did not face the heavy anti-aircraft fire or SAM missiles that pilots had to contend with over North Vietnam. We would roll into our diving passes from 10,000 feet and pull out, usually, above 2000 feet, with delivery speeds of 450 knots (over 500mph). So it really took a very lucky hit from small arms fire, a "golden BB," as we used to say to cause trouble. My plane was hit once in the wingtip but I didn't even know it.

JUST ANOTHER ASSIGNMENT. Contrary to the beliefs of some, Vietnam was not a traumatic experience for all who served there. By 1968, I had been flying "huns" (F-100's) for eight years and was a graduate of the USAF Fighter Weapons Instructor, or "top gun," School. For me, Vietnam was another assignment to another fighter squadron, my third. Like my brethren fighter pilots, I had been flying jets for some time and dropping practice bombs nearly every day on some little AF bombing range in Arizona, California, North Carolina, Germany, Nevada, New Mexico, Libya or wherever. The quip among "fighter jocks" of that era was that the only thing different about Vietnam was that it was the "world's biggest bombing range." Although the story was different up North, combat losses were not that great in the South. As I recall, seven or eight Bien Hoa F-100's were shot down the year I was there. And most of the pilots were safely recovered the same day. Indeed, when I got my orders to Vietnam, I was stationed in Libya and part of our European AF command. Curious about the danger, I checked casualties for that particular week (it was in November 1967) and learned that two F-100's had been downed in Vietnam and both pilots rescued. The same week, we lost four F-100's to accidents in Europe (where the weather was much worse) and two of the pilots were killed. In other words, it was dangerous to fly F-100's, but Vietcong ground fire did not really add much to that danger.

LIVING CONDITIONS. Living conditions for Air Force fighter pilots in-country were quite good. We had air conditioned BOQ's and all but the most junior lieutenants had private rooms. We ate in air conditioned mess halls or more often in the officer's club or a flight line snack bar. We also consumed lots of beer. We generally flew about four or five days a week, one or two missions a day (with everyone angling to fly more). When not flying, most pilots were "at the beach," that is sunbathing, reading and drinking beer or soda pop out behind the BOQ. I and nearly all my colleagues had small Honda motorcycles on which we buzzed around the base. Gee, that was all a long time ago...

FORWARD AIR CONTROLLERS. In 1968, we almost always flew (in-country) missions in flights of two F-100's. All of our missions, except "Sky Spot," were flown under the control of a Forward Air Controller (FAC), usually a very junior USAF fighter pilot assigned to an Army (Marine or ARVN) unit and flying an 0-1 or 0-2. That included missions when we escorted C-123 Ranch Hand "agent orange" spray planes. I recall of no instance when I or any other pilot in the 3TFW expended ordnance without specific clearance from the FAC. We would take off from Bien Hoa and fly to a predesignated TACAN fix (usually within a 150 miles of Bien Hoa/Saigon) and rendevous with the FAC, who generally was airborne in an 0-1 (Cessna 182, I believe). The FAC would designate the target with a WP rocket then clear us in for each pass. "Buzzard Lead in from the south with nape," we would radio, (rockets, CBU's, hi drags, 20mm cannon ("mike-mike"), or whatever), and the FAC would reply, "Buzzard Lead cleared hot." Or we didn't release on that pass. The rules were strict, and as Wing Stan/Eval officer, I was "policeman" for the rules.

ONE MISSION. Which reminds me of one night mission down in the Delta. We were flying under flares, supporting a Navy Riverine force in a heavy firefight. I was Lead, and I had expended all my 500lb bombs and came in on a strafe pass; the VC (no dummies, they knew when all the bombs were gone) suddenly opened up with AAA fire. The tracers (probably 23mm) were heavy going over my cockpit and I could see just where they were coming from. I stood my Hun on its tail and came right back down prepared to hose them down with 20mm. As they opened up on my wingman, I put the pipper right on their position. But when I called the FAC for clearance to fire, I discovered my UHF radio was dead. (Recoil from the 20mm cannon on my previous pass had shaken the antenna loose.) That hurt. I didn't like it, but like a good soldier, I held fire since I could not get clearance. And flew back to Bien Hoa.

For awhile we flew in "hunter-killer" teams of sorts with Aviation units from the Army 9th Division in the Delta. First a Loach (Army light helicopter) would fly down low along tree lines and canals, hoping to draw fire. When he did (and he usually did), the "team" backed him up with 2 Cobra gunships followed by two F-100's lurking 10,000 feet up and out of sight. Worked great until Saigon found out we were improvising and prohibited any USAF-Army cooperation that wasn't coordinated by the Operations Center at Ton Son Hout. I can't recall whether or not we used FAC's in that lash up (which came about only because my wing commander had been a West Point classmate of the 9th's Division commander.) But I think we probably did.

SKY SPOT. There were a number of SAC radar bomb scoring stations around Vietnam. Back in the states, for many years their job had been to "score" high level nuclear electronic practice bombing by SAC B-36's, B-47's and B-52's. Transferred to Vietnam, they just reversed the procedure. Instead of scoring where the pilot released, they used their radars to drive the pilot to the release point for high level missions. It was like a GCA at 30,000 feet, with lots of minute heading and airspeed changes directed by the ground controller until he finally gave the bomb release count down and drop order. Although we didn't fly too many skyspots in F-100's, ALL B-52 (Arc Light) strikes, day or night, were high altitude Sky Spot deliveries. (B-52's aren't that great at dive bombing.)

LITTLE KNOWN FACTS ABOUT VIETNAM. Contrary to popular images, Vietnam vets are, as a group, more squared away than the average in their age cohort. That is, they have today higher income, better health, less divorces, less convictions, less unemployment, less drug and alcohol abuse, etc. That is not to say that there are not a lot of Viet vets who still have night mares and still suffer from post traumatic stress disorder, or are homeless, or whatever. But it is to say that the suffers are not the norm. All age cohorts in our society (or any society) have a certain percentage who are poorly adjusted, or in poor health. Among Viet vets, that percentage is smaller than among non vets of the same age cohort. While not widely known, that info is nevertheless widely available. Basic source is census data that tracks age cohorts. There have been a number of academic and press articles that have told this story over the years (none of which I have in front of me now).

The basic reason for better adjustment today is better adjustment then. In order to get into the military you have to be in perfect health, emotionally squared away and without a criminal record at age 19. While many of that generation who didn't serve were equally healthy and squared away (Bill Clinton), there were many who had terrible health, or had alcohol, drug, crime, emotional, or whatever problems THEN, and therefore did not serve. There are 100's of thousands of young men in every age cohort who can't meet the military's criteria because they are drug addicts, or have leukemia, or have lost a limb, or have felony convictions, or are illiterate, or have substan dard intelligence, or are blind, or have had emotional problems, or whatever. In the 1960's, those types weren't accepted or drafted for Vietnam. So 30 years later, it is not surprising that the group that was, at age 19, more healthy and squared away is still, as a group, more healthy and squared away, on average, than the group which included back then felons, cripples, cancer suffers, drug addicts, imbecile, alcoholics, and what have you.

HERE'S ANOTHER LITTLE KNOWN FACT. Young men who went into the military in the 1960's had a far better chance of surviving their young manhood years without being killed or seriously injured than if they had remained as civilians. It was safer, in other words to be in the military than to be a civilian, even during wartime. Why? For two reasons. First, only a small percentage of Viet era vets actually served in Vietnam or saw real combat, and, second, Army regimentation and regulation protected GI's from accidents. And accidents then as now killed and maimed far more young men than combat. Of 27 million young American men who came of age during the Vietnam war period, 11 million served in the military (that doesn't count the older, career military, like myself, who came of age in the fifties but nevertheless served in Nam). Of the 11 million young men of the "Vietnam generation" who ended up in uniform, only 2.7 million, or about one-fourth went to Vietnam.

COMBAT. During the Vietnam era, virtually all career military personnel like myself served in southeast Asia at least once, but less than half of short timers and draftees went there. Others served 2 to 4 years in uniform but went to sea, or to Europe, or stayed in the US. Of 3.5 million in uniform in each of those years, only 500,000 were in Vietnam. Furthermore, most of those who went to Nam were not running around rice paddies with M16's like we see in the movies. Some were, but more than 80 percent were back at bases where they were clerk typists, dental technicians, truck drivers, helicopter mechanics, supply sergeants, radar technicians, intelligence analysts, nurses, computer programers, press briefers, etc, etc. Most of those people were subject to occasional rocket or mortar attacks, but such attacks caused relatively few casualties. In other words, during the Vietnam war, as in all other US wars, most short term military personnel did not serve in Vietnam. And of those that did, most did not see serious combat. The roughly 6% of the total 3.5 million in uniform who really did get out in the paddies where they came up against Charlie, of course, took a lot of casualties. But not enough to equal the far larger number within their age cohort back in the US who were getting killed or hurt in accidents.

ACCIDENTS. Young American men had a very high accidental death and injury rate in the 1960's just as they do in 1997. Motorcycles killed more men in the 1960's than the Vietcong did. Ditto for drowning. Then there were car accidents, hunting accidents, boating accidents, rock climbing accidents, gang fights, murders, and so on. Fifty thousand Americans were killed every year during the 60's in traffic accidents. And, as the insurance companies can verify, those killed were disproportionately young, draft age males. At the same time, about 10,000 a year were dying in Vietnam (quite a few of those in traffic accidents as well, by the way, but seldom so reported). Because of regs and regimentation, GI's were partially shielded from accidents. During part of the 1960's, for example, the military prohibited young enlisted men from owning or operating motorcycles. Also, the Army left GI's little time or money to go sailing, scuba diving or hang gliding.

You could verify all this by taking an age cohort, such as born in 1948 (19 years old in 1967), and tracing cause of death and controlling for Vietnam. Accidental drowning is number one, I am told. You could also check out the status of that same cohort today as far as still surviving, divorce, employment, criminal convictions, income, and so on, again controlling for Vietnam service -- JOHN MACARTNEY, PhD Professor & Director, Syracuse U Semester in Washington jdmac@syr.edu http://www.maxwell.syr.edu/ir/semeste4.htm
