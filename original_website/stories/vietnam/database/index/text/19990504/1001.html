I was a Corpsman with the 1st platoon Lima Company 3/1. I returned to the world on January 29, 1970 without serious physical injuries. From that date until of 1989 I spoke little of my Vietnam experience. I live in Baltimore, Md., not more than 40 miles from The Wall but I had never visited. As Desert Storm unfolded, I felt a need to talk about war to my Senior High Sunday School class. As I formulated my approach, I decided to make the three week "lecture" non-judgemental but factual (after Vietnam I majored in History and Political Science at The University of North Carolina, Chapel Hill). I thus began an unexpected trail that has lasted 10 years.

Through the first two sessions with my class, we discussed the moral implications of war and how a person can reconcile actions in a time of war with Christian values. The Bible is an interesting resourse as if one looks hard enough a person can justify almost anything. But justification was not my motive, education was. The young adults I was teaching were intelligent and quisitive. If I did a good job, they would come to the appropriate conclusions for themselves and that conclusion might not match the thoughts of their classmates. As an extension of this, I stressed that it was good that different reactions were expressed as long as they learned to respect and value the different thought. As I explained, some of the greatest damage to participants in the Vietnam War was not necessarily what we had experienced but the extreme negative reaction we received from the "Peacemakers" who angrily confronted us when we returned. I thought I had expressed my thoughts reasonably well and for our third session I planned a trip to The Wall.

As I said, I had not been to The Wall and I had no idea of how it was chronologically planned. None of the kids had personally known anyone who had been killed in Vietnam so I began to recall members of my platoon whose names were on it. As i recalled the people, I wrote a brief "recollection" of each individual I knew would be there. I did about a dozen "recollections" and planned the trip. We parked on the tidal basin at the far end from The Wall (early on a Sunday morning there is plenty of parking available; I knew this because I was stationed in D.C. in 1970). We then had breakfast which was unmarked cans of fruit salad, pork & beans etc.. I gave eack student their own "John Wayne" can opener and taught them how to use cans as stoves to heat their meal. I had located some heat tabs but missed the small balls of C-4 for the equivalent of a field mocrowave. I then gave each person a separate "recollection" and one at a time 15 - 20 yards apart sent them off in the direction of The Wall with instructions to find their name. I went last.

When I caught up with the class at The Wall I found them clustered in front of one slab near the middle of the structure. I looked at the marble and there were many of the people I knew from long ago. I was stunned and ceased to feel like a teacher. I knew that I was the student and was learning a valuable lesson. My young friends knew I had changed and from the reactions I received, they in some way understood, that my seeing all of them looking at the slab, had deeply moved me.

Since that day, most of these friends have gone to college. Some joined various branches of the military and some can not conceive of that course. They all, however, understand respect and universally have approached me to ask if I am still teaching "the lesson". I am. I do it every chance I get, not just in Sunday School but in regular classrooms, because I and every person who served should. We have important information to relay to subsequent generations. The ancillary benefit is selfish. The more I teach, the more I understand and the more I understand, the more I accept.

Robert D. Slingluff
1947
Baltimore, Md. USA
