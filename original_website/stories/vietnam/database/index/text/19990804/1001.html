Although I was born in 1966, the Vietnam War has always been a large influence on how I saw Asia. As an Asian-American, it was even more confusing for me to identify with white americans since nobody in my family had been called to fight or drafted for the war. However, when I joined the Army that begun to change. Many of my instructors, officers, and NCO who were older than 40 still remember the war and wear their pain like silent scars.

I would hear their stories and in some cases realized that the war had damaged them emotionally or psychologically. I was branched Military Intelligence and got to learn about the Viet Cong strategy from an operational standpoint. I came away with an appreciation of the military genius of Gen. Giap of N. Vietnam and the historical importance of Ho Chi Minh to the Vietnamese people. George Washington is probably one of the greatest military leaders in world history and the Vietnamese fought the US with similar attritional tactics that the Continental army used against the British. Our arrogance and faith in technology and our ability to wage war fell far short to an enemy driven by a nationalistic drive to unshackle itself from colonialism.

I feel sad for the men who died in that war and gave up their lives if only the US had not jumped to support the French so quickly.

Vietnam for me was a mythical location fashioned from the movies and from the war stories of veterans. However, in 1998, I went to Vietnam only a few years after relations were normalized to allow US citizens to visit. To my amazement, I saw no hate or contempt for Americans, of course I was in Saigon and not Hanoi, but I felt that general attitudes were "that's the past" let's move on. If one were to see Vietnam, you wold realize how primative and poor the country really is and wonder how could we have ever fought...and lose a war to such people? Of course we underestimated their will and tenacity and were fooled into believing that hardware=strength. And when you walk there in person you understnad why. Going to Vietnam, changes your impression of the country from what you learned in the movies for those of us who never served in the war.

When you walk in Saigon, there are little kids trying to sell you postcards and maps all the time. Although there trnikets are expensive relative to the stores, they still cost usually less than a dollar. In almost all cases, the kids are quite charming and are fluent in English. One little 11 year old girl who I bought some post cards from and bought a can of coke for allowed me the opportunity to ask questions about the war to her in downtown Saigon.

I asked her, "who won the war between the US and Vietnam?" sheepily she said, "vietnam?" "Correct" I said.

"OK" I asked, "who won the war between France and Vietnam?" with a little more confidence she said, "France?" "No, Vietnam won it" I said.

"alright, who won the war between Kampuchea (Cambodia) and Vietnam?" I asked. She replied confidently "Vietnam!" "That is absolutely correct." I bellowed.

"Last question," I said. "who won the war between China and Vietnam in 1979?" With confidence again she shouted, "Vietnam!" "Wrong," I said. "It was a draw."

She looked a little puzzled but then I just began to laugh and so did she. In the end, it really didn't matter who won. It was a great day and we sitting outside with a can of Coca Cola.

For me, that was the impression of Vietnam that I would like to remember.

Frank Yu
1966
