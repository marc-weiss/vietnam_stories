I never was in Vietnam but, like everyone of my generation, the war there had a profound effect on my life. At the time that President Johnson dragged this country deeper into the Vietnam war, I was still a naive young man from Brooklyn, New York. I was ready to believe everything our President said and I was ready to serve where my country sent me. All that changed in a few short years. I remember arguing, on the corner of Eighth Street and Sixth Avenue in Greenwich Village, which was a hot bed of political and artistic ferment at that time, with a young woman from the anti war movement about whether or not President Johnson was lying about the Gulf of Tonkin incident which the President used as a justification to send massive amounts of arms and troops to South Vietnam. I remember walking along side of, but not joining, a massive anti war demonstration that marched up Fifth Avenue to Central Park. Like most Americans I didn't want to believe that our goverment was lying to us and that we were shedding the precious blood of our young men for nothing. Like most Americans, I was more conservative in my thoughts and actions than the anti war movement hoped I would be. But, slowly and surely, I began to study the issues more and more till I came to the conclusion that the war in Vietnam was a sham and that this country was wasting precious resourses to fulfill the unrealistic political strategies of an inner circle of men in our government . The reasons that this inner circle brought us to war, in a part of the world we as a nation should never have gone, is another story for another time. As for me, I was just a New York City kid that was 1A and could have been drafted into the Military at any time. I remember going to that magnificent old building on Whitehall Street, in the Wall Street area of Manhattan, a building that dated back to the Civil War era, to have my physical examination. I remember going to my local Draft Board and telling them quite bluntly that there was no way I was going to Vietnam. I told them I would rather go to jail than serve in an unjust and immoral war. I remember the relief I felt when my father retired from work and my classification was changed from 1A to 3A because I was now the only source of income my family had. I remember participating in the great anti war demonstration that was held in Washington, D.C., in November of Nineteen Sixtynine. I remember being swept away by the great cultural revolution that was shaking up this country and sweeping the world. I remember. What do I feel about all that history that I was a part of? I feel that the war in Vietnam was a terrible waste. I have reconciled with the fact that the United States lost the war with Vietnam. I keep thinking about the more than three hundred thousand casualties the Vietnamese suffered to gain their independence. I think of the more than fifty five thousand young men and women on our side who fought bravely, with honor, and gave their lives for this country. I think about how misguided I was by not honoring those men and women who fought in Vietnam. Ithink about how unfair our society was to the soldiers and sailors and airmen who served our country well in Vietnam. Ifeel sorry for all those little Vietnamese kids that had American fathers and Vietnamese mothers and were rejected by both societies. I feel angry that the men that got us into the Vietnam war were not punished during their lifetimes and I can only hope that they will go down in history as the true bastards that they were. I feel angry that because President Johnson had to resign, this nation had to endure and finally expel that dark and malevolent evil power that was Richard Nixon from it's political landscape. Now old Vietnam veterans of both sides greet each other as brothers. My son's generation is only vaguely familiar with the events that had such a powerful effect on me. Let us hope that those old boys from both sides will act as pioneers of the spirit so that the United States and Vietnam can truly come to an understanding of what that war was all about. And with understanding perhaps will come forgiveness too.

John Mestres
1945
L.I.C., N.Y., U.S.A.
