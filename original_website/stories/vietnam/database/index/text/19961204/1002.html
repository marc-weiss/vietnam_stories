When I went to RVN, I was a young man of 23, but, because of accelerated promotion practices in the wartime Army, I was already a captain. My first unit was the First Air Cavalry Division. My job was in the 1st Squadron(Air) of the 9th Cavalry. This was the division's air cavalry recon squadron, and we thought we were BAD! This was because we were! Our helicopters found the enemy that others could not, and the rest of the division would then "pile on." Our traditional head gear of the black Stetson "cav hat" set us apart. I got a real feeling of team work in this unit, and we really did take care of our own. One day when one of our small ground recon teams was in trouble, but the designated infantry reinforcements weren't ready to go, we rang a bell in the squadron quadrangle and everyone came running with whatever you had in the way of a gun. We piled onto whatever choppers were around and took off to save our guys. Thank God they got out OK without our needing to actually land as I only had a pistol and about 25 rounds of ammo. Even though I wasn't a flier myself, I was accepted and made to feel a valuable part of the team. When the division started sending people home as part of the return to the States, I wasn't to go because I had not been "in country" long enough. My friends in G-1 (personnel)answered a wire that asked for a captain to command a unit in the Central Highlands. So off I went.

This new unit was an armored cavalry unit left behind when the 4th Infantry Division rotated home. The troop I was to command was without its captain because he had been shot be his own men. I was not warned of this by anyone (including my new colonel) until I had actually signed the assumption of command order. This was one of the most stressful periods of my young life to lead men into combat who might do to me what was done to the last man. Further, this troop was the dumping grounds of all the un-cooperative soldiers , dope heads , and other undesirable that the other troops didn't want. Over time, I gained their trust (I feel), and we did some creditable service. The bad situation was a distillation of the anti-war feelings back home, the limits put upon our efforts by the politicians back home, and the one-year rotation schedule that created turmoil in every unit.

These experiences changed me forever. I look differently at responsibility and the attendant authority, at authority above me (and what they aren’t telling you), trust, and the value of loyalty both up and down. When it worked well (First Cav) it was a great place to be. When it did not, it was hell on earth.
