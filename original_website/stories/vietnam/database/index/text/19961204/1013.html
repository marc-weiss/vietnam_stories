What the Viet Nam Experience Means to Me

In high school during the late 1960s, I dated a girl whose next door neighbor went to Viet Nam as a Navy Corpsman. This experience is the defining expression of what Viet Nam continues to mean to me.

The boy living next door to my girl friend was a year older than we were. He was an only child from a very simple, blue-collar couple living in Houston, Texas. The three of us were friends, and knew each other fairly well.

He graduated from high school a year ahead of us, joined the Navy, went to boot camp and then to corpsman school. He came home on leave after that school, and told us that he had orders to join a Marine Corps unit in Viet Nam. He was depressed because he had been told (as he relayed to us) that the corpsmen with the Marines in Viet Nam had a very high casualty rate. He was afraid that he might not come home.

Exactly as he feared, he was killed in Viet Nam.

His parents lived in what I remember as a little gray-colored house. I saw them occasionally after he died, and I remember them looking gray as well.

All these years my heart has been heavy with the memory of a simple American couple, whose only child - really all that they had in this life - was killed in a far away place. They were nothing special; he was nobody special. But they were three individuals deeply affected by that war.

Even though I later joined the Navy and served as an officer off the coast of Vietnam and on four other ships in the Atlantic, and even though I think of myself as a patriotic person, I still can't come to grips with that young man's death.

Why did he and so many others die? What was their sacrifice for? Will they be remembered?

Ironically, I have struggled for atleast the last fifteen years to remember that boy's name; and I can't. I've tried to recall the family's last name; and it escapes me.

I haven't forgotten him, though, and I haven't forgotten his parents. I can't forget the little house, either. But I remember these with the most profound sense of emptiness.
