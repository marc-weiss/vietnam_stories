Dear Mr. President:

I have been informed that James Valley, an honorably discharged, highly
decorated Vietnam veteran, recently applied for Vietnam Veteran "vanity"
license plates in his home state of Florida. In reply to his request, he
received a letter from the Florida DMV which read, in part, that "...any
such plate commemorating Vietnam Service is both offensive and politically
incorrect."

After placing telephone calls to Senator Robert Smith (New Hampshire), of
the Senate Armed Services Committee, and Senator Bankhead of Florida, both
of whom personally placed telephone calls to the Florida DMV, Mr. Valley
was informed by Mr. Tom Joyce, Assistant Director of the Florida DMV, that
the plates would be approved.

Such callous, insensitive behavior on the part of a government employee is
yet one more insult to not only Mr. Valley, but to all veterans of all
conflicts who have served this country.

The Vietnam veterans of this country have been treated with disrespect,
have been insulted, have been abused, and have been ignored by the
government of the United States for over 30 years. This incident is yet
another mark of shame which those who served their country must bear. It
is a mark of shame directed not only at the thousands of soldiers, sailors,
and airmen who returned, but one directed at the over 58,000 of our
brothers and sisters whose names are engraved on the Vietnam Veterans
Memorial in Washington, D.C. It is a mark of shame directed at the over
2,000 of our brothers who are still listed as POW/MIA.

Mr. President, I am a Vietnam veteran who proudly displays Vietnam Veteran
license plates on my vehicle. The fact that one of my brothers had to
endure the above pain and humiliation to acquire such license plates angers
and hurts me. It will seem a minor thing to you and other nonveterans, but
it is not to us. To us, it is one more slap in the face, one more piece of
evidence that we, the Vietnam veteran, are looked upon by government
entities as second-class citizens.

Our country called us; we answered. The least we can expect is that our
country honor the unwritten contract which is implied: that we be
respected and honored for our service.

There is a saying which we use: Hate the war; love the warrior.
