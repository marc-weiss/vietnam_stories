CIRCLE for a LANDING by John B. Mellquist

Late one evening shortly before Christmas, in the Year of Our War, a chartered Pan-Am 707 touched down on a dark, wet runway millions of miles from where it originally departed.

It landed quickly, then hurried along a taxi-way behind a small truck that carried a sign for the pilot to read -- "Follow Me" -- toward the protective revetments, its jet engines now whining softly in the humid night. Inside, the pilot kept the cabin lights off and except for the rattle of the overhead compartment doors when the landing gear tires thumped over the seams in the concrete runway there was silence as private fears danced in the heads of the 200 passengers, like the visions of sugar plums they unknowingly left behind.

The front of the plane dipped, then bounced up and down one or two times when it finally stopped rolling, and before the engines had finished unwinding the cabin doors were burst open by two men wearing flak-vests and faded jungle fatigues. They carried M-16 rifles. After a few sarcastic welcoming remarks the MPs informed us that as we were landing the airport had come under attack from incoming enemy rocket and mortar fire. We must, therefore, hasten to disembark to the safety of the ever present sandbag bunker.

For the next three days hundreds of other disoriented passengers and I milled about the US Army's reception complex in the tropical heat and humidity of Cam Ranh Bay, Republic of Vietnam. Here, orders were being processed that would assign us to companies whose duties we were to assist in some small way, assigned to companies of other young men also delivered into that twilight zone by Santa's jet powered sleigh. During this time in limbo I was not afraid, the experience was too new and exciting.

Early in the morning before daylight on the fourth day I was summoned from a fitful and sweaty sleep. Without benefit of breakfast or explanation 119 other men and I were hustled back to the airport for a fast, but noisy, getaway in a large camouflaged cargo plane, destination unknown. There, huddled in that crowded cargo bay where I was tired, surprisingly cold, not surprisingly hungry, and almost deaf from the noise of the four turboprop engines, more than the morning sun started to dawn on me. I was trapped. Not just inside this airplane, but trapped in the machine called war. No longer were those TV newscasts and special reports just carelessly ignored omens. I was to be a participant now, no longer a disinterested bystander, in producing the machine's horrible product. We were all trapped. From the President of the United States on down to we 120 souls in the cargo bay of that plane, from enraged Kent State students to frightened National Guardsmen, we were all trapped. The machine would be hard to stop, it had been gathering speed for over ten years. Those with enough courage and conscience fought its momentum, but cramped in that noisy airplane, I realized that it could not be stopped. Even the Vietnamese were powerless and the machine rolled over them too.

Two hours later the plane landed at the Bien Hoa airbase not far from what was then called Saigon. We were greeted with the same ceremony as before, but now familiar with the procedure, I ran like hell for the sandbagged bunker as soon as the cargo bay door was low enough to jump out. As before, there were three days of processing paperwork, weapons familiarization, and training in jungle etiquette (booby-trap school) before I got on another, smaller airplane and was singly flown to a forward base camp not far from the Cambodian border.

The planes had gotten smaller, the bases had gotten smaller, but I did not mind. Among other things, I hated the formality the military demanded when it gathered in large numbers. In the forward areas no one cared if your boots were shined or your fatigues pressed. Life was simple.

The flight from Bien Hoa lasted about an hour and in the late afternoon the sun was hidden behind a bank of dark cumulus clouds. The C-120 Caribou made a bumpy descent to about 1000 feet, banked left and circled the small runway at Phuoc Vinh. As I peered out the window, the ground looked red, dark, and barren. It was covered with pock marks filled with rain water, craters made from enemy rocket propelled grenades, 122 mm rockets, mortars and, quite likely, "short rounds" (artillery rounds fired by us that for some reason barely cleared the end of the howitzer's barrel). The scene was foreboding and a momentary chill caused me to shiver.

I quickly disembarked after a hasty landing and waved to the co-pilot to signal that I was clear of the aircraft. He returned my gesture and an instant later, like old Saint Nick, he was gone.

Everything in Vietnam lasted only an instant and then was gone. It might be your arm, legs, buddy, or your life. Now you see it, now you don't. Or, it could be a perception, idea or fantasy. For me, it was the naiveté that this war could really be happening, it was not actually just a script for John Wayne and the Hollywood Green Berets. But it was real and it was ugly. At the local Graves Registration point black plastic body bags were stacked five high along a fifteen foot long section of wooden fence at any given time.

From Phuoc Vinh I was to hitch another ride to my final destination, but missed the last "bird" out, the one that would have taken me to Lai Khe (a.k.a. Rocket City) and the helicopter that I had been trained to crew. A chance conversation with a young lieutenant in the battalion S-2 (operations) office, however, changed all of that. It turned out that the headquarters company for the 227th Assault Helicopter Battalion was in need of a company clerk and, when the lieutenant learned that I could type more than two words per minute, he said I qualified for the job. Curiously, this position had traditionally been held by UH-1 crew chiefs, like me, who had been waylaid in similar fashion enroute to other line companies. I was 20 years old at the time, and none to keen at the prospect of becoming filler material for a black plastic body bag, like those stacked up at the airstrip. I accepted the offer.

In this trusted position I was privy to the classified daily casualty reports. First, the dead were listed and an abbreviated description of their condition was stated: "Butler, Gary F., SP4, Co. B, 158th Medivac, 75% third degree burns, traumatic amputation left arm and legs." The wounded followed. They were ranked like records of the Top 40 Hits back home, the dead and those with the most severe injuries listed at the top. Beyond the lists, the unrecorded mental and emotional damage brought on by fear, neglect and boredom. Venereal disease and drug addiction were injuries for which no Purple Heart was given.

I returned to California a year after I went away to Vietnam. I came home deeply tanned and very lean (mostly from refusing to eat maggot infested food) and miraculously unscathed. I was, however, disturbed. Something just wasn't quite right. My parents looked the same, except for having more gray hair and looking a little tired now. The rooms of their house still had the same pictures hung in all the same places, walls the same color. Even the cat was sleeping upside-down on the same corner of that familiar old couch in the living room just as he had been when I left a year earlier. But everything, not just my folks and their house, but every familiar thing, everywhere, was somehow undeniably different. Slowly, I came to understand that it was not anything physical or anyone else that had changed, and that's what disturbed me. My eyes and ears told me I had gone nowhere, but my mind struggled. I had been somewhere indeed!

My first Christmas at home, after leaving Vietnam and the Army, was just as Christmas had always been for as long as I could remember. The same kind of tree had the same colorful lights, the same figurine ornaments were hung in exactly the same places. The house was decorated just as it always had been: lights along the eaves outside, our trusty old wreath on the front door. And hung by the chimney with ageless parental care was the stocking my grandmother, Sarah, knitted for me just after I was born. It hung empty against the brick fireplace now, a symbol of what was good and what was love, but it would never again be filled with toys.
