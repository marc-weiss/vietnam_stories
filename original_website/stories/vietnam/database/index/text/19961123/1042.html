A Relic in the Family Closet

The other day at my parents' home, I chanced upon a relic from my incongruous past. Hidden in our family closet, behind a row of business suits and wool sweaters, was my father's South Vietnamese Army uniform, a paratrooper's insignia meticulously sewned above its left breast pocket and three silver stars pinned on each lapel.

Once in that tropical country at the southeast end of Asia, my father had wore it regally, a warrior of a civil war he bravely fought and lost. Once, as a child, I had looked up to a man who had seemed more like a deity, a war hero -- Hadn't I imagined myself as an adult walking in his soldierly footsteps?

A particular night emerges from my memories: The cool wind blows through a certain villa, where distant B-52 bomb explosions resound, melding with the monsoon rain to form that strangely soothing rhythm of my childhood lullaby. I am 9-years-old, an army brat living with my family in the imperial city of Hue, near the DMZ. Somewhere in the garden the dogs are barking; my father is coming home. A few army jeeps screech to a halt on the courtyard outside and my father emerges, an entourage of soldiers with M-16s and officers with maps and, army radios trailing behind him. He enters the living room, lifts me up for a warm embrace. His rough uniform emits the smell of sweat, mud, cigar and gunpowder -- what I will always associate with the odor of battlefield. "We won!" he says, laughing. "We won!"

But that was a long time ago, of course, and only a battle, and in the aftermath of a defeated war, much has changed. When we set foot on the American shore, history was already against us. Vietnam goes on without us; America goes on without acknowledging us. In America, then, there are no territories for father and son to defend, no civil war to fight.

In America the Vietnamese refugee's first self assessment is instead to his own helplessness. It is characterized by blushing, by looking down at one's feet, by avoiding eye contact, and by waiting: on the way for food in the refugee camp; in line for welfare and food stamps, for the free clinic exam, for jackets donated by charity, for green cards.

As for the Vietnamese child, he will soon learn that he must face the outside world alone and that to grow up Vietnamese in America is to grow up with the Iegacy of belonging to the loser's side and to endure all,that entails. One morning in the locker room of my junior high, blond and blue-eyed Johnny M., standing gloriously naked, asked, "Refugee boy, which side were you on? The winner's or the loser's?", English still an unpendable language on my tongue, I answered, "Me: Loser's side!" And the room immediately erupted into laughter and I felt Johnny"s wet towel on my face -- the white flag, I supposed, to surrender with.

In geography class, Mrs. Collier brought out the new map of Indochina. Vietnam, that country coiling in the voluptuous shape of an S, was no longer mine. It was repainted now in a uniform color - red -- the South flooded with blood. Mrs. Collier didn't know why, exactly, that strange Oriental kid, so quiet, suddenly buried his face in his arms and wept.

It was my father's passion I was feeling. At dinner time, after a drink or two he would talk of our homeland and relive the battles that he had fought and won. Napalm fire, helicopters exploding, paratroopers landing, bombs fell from a drunken warrior's fragmented sentences, transforming the dilapidated apartment into a battleground. Did I not hear the wailing voices of Vietcong under fire, see a helicopter burst into flame, smell burnt flesh?

What my father does not talk readily about, however, is the last time he wore his uniform. A day after Saigon fell to communist hands my father had commandeered a navy ship full of army officers to the Philippines. Nearing destination, he put away his uniform, changed into a pair of jeans and a T-shirt and threw his Colt 45 into the Pacific Ocean and; along with a hundred or so officers, asked the U.S. authorities for asylum.

That image, perhaps more than any other, had haunted me. It spelled the end of my childhood mythology. At 12, I too had already become a bitter veteran of a war I never actually fought.

And I remember, fleeing. I fled, from the exile's home where the language of nostalgia and memories of an unending war can suffocate, zooming down the hyphen that stretches like a freeway from the mythological kingdom where helicopters hover over rice paddies toward a cosmopolitan city, the wind in my hair, Springsteen on the radio.

Indeed, I wear no army uniform today; I carry a lap-top and a press card instead. If my father had boasted of victorious battles, I talk of my own rebirth and of exploring the public realm beyond ethnic and private enclave. I substitute the medals I once saw decorating my father's army uniform for the colorful entry and exit stamps on my American passport.

When I turned 30 recently, my father said, "At your age I was already a colonel."

"We are very different now, dad," I snapped, a little irritated. "I don't have a need to be a warrior here in America."

My father smiled a sad, knowing smile. What relevant words of wisdom can an exiled general pass on down to his fully grown son? I am, after all, the one who my father sometimes humorously introduces to his ex-army buddies as his "American son" which in Vietnamese, could sometimes translate to mean soulless or traitor.

And it's true. I have not, in Vietnamese eyes, been a very good son. I have denounced my father's extraterritorial passion as parochialism, have learned to hear his war stories as tales of nostalgia, have, as a writer, taken the private angst of his generation and disseminated it in public light -- an unfilial act.

But it could not be helped. A different course of history flows through me. For I have read Hamlet and found his unfocused vehemence not to my taste, his bloody rampage inappropriate to the new world. I am, by contrast, more intrigued by the complicated character of Indar in V.S. Naipaul's "A Bend in The River", who has lost his family home and fortune to political upheavals and been forced outside of territorial boundaries. "I'm tired of being on the losing side," he says. "I want to win and win and win."

There could be, in all assessment, no final solutions to a Colt 45 that lay rusting at the bottom of the Pacific Ocean nor to memories of helicopters and the monsoon rain. Children of defeated warriors must take on new journeys while rusted guns at the bottom of the ocean have no sentimental values beyond wit.

The army uniform, redolent now of moth balls and old dust, placed back to its hiding place, the week-end visit ended, and I took leave. In a ritual of familial reticency I hugged my mother but could not bring myself to hug my father. The exile Vietnamese and his American son regard one another from a distance, nod, and no more. We are veterans now from different wars, and I have won mine.
