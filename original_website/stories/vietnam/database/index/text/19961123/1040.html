Five years ago last month I wrote my first OP-ED article for the Seattle Post Intelligencer. Reading over that piece I found a man struggling with issues he didn't understand and emotions he had never experienced.

I wrote the article to help me face my past and to begin the process of healing from the Vietnam War. The terrible pain and guilt I felt because of my service during that war made me lose faith in myself. The article was a way for me to begin the long recovery process and reconnect with others in my life. It was a call for help and a way to share some of my grief.

At the time I had just begun treatment for a condition called Post Traumatic Stress. I began my therapy several months before writing the piece as a way to overcome lingering problems stemming from a failed, six-year relationship that ended the year before. This relationship dissolved into bitterness and anger as I struggled with a pain I felt powerless to overcome. The breakup proved to be the catalyst that caused a dramatic change in my life. Slowly, I began to tear down the walls of resistance and denial I had carefully, yet unconsciously, constructed since coming home from the war.

Change always brings anxiety and the more drastic the change, the greater the anxiety. I thought my problems were all connected to the breakup of the relationship but I was to find out they were only symptoms of an older and deeper pain surrounding my involvement in the war. I had never confronted the ghosts of that war because I was afraid and somehow knew it would cause even more pain. Only after several years of intensive therapy was I able put together the broken pieces of my life and look with hope toward a brighter future. A good part of the healing comes from the help and support of my wife. I'm indebted to her for her patience, kindness and understanding. During our four years together I've learned to love myself and this has increased my capacity to love others. Throughout my ordeal other members of my family have supported and helped me heal my emotional scars.

Although I have completed my intensive counseling program, the job of recovery is a day-to-day experience. Just like a chemical dependency, healing from emotional trauma is a step by step process that relies on the persistence of the individual. The long term effects of any self-help program are directly related to how motivated you are to reach your goals.

By writing this column each month I have achieved several important goals in my life. First, the column gives me a forum to express myself and address important issues. I am most thankful to the P-I for their continued support during the past five years.

Writing is sometimes a lonely endeavor. I often feel like I'm working in a vacuum with little connection to the outside. The responses I get from readers give me the opportunity to make that connection. My first article provoked several comments from readers. I was surprised by these comments because I never thought anyone would take the time to share their views with me about my writing.

Today, I've grown accustomed to the comments and greatly appreciate those who call or write to express their views. These comments have resulted in ideas for future columns and have helped me become aware of issues that may not always make the news. This response is, at times, rather pointed and very often critical but I've learned to accept this and grow from the criticism.

In writing my monthly piece I try for a mix of topical information, opinion and analysis that I hope stimulates readers to consider viewpoint different from their own. Analyzing several viewpoints gives us the chance to access the relative merit of our own opinion against that of others. The more we learn about an issue, the better able we are to form an educated opinion.

Another goal was to establish contacts within the veterans community. During the five years I've met with many veterans groups as well as local, state and federal representatives to learn more about the complex problems facing veterans. I am grateful for their help and am inspired to continue my writing.

Writing this column requires continued research to keep abreast of current information regarding military and veteran issues. This research exposes me to the many facets of an issue. It forces me to confront other opinions and ideas contrary to mine. This exposure opens my mind and gives me a greater tolerance toward the feelings of others while creating a more balanced approach to my writing.

The column has given me a sense of confidence about myself and provided a valuable means to connect with other veterans and those working for their welfare. The comments I receive have helped me write with a clearer mind and stronger purpose. Thanks to all of you who continue to support and encourage this effort.

Patrick Pilcher writes on Veteran and military issues.

Written 9/69

GULF OF TONKIN JOURNAL

Three years I spent upon its decks it plows the seas from dawn to dusk. A thousand feet from bow to stern a ship of war - a power in trust.

Within its skin five thousand live and struggle to survive. I think of home and all I love and hope to make it back alive.

We send our planes far over land to targets mere points on a map. They bomb these spots and return again to reload and widen the gap.

This war's not right this I can see I say I'm just doing my job. To help me forget I drink all I can and try not to utter a sob.

We're sent off to fight this terrible war which no one can truly explain. We see all the death and wonder inside are we all just going insane.

Day after day, night after night oh when will it ever be done. This grueling routine has taken its toll can ever this war be won?

I'm hollow inside, my energy spent I feel like I've visited hell. Someday this all will be far behind And maybe I'll then be well.

I leave the ship for one last time soon I'll be back in the States. I'm not too sure I'll ever forget the ghost I know now awaits.

USS Constellation CVA-64, Gulf of Tonkin 1966, 67, 68
