After my long, long year (1969-70) at war (first in Vietnam, then in Thailand flying combat missions into Laos and writing official Air Force histories about the "secret war"), I vowed to put the experience behind me.

I returned to teach English at the Air Force Academy, but I soon realized how little anyone knew about what was going on in Laos. In part because I could see my government lying through its teeth about this phase of the Vietnam War, I wrote the novel THE LAOTIAN FRAGMENTS (1974; Avon, 1987). A piece of fiction, the book was stalled by the Pentagon for nine months because it was perceived there as fact. When finally published, it was attacked by left wing reviweres for showing pilots as heroes, then slighted by right wing critics for its tragic message.

I did not intend to make the Vietnam War my prfessional emphasis, but it has dominated. I've published essays about the impact of American Western myths and CATCH-22 thinking; a book with Tim Lomperis about the literature of the Vietnam War (1987); the big one, VIETNAM VOICES (1983;to be reissued in 1997 by Georgia Press); an edition (1996) of Greene's THE QUIET AMERICAN, the prophetic novel about the early American presence; and I've spoken at numerous conferences--in New York, Colorado, California, Texas and Manchester and Swansea, England. All this activity has compellingly resulted from my experience in country.

The idea for my current college course featuring the fiction of the Vietnam War came from some students--sons and daughters of the Vietnam Generation--who urged me to do it because they knew absolutely nothing about the war. As a result, my job from now on is to help them and others, as best I can, understand how it really was.

One memory prevails: shortly after the Pentagon finally cleared THE LAOTIAN FRAGMENTS,I received a telephone call. The man whose deep voice resonated of high office would not identify himself. He said only, "Pratt, I just wanted to tell you that your book has materially changed American foreign policy. Read tomorrow's NEW YOUR TIMES." Then he hung up.

The next day, the press revealed the United States government's admission that we were at war in Laos and were bombing the Ho Chi Minh trail.

Will what I have written and said continue to make a difference? It's this hope that keeps me going.

John Clark Pratt, LtCol, USAF (ret) Professor of English Colorado State University
