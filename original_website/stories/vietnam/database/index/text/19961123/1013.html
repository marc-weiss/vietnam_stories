Five years after the fall of Saigon I worked for UNHCR, teaching English on the Malaysian island of Bidong, then the temporary home of more than 40,000 Vietnamese refugees. For these boat people, the journey from Vietnam was often deadly; though no one knows how many lives were lost at sea, reliable estimates range upwards of 100,000.

I was awed by the fortitude of the people I knew. Their patient, tough- minded response to suffering seemed to me a kind of grace. I admired them. Their courage shamed me, and I grew contemptuous of my own good fortune, even as I was grateful for it.

I don't know how I could have felt otherwise. I recall very clearly my first week on the island, talking to an older Vietnamese woman in order to determine her English level and consequent class placement. "How many children do you have?" I asked, figuring to put her at ease. She hesitated. Her shoulders began going up and down, and her chin quivered. Three, she told me: the fourth had been killed at sea by Thai pirates. She began crying.

I felt terrible, of course. But no moment has ever whispered to me more clearly that my youthful sense of virtue--a teacher working with the victims of war--was a sham. I was complacent and trivial, and therefore dangerous. over the course of the year, I became much less so. For that, I am unreservedly grateful.

Paul Eggers

Iowa
