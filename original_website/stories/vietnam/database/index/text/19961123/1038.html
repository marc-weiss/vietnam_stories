November 19, 1996

The Vietnam war altered my life forever when the devastating news was received that my brother, Lt. James B. Mills, USNR, was missing-in-Action. That traumatic event led to near-total immersion in a 30-year quest for answers about America's POW/MIA's that would take me to Vietnam Laos, Cambodia, France and Thailand. Official talks at private dinners with the highest leadership level of Vietnam in New York, Washington and Hanoi became part of my life.

This quest would also mean nearly two decades of dialogue at the highest levels of the U.S. Government, meetings with countless Members of Congress and testimony before their respective committees, a SECRET clearance and participation in the official interagency policy process, appearances on all major networks, media interviews and public speaking and awareness events throughout our country. All of this, though totally unexpected, was necessary; I inherited a mantle from my father who preceded me as Executive Director of the National League of POW/MIA Families.

Before Jimmy became MIA, I had monitored news about the war since first one brother, then the other, and my brother-in-law were serving in the U.S. Navy. In 1966, during the 7th mission of his second tour, Jimmy disappeared while on an F4B mission over North Vietnam, flying off the USS Coral Sea. He had flown 100+ missions over North Vietnam during his first tour in 1965, flying off the USS MIdway. My brother volunteered to go back; he and his single friends, even knowing the danger, recognizing that others had even more to lose, especially those with young children.

The next few years were hard. Was Jimmy alive? Captured and being tortured? Would he survive to come home? Or was he already dead? As the war dragged on and so many lives were lost, I recalled vividly Jimmy's concern about friends and colleagues downed by AAA and SANS, targets that Washington had designated as off-limits during that period.

My experience with this real aftermath of the Vietnam War has convinced me that uncertainty is devastating, whether over health, job security, personal relationships or any other important aspect of life. I have seen POW/MIA families' faith in our government shattered by political inattention, deceit, distortions and incompetence. I have also seen their faith renewed by the seriousness and dedication of President Ronald Reagan and others.

Conspiracy and cover-up theories are a myth; there are too many good people involved. Besides, the U.S. Government is totally incapable of an EFFECTIVE cover-up. I've endured self-deluded RAMBO activists and con-artists that have preyed upon some families and the public. Other forms of exploitation have occurred and continue.

The real manipulation, however has come from Hanoi -- Vietnam's leadership, not the Vietnamese people. Before it was politically correct, the POW/MIA families supported humanitarian aid to the people of Vietnam, Laos and Cambodia, assistance to the disabled, school construction and other projects. We still do.

We also support a rational policy to meet Vietnam's political and economic objectives -- as they meet ours in terms of accounting for our missing -- not payment in advance in the naive hope that Vietnam will respond in good faith. Hanoi's record over the years proves my point to objective observers. Vietnam's leadership has enjoyed total continuity through seven U.S. administrations, and they have not been reluctant to exploit those changes, alternating between surges of cooperation and stonewalling. This pattern continues.

Ending uncertainty and bringing facts to waiting families and our nation has been my mission for nearly three decades, over 18 years of which were as Executive Director of the National League of POW/MIA Families. We simply want answers that, according to senior U.S. Government officials over many years, could readily be provided on hundreds of missing Americans if the Vietnamese leadership makes the decision to cooperate seriously. To date, Hanoi has not demonstrated good faith, much less met President Clinton's certification to Congress that they are "cooperating in full faith."

One of my greatest frustrations has comes from uninformed people who, in the name of "healing" and "putting the past behind," seem to believe that facts, principles and justice for those who serve can be ignored. It appears that in the current environment, commitments can be summarily dismissed to purse economic and political objectives, even when answers are being deliberately withheld as is the case with Vietnam. And, despite the smaller number of Vietnam War missing compared to other wars -- it is very different -- the U.S. was not the "victor," had no access to the battlefield and faced a national Vietnamese policy of exploiting the issue for their political and economic objectives.

We waited impatiently as president Carter granted amnesty to those who avoided the draft or deserted, asking anyone who would listen, "What about us? What about unreturned US POWs and other still missing who were last known alive?" Answers didn't come. Oh, there were passive excuses for policy developed by then Assistant Secretary of State Richard Holbrooke. His solution? He told us, "Accounting for America's POW/MIA's is the "hoped-for by-product" of normalization of relations with Vietnam." That naive assumption failed in the late 1970's, and it isn't working now.

My experiences related to the Vietnam War were not sought, but were instructive, educational and strengthening. Much of what I have learned has been eye-opening and not necessarily positive, but my appreciation for those who serve our nation has also increased, and there are many ways to meet that obligation.

Ann Mills Griffiths is Executive Director of the NATIONAL LEAGUE OF FAMILIES OF AMERICAN PRISONERS AND MISSING IN SOUTHEAST ASIA
