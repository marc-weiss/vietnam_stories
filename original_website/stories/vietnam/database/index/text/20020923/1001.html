My life has been changed forever. The United States didn't send me to Vietnam for 13 months, it sent me to Vietnam for a lifetime. There hasn't been a day since January 28, 1968 that I don't think of Vietnam. I don't dwell on it for hours, but every day I think of Vietnam even if for only a few seconds. I tell my students that it was the greatest and worst experience of my life. It was a great experience because it got me to look into the feelings and sufferings of others and that has made me a much better person. I think that my experiences have made me a better teacher because I can feel empathy for my students when they are scared or nervous. I returned home from Vietnam in 1969 and went back to college. I wasn't able to maintain any long lasting relationships because it took a long while for me to straighten mysaelf out. I got married in 1980 and I have two children, a son who is 17 and a daughter who is 8 years old. My values haven't really changed, I've alays been a sensitive person and I believe I still am. I've always judged people by the goodness of their heart and not by the color of their skin or their religion. My time in Vietnam aged my parents a great deal. I just pray that my son never has to go to war. I believe that my family has fought enough wars for this country. My grtandfather fought in WWI, my father in WWII, my uncle who lived with us fought in Korea and I fought in Vietnam. I haven't changed my politics all that much, fiscally I am a conservative and socially a moderate. After graduating from college, I became a teacher and I have been doing it for 30 years. I am going to retire after this year. I don't even like to see a dead animal, death scares me. My philosophy on life is to be as kind as possible to all. It is hard for me to understand how our country has become so hateful and mean. It amazes me that so many of our people are so angry and they have had nothing but the best that our country has to offer. So many of us that have suffered the worst that our government can give still love this country.I was a soldier. I was drafted in 1966 and ended up enlisting for an extra year so that I didn't have to go to Vietnam as an infantryman. I ended up going to Vietnam as a Military Policeman. I arrived in Vietnam the day the Tet Offensive started and stayed in Vietnam until Feb, 1969. My most vivid memory of the war is that two friends and myself were stationed at Ft. Bragg before going to the war. We all came from Belmont, MA and one at a time we went to war and one at a time they died. I am the only one to survive and it hurts.

Paul MacAuley
