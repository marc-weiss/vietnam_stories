VIETNAM TRILOGY

I. In 1958, when I was 15 years old, I accompanied my mother to
Saigon. She was a Foreign Service Officer with the US State
Department. From 1951 to 1956 I lived with her in India on another
assignment, so I was prepared to make further adjustments to yet
another culture, another language, another school, another set of
friends. After the chaos and color of India, I was not prepared for the
relative serenity and European flavor of Saigons tree-lined
boulevards. The French left behind not only their lilting language, but
a flair for creating a visually enchanting downtown. Until the
American Embassy was able to find us a house, we stayed for
several weeks at a quayside hotel overlooking the Saigon River.
Plans were made for me to begin 9th Grade at the Embassy
Correspondence School, not-so-charmingly located in a Quonset hut
near town.

Life was easy for this typical teenager during the six months we lived
in Vietnam. I swam at the Sports Club, scootered around town on a
friends moped, did my homework, and cavorted in the South China
Sea, which lapped against beautiful beaches to the north. Little was I
aware of the political unrest roiling beneath the calm surface of my
world in Vietnam. As we left Tan Son Nhut Airport for four years in
Bangkok, Thailand, President Diem was about to be overthrown and
this beautiful country of peaceful people was at the brink of a plunge
into a terrible war.

II. In the fall of 1962, I returned to the US to attend college, after
graduating from the International School in Bangkok. Although I
attended a very small, liberal college in Vermont, after so many years
abroad I felt very much like an outsider. My roots in Asia were strong
and my empathy for its countrymen was unswerving. After all, my
childhood was essentially molded by the customs, culture and
people of Far Eastern lands. When the war in Vietnam invaded the
consciences of many of my college friends, I joined them in a stand
against the killing and we traveled to the March on the Pentagon in
Washington DC. My sister, attending a Quaker boarding school in
Pennsylvania, met us there. And we marched - thousands and
thousands of us - from the Lincoln Memorial across the Potomac to
the Pentagon, which was ringed with hundreds of armed troops. We
went peaceably, and placed flowers in their rifle barrels. My sister
was separated from us, and the next morning she called from a
Virginia jail requesting that we post bail. She told us she was
unceremoniously dragged by her hair to a police van and later
shared a cell with Joan Baez, joyfully singing anti-war songs through
the night. We were almost envious.

III. In the early 80s I visited a dear friend in Virginia who shared my
high school days in Bangkok. She was a close assistant to Robert
Kennedy in Washington, and in the course of her work after his
death, took many visitors to see the Vietnam Memorial. Although I
only had a few days with her, she insisted I visit The Wall. She told
me that every time she went, she wept. I wondered if I would also
weep. After all, this was a war I detested and protested.

I weep now, as I did then. Walking closer and closer to those black
slabs and seeing names -so many names-I was overwhelmed with
sadness at the loss of brave, young men who fell and died so far
away. Still, a part of me has to wonder: What if there were also a wall
in Vietnam inscribed with the names of so many Vietnamese loved
ones gone?

Ellen Schaefer
