My name is Jude Rodamer, and, in 1997, I am 46 years old. I've recently
signed on to the internet, and have had many travels on it. I have
recently been contacting an 18 year old German man, who recently told me
that he thought that 1968 must have been an exciting year. The memories
it brought back to me. I can't imagine how anyone who is my age could
NOT be affected by what happened in Vietnam. Somethings as simple as
helicopters flying overhead effect me now. How do I explain to this
young man what those years were like. My generation, I had thought, had
been so lucky. Our parents had worked so hard to give us a good life, a
good education. Suddenly, our whole world blew up in our faces. We were
the last generation who, at least for part of their lives, truly
believed that the government was telling us the truth. Not only did we
find that they were lying to us, they were trying to kill us too. I've
never been one of those people who thought that the u.s. soldiers were
murderers, but I don't think they understand that there was a war here
too! If you were politically active in the late 60's & 70's you got to
be rather paranoic. Vietnam changed my whole life. It changed the way
I see my government. It changed the way I see big business. It changed
the way I saw my parent's generation. It changed the way I see
politics. It changed the way I see my generation. It changed the way I
see the media.
All my trust in these things are gone. I question everything now.

I live about a 10 minute drive away from Kent State University. I've
watched through the years how the university has tried to do everything
it could to try to bury what happened there. The monuments and the
yearly programs around May 4th only occur because of outside forces
working hard to make the events come about. Most of the students there
haven't a clue as to what happened, all those years ago. Most could care
less. If it weren't for a small group of truly dedicated people,
nothing would happen on the 4th there. I understand now why the Jews try
so desparately to keep the memory of the holocaust alive. Some of us
will never forget Vietnam and May 4th...but what will happen when we are
all dead?

My only hope about Vietnam is that the veterans of it will think twice
about sending their sons to go and fight and maybe die for some cause
that the government has thought important. If, when that time comes, and
they too, blindly send their sons off to die, then, certainly, we have
learned nothing from Vietnam. I truly hope that day won't ever come.
