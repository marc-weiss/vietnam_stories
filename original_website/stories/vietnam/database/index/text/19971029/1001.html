I have spent a lot of time reading your stories about the Vietnam Era. I was in Vietnam in 1968 with the 1/9 1st Cavalry Division, on the top of Chon Ho Mountain in the Ahn Khe Pass. I flew with Charlie Troop using gunships and slicks (UH1-B Hueys) I have gazed at the questions: "How are you different TODAY....." It seems my whole life goes up to Vietnam and there is nothing after it. Vietnam was supposed to mean so much to America, and we gave it our best...and we are still recognized as losers of that war. The best and worst times of my life happened there. We lived together, laughed and died thinking it was for our country. This not the same country I went to give my all for. I was 18 years old, and turned 19 positioned behind an M-60. I learned how little America cared for us. We lost as many men in my company to mechanical failures as we did to the VC/NVA. We flew with rotor blades with over 1200 hours on them...and they were supposed to be replaced every 100 hours. I learned my values in Vietnam, I learned to be discreet in relationships, I learned not to trust politicians. I learned that dying was easier than death, because you only die once. I sit here in my wheelchair that I resigned to over there. I have four children...I know how they feel, but they cannot understand mine. I have taught them that the more so-called friends they have, the more problems and pain they will have. Now, they know what I meant. I don't think America even can understand what Vietnam did to us. When they turned around and abandoned what we had died for, they disgraced our dreams. If Vietnam meant so little to them, how can they expect us to place any relevance at all to anything. Here, in America, young boys die in a loved one's arms...in Nam, there was nobody but us to hold a dying friend. I still feel those pains and I will take them to my grave. Every one has been taught that we want to be alone...we was forced to take that position. When we needed to reason with it, nobody wanted to listen. Now they wonder why we have nothing to say. We are safer that way...when you let someone control your emotions, then they have control of you. My wife has told me many times that she wished she had some other woman to deal with...she would know what to do...she doesn't know how to deal with this. I am still dwelling deeply on Vietnam. As far as philosophy goes, the Bible says, "No greater love hath any man than this, that a man would lay down his life for a friend." We lived by that virtue, but our "friends" had their backs turned against us.

Eldon Murray
1948
Bowling Green, KY USA
