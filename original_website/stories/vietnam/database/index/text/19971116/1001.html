I am only nineteen years old. I was born three years after the Vietnam War was over. Some might say I have no business writing about something for which I was not alive yet, however I too have a story. When I was in high school, I voluteered at the Wall as a Park Ranger. During the summer months I would bake under the glaring black marble and watch the tourists stare at the memorial. On my very first day as a voluteer, however, my eyes were opened to the tradegy that is war. That day a short, heavy, Mexican veteran, dressed in casual clothes and dog tags around his neck, approached me with tears in his eyes. He couldn't find his friends, he said. He needed help, he said, because it was his first time visiting the memorial. I took him to the apex of the memorial, retrieved the list of names, and started to look for each name he gave me. There must have been fifteen or twenty. When we had found them all, I walked him to each panel. He was crying so hard that I had to guide his hand to the panel to touch the name, because his tears were affecting his vision. He told me the story of how each one died. One was shot by a sniper through the eye. Another was claimed by a land mine. Still others had been killed as they fought right beside this Mexican veteran. I made a rubbing of each name for him, put them all in a plastic bag I had in my pocket, and gave them to him when we were done. After I had made the last rubbing, he turned to me, his eyes red. He had stopped crying. Without a word I gave him the rubbings. He took them, then grasped my hand. We stood there, in the middle of the memorial, looking at each other; a man who had experienced hell, and a boy who couldn't possibly imagine it. Thank you, he said and turned away. He walked up the path towards the kiosk. I watched him go, then I too turned and walked back to the apex.

Ian Lear-Nickum
1978
Bethesda, Md USA
