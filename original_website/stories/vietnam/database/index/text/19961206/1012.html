Folks:

I was a young mother, poet, student and protester during the Vietnam War. However, it wasn't until a few years ago that I could bring myself to visit The Wall. And, even then, my reaction to it could only be expressed obliquely.

There follows the prose poem I wrote. Thanks for taking the time to read it.

Marie Harris

LEAVING THE WALL

Veteran's Day, 1992

The dead bird ended up with a homeless man.It wasn't an urban bird, sparrow or some other savvy pretzel-crumbivore common to city sidewalks and public spaces around monuments, but a hapless woodcock. Tweed feathers; long, tapered beak; stocky; shy. Not streetwise in any way. Was the cruise season window display--the palms--its undoing? When we found it on the pavement,the eye was still bright,the body not yet cold. We moved it to an iron grate under a ginkgo tree near the curb halfway up the block. At the intersection of 14th and New York,a wiry man in a camouflage jacket left his bundles and approached us. Unable to encounter a stranger without offering some scrap of chat, I say, Looks an awful lot like rain. Have you ever lived in the country? Next thing we're all three talking like neighbors about the weather as we walk with him back to where the woodcock lies in slick yellow leaves. How about that, he says, I don't believe I've ever seen this bird. And he tells us where he grew up and he asks where we're from and he's cupping the bird in both hands while he's talking so there's nothing for it but to tuck the ten in the coin pocket of his jeans.

Marie Harris Barrington, NH 03825
