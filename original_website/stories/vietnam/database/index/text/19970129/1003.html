A RECKONING

The following is a letter I sent out by e-mail over the holidays to a
list of old friends from high school days who have stayed in touch with
each other. I have
been the stranger in this group, checking in once a year, perhaps. It's
self-explanatory. I was afraid I would get no responses. Instead I got
many very thoughtful responses that have begun to renew old friendships
long separated by the Vietnam War


******************************************************
Happy Holidays once again to old friends.

Christmas/New Years is always a good time for reflection, particularly
on things concerning friends, family, etc. Thought I'd take a moment and
catch up a bit with you folks. Hope you don't mind my hogging your
bandwidth for a bit.

This has been a strange year for me. Actually, a strange 7/12 of a year.
Last Memorial Day, I began a bit of a quest to flesh out a part of my
identity that has been little known even to me, let along to most of my
friends. It started when I made a conscious effort to remember friends I
had made, and some I lost, in Vietnam. It was 30 years ago this year I
made my trip to Uncle Sam's all-expenses-paid SE Asian vacation
paradise. I realized I had practically no memories at all of that year.

I turned to the Internet for some sense of familiarity. I began using
various vets pages and services to seek out those who might have known
me in Vietnam. I lumbered into a few vets newsgroups and lists and began
to make new friends who had shared some of the same wierdness, both
in-country and in the years following our homecomings. Several of these
folks encouraged me to write: to begin simply just trying to write down
what I remembered, starting with a simple description of how I got
there. Some things began to come back, but so did the dawning
realization that this selective amnesia is a very widely shared
phenomenon. And so, of course, are other things, like sequences of
busted marriages, drifting away from friends, strings of job changes,
lengthy bouts with drugs, alcohol, escapism and narcissism, all of which
pretty well characterize the six or seven years after I got out.

I spent much of that time bouncing through shallow relationships,
chasing enlightenment, plugging through therapy, acid, and mysticism,
saturating myself with largely impersonal sensations through sex,
politics, travel, etc. I bounced from the East Village and the happening
scene of New York to the hippy happy days of Virginia communes and some
crazy, though really wonderful times, playing naked and stoned in the
woods and streams of a made-up vision of paradise. Surely much of it was
the times, of course, but then I now feel that Vietnam affected us all
in strange ways, whether we went there or not. At no time, then, did I
attribute any of my persistent anxieties to the war and its cultural or
social meaning at home. Nearly three years of intensive group therapy
did a good job of purging me of the crippling fears and craziness,
but never once in any of those weekly--sometimes daily--sessions did I
ever talk about my taste of the war, nor did anyone ask. It was a taboo
subject. Slowly, but surely, I purged myself of Vietnam, or at least of
any conscious awareness of it in my life. Over the years sanity, peace,
love and fulfillment crept into my life in spite of myself. But I have
never lost the sense that I was hiding something, running from
something, not coming clean with anyone around me. I forgot about
Vietnam, and it never occurred to me to look into that part of my soul
for answers to anything.

Beginning five years ago, I made a conscious decision to think about
Vietnam twice a year: on Memorial Day and Veteran's Day. I began the
first few years by sneaking quietly out of work and going down to the
Virginia War Memorial overlooking the James. The WWII and Korean vets
would be marching and flag-waving and toting chests of medals and their
VFW and American Legion hats. They'd see me and turn their backs
knowing, from my age, I suppose, that mine was THAT war. I would look at
the War mementos encased in acrylic blocks, and at the names of the
Virginia dead, but I didn't know any of these people, and I, after all,
came home whole. I had visited the Wall when it first was erected and I
felt a wave of sadness I could not explain. My wife's support was
unwaivering. Finally, this past Memorial Day I fully realized how much
memory I had lost of an episode that had colored my life as much as
any--more than most.

I now spend a couple hours each night on-line. Listening, talking when
so moved, on a list of fellow vets. Some are clearly in serious pain;
some have fought the Vietnam devil every day of their lives since
returning to "the world." Others tune in from time to time and tell
stories: sad stories, anguished stories, hilarious stories. The list
includes men and women: soldiers, sailors, marines, nurses, Red Cross
volunteers, and even stewardesses who ran the flights of troops back and
forth. All of us recognize the scars, the joys, the depth of feelings
that we carry from our experiences. I cannot begin to imagine the
nightmares some of these new friends must have: nurses who held dying
patients nearly daily, grunts who found themselves under fire week in
and week out. But we all share an ability, a willingness, maybe some
would say an unhealthy eagerness to talk about it, remember it, put it
into some perspective. To heal ourselves of fears and pains we know will
never leave us.

This past Veteran's Day I met some of these Internet friends in DC. We
drank beer and laughed. We visited the Wall. It was one of the most
important events in my life of the past 30 years. I remembered that I
had a snapshot of a bridge my unit had built, and I remembered there was
a sign on the bridge, commemorating it to the memory of a soldier who
had died building it. It was a memory I didn't know I had. My failing
eyes could not make out the name, but Robin could read it, so we visited
his name on the Wall. I did not cry. There was too big a crowd, and I
was with a new friend who had clearly survived a war that was far more
ferocious than my own. I felt ashamed more than anything else, although
I realized, too, that was just a way of protecting myself from some
pain.

I have known most of you since we were in high school. Some of you I
barely know, and others I have loved as dearly as I could. But we have
never, ever, talked about this part of my life. It's not your fault. I
have spent most of my life trying not to talk about it. Now here I am
flooding your mailbox with diarrhea of the electric page. Our world
tried to deny that war, or to paint it in two-dimensional, politicized
colors. But it was as real as anything you know in your everyday lives.
And its effects on flesh, blood, mind and spirit is hard to imagine,
even if you were there; hence the forgetfulness. I want to retain the
friendships of the past, but I can no longer relate to anyone on any
meaningful level unless we all can deal with this part of my identity.
It's not all that I am, by any means, but it is important to me, and I
can no longer pretend it doesn't exist. Most of us have grown apart over
the years, and I blame the war for that as much as anything. The price
of my forgetting has been too high. Over this next year I expect to do
some serious remembering. I may not succeed, but I will try. I hope
you'll all hang with me.

Peace, love, and please have very happy holidays and a great New Year.

Dan

Vietnam Service: 511th Engr. Co. and 70th Engr. Bn. (Combat) An Khe, RVN 1966-67
