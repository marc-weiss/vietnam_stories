Vietnam Stories

by John C. Ratliff

Vietnam was one of those experiences that changes people, and the nations in which they inhabit. The Vietnam experience was individual, and yet universal. It has changed each individual, sometimes in ways unknown to them, and it has changes all of us. We are a different nation than we would have been, and we are different individuals too. You ask how Vietnam has caused me to be different, and how it affected my values, relationships, children and/or parents, politics, livelihood, attitude towards life and philosophy of life. A book could be written about this topic. My Vietnam experience has changed all this areas. My values solidified. I am now married to a woman I would never have even met without the Vietnam experience. My children hear my stories (they aren't bad ones, some are very funny), and understand that even in war, people are people. My trust of politicians has been tested, and there is at times a distrust of politicians. I have always considered life precious, but war makes it more apparent that life can also be short. Therefore, I feel a deep commitment to live life to my best, and not waste it. This also leads to my contributing where, sometimes, it isn't appreciated. This has affected my livelihood, in that I was recently fired from a job because I wasn't a "team player." I was, but this particular team leader couldn't stand alternative points of view. I can only speculate, but I had written an article in answer to a war protester's article wondering why there were no memorials to protesters. I told him. It turns out that my supervisor was a war protester too. Whether this had anything to do with my dismissal from a job in occupational safety and health after 18 years experience, I do not know. However, it is within the realm of possibility. But my philosophy of life is that it is important to live ethically, and that the universal love concept is centerpiece in my being. This was brought into sharp focus during my days in the US Air Force, and especially in Vietnam. I was stationed with the 37th Aerospace Rescue and Recovery Squadron at DaNang Airfield, RVN between October 21, 1970 and June 9, 1971. My duties were as a Pararescueman. While there, although the air war had wound down a bit, I was involved in one rescue behind enemy lines in North Vietnam and with numerous incidents around DaNang and Bien Hoa. Seeing rocket attacks, and the horrible effects of women and children literally blown apart by them, has had a lasting effect on me. It has shown me how lucky we are to live here in the United States of America, where for the most part this type of thing doesn't happen. This is perhaps the most vivid memory I had in Vietnam. We were in the operations room of our alert hanger when we heard the rockets "incoming." We hit the floor, even though we were on the second story of the building. There were two explosions, and it was over. We went downstairs and outside the room to see where the rocket had hit. The rocket had landed on the shore-side of the base, in the military housing for the Vietnamese. We were still rattled by the explosion, which was a few hundred yards away, when one of the other pararescuemen ran up to me and said, "John, there's some injured people there. Let's take a medical kit and see if we can help." So we grabbed the med. kit and ran to the destroyed building. By the time we had reached the building, the ARVN ambulance had already picked up the injured, which I heard was a little boy with an amputated leg or arm. They said there were two people killed. We found out later that the rocket had made a direct hit on the bed in which they were sleeping. We searched for others in the small building, which was torn into pieces of wood at this point, when I came to the realization that the dead people were all around me. Their bodies had been blasted apart and were smeared in small pieces on the building. It was a sickening sight and smell, because the smell was as much a part of the experience as the sight. The lingering smell of human bodies blown to pieces and all around you is one which cannot be described, but which leaves an vivid imprint on the mind. We helped pick up pieces of the bodies for the authorities. I had volunteered to go to DaNang, and had extended my enlistment to do so. Before getting to DaNang I also went to Oregon State University and asked for an acceptance letter to summer school in June of 1971. With this letter, I worked an early out off the extension to leave Vietnam. I went directly from Vietnam to summer school at OSU. When I got back, I was starting all over again. In the Air Force, I had attained the rank of Staff Sergeant. Now, back at school, I was just another student. Nobody asked about Vietnam. Nobody paid much attention to the news about Vietnam unless it dealt with protesters. When, some six months later, there was a brief news clip about a rescue helicopter from my old unit being lost, and the crew missing, I couldn't find out much information. I did understand that one other pararescueman I had known had died. It wasn't until this year (1996) that I joined the Pararescue Association and found out about others I had served with who died just before or after I was in-country. It was strange, but I felt closer to the Chinese community on-campus than to the other Americans. I took a tennis course that summer, and there were three Chinese students there. No other students would team with them, so I volunteered. We got to know each other and talk about our experiences. One was a Chinese guy from South Africa, and the other two were from Hong Kong. Each had their own experiences, but I felt a closer link to them than to the Americans. I also roomed with a Chinese guy, and through him finally got to know the gal from Hong Kong who eventually became my wife. Had I not joined the service, actually to gain a choice rather than be drafted, I would never have met these people, and never met my wife. I would be married to a totally different person, probably my childhood girlfriend, and everything would today be much different. Obviously, I am no longer the country boy from Oregon, but have seen much of the world, explored areas and done things that few of my countrymen could contemplate (I was a part of the Apollo 13 recovery team too--except that the capsule came down in the wrong ocean, next to a Navy ship), and met so many different people with different perspectives, that I could not be the same person as before. This has led to some very interesting experiences on the job. In this way, I am quite thankful for the entire experience, because I have a fine family, with two teenage boys, and am for the most part happy. But the strains of loosing my 18+ year job recently are having an effect. My job until last July was as a safety and health consultant for a state-run insurance company. We specialized in workers' compensation insurance. But my commitment to workplace safety had ties to my service experience. I've seen the results of accidents, such as the F-100 that crashed on takeoff at Kunsan AF, Korea. A wind tank came off the lead F-100, which flipped it into the pathway of the trailing F-100. The pilot of the trailing aircraft dragged the right wing on the runway to avoid a collision with the plane which was cart wheeling to his left in front of him, almost stalled the aircraft, but made it off the ground. He flew a circle of the base and immediately landed, completely rattled but okay. His reactions had saved his life. We heard that he was then about fourth on the list for Astronaut training, and knew why he deserved it. Accidents such as these, and the combat ones I as well as the accidents I worked on in Eugene, Oregon as an EMT gave me a much different perspective on my job of accident prevention than other consultants. I probably felt much more deeply about the situations, because I had picked up the results of these accidents. I therefore got into some hot water, and was ultimately fired, because I disagreed with my team leader about how to implement prevention strategies. Never mind that I had seventeen years experience in the field, and she had none. I had gotten into hot water before. The trouble came when I took over for an OSHA inspector to investigate an electrocution. A guy had gone up on a ladder truck to replace a florescent light in an outdoor sign. Somehow, he had been electrocuted in the process, and fell out of the basket. At the time, we didn't know if the electrocution or the fall had killed him. But the inspector was clearly shaken, and did not want to go up that ladder to investigate. He did not have training in electricity or working at heights. I took over, made a seat harness of webbing I carried with me, went up the ladder and, using a hand-held volt meter, determined that the metal case for the light had become electrified to the tune of 450 volts. The sign, over the years, had lost its ground. When my supervisor found out about it, he issued a written reprimand for taking over the inspector's job, even though we insured him too Last summer I was fired for not getting along with a new supervisor. Again, I do relate this to my service and Vietnam experience because when it comes to what I felt were serious issues about servicing firms, I wouldn't give way. I felt there were ethical issues involved. At one point, my supervisor said "These ethical issues are important to you, aren't they?" When I said yes, she implied that they weren't to her. To be honest, I think that my old company was looking for reasons to fire me as I had told them of problems within its management, and because they were downsizing. But that doesn't make it easier, and they used my values against my continued employment with them. I learned during this process that my supervisor had been a war protester, and perhaps an article I had written for the Oregonian newspaper influenced her (although I'm not sure she was aware of it). The article was in reply to one written by a former war protester, Dwight Morrill, entitled "America's real war heroes forgotten as losers rewrite history," and published in The Oregonian newspaper, April 30, 1996. I'm old enough to have been one of those kids who was supposed to go. "It has bothered me for some time how that time in our past gets 'creatively' rewritten, almost on a daily basis. "During the Reagan/Bush years, you'd have thought the only thing wrong with that war was the fact the politicians wouldn't let the generals use nuclear weapons. "More recently, the speed at which Clinton rushed to apologize for the little bit of protesting he did was disgusting. "When is one of these spineless politicians going to stand up and say, 'you're damned right I marched against the Vietnam War, and my only regret is that I didn't do more to stop it.' "But more alarming to me today is the making of heroes from the Vietnam War and the making of villains of those who protested. No matter how wrong the war was, those who fought to stop the war are villains. "Are these the lessons we want passed on to our children and grandchildren? "Where are the memorials to those who fought to stop that war? Those who went to prison for refusing to go, those who were drafted and then were imprisoned for refusing to go, and those who had courage to question the war and demonstrated for the first time in their lives. Where are the GI bills for them? "Whenever we talk about Nazi Germany, we ask where were the 'good Germans.' Why didn't they stand up and stop Hitler? Well, in our own culture we've had examples of those who have stood up to be counted and quickly got lost in the rewriting of history by politicians. "When will we start to honor those who have had the courage to go against popular opinion to fight for a just cause?" "Dwight Morrill is a computer consultant who lives in Colton. He served 10 months in a federal penitentiary in 1969 for refusing to report for draft induction." __________ I wrote a reply to this article. __________ Memorials are for Dead People The Oregonian renamed it: Real Heroes of Vietnam Too Often Still Nameless

Mr. Dwight Morrill's article, "America's real war heroes forgotten as losers rewrite history" has stuck a nerve with me. He asks why there are no memorials for war protesters? I'll tell him--there are very few dead war protesters! Memorials are for dead people, usually those who lost their lives on the field of battle. Bravery is not in protesting an unethical war. Bravery is in facing a life-threatening event, and heroism is in deciding to potentially sacrifice yourself to a greater good. I also have a song we sang during the Vietnam War:

Rescue, rescue is our cry. Save that man or he may die. Husky, Husky hover low So I can save ol' GI Joe.

I don't give a damn, So drop me down In that jungle On the ground.

GI Joe He ain't a feeling' well, So I pick him up and I run like hell. I put him on that rescue sling And I'm in low where the bullets sing.

It's on that sling and up we go, Leave the enemy down below. On my way back to my base, I look my GI in the face.

I hand him a smoke And he shakes my hand. And I'm so glad I'm a rescue man.

Few in America know the names genuine heroes from the Vietnam War. Who can say who Steve Northern is? He had over 50 lives saved from behind enemy lines. Who is Charlie King? He was on a mission in Laos to save a pilot, and went down the hoist to get him. He was shot and wounded in a trap to down the helicopter. He waved the 'chopper off to save his crew. I wore his POW/MIA bracelet. These names have been lost to the American public. Revisionist history? Silvester Stallone isn't a war hero. Neither is Chuck Norris. We've forgotten who really did the work, and given credit to actors. Actors--those who play other people under very controlled conditions. A Vietnamese sapper would have gotten them in about two seconds! War protesters if they are genuine in their concern, deserve recognition. War is hell! "War is not healthy for children, and other living things." Just remember, we didn't start the Vietnam War. We tried to end a takeover that ultimately displaced a population. We held off genocide for a decade in Cambodia. Mr. Morrill thinks we should build memorials to war protesters. This is an ego trip. These are people who actively discriminated against US Servicemen and women when we returned; spat on us, yelled obscenities at us! If there is to be a memorial to war protesters, it should go to those who fell at Kent State. The protesters who were real heroes deserving of memorials were the Buddhist monks in Vietnam who set themselves on fire. Do we remember their names? The protesters in the U.S. at times jeopardized U.S. citizens by their action. Is that heroism? I don't think so. If we really wish recognize heroism, we should find out who they are, and start honoring them. Were there protesters who were heroes? Certainly, but most weren't. Were there heroes we don't know? Certainly, but it seems we don't care. Let's find out these names. We now have an immigrant community from Vietnam, Laos, and other Southeast Asian countries (Koreans took an active part too). Many of these people are also genuine war heroes. They have become us, U.S citizens, because they could not stay where they were. We should find out about them. I thank these people for their sacrifices. I challenge the Oregonian, and other Oregonians, to find out the history of those around us, and give tribute to those whose heroism deserves honor. It would be a fine gesture for Memorial Day. __________ I also received some very interesting letters from other readers: __________ 5/9/96 Mr. Ratliff, Good article on war protesters. __________ May 10, 1996 Dear Mr. Ratliff, I read your article "Real Heroes of VietNam Too Often Still Nameless" in the Oregonian. I was moved by your words and felt I should write you and lend my support to your thinking. Despite the years that have passed since the VietNam War, it still feels like it was just yesterday, sometimes even closer. I think that we, as a country, have no idea the healing that is yet necessary as a result of our experience in VietNam. I agree that memorials are intended for those who have died so that others might live. This would certainly include soldiers, the murdered students of Kent State, the Buddhist monks that doused their bodies with gasoline and burned their bodies to ashes in the hope that the killing would stop, to the smokejumpers that die trying to keep deadly fires from killing more of God's creatures, human and otherwise. The list goes on. Protesters, simply due to their activities while protesting, in no way qualify for any kind of memorial. I think that they, along with the rest of the people that experienced the VietNam War, were and will be eternally affected by the demons of those times. But no memorial is warranted. They may have been right; they may have had strong convictions; they may have been courageous; they were not heros. Only a small portion of this country's population knows the kind of heroes that you and I speak of: good men and women with families, dreams, futures, a lifetime waiting for them, killed in the name of humanity and altruism, saving others irregardless of what they personally felt about the "rightness" of their war. God knows, I often wish others, more deserving, less "good, had died instead of my friends; I am ashamed to admit it. But I certainly will not tolerate the kind of bullshit the Mr. Morrill peddles about heroes. What the fuck does he know about heroes, life lost to death? CG U.S.N. 1969-1976 P.S. Ratliff REGARDING YOUR "IN MY OPINION" ARTICLE, 5/19/96, THE OREGONIAN In what way did your "dead" heroes of the Vietnam War sacrifice themselves to a grater good? That particular war was immoral, illegal and not supported by one other country. Your dead heroes sacrificed themselves for big business, which made a financial "killing" on the bloody battlefields of Vietnam. It took just as much courage on the part of the draft-age civilians to protest the Vietnam war and risk the unfair taunts of their peers as it did to become involved in it. You have put all war protesters into one category. Most of them did not "spat or yell obscenities" at returning servicemen involved. Our intent was to help end the war and save lives. My son and half the men on his ship came back protesting the war. Too late they discovered just what a farce it was. My idea of heroes are the servicemen and women who fought in that war of infamy but can now admit that it was a mistake instead of supporting the killing of thousands of innocent people on both sides just because they were caught up in the devastation. Admitting defeat takes real courage. We may not have started the Vietnam War, but we certainly prolonged it. Who is to say that the protesters didn't help end the killing. They brought a president to his knees. And you might bone up on your history to see what part Richard Nixon and Henry Kissinger played in prolonging the genocide in Cambodia. This may have been a mistake, because the letter in all capitol letters above, by JW, was not the last he was to send to me. In fact, I received four more letters, and did not reply. This person seemed disturbed to me, and I had hoped that he wouldn't come to my home town to confront me. I felt LMS was wrong on several counts, and showed her an article from National Geographic on Cambodia. But she never returned the dialog. As can be seen, for many, including me, Vietnam has an enduring meaning. I don't tolerate unethical behavior well. I try to make important decisions with everyone's input, but know that ultimately I am responsible for my own actions. If I disagree with a position, and find it intolerable, I will resign rather than compromise. But I can compromise on the small, everyday things that we all have to do. My wife disagrees with me on some of these issues, and that has led to some discussions and conflicts. But this is what marriage is about, being able to discuss things, and get other people's opinions on things. I've found out that I was wrong on some occasions, and will change my opinion or decision. We made so many decisions in Vietnam and stuck to them even when we knew they were wrong that I have learned to undo a bad decision, if that is possible. But overall, everyone experiences life. How we react to it decides who we are, and what kind of character we have built. Each part of one's character is built brick by brick. Sometimes the rough edges need to be sawed off, or shaped to fit different circumstances. But our decisions build our character just as surely as each brick can build a patio. I cannot take the bricks out, but I can look back and decide whether I've done well or poorly, and go on from there. Right now, I'm picking myself up, working to establish a business or find another job, and writing. This is one of the projects, and I'm also writing a book on my service experiences. I think we have much to learn, and to understand that there was more to the Vietnam experience than the war, that real people were there, funny guys who worked hard, and epitomized the best of our society as well as the worst.

John C. Ratliff
