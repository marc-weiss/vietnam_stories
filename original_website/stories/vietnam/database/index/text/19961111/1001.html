In January 1965, I was 23, in a 1-year grad school program, and totally against the war. I was horrified by the murder, wounding and dislocation of innocent peasants. And I read my Bernard Fall and Jean Lacouture, and felt we were intervening in an area where we had no business. I could not honestly say I was a conscientious objector - I knew I would have fought in World War II against Hitler. But in this case I saw my own country as the aggressor.

In this context, the idea that my government wanted to draft me to fight in this war threw me into psychological despair and a moral abyss. I had strongly believed in my government and nation growing up. Vietnam now proved that my own government was not only made up of murderers of innocent peasants, but was willing to murder me in this cause. I felt betrayed not only by the government but an entire older generation - including virtually all our elected representatives, the media and other "opinion-makers" - who were clearly willing to kill peasants, kill me, and kill my generation on behalf of a cause in which neither I nor really they believed.

It was bad enough that they were liars. But the bottom line was that they were also murderers. There was not a day during those years when Vietnam did not dominate my thoughts, feelings and psyche on some level.

Now, thirty years later, Vietnam remains the dominant societal event of my lifetime, and continues to shape how I experience the world.

On the one hand, it had made me a great deal more cynical about human purpose. The behavior of my leaders during the war, combined with the post-war failures of the Vietnamese revolutionaries I once so admired, have led me to conclude that human problems lie in the human psyche not institutional arrangements. I tend not only to distrust the behavior of all governments and opinion-makers, assuming it is at best self-interested and misguided, at worst as venal and debased as that of Johnson, Nixon and Kissinger. I also question the whole idea of "the meaning of life." I wound up as an educational advisor and then anti-war activist in Indochina, and learned of things there - including the destruction of whole societies by U.S. bombing - that can never be made right again. On some level, I do not feel I have developed a new moral worldview to replace the one that was destroyed by Vietnam, and that on some level I am internally off-balance as a result.

On the other hand, Vietnam brought blessings. It helped me trust far more in my own instincts, to be far less inclined to give away my power to others, to be more realistic and less naive, more courageous and wiser. Above all, it helped propel me on a search which had led to finding a significant sense of peace in spiritual realms transcending the separation and division of the polity. This sense of peace is internal, non-verbal, and beyond the categories of human purpose.

I have, for example, come to make a distinction between the behavior and the person. I hate what MacNamera did in Vietnam, but no longer hate him as a person. I believe that had I been born into his circumstances I would have done the same thing -- and that we are all, all, flawed by the mismatch between our human brains and pre-human instincts.

I would tell our descendants that Vietnam teaches us to trust ourselves not leaders, to find comfort in the spiritual not political realm, and to identify primarily with other living beings not a particular nation, race or gender. I would also say that Vietnam teaches that in this realm it is necessary to resist social injustice and environmental blindness, not only because it is morally right but evolutionarily necessary. We thought we could destroy Vietam without destroying ourselves. We were wrong. The roots of our mounting social problems today go back to Vietnam, and its destruction of our post-war moral order. Similarly, we cannot continue to poison our environment or ignore growing inner-city and Third World poverty without, sooner or later, poihsoning the rest of our world.
