I found the Nov 27 Maya Lin piece very moving and so pertinent to both USA and Canada given that we are both "melting pot" countries. I missed not having the current views of the mustacheod protester who requested at the Senate/Congress hearings that the matter be re-opened. The Maya LIN update was of great interest as I often wondered what had become of her after the controversy surrounding her memorial design. How poignant that even today artists come under pressure and after a period of time when the viewers minds adapt to what was a leading edge design.

I have recently returned from HoChiMinh/Saigon and I was amazed to learn that younger children (upto about 16) are learning lessons in school using the English language, they have to go to school during the vacations if they want to learn Vietnamese. The use of the US dollar is rampant and open, although not demanded instead of VN Dong. The US might have lost the fighting war but it won the country. I went not as a tourist but as a technician and stayed for periods of time (several) with VN families at their homes, living and eating with them. It was a great eye opener and only proves that this war should have been avoided and what a waste of resources it was.

Both our countries have large VN populations, and I found that my presence in the VN countryside (classified as restricted areas) was not met with curiosity or any other negative feeling but rather welcomed as a friend. These were not organised parties but rather one on one contacts with the people as we worked.

The police are present - both the uniformed as well as the "secret" types - they all carry cellulars - there appeared to be considerable "freedom" inasmuch as US papers were freely available on the streets of HCM - no censorship unlike the "free" country of Singapore - anyone could buy a telephone calling card and there were no restrictions on the use of the public telephones to call overseas - one of my co-travellers called her husband in Toronto several times using the familiar +1-416- etc and although the call quality was noisy (poor signal to noise) the access was unimpeded.

The InterNet is there - e-mail only because of bandwidth constrictions - and I am in frequent contact with several e-mail addresses over there and the subjects have been quite wide ranging and no restrictions or reaction from any government body has been reported.

As I said, not a completely free country, but very open given the circumstances (less so outside HoChiMinh) and only serves to highlight the total waste of US resources and lives.
