I was ten years old when the Vietnam War finally ended. I don't remember the frantic airlifts or the grainy newscasts; my mother turned these off, having seen the depressing story far too long. To me, the war was less an event than a state of mind. People were dissilusioned with their government and their leaders. Something of American greatness got lost over there, and it never really found its way back in one piece. This was an ever-present feeling that existed for me as I was entering my teenage years. The war rode me piggy-back for years. When I entered college and began learning the truth of the conflict - through stories and documents collected - I was stunned to realize how little had been disseminated in secondary school education. The longest war in American history was relegated to a few paragraphs. But even in a college classroom it was still just an historical event to be studied and analyzed. Five years later, I had the privelege of working for then Secretary of State Max Cleland, in Atlanta, Georgia. I was given a book upon entering the office on my first day, which I read that night cover to cover. Cleland, an army captain who had lost three limbs to a grenade explosion in Vietnam, had written a book about his war experiences and triumphant return to a vibrant, fulfilling life as a Georgia state senator, Director of the Veteran's Adminstration under President Carter, and Georgia's Secreatry of State. I had read accounts like this before albeit less inspiring, but it was the man himself that made me stop and take notice. He had a larger than life presence about him that was infectious. Charismatic, intense, and fully in love with life, he exuded something that had been edited out of the simple textbook explanations of my childhood education: that those who fought and were wounded and returned were among us, changing us, educating us in the power of life. He rolled in his wheelchair through the century old state capitol, pushing himself with his one massive arm as if on a great crusade. And a great crusade it was. Max Cleland represents to me the epitomy of the public servant. He took his wounds and used them to constructively help others. He was always there to reach out to the veterans who came to visit the state capitol. His compassion was overwhelming, not only for ex-soldiers but for the disadvantaged and wounded of all walks of life. The opportunity to work and observe this man changed my mind about the duty of history education in this country. It changed the war from a textbook exercise to a real life tragedy. As a nation we have the obligation to tell the stories of these courageous men and women and to help those less fortunate find their wings again. Those who participated in that war are all around us. They are a treasure-trove of information and inspiration. It is my hope that we will utilize these life stories, learn from them, use them, and become more enlightened ourselves.

Alan Spring
1965
Painesville, Ohio, USA
