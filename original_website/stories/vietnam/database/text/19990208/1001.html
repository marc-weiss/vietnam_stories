It seems like I have always lived a double life. One in 1972 and the one I live today.

I was eighteen years of age, fresh out of high school and just released from a southern baptist orphanage. I had no one or thing to call my own or be called apart of. All I knew about Vietnam was what I watched each night on the national news with Chet Huntly and David Brinkley.

I did have two college scholarships to two different universities (Oral Roberts University and Western Kentucky University); but, gave no serious thought to pursuing a higher education until I knew I was mature enough to succeed at it. So, I sought out my nearest Air Force recruiter and eagerly accepted another extension of my previous institutional life.

I took the standard enlisted man's entrance tests and managed to do well enough to land a job as an air traffic controller. Unfortunately, my luck quickly changed by the time I successfully completed basic training. This is when my Training Instructor (TI) personally spoke to me in a caring way and gave me my orders for Air Force Security Police, Ground Base Defense School. My TI (SSgt Vincent)explained how the needs of the Air Force came first. He ended by explaining how the war was coming to an end; unfortunately, there was a bigger need for Security Police to protect our air assets while on the ground in Vietnam.

Needless-to-say, I was not a happy camper and grew less excited on the day I arrived in Vietnam. First, I had just taken the longest flight in the tightest fitting seats I had ever taken. Second, I lost my breath as-soon-as the aircraft doors opened. The humidity was so thick and the air was hot from the sun baked air ramp. I could smell immediately aircraft fuel (JP4) and something else like blood. I soon found out it was blood and decaying flesh as I stepped over an above ground sewage drain called a benjo ditch in order for me to reach my tent like quarters and back again to the mess hall (dinning room). I lost an additional 15 lbs. weight after my firt month crossing the big puddle (ocean).

Today, I can still remember the aircraft, music, and small arms fire sounds. Of course, it doesn't take much to remember the screams from the darkness as our tracer rounds found their mark anyone or thing getting too close to our fields of fire during the night. Most of all, I can still hear my own heart beat and breathing as I laid still each night I had perimeter guard duty. It seemed the more I tried to be quiet and listen to my surroundings, the more my heart and breath grew louder from the fear I deep within side me.

My fear never left me after I returned to the world (USA). It continues to visit my dreams. The smells and sounds come and go like the wind from an ocean breeze. Still, my dreams rule my night as a ghost that refuses to die. The sights of death have no faces for me to remember or identify with; but, the bodies as I watched them drain will forever be imprinted on mind.

I will always love and appreciate my country. I will never understand or accept the statement, "America lost in Vietnam." We (American society)only lost the American dream and the will to give unconditionally for what we truly believe. We lost faith in each other to do what is right for the right reasons. We lost our innocents and trust in our political leadership to hold office for the people; instead, of for themselves. Today, the only thing we can believe in is the American men and women who continue to wear the uniform and carry our flag on distant lands far away. God bless America and save us from our politicians.

Thank-you


1954
Louisville, KY, USA

America, Vietnam, faith
