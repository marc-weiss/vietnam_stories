my story starts off like most others. i left school in my jr. year a military brat whose dad had served in ww11 and korea. life was confusing as it is for most teenagers, but i was treated like a soldier all my life.i could drive almost any type of military vehicle at that time. andunderstood what army life was all about or so i thought i threatened to leave home and that i was tired of being treated like a child. i talked my parents into signing me up in the army with a friend of mine from school. in sep. of 1967 perry and i were sent to ft. polk la.for basic. after that i lost track of perry and i went on to jump school after tank school. in january of 69 i got orders for the nam. after the usual leave at home with family i was on my way to a place i had heard about ever since my enlistment. when we landed the first thing you noticed was the heat.the next thing you noticed was even in a group of over 300 men and women you were alone and treated like a piece of meat. we were told it would be a couple of days before we were assigned to our new unit.later that day several of us were sent to our new units. so much for a couple of days. after being assigned to our new units. we were loaded up like cattle and flown to our basecamps. there you were supposed to spend a week at a school to show you what to look for, but that little program lasted a day and a half. lifes a gamble and i was hit with a marked deck of cards. i wasn't in a tank i was point on a rif through the jungle. when your an f.n.g. you are low man on the pecking order. you haven't earned your life yet. if you manage to beat the odds down the line you then become a one of the chosen, but again in all the confusion i became one of the lucky ones with a guy i only knew as maggoo. he showed me the ropes and how to do your job and stay alive. f.n.g.'s were of no concern for most of the old or short timers. they didn't know you so if you caught one sinloi. after your tour is up and your on the freedom bird home you really don't know how your going to react to the people who have labeled you a child killer, a rapist, and other choice names that seem to suit the times. you have to be almost invisible and unnoticed to get by the swearing and name calling from people who didn't realize you had no choice but to go and survive in a land that people only knew from a map and daily news reports. in 1970 the turmoil of being back in the good ole u.s.a. began to become obvious. the country and i had changed. life as i knew it before nam had dealt me a deadmans hand and i cantell you today it hasn't changed. with three broken and destroyed marriages noww on my fourth and hanging on by a thread it's impossible to get your life straight. for the longest time i knew the nightmares were vivid beyond belief. the images of where you were, were totally changed because of stress of a war that was labeled a police action. if that is so then why did 58,000 plus wind up on a wall. if it was a police action why did it go on from the late 50's until 1975. our beloved government needed the money and not declaring it a war made it easier to get support in the begining. a lot of promises were made to the people of south vietnam by our government, and the us. soldier was expendable. even though big brass had a plan that would bring the war to an end much quicker, our government didn't want to step on anybodies toes and upset them. it goes on forever and still today the v.a. continues to ignore the men and women who have p.t.s.d. most of us have had up to 60 jobs or more since nam. we've had the highest rate of divorce, to many suicides, to many incarcerated, and to many homeless vets. i've managed to survive so far but i feel that living a lie and raging at the v.a. and the government as a whole is useless, but it's our only chance to make them pay for what they've done to us as a whole. if you want to know how our lives have changed. all you have to do is look around at a reunion of people who served with you and watch how react to an outsider who wasn't a vn vet.a few more things that you'll find in a combat vet. is there is no trust towards anyone. this includes your own family. your life becomes confused, your memory short and long term is a joke. your body becomes a mess. since i.ve had over 15 operations. allergies that i had never had until nam. though they still refuse to admit to the actions of agent orange. there is to much cancer and internal organ problems and different lesions that show up on different parts of the body. suicidal and homocidal intrusions become a part of your life. they come when alert and wide awake or in your dreams.you become hyper, vigilant. you also have a tendency to strike out in your dreams and can hurt your loved ones. you don't even know you did it but the scream will put you on guard in an instant. sleep is almost a joke 2 to 4 hrs. of restless flopping around from one position to another jumping up out of bed and chasing shadows. my life as a whole sucks. my only claim to life these days is that i survived one more day . and vietnam is a nightmare that will haunt me to my grave and my hate towards dinks is still as strong today as it became known to me in nam. respectfully robert kinney 6741 nw 52 bethany, okla. 73009 #405-787-7959
