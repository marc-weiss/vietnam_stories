Memorial Day, 1996. NPR news. One of the fuzzy humanist radio essays is
going on about memories, memorializing, commemorating, remembering. The
dead, mostly. ThatÆs what we do once, twice a year. Remember the dead,
and our veterans. Always *our* veterans.

I have tried to remember. I visited the Wall ten or twelve years back. I
have begun every Memorial Day to duck quietly out of work in the late
morning to go to the Virginia War Memorial. Statues and our own
home-state Wall, perched high above Tredegar Iron Works, the armory and
manufactory of ships of war. The war. That war. The late unpleasantness.
War Between the States. Richmond is bedecked with statues of the glory
of that War. But this memorial is for our wars: those of the 20th
century. And there are names, by county. Nobody I know. I didnÆt live
here during any wars. But I go there anyway. Buried in little cement
crypts, encased in acrylic blocks are artifacts of wars, each war has
its own piece of wall, and its own gallery of artifacts. Vietnam is
where I hang out. The blocks of plastic contain p-38s, purple hearts, a
VC grenade, dogtags, ribbons. The trinkets we all recognize. Many have
decayed, rusted, decomposed, their temporality undisturbed by their
placement in plastic, in concrete, in memorium.

I donÆt remember. I donÆt remember. I donÆt remember who I spent a year
living with in a 12-man tent. No names...well, only one. I donÆt
remember which platoon or squad, or what we did. Snatches and scraps
here and there, thatÆs all. How is that possible, I wonder? Trivial

Memorial Day. Remember the dead, and our veterans. I cannot remember.
But I am a veteran. The thought forms and passes almost without my
noticing. I am a veteran. I was in the war. Thirty years ago this year.
Thirty years! IÆm a veteran. I turn and hesitate. Beside me is a

why.

Veterans Day 1996. I committed myself to one year as a veteran. Find
those memories, I charged myself. Wear that identity and see how it
fits. Find old friends, dig up old photographs, read old letters and
poetry, talk to relatives, go to the Wall and try to remember who might
be there. Names, faces anything.

My quest took off like a shot. The computer has played a major role.
First I discovered the Vietnam Veterans Home Page. I read stories,
discovered the Memorial Day Writers Project which directed me to DustyÆs
poems. Eventually I found a list of vets and friends and interested

experiences, their memories, and a lot of other stuff of no particular
interest to me. Soon I met other friends and other lists. They urged me
to seek memories through writing, which I did, but mostly they kept me
coming back to the computer night after night. I met many of these
electronic friends over beer, and dinner, and the Wall on 11/11.

Names. In the mail. Memories arriving by first class post. Fragile. Hand

I tell my wife about it. We both love New Orleans, but, she says, we

battalion and my company during the year before, the year after, and the

Not to the guys with the flags. To myself. IÆm learning to be
