My story? Thirteen months incountry: One on chow hall duty; one in the hospital and the rest flying in the skies of South Vietnam almost every day... 27 air medals to show in one piece of brass the times that went on for a 20 year old who was once an altar boy in a Lutheran church and was quiet... But the Marines Corps and the Nam changed all that... forever and ever.

First, I don't like talking to non-combatants about . people who only listen and have not "been there" can not ever "feel" and "smell" the place. I never knew that you could see your own fears before your own eyes, nor if anyone had told me what it was to be afraid to the point of wanting to close out your own account, I wouldn't/couldn't think on that scale. But Vietnam did just that. Everyday wasn't like the movies that have come out with the war shown in its different moods. Yes many moods are depicted now, but you had to be there to "get it". What does the phrase, " My fuckin' balls were in my throat" mean to you? Little children... the little bastards would just as soon blow your ass away as look at you. No Santa Cluse didn't come to these kids... I treaded them as one would treat a hadicap person : Talk and get the hell out of there. trust no one .Artelary shells going off some few feet away... lucky only because the stupid bastards think you hole is designed like the ones at DaNang and they put delay fuses on them and aim them at the "hooches", only ours ran parrallel to the hooch.. I had thought that to keep from getting a Purple Heart you either one; crawled low or two, ran fast. I got mine from not being able to crawl fast enough and low enough... and I was on the ground ! I look at the questions to my right on the PC screen and I shudder. "How did it affect you?" Shit! You kiddin'? Values? Who gives a crap? My wife and my kids say that I'm marked by the conflict. I ain't. I'm conservative anything else is commie and I don't appriciate it one bit! I work with chemicals... some say it ain't the safest place to work, but the money is good, at least for the past 23 years. Life... mine is more important to me than yours is to me. what is death anyways? Only the living morn. You say, "Gee, he never made the trip he always wanted to go on." You won't hear me say it in the grave. It's ALL over then. Spiritually you make good on this side of eternity the rest will take its own direction and que. I carried 113 dead Marines at Tam KY one day in May of '67. Most sickning site that I had , up until then, seen. My gunner threw up. The smell... what it ment...then prisoners were put on our planes to be taken out of the area and questioned. Imagine the little ! ! ! smart asses grinning. defiant? No. Just scared and tring to be not-scared. A knife under their throats and the grins became tremblings. Old women shitting next to you at the bus stop. walking down a dirt road after having been released from the hospital ship and taken to the docks. No weapons, no money, just head that way.A a firefight is going on with a squad of Marines not 200 yards away and you and a couple others are walking along with a finger up your ass and just look that way towards the fighting. Yea, stupid... but think...what else were ya gonna do? Hid in the dirt til... when...it got better?...Good thing a truck came along. A boy of about 5 years old had a bullet hole in his side. It was some three days old, and dried out. I asked the man with him ( his father) why it was so long before he called for help. his words were, " He was shot three days ago. My mother said 'If he is still alive day after tomorrow then it must mean that he is to live.' and he was still alive this morning, so I called for help." And we are the bastards? Give me a break. Their society is not anything to compare with ours. The poorest people that I've seen here on this side of the "pond" are wealthy compaired to Vietnamese. Their beliefs are nothing to ours. They hhad pain and they suffered. But they looked after their own asses too. Hump rice in the day time and wave at you as you flew over them. At night they humped rockets for the enemy. At the E-club the girls would say,"Tonight you die Marine!" wwith a very hatefull face. We'd laugh and continue to drink warm beer. All beers had to be opened at the bar before you walk! ! ! ed away from it. No matter if you bought one or 200 they had to be opened. Imagine flying over 10 hours on a hot day, drinking three hours, and walking back to your hooch with about 12 open cans of beer... I still laugh. There was our bunkers. Aw! That is where we would head for sometimes after the club close. There a group of us would have what we called "Bunker party". Our favorite games were: 1) One person would sing a part of a song, the next man took the last word of that song and begin another song that had that word in it, beginning with that word. jesus! A type of scrabble..(2) think up songs on our own...The Crew Chief Who Never returned using the tune of the Kingston Trio's famous song about the Boston subway. "He may ride the streets of Hue forever.~~~" and so it went. A passing inccedent: On one of these club visits our squadron was sitting down and drinking heavely. it was the day that I actually saw three men fall under my michine gun fire. I was troubled and i! ! ! n heavy thought, others helped me. Then came a comotion down at t

he other end of the tables we had lined up for our drinking. A WHITE Marine was yelling, kicking beer cans on top of the table. Someone said he was from the HH&S group. This bunch took care of tolets and electricity among other base things. And they were jealous of our "fighting" and action as they saw it. To much was to much. All hell broke up. Frustrations were being relieved by the ones who didn't get to see combat. Therest of us said fuck it and grabbed our beers and headed to our bunker. Sometime durring the night around 3 am we got out of our bunker and MPs were patroling the area with loaded weapons. Were we under fire? "No, but we have a race riot on our hands," one of the MP said. "How'd it get started?" "Over at the E-club. Sure enought the Stars and Stripes had it on front page about a race riot at Marble Mountain. Honest to god we laughed and laughed. That was where I made up my mind that the press was bullshit from the word go. Where was I? Oh yea! Bunker parties and staying drunk, "The only way to fly!" was our motto. There were a few times that the officer of the day at the squadron would round up everyone because an emergency air lift was called. someones ass was in a sling and they needed help. We hauled ass, without the beer. Our fellow Marines were in a world of ship and needed help...NOW! But we couldn't leave our drunken attitudes behind. One guy, Buzz, woke up at 4000 feet and found himself hanging out the door of his UH-34 with fire lights below. It was dark and we had been drinking heavily when the Sparrow Hawk was called. Along the coast about where the Asau Valley was located he woke up. he became stone sobber from then on...(just that night...) . Sometimes the Marines wouldn't pass out beer because the enimy was all around us. The junior troops couldn't have cared less. But there was always the Army... they'd sell anything for a price. They had a place that sold beer by the palletat Hu! ! ! e Phu Bi . There has been some pictures of a Marine Huey shot down at that LZ in a lot of photos that I've see sence the war. All four men died in that direct mortar hit. And we carried on as life as usual.... You got that way after a while.

And to a certain point I still am. I've been ordained as a Baptist minister. A fellow preacher was a Marine who went out at nights and collected VC ears. The bullshit from the religous groups left me sick. I'm not mad with God. I'm sick of so called righteous people. But they only seek something that only a war can do let you see. I didn't live in a fox hole, I didn't do patrols, and I saw and did a lot that in my later years I wouldn'd do again. Nam Jan 67-Feb68 I came home. I called my dad in California and asked him not to tell anyone but I was on my way home. he met me at the detroit airport alone. My sisters were mad because they had signs and all. That was why I wanted just dad there. He knew why. He was a Marine in WWII.

Yea I remember a lot.... and more. you just got two hours of my peckin' . Later
