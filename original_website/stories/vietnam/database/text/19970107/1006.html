VI ______________ GUTS AND GLORY

When Ryan thought about Steve again, that terrible, private, frightening moment of painful recollection, it seemed so remote, so far away, the napalm, motherless babies, POW's, Hanoi Hilton--death--and maybe that vivid flash pried his mind open wide to the possibility of his own demise in a strange, jungle-filled world of inefficacy. It was only by the barest shred of good fortune, and his tenuous grasp upon a student deferment, that he did not sit in some stinking hole, rifle poised to deliver death to any living thing that moved. He wandered north along Tennessee Street, somberly facing the cold December air and, yet, he imagined the searing heat and wrenching humidity of a dangerous land on the other side of the world. He rewound the tape in his mind of those few seconds in Steve's room. The gregarious Hippie had relived the moments preceding one of many confrontations with death--a few seconds carved from a year where he faced Thanatos without nobility or bravery, without innocence or conviction. But he survived. But did he survive? Following the chaotic aged sidewalk, Ryan wandered by one Victorian home after another, struggling in his mind to escape the painful fantasies. He studied the comfortable, timeworn domiciles, all snugly and warm and spilling golden light from frosty windows, as the day drew quietly into the dusky world of ambivalent transitions. He inhaled the biting air to clear his mind from the foggy perceptions and floating mental demons: those unwanted mergers between bad memories and marijuana. Reaching home shortly after dark, he settled upon his front porch step and listened to the muffled background sound of his stereo portraying the pain of young love in the melodic appeals of Johnny Rivers. The afflictive music reminded him of Jenny--how he missed her and how badly he needed to hold her at that moment. She would help him forget the cruelty of a bad trip: that awful time when drugs became an indictment to obsessive ruminations. He asked himself once again: "If it isn't fun, why do it?" Inside the house, he found that Richard was the only roommate at home. Predictably, the hulking red-neck was buried in his room, the door shut as a warning to trespassers. Anger flared up inside Ryan because the door was also a barrier to the singular bathroom. Richard might come out to make popcorn during a study break, but otherwise, he would spend the evening persistently buried in books and solving mundane problems of economics or calculus. It was Richard's style to get a jump on the next semester by studying ahead. Even a meaningless conversation with him about the few comfortable subjects they could share--girls, beer and football--would have offered a modicum of comfort at that moment. He strolled through the house and thought anxiously about Western Civilization. He had dozens of books to read before he would be ready for the infamous spring test. But he was not ready to study. He went to his room, took off his coat and flung himself upon the neatly made bed. He stared upward at the Tiffany lamp which added a colorful menagerie to the dull white walls of his room. He thought again about calling Jenny, but then remembered she was involved with pledge initiations until the following day. He could call his old friend, William, who always seemed to understand these horrific moments of existential confrontation, but the long distance telephone bills to Paxico were becoming much too large. He rolled to the edge of his bed, sat upright, hesitated for a few seconds, then ambled to his study desk. Reaching into his jean pocket, he grabbed the red stone given to him by Steve, placed it on the desk, then put a sheet of paper in the old Smith-Corona and began to type.

December 14, 1969

Mr. William Barringer Paxico, Kansas

Dear William,

Today I watched Steve (Lanny's roommate) travel back to Vietnam in the melodrama of his mind. I admit, he had the help of a dose of windowpane acid, but he went back there, vividly, and through his terror, I felt him re-live the nightmare, and it hurt me to feel his pain.

How innocent you and I are of the brutality and rage of our brothers--those unfortunate men who lost the lottery, drew the low number, and are whisked off to fight for freedom. Lanny tells me that most of them are poor or black or desperate.

Imagine that it goes this way: you land on that foreign turmoil and life becomes a trivial pursuit, a meaningless path through 365 days. You exist with futility and soon you understand that your mission is a condemnation to hell. (It is a destiny ironically bequest by arrogant politicians and "guts and glory" military men, people who remember war as the dignified destruction of Hitler. War, to them, is a glorious metaphor for nationalism triumphant.)

One problem with this war is that the enemy looks like the ally. They are the faceless; we can't tell them apart. They are "Gooks": little evil looking sub-humans who have slanted eyes, protruding teeth and hateful expressions. The East Asians mystically navigate their paths through the the profuse disorganization of twisted vines and thrive on the steamy heat of bewildering jungle. They don't live; they subsist. They smell different, act different, and care about things Americans think absurd.

Our brothers come from small cities like Hope or Salinas or Butte, and fight an enemy they don't understand, for a victory cause few Americans really care about. But those guys are like us--like Steve--because they also taste the sweet magic carpets of escape. Instead of smoking marijuana in the predictable comfort of a college town nestled in the middle of the Kansas flint hills, they toke their pipes in "shitters" parked near Cambodia (or, according to Steve, maybe even in Cambodia) and "trip" on the flak and streamers which dot the midnight sky. They listen to Grace Slick and Bob Dylan and Jimi Hendrix; they discover the protest music and the implicit messages of bitterness and discord. They are nameless to us and our fathers, except for the occasional few we know...like our former classmate, Rodney Suthers. (In case you haven't heard, William, he is dead. He died trying to rescue a small child who was left behind during an evacuation of a peasant village. The child died too.)

Like all of us, they aren't saints either, old friend. Our boys kill people brutally. Steve killed a bunch of them. Legs evaporate with compassion. Heads are smashed beyond recognition by the butt ends of rifles. Human love is set on fire by napalm. Penises are dismembered and stuffed in mouths like unripe cigars. Some of the most wicked are rumored to rape adolescent Vietnamese girls and beat grandmothers after discovering small caches of rice, hidden to stave off starvation.

What is the reward for this degradation of the human spirit? If you're in Vietnam and are "lucky" enough not to find a body bag with your name on it, as a survivor, you probably die anyway. You may die morally during that year in hell because there is no higher moral principle behind the sacrifices you make. When you return home, you are detested by your own countrymen. Yes, we hate you because we're uncomfortable around you; we ignore you, banish you from jobs, ridicule your heroism, and diminish your sacrifices. It is a time when the stupidity of your sacrifice is highlighted by conscientious objectors running to Canada and demonstrators cramming the streets of my campus and campuses throughout our nation.

In twenty five years--if we survive until then--most of us probably still won't understand what face of humanity we are uncovering in Southeast Asia today. And because we don't fully confront the experience of Vietnam now, that pure, clear ring of Freedom sounds flat.

Remorsefully,

Your friend, Ryan

P.S. Have you heard from Woodrow? Poor guy is just another victim.

excerpt from "INTOLERANCE: The Nineteen Sixty-Nine Story," a novel I wrote in 1989. The novel is set at the University of Kansas in, of course, 1969.

This chapter conveys the aftermath of a college student's stunning encounter with a Vietnam veteran who has lost his soul to psychedelic drugs and dread. The subsequent letter my lead character writes and sends to his closest friend is particularly revealing about the angst and anger set free by those returning from that "foreign turmoil."
