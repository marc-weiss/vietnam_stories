As I write this to you, the thousands....millions who will never know what it is like to fight a war....I have just finished an article with an AUSTIN, Texas (AP) by-line. "Tapes: Johnson critical of Vietnam: There is a law that protects our Presidents most inner thoughts for a finite period of time, several years, following his death. Those tapes divulged his thoughts on a day, May 27, 1964....a conversation with his national security adviser, McGeorge Bundy; President Lyndon Johnson called the Vietnam War "the biggest damn mess I ever saw: and lamented; "I don't think it's worth fighting for, and I don't think we can get out."

Last year, 1996, similar thoughts were spit out, in print, by his Secretary of Defense, Robert S. McNamara. And yet, both were still thinking about the military buildup to one-half million in Vietnam. Why the "Tonkin Gulf Resolution"? Was it just to justify a wall with 55,000 names?

As a Captain in the United States Air Force, I went to Vietnam in March of 1966. I had trained at Holley Field in Florida to do my job. I had gone to survival training in the dead of winter, and four feet of snow, in California's "Truckee" mountains and the famed "Donner Pass" area. I was programmed to continue survival training at Clark AB in the Philippines....but that was waivered due to an urgent need. I was trained as a "Forward Air Controller" (FAC). Although I was Air Force....I was assigned to an Army unit.

Upon arrival at Ton Son Nhut airport in Saigon, a brief sign-in, a brief snack, a nap, and off in the middle of the night to Bien Hua airbase a few clicks north. An in-brief, an in-country assignment orders, and I'm on the next C-130 north to Pleiku. Greeted by my predecessors, given a "hootch" assignment, some initialization of records, how to change "green" or military "script" into Piasters, often just called "Pea"....and I picked up a bug that put me in the sack for the next three days. Once recovered, I was then shown by the medics how to take my "weekly" ration of malaria pills....which kept my stomach in knots for three days and my bowels moving for the next four. The it started all over again.

You toughed it out....you had a job to do. You went out to fly anyway.

For the first week, I flew with someone who had been there a while. They would show me the local area....where the Cambodian border was in relation to specific landmarks....where "our" Grunts were based, and the proper procedure for filing a flight plan. My first day up with an instructor, our final landing provided some thrills....since the tail wheel of our O-1 "Birddog" was broken. In flashbacks of time in nanoseconds, touchdownm, hard right turn off the paved runway, bounced back into the air....about 60 feet up and no airspeed....well, you can imagine the rest. Scratch one Birddog. At least neither one of us was hurt. Continued the checkout over the next couple of days and was turned loose.

I had met the man I was to work for a few days earlier. Lt. Colonel Procter, Battalion Commander for the 1/14th. nicked named the "Golden Dragons." They were one of three Battalions of the 3rd. Brigade of the 25th. Infantry Division. I was to spend the next nine months working with them. I would go to their briefings, their preparations for the next days operations. We would talk detailed expectations. What did they want from me, and how would we accomplish their goals. Assigned frequencies, call signs, landing zone (LZ) preps, troop landings. How would we coordinate artillery, fighters and the helicopter lift, etc. We worked well together. I spent time every six weeks with them on the ground at their forward base.

They/we operated in the Central Highlands of South Vietnam, closer to the Cambodian border than the coast....but circled by check-points called "pussy mountain", Ple Mei, Cattaca Tea plantation, Ia Drang valley, Ple D'Jeriang, Dak To, Kontoom, Quinhon, Nha Trang, Camron Bay to complete the circle. A mountain range separated us from the 1st. Air Cavalry Division to our east. It was all down hill from there to the coast and Quinhon. It was all search and destroy and a few reconnaissance missions. The "in-country" war was different than north of the DMZ...or the out of country operations from NKP....or the Delta....or even the difference of working with RVN or US troops.

Each of these differences was documented and a "Theater Indoctrination School" established at Binh Tui south of Can Tho. This was Delta country or IV Corps. Rice paddies ran forever. The Mekong and its tributaries ran threw it. This was 9th Division country in 1966-67. This was also breeding grounds for the Navy's PACV's....shory for Patrol Air Cushion Vehicle, or hovercraft.

No....I was no hero....but I do think I worked well with my Grunts. We kept each other alive. And we innovated some of the damnedest ways of improving things for them and to help the War effort. The O-1 can fly a pretty tight circle....and from two or three miles away, it may appear it is standing still. There is a UHF homing device in Air Force aircraft that will turn a needle towards a radio transmission when one holds down the mic-button. That Birddog standing still over the highlands short of the Cambodian border acted as a radio beacon for the C-130's making air drops of supplies or ammo when there was no landing strip.

The same principle worked well with F-4's when there was sufficient ceiling below the plateau west of Pleiku. Troops in contact had to have close air support. Ask one of the fighter jocks if he has ever penetrated cloud cover off a mic-button before.

I made it back....I am no hero, and yet I have been privileged to have known three Congressional Medal of Honor awardees. First was Major Bernard Fisher for only a few months at Pleiku. He had rescued a fellow A-1 driver from the Au Shau valley. I went to school with another....Captain Hilliard A. Wilbanks, awarded posthumously....and one I know now. In Northwest Florida, there is Colonel George "Bud" Day, an attorney, currently asking for support with his class action suit of the Federal Government to restore promised benefits that have eroded away over the years. Bud was also a POW for about six years. Each was/is unique in his own way. Me....I am no hero. I made it back....unharmed....a few medals that assured my promotions and gave me the experiences and traing that changed my outlook on life.

I am retired now. I have my health, a comfortable income, a wonderful wife, wonderful children and grand children....and many heart rendering memories. There are a couple of hundred more stories between those lines above. Maybe one day....

Just before Christmas 1996, through a coincidental happenstance, I have been privileged to re-establish a friendship after 30 years. One with a friend we schooled together, went to Vietnam together, they had parted us that first night at Ton Son Nhut. I went to Pleiku and he to NKP, Thailand. We lost track of each other following the War....until my cousin called to ask if I remembered who I left that first night at Ton Son Nhut. My cousin and my friend now work together.

Bruce Hoon
1934
Crestview, FL USA
