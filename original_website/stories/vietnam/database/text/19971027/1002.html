In 1969 I was a teenager and lived with my parents in Connecticut. After school, several evenings a week, I volunteered answering the phone at the Youth Hot Line. This was a service which provided various resource contacts, comfort or just an open ear to anyone (usually other teenagers in turmoil) who called in.

The Youth Hot Line office was located in the donated basement of the local YMCA. There was an adjoining room. Shortly after I started volunteering, this room was also occupied several evenings a week by a continually growing group of young men.

I soon learned that these men were participants in either group therapy or a support group related to the VietNam War. They had fought and returned broken.

I could hear them through the wall. I heard their stories of regret and loss, betrayal and loyalties, survival and pain....and many times, I could just hear someone ask 'Why?'. I could hear them crying.

I had never heard men cry.

I didn't know them. I never spoke to them. I could hardly look them in the eye. Their pain took them somewhere that was so foreign to me, I couldn't even start to relate.

These are the men who inspired me to protest the war. There was no disrespect intended. I just wanted them to stop crying.

L. F. Clark
1954
Kenmore, WA USA
