I first viewed the Vietnam memorial in 1982. Enroute to Moscow with a group of kids to talk face to face with the enemy we stopped to see the memorial. The images of all those names, of all those lost lives, and of the children who wept with me on that cold December day in Washington D.C., later inspired me to create "The Banner of Hope." The Banner is a mile long memorial of red silk with names and ages of children killed in wars written in black and with white silk birds sewn on as a symbol of hope. The names were written by people from across the planet. The idea, the realization, and the ten year history of the Banner of Hope have made it one of the truly important emblems of its war-torn, violence-ridden time. The waste and pathos of the events commemorated, the skill and devotion of the makers- designer, artisans, teachers, parents, and above all the children who inscribed the names- show vividely in every pannel, and impressively, shockingly, in its vast length. I have carried the Banner of Hope to war sites around the world, to Moscow, Belfast, Hiroshima, Poland, Iraq, and all those death camps from ww2. Used to open the International Women's Congress in the Kremlin in 1987, the sight of all those names brought Gorbachev, and the audience of 6000 to tears. A million children are still being killed, seriously injured, or disabled each year by war- their families, homes, and the earth is also wounded. We will continue to display the Bnner of Hope, until all wars end.

Carried by children its message rings true just as the inspiration for it does and will for an eternity. When the Berlin Wall came down in 1989 the Banner was carried by 200 children from the East as they joined, for the first time, their peers in the West. Together they carried the rippling silk memorial through Check Point Charlie. So many people throughout the world have attended the unfurling cermony and given us names to add to the hundreds already memorialized. They are deeply grateful to have their children remembered in such a way;they thought no one knew or cared that their child, their son, their daughter, many new born babies, were killed by the violence of war. Never did they expect to see the name on a red silk memorial, carried by other children. There are many stories around the Banner of Hope, and we expect to put them into a book to accompany a documentary now being created.
