How did the Vietnam war affect me? How did it not. I was a common infantryman with the 196th Bde in 1970-71 and the experience was so profound that I now measure my life in three phases; before, during and after the war. The boy I was before the war is only marginally related to the man I am now. It has affected everything I think, do and say. This is not to say, however, that I am incapacitated by the experience. Like MOST Vietnam veterans, I made the transition back to civilian life successfully. I have been married to the same woman for 26 years, kept the same job for 17 and do not have drug or alchohol problems. Of the men I served with, all but two could also be described this way. We are normal, every day people whom you meet in the checkout line at Walmart, who stop beside you at the traffic light. We are neither nuerotic nor a danger to you. We are just people with an interesting past. This does not mean, however, that the war has no affect on us at all. It does. There has not been a single day in the 27 years since I returned from Vietnam that it has not crossed my mind. A smell, a sound, a stray thought can bring images roaring back into my mind. Sometimes, it will be a fleeting rememberance. Sometimes, it will last for weeks. If I allowed it, these memories could be debilitating, but I don't for the simple reason that it is the past. Everything must kept in it's proper place. If I remained focused on the past, rather than the future, I too might be on disability for PTSD. I realize that I am rambling, but it is difficult to verbalize how I feel about the war. It was something I did by choice and the consequences are mine to bear. I believe the experience made me a better man, but the cost has been more than I knew at the time. Some of the things I now do because of the war could be described as "eccentric", such as not being able to sleep well unless my boots, with socks inserted, are not right by my bed where I can find them in the dark. For years, I also had to have my shirt folded just so and placed over my boots, just as I used to do in the bush, but at least I have gotten away from that. For many years, I suffered from a kind of survivor guilt, but did not know it. It kept me from establishing deep intimate relationships with my wife and children, although I did not recognize it at the time. I lived my life as if I was supposed to be dead, as if some monstrous mistake had been made and, soon, someone would notice and come to get me. Although I did not know why,I seemed toI live a temprorary existance while displaying the outward signs of success. Everything in my life seemed normal and complete, but I didn't seem to fit in. It was only when I confronted the Moving Wall at a Vietnam Veterans parade in Houston, that I became aware of these feelings. When I saw that my name was not on the wall, it hit me like a thunderbolt that I was not supposed to be dead. Does that make sense? Probably not, but that is how I felt. Since then, I have felt free to enjoy life, something I could not do before. The war also has made me more cynical than I perhaps should be. For instance, I cannot tolerate flag-waving sunshine patriots who will cheer the boys off to war, but will not go with them. At least in this century, with the exception of WWII, wars have been about money and too many fine young men and women have been killed so that General Motors and Lockheed stock holders can make a fine return on their investments and then they wrap the dead in the flag, call them heroes and by so doing, encourage the next generation to believe the same lies. Please leave me out of foisting that onto our children. Does this mean that I am not proud of my service in Vietnam? No. Of course I am proud to have done my duty, but only because of what I learned about my self, not because of any good that was done for freedom and liberty. During my service, which was near the end of the war, we knew that what we did, what we suffered, was for nothing. We knew the war was lost and that the American people could care less about our sacrifices. We all felt like the biggest suckers in the world, and we were. Those feelings of disillusionment and betrayal are still there, just below the surface and they do affect the way I relate to persons in positions of authority, whether it be the President of the United States or the local cop. On the whole, though, I am glad that I went to Vietnam, though I would not recommend it to others. The experience was worthwhile, but not to be repeated. I fully believe that I am whatever I am today because of the war and would hate to live now with the knowledge that I could have gone, but didn't.

.

Gary Capshaw
1949
Pottsboro, Tx, USA
