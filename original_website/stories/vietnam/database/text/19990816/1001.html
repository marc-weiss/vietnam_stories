Vietnam had a tremendous affect on my short life. I was, unfortunately, drafted, and felt rather secure that I would NOT be sent there or else I NEVER would have gotten married on 26 Dec 64. On 23 May 65, I found myself on a boat headed for Vietnam. The reason that I felt secure was because I had turned down the "opportunity" to become an officer. I felt that that was a sure ticked to get to Vietnam. I was quite content to be "just" an enlisted man, and awaited my ETS with great anticipation. That was all to change for now I anxiously awaited my separation from the army and Vietnam. That day finally arrived----29 Jan 66! I thought, erroneously, that I had survived that immoral war and the asinine orders that I took while in the army and while there. I, physically, did. Mentally is, indeed, another story!

I went to Vietnam, thinking that this country would take care of any and all wounds, mental or physical, that were received there! Boy. Did I dial a wrong number!

On 15 Feb 72, I suffered an AVM (an Arteriovenous Malformation) and was, supposedly successfully operated on. However, they turned me into an epileptic, traumatically brain injured me, and thoroughly disrupted my life, even more than that totally avoidable Vietnam war did. That was this country's broken promise to one of its somehow honorably discharged vets.

On the bottom of the VA's letters, is this note: "America is #1--Thanks to our Veterans." They showed what it meant by "thanks." I'll NEVER forgive their "thanks."

Sincerely,

The "trained killer," and AVM and Vietnam survivor, Dick Dettrey\ A once-proud man without a country
