Too Young To Be Afraid

I lied about my age, joined the Army, and went to Vietnam in October 1969
when I was 16 years old. I turned 17 after being in Vietam three months.
Although I was trained as a clerk typist, I wound up in a two-week crash
combat demolitions school after arriving in country. Next thing you know
I was attached to a unit of crazy K/75 Rangers and it was off to the
jungle with a team of six. I did not see civilization for 8 months, just
us six in the boonies. I was too young to be afraid or to appreciate the
significance of what I was experiencing. Everything was almost surreal in
the boonies. I remember waiting in an ambush for three hours without
moving. During that time every insect or crawling creature in the jungle
enveloped me. To this day, I get almost violent when I encounter a bug in
my home.

Once we were pulled in from our long range patrol to help some infantry
unit that had taken several artillery hits. It was my job to clear out
the underbrush using explosives so that medical evacuation choppers could
land to pick up the wounded. The wounded were screaming and crying all
around us as we worked. Finally the choppers could land and the men
started hauling the wounded to them. One of the wounded had his leg blown
off and was screaming that he was not going to get on the chopper without
his leg. Me and Dan Duran searched around the area for the leg. We found
it and I remember thinking how heavy the human leg is as we both carried
it to the chopper. When the med-evac took off the wounded man was
clutching his leg like a person hugs a tree trunk.

Suddenly Dan starts laughing hysterically and pointing at the chopper. I
figured he had cracked (it happened to lots of guys) at all the horrors
we were seeing. When I asked him what was so funny, he says, "Look at the
guy," pointing at the fellow missing the leg in the chooper. At first I
didn't get it, but then I too was laughing. Why? The wounded soldier was
holding a left leg, but his right leg had been blown off. Yes, it is now
a sad story to me, but at the time we just coped any way we could. The
real tragedy about that day was that all those men had been wounded or
killed because our own artillery unit's incoming rounds had fallen too
short, landing on top of the infantry patrol. Do you think the Army ever
told the parents of the dead how they children had died that day? I think
not.

The whole Vietnam experience hit me after I left Vietnam and was assigned
to a hospital (Camp Zama) unit. There I saw the horrors up close without
the veneer of combat that repressed them from me in Vietnam. I became
very disillusioned about the war and started protesting the U.S.
involvement. The story is too long to tell, but the short version is that
I was eventually discharged from the Army six years later in lieu of a
court martial for espionage and impersonating a government intelligence
agent in order to obtain classified information about illegal military
weapons storage facilities.

Vietnam changed my life. It is the single-most defining experience I have
ever had and is the filter by which I judge my actions and decisions
today.


MOS: 12B30
4th Infantry Division (10/69 - 10/70)
An Khe, Pleiku
