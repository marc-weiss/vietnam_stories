The Five Big Lies Of Vietnam “History is lies agreed upon.” Napoleon

A few months ago The Haldeman Diaries revealed, in a straightforward, matter-of-fact way, that Richard Nixon and Henry Kissinger purposely prolonged the war in Vietnam as part of Nixon’s 1972 reelection strategy. Now Robert McNamara’s memoir, In Retrospect, The Tragedy And Lessons of Vietnam, in which he admits that the war was “wrong, terribly wrong,” and, even more importantly, that he and the rest of the Johnson cabinet knew it, is all over the news. Could it be that the boil has been lanced? Could it be that the logjam of lies is finally starting to break up? Could it be that after all these years the pent-up truth may finally be ready to wash the wounds clean? America seems poised to enter a new phase in our long-running argument over Vietnam. All the elements are there, and even before the McNamara book there had been a remarkable increase in Vietnam-related stories. Last summer at the fiftieth anniversary of D-Day President Clinton was blasted by every conceivable conservative for his anti-war activities. More recently, Speaker of the House Newt Gingrich called Clinton a ‘McGovernik’ (which if true would make Gingrich a ‘Tricksterite’). Congressman Robert ‘B-1 Bob’ Dornan took to the floor of the House of Representatives to call the President a traitor for “aiding and abetting the enemy.” Columnist Mark Shields called for Republican candidates like Phil Graham, who falls into the ‘chickenhawk’ category of politician for his non-service during the Vietnam era, to be subjected to the same scrutiny over the draft that Clinton was; columnist Robert Novak responded, calling Graham’s lack of service “the great non-issue of the election,” saying the two cases aren’t equivalent because Graham had a routine deferment while Clinton actively opposed the war, which, although Novak misses it, is precisely the point, because Clinton was right. The President, responding to a question about McNamara’s book and whether he felt vindicated, said yes, but that because in college he had worked in the office of Senator J. William Fulbright, the leading opponent of the war, he had access to information not available to most Americans, and knew since his college days that the war was wrong. In addition to all this, the twenty-year anniversary of the fall of Saigon in late April will unleash another volley of media coverage. McNamara’s In Retrospect is the first hard-cover book in my entire forty-seven year lifetime that I ran out to buy during its first week of publication. I was hopeful that he would provide an insider’s glimpse into the diplomatic and political history of how we waded into the quagmire. The fact that he comes clean on his own actions bodes well for the historical understanding of how the war engulfed America. Unfortunately, he dwells on the mistakes made by himself and the Kennedy and Johnson administrations and ignores the roots of the war. He glosses over the mistakes which were made and the lies which were told by the Truman and Eisenhower administrations which are to this day still used by some as the legal, moral and historical justifications to convince people that what we were doing was good and right and just. I watched McNamara with Ted Koppel on Nightline, and while I was grateful they were at least talking about the lies, like every discussion I have ever seen or participated in on Vietnam since 1968, they missed the most important historical points. To illustrate the historical ignorance of the American people on the roots of the war, and their misunderstanding of the justifications for it, I would like to take you back just two years, to June 7, 1993. It was a Sunday, the morning after the twenty-fifth anniversary of the death of Bobby Kennedy, and I was watching This Week With David Brinkley. At the very end of the show, apropos of nothing they had been discussing, George Will criticized John Kennedy for sending troops to Vietnam, and Sam Donaldson said Eisenhower started it, and Will said you can’t criticize Eisenhower for something Kennedy and Johnson did, then Brinkley said “good-bye” and there I was, screaming at the tube, “No, you _ _ _ _ _ _ _ assholes, you’ve still got it wrong. Harry Truman let the French back in–that’s what started it! Then in ‘56 Ike and the Dulles brothers refused to let Vietnam have an election, fabricated a government in the south and put the butcher Diem in power.” If Brinkley, Donaldson and Will, who are arguably the three best newsies in the business, can’t get it right, how is the average American ever supposed to understand the truth about our involvement in Vietnam? Since the spring of 1968 I have been frustrated every time the subject of Vietnam has come up. No one I have ever discussed it with, or whom I have seen speak of it in public, has the same ‘take’ on Vietnam that I do. It has often seemed to me as though I am the only person on the planet, left or right, liberal or conservative, Democrat or Republican, who recognizes what I have come to call The Five Big Lies for what they are. As a consequence I have come to see it as my personal obligation, a kind of moral, patriotic, even karmic duty, to somehow, some way, before I die, convey to America the truth about The Five Big Lies. I came to my realization of the truth about Vietnam in the spring of 1968 when I was a sophomore at Penn State, majoring in political science. I had dreams of graduating and then joining the army, going to officer candidate school, winning some medals, and then going to law school and joining the diplomatic corps, where I would hang out in nifty foreign capitols outwitting the commies. I dreamed of becoming an ambassador, then Secretary of State and, finally, President. I had grown up in a household steeped in Republican tradition, where FDR was “that damned Roosevelt” and Harry Truman was “the bankrupt haberdasher.” When the spring quarter began I was a member of a fraternity and a card-carrying Young Republican, a hard-core Goldwater Republican, and proud to call myself a conservative. I was thrilled when Lyndon Baines Johnson said he would not seek reelection after Gene McCarthy almost beat him in the New Hampshire Democratic primary. I thought this opened the door for Barry, and I hoped that he, and not the wimpy Rockefeller or the sure-loser Nixon, would become the Republican nominee. I was convinced that if Barry ran again he would be vindicated for his landslide defeat in ‘64 at the hands of Johnson, who had run as the ‘peace’ candidate and then sent the troops over just weeks after the election. If Barry had won he would have nuked ‘em and the Vietnam problem would have long been over. For America 1968 was, according to a recent PBS documentary, the ‘saddest year of the twentieth century.’ The previous December the naval ship Pueblo had been captured and its crew was still being held by North Korea, a situation which threatened to reopen the Korean war; in February the Tet offensive in South Vietnam shook America’s confidence in the war to its core; on the last day of March LBJ announced he would not stand for reelection; on April 4 Martin Luther King was blown off the balcony of a Memphis motel; on June 6 Bobby Kennedy, only moments after winning the California Democratic primary and flashing the peace sign at the end of his victory speech, would be gunned down in a hotel corridor. That was just in the spring. The upcoming summer would see the Chicago police riot against the peace protesters at the Democratic convention, a spectacle which, because the voting age was still twenty-one and those who had to fight the war couldn’t vote, was to insure the narrow election of Richard Milhous Nixon over Hubert Horatio Humphrey in November. I turned twenty that March, and I was finally finished with the prerequisites for a political science major. At last I could choose what subjects to take, and I signed up for two upper division courses, American Diplomatic History and International Law, each of which required a term paper. Scammer that I am, I wanted to find a common subject which would allow me to do the research for both papers at the same time. Vietnam was the perfect topic. I figured to kill two birds with one stone, get two A’s for the research time of one and have the ammo to shut up the long-haired, commie pinkos once and for all. To hell with left-wing teach-ins, I would do the research myself! I would conclusively prove, once and for all, that America was in the right. I was motivated by the belief that the demonstrators were sullying America’s honor, that they were misinformed at best, treasonous at worst. Barry Goldwater would be vindicated! For the diplomatic history course I set out to prove that our involvement in Vietnam was in our national interest, and for the international law course to prove that what we were doing was legal. I would prove that America was, as we had always been throughout our history, indisputably in the right. It was the first serious academic research I had ever done, and I set out to amass my evidence and prove my hypothesis using the non-judgmental methodology of an impartial scholar. A little knowledge is a dangerous thing, and what I found after all those weeks of library research turned my Republican rock ribs to sand. By the time I took the history final, the morning after Bobby Kennedy was assassinated, I would never again be the unflinching, flag-waving Goldwater conservative I had been before I looked long and hard at the evidence. During the weeks of laborious research I practically lived in Penn State’s two million volume Pattee Library. I read everything there was on the subjects of Indochina, French colonialism, Vietnam, Dien Bien Phu, the Geneva Peace accords and so forth. When I finally sat down to write the term papers I was forced to conclude the exact opposite of what I had set out to prove. It was an intellectual serendipity, in that I found something I had not intended to find. It was also a kind of moral and political epiphany which changed my perspective on the world forever. My research may have also, quite literally, saved my life, because if I had stayed on the track I had laid out for myself I would have joined the Army after graduation and gone to Officer Candidate School in order to play John Wayne in Vietnam. But I was lucky, and unlike so many others, I learned the truth before going to Vietnam. I graduated in June of 1970, and because Nixon had ended graduate deferments, and because I had drawn a bad draft number and was healthy as a horse, I had to make a decision: Should I let myself be drafted, a two year commitment, and probably be sent as an infantryman to Vietnam? Should I resist the draft and go to jail? Should I go to Canada? Should I join one of the uniformed services for three or four years with the promise that I wouldn’t go to Vietnam? My knowledge of the truth meant that I could not follow through on my goal of becoming an officer because I could not lead men to their deaths for a lie. At the same time, my political science education had convinced me that while Vietnam posed no threat to us at all, the Soviets in Eastern Europe did, and I felt that there was a legitimate need for a strong military presence in Europe. I could not fight in an evil, un-American war, but I was still an American and felt I could wear my country’s uniform in some other capacity. So I became what the army called a D.I.E., a draft induced enlistment, and I put in my time, serving as an army MP in Germany. This was late in the war, and the army had none of the optimism that the guys who were in when the war was cranking up in ‘65 and ‘66 had. Like every other ‘late war’ grunt in uniform at the time, I hated every minute of it. But like two out of three who served in uniform during the Vietnam era, I didn’t have to set foot ‘in country.’ I was able to scam the system so I didn’t have to fight, kill or die in what I knew to be an unjust, un-American, and illegal war. Now, twenty-five years later, because of what I learned, and because of how I dealt with my military obligation, I have developed a peculiar type of survivor’s guilt that has made getting the truth out an obsession. I had grown up believing in the good guy ethics and moral certitude of the cowboy ethos, which had been imparted to most older baby boomers in our early youth by singing cowboys in white hats, Davy Crockett, John Wayne and World War II movies. As a twenty year old self-proclaimed conservative patriot who whose beliefs in the righteousness of his country had been shattered I didn’t know how to relate to what I had learned, and ever since I have struggled to impart it to anyone who would listen. For the three years after I had done that research on the origins and legality of the war it seemed I was the only person who really knew the why’s and the wherefore’s of what was going down. I couldn’t get anyone, left or right, to take my views on the war seriously. Then, in 1971, The Pentagon Papers were published, shocking the nation but confirming all of my conclusions. Thank god for leaks and freedom of the press, or they’d have locked me away for babbling a long time ago. In the process of writing a novel about the lies of Vietnam I developed a nomenclature about the war that seems to help some people understand it better. Most people who have not studied it as deeply as I have often have a hard time seeing through the layer upon layer of lies which have been used to justify the war, one administration after the other. It is as though once a lie was told to justify an action it is no longer treated as a lie, even when it is revealed. Instead, it is treated as history, something to be built on rather than something which should be torn down. In 1776 the American Revolution stunned the world with the radical notion of political Liberty, and for nearly two centuries the American example gave hope to colonized and enslaved nations everywhere that one day they too might be free. This noble premise was reaffirmed at the beginning of World War Two by Franklin Roosevelt in the Atlantic Charter when he said that all nations had the right to self-determination, boosting the morale of the nations under the Nazi jackboot. I find it ironic that the fiftieth anniversary of the death of Franklin Roosevelt came the same week that McNamara’s book was released, for if Roosevelt had lived just a few months longer it is unlikely that there ever would have been a Vietnam conflict at all. Roosevelt was no great admirer of the French, and is reported to have had a strong distaste for Charles DeGaulle. Roosevelt and his advisors blamed the vindictive, chauvinistic attitude of the French after World War One for the rise of Nazi Germany. Nor was Roosevelt impressed with French military prowess, and he made it a point to exclude the French from the highest policy conferences during the war. There are many historical references to the fact that when the war ended he had no intention of allowing the French reclaim their colonial empire, since to do so would have violated America’s most sacred heritage of Liberty. His idea was to set up a protectorate for Indochina to ease the path of those nations to full sovereignty. But Roosevelt died prematurely at age 63, and the presidency fell to Harry Truman. Unlike Roosevelt, who thought the French arrogant, vain and pompous, Truman was a Francophile. He had made a name for himself as an artillery officer in France during World War One and had enjoyed himself thoroughly during his time there. When World War Two ended he thought it would be good for French morale if they could recapture some of their lost glory in case we needed them to help fight Stalin, and apparently had little compunction about letting them regain their former colonies. Instead of the protectorate and eventual elections which had been envisioned by Roosevelt, Truman went along with Charles DeGaulle’s plan to help French troops reinvade Indochina. This is where Big Lie Number One first gets told, the lie that the Truman administration put forward when they explained that we were neutral regarding the dispute between France and its former colonies. Truman did not formally declare our support for the French because the American people, so proud of our heritage of Liberty, would never have gone along with a policy which openly supported a French war of colonial conquest. Instead, he let the French use American equipment and supplied American troop ships to ferry the reinvasion force from France to Vietnam. The Marshall Plan provided enormous economic assistance to France, which in the early years allowed them to divert enough money from their crushed economy to finance the war with free American equipment. As long as the American markings were removed from the trucks, jeeps, machine guns, tanks and artillery pieces, the French could use it however they wanted, and who could say where it came from? In army basic combat training the drill sergeants derisively called us all ‘trainees,’ using all sorts of verbal exhortations to keep us from making stupid mistakes. The newer a trainee was, the more likely he was to screw up. One day our drill sergeant cracked up our platoon by screaming at the platoon geek, “Don’t step on your dick, Traineeee.” It is too bad no one was there to shout it at Trainee Truman, who, just months after Roosevelt’s death, went against everything America proclaimed to stand for and stepped on America’s dick by letting the French back into Indochina. I find it impossible to understand how an American president could acquiesce to a foreign colonial invasion of another nation, but that’s precisely what he did, violating our most sacred heritage of Liberty. The French history in Indochina had been particularly cruel. Political dissent was not permitted, and whips, prisons and guillotines had kept the Vietnamese under serf-like control before the Japanese invasion. Whenever any dissent sprang up, it was ruthlessly crushed. The only organized opposition group which had been able to survive the French guillotines were the Viet Minh, an umbrella anti-colonial organization composed of many factions operating out of southern China under the leadership Ho Chi Minh. When the Japanese invaded Indochina the French colonials rolled over without a fight. Rather than rule directly, Japanese pulled the strings of the French colonial administrators. To keep their new masters happy, and their own positions secure, the French forced the Vietnamese to grow rice and rubber for the Japanese war machine for its operations against the Philippines, Burma, India, China and the Dutch East Indies. The Vietnamese were forced to give up almost everything they grew, and the Japanese and the French collaborators took so much of the rice harvest it caused an enormous famine in which nearly two million Vietnamese starved to death. The famine was ended only when the Viet Minh freedom fighters, who were fighting both the French and the Japanese, staged military raids on the brim-full warehouses and distributed rice to the people, thereby becoming instant heroes. The political situation in Indochina was much like the situation in Morocco, as depicted in the movie Casablanca. Remember how the Teutonic Col. Strasser was able to twist Louis, the charmingly corrupt French prefect played by Claude Rains, around his little finger while allowing Louis to maintain the façade that he was still in charge? During World War Two the situation in Vietnam was identical, with the Japanese pulling the strings of the French marionettes like a Punch and Judy show. I bring up Casablanca because it is instructive in an historical sense, not for the insight it gives us into the relationship which existed among the peoples of the warring powers, but rather for the insight it provides regarding the colonial populations of Franco-German Morocco and Franco-Japanese Vietnam. On this point the movie is more instructive for what it leaves out. Except for Sidney Greenstreet in his fez, who plays the fat and shady proprietor of the Blue Parrot, the only actors with speaking parts playing native Casablancans are a beggar giving directions and a street vendor trying to cheat Ingrid Bergman out of a passel of francs. Think about this for a moment: In the entire movie no mention is made of what the locals think about which invaders they’d prefer having as masters, the Germans or the French. It was the same in Indochina. The next time you see Casablanca, in the stirring scene when the crowd at Rick’s Café Americain stands and sings a rousing chorus of the “Marseillaise,” drowning out the oompahs of the hated Nazis, notice that there isn’t a single Arab among them. Zero, zip, nada, zilch. You see the French colonials and European refugees on their feet, singing at the tops of their lungs, but not a single Arab is singing for the greater glory of France. Why? Because the French had conquered the Moroccans in the same way the Germans had conquered the French. If a similar scene had occurred in a café in Hanoi, Hue or Saigon it is doubtful that any Vietnamese would have joined in the chorus. In my international law class I learned that there are three main requirements for a nation to be recognized as sovereign: 1. control of a geographic area; 2. control of the government; 3. support of the people. At the end of World War Two Vietnam met all three of these conditions. All the legal formalities under accepted international law had been observed. Three months before the end of the war , in a last ditch attempt to win over the Vietnamese to help them stave off the onrushing Americans, the Japanese formally deposed the French colonial government and made the playboy Bao Dai , the hereditary Emperor, Head Of State. After the Japanese announced their unconditional surrender, the Viet Minh accepted the surrender of the Japanese troops in Vietnam, and the puppet Emperor, who was seen as a traitor, abdicated the throne and in a enormous public ceremony surrendered his ceremonial sword to the Viet Minh. A Declaration of Independence, modeled on the American document of the same name and stating that “All men are created equal,” was read by Ho Chi Minh over the national radio before a throng of 200,000 in Hanoi, and there were celebrations throughout the length and breadth of Vietnam. The Viet Minh replaced all the French colonial administrators and were in complete control of all government functions. Streets named after French invaders were renamed after Vietnamese historical patriots, and after eighty years of French colonial oppression it appeared that Vietnam was once again a free and sovereign nation. During World War Two the Viet Minh had worked closely with the American O.S.S., collecting intelligence on the Japanese, rescuing downed American aviators, and tying down several Japanese divisions which could otherwise have been used in the war against us. They considered themselves to be allies of the Americans, and looked to America for inspiration. The Viet Minh were heroes throughout the nation, and they had the active support of all segments of Vietnamese society, including the business community and the Catholic church. Only the French colonials and their Frenchified lackeys, the equivalent of the Tories in colonial America, opposed the establishment of an independent state of Vietnam. If Truman had only seized the opportunity and told the French, as an American President should have done, that we would not support their colonial ambitions the entire history of the last fifty years would have been far different. In my 1968 research I read of the many letters which Ho and his new government wrote to Harry Truman and other American officials, but none was ever answered, not one, and nowhere on the public record was there any mention that they were ever received. Somewhere in Washington, in some dusty Top Secret file they sit, waiting for a President with the guts to finally get at the truth of what really happened to order them declassified. The closest thing we have to an official record is a memoir, Why Vietnam?, published in 1980, by the senior O.S.S. officer stationed in Hanoi at the end of the war, Archimedes L.A. Patti. Patti details the letters and the channels through which he personally sent Ho Chi Minh’s many queries and statements of friendship and offers of cooperation to Truman. He reconstructs his many conversations with Ho, and tells of Ho’s hopes for a peaceful relationship with America, a country Ho greatly admired. The Viet Minh had fought with the Americans against the Japanese, and President Roosevelt’ statement in the Atlantic Charter reaffirmed America’s commitment to freedom. Patti writes of Ho’s astonishment that America, the beacon to all who were fighting for their freedom, would support the French, who had collaborated with the Japanese. This book is a must read for anyone who wants to try to understand the incredible, un-American blunder made at the Potsdam Conference by Harry Truman to appease Charles DeGaulle. Rather than simply allowing the Vietnamese to disarm the Japanese troops, Truman agreed to allowing one of Chiang Kai Shek’s Chinese armies to march in from the north and a smaller British force to sail in to do the job in the South. The Vietnamese acquiesced and did not resist, trusting that the Americans would make things turn out right. In the north the Chinese allowed the Viet Minh to maintain their public offices, but in the south the British commander promptly threw the Viet Minh out of the government offices and reinstalled the French colonials. The rearmed French colonial police, who had been languishing as virtual prisoners in their barracks, went on a rampage, killing any Viet Minh they could lay their hands on. When the Viet Minh fought back, the out-gunned and out-numbered British ordered the Japanese generals, under the threat of being tried as war criminals, to use their troops to quash the Viet Minh. General Douglas MacArthur, watching these events from Tokyo, loudly objected, saying, “If there is anything that makes my blood boil it is to see our allies in Indochina and Java deploying Japanese troops to reconquer the little people we promised to liberate. It is the most ignoble kind of betrayal.” That the Vietnamese thought they finally had their independence there can be no doubt. One American, Harold Issacs, writes of arriving in Hanoi in November of 1945: “...when I drove into Hanoi from the airfield in the sleepy warmth of that mid-November afternoon, many evidences of great changes flickered past on the walls and on the shop fronts. The Vietnam flag flew everywhere. Across every open surface there were slogans chalked or painted or printed: ‘Doc lap hay chet!’ (‘Independence or death!’); ‘Nuoc Viet Nam sua nguh Viet Nam!’ (‘Vietnam to the Vietnamese!’); ‘Tha chet cou hon trelaine le!’ (‘Death rather than return to slavery!’).” (from Vietnam, 1965, Fawcett World Library) Was Harry Truman ignorant of the brutality of the French? Did he ever even consider the right of the Vietnamese to self-determination? After all, America would set the Philippines free in 1946. Why was this same consideration not given to Vietnam? Was it religious prejudice against non-Christians? Was it because the Philippines was a over ninety percent Christian and Vietnam was over ninety percent Buddhist? Was it racial prejudice against funny-looking yellow people in black pajamas? Nothing in my research has turned up any satisfactory answers, and none of the biographies of Truman or the writings of his close associates touches on it. America would have had enormous leverage to influence the fledgling government of Vietnam if we had allowed it to survive. While it is true that the Viet Minh’s top leadership said that they were communists, they took great pains to recruit people from all segments of society into the movement, which was first and foremost dedicated to achieving Liberty from their French and Japanese masters. McNamara writes that perhaps Ho would have turned out to be another Tito, a communist but wholly independent of control from Moscow. But he leaves it at that, justifying the decision made by Truman under the delusional fears of the international communist conspiracy foisted on America by conservatives. McNamara, like so many others, ignores the wisdom of America’s heritage of always supporting Liberty. I would go much further than saying Ho might have been another Tito, and argue that, given the two thousand years of hatred between Vietnam and China, that if Truman had recognized Vietnam’s sovereignty in 1945 in succeeding years Vietnam would have acted in its own self-interest, and would have done all it could to prevent the unification of China under any strong government, communist or otherwise. Witness the border war fought between Vietnam and China in 1979. Imagine what the world could be like today if Trainee Truman had not made that fatal misstep and insisted that America live up to our heritage of Liberty. But in 1946 the French did come back on a ‘temporary’ basis under an agreement reached with Ho and the Viet Minh which was supposed to lead to recognized Vietnamese nationhood under something called the French Union, under which Vietnam was supposed to be an equal partner. The Vietnamese were not as afraid of the French as they were of the huge and ancient enemy, China. Chiang Kai Shek’s enormous Chinese army, which had been authorized to disarm the Japanese in the north under the Potsdam agreement, posed a great threat to Vietnam’s independence. The two countries had been at odds for two thousand years, and China had occupied and controlled Vietnam off and on for hundreds of years at a stretch. The Chinese army was looting everything in sight, and showed all the signs of becoming an permanent occupying force. When given the choice by the Americans of which of the occupying forces they would deal with, France or China, Ho Chi Minh is reported to have argued to his compatriots, “You fools!. I would rather eat French shit for five years than eat Chinese shit for a hundred years.” So the Chinese left and the French returned. Negotiations on the nature of the new French Union began, but it soon became apparent to the Vietnamese that the French had no intention of giving up their colonial prerogatives, and when negotiations bogged down the French navy snuck some warships off of Haiphong and bombarded the city, killing six thousand civilians. The war had begun. The war of French colonial aggression was made possible thanks to the American weapons and financial support and the constant repetition of Big Lie Number One by the Truman and Eisenhower administrations to cover it up. By the time of the battle of Dien Bien Phu, in May of 1954, America was directly subsidizing eighty percent of the cost of the invasion, and the once-moderate Viet Minh had been brutalized and radicalized by eight years of bloody warfare. They felt betrayed by the hypocrisy of what they saw as a forked-tongued America, and they had suffered hundreds of thousands of dead at the hands of the French invaders. With the fall of Dien Bien Phu the Viet Minh found themselves on the verge of total victory. The French army was near collapse, its morale was nonexistent, and the continuation of the war threatened the stability of the French government. In the last year or so I have read many reports, mostly in veterans magazines, that the Viet Minh did not have the resources to continue the war, which is why they agreed to the terms of the Geneva Accords in the first place. I would like to relate a personal anecdote which I think should refute this notion once and for all. I have a friend, a naturalized American of Jewish descent who was born and raised in Algiers when it was occupied by the French. He is a successful businessman and has been in this country for over thirty years, and he turned sixty the same week McNamara’s book came out. Several years ago the subject of Vietnam came up in a casual conversation, and he told me the story of how he heard about the fall of Dien Bien Phu. At the time he was a nineteen year old conscript in the French army, and had just finished his basic training. He and his unit were stationed in a barracks outside of Paris, waiting for orders to go to Indochina, when the news of the fort’s fall came over the radio. I said, “Gee, that must have been tough.” He looked at me like I was nuts and said, “Oh, no! Everyone went crazy, dancing around and singing and whooping for joy!” I was dumbfounded and asked, “But how could you be so happy over the defeat of your own army?” He looked me in the eye and said, “Because we thought it meant the end of the war, and none of us wanted to die for Michelin Rubber.” I would argue that his unit was typical of the state of the French army, and any suggestion that the Viet Minh, fueled by the cause of freedom, could not have prevailed against such a demoralized force of colonial aggressors is sheer idiocy. My friend was lucky he wasn’t just a little older, or he might well have been one of the 90,000 French who actually did die for Michelin Rubber. At the Geneva Peace conference the French agreed to bug out. Laos and Cambodia were to become independent, and the French and Viet Minh military forces were to disengage and regroup behind what the Accords specifically and categorically stated were to be temporary boundaries, and national elections were to be held two years. Based upon the military situation, the Viet Minh could have held out for much better terms or pushed on for total victory, but China’s Chou En Lai, who was supposedly on their side, sandbagged Ho by supporting the softer terms proposed by the French. The Chinese were playing a double game: they wanted the white colonials out of Asia, but at the same time they wanted a weak and disunited Vietnam on their southern border. The American delegation, under Secretary of State John Foster Dulles, wanted the French to hang on, and Dulles was not pleased with the idea of free elections in Vietnam. In America it was the heyday of Joe McCarthy, at the height of our national anti-communist paranoia, and all the old “China hands” at the State Department had been purged from the government because the right-wing zealots wrongly blamed them, rather than the incompetence and corruption of the darling of the American right, Generalissimo Chiang Kai Shek, for the “loss” of China (the American who knew Chiang best, General “Vinegar Joe” Stilwell, commander of the Allied forces in the China theater of operations, so thoroughly despised the corrupt Generalissimo that he publicly called him “Peanut” as much for his preening vanity as for his resemblance to the Mr. Peanut logo on the Planter’s jars). As a result of the right-wing purges by men like Joe McCarthy and Richard Nixon American foreign policy in the Far East was flying blind, and Dulles, assuming he was telling the truth, was wrongly convinced that the Soviets gave the Viet Minh orders from the Kremlin. The fact of the matter was that the Soviets, as well as the Chinese communists, were arm-twisting Ho into accepting terms which did not reflect the Viet Minh’s superior position on the battlefield. The Soviets did not want to be viewed as supporting the Vietnamese, and despite what the Americans believed, they had given the Viet Minh only token material support. Patti details the letters and the channels through which he personally sent Ho Chi Minh’s many queries and statements of friendship and offers of cooperation to Truman. He reconstructs his many conversations with Ho, and tells of Ho’s hopes for a peaceful relationship with America, a country Ho greatly admired. The Viet Minh had fought with the Americans against the Japanese, and President Roosevelt’ statement in the Atlantic Charter reaffirmed America’s commitment to freedom. Patti writes of Ho’s astonishment that America, the beacon to all who were fighting for their freedom, would support the French, who had collaborated with the Japanese. This book is a must read for anyone who wants to try to understand the incredible, un-American blunder made at the Potsdam Conference by Harry Truman to appease Charles DeGaulle. Rather than simply allowing the Vietnamese to disarm the Japanese troops, Truman agreed to allowing one of Chiang Kai Shek’s Chinese armies to march in from the north and a smaller British force to sail in to do the job in the South. The Vietnamese acquiesced and did not resist, trusting that the Americans would make things turn out right. In the north the Chinese allowed the Viet Minh to maintain their public offices, but in the south the British commander promptly threw the Viet Minh out of the government offices and reinstalled the French colonials. The rearmed French colonial police, who had been languishing as virtual prisoners in their barracks, went on a rampage, killing any Viet Minh they could lay their hands on. When the Viet Minh fought back, the out-gunned and out-numbered British ordered the Japanese generals, under the threat of being tried as war criminals, to use their troops to quash the Viet Minh. General Douglas MacArthur, watching these events from Tokyo, loudly objected, saying, “If there is anything that makes my blood boil it is to see our allies in Indochina and Java deploying Japanese troops to reconquer the little people we promised to liberate. It is the most ignoble kind of betrayal.” That the Vietnamese thought they finally had their independence there can be no doubt. One American, Harold Issacs, writes of arriving in Hanoi in November of 1945: “...when I drove into Hanoi from the airfield in the sleepy warmth of that mid-November afternoon, many evidences of great changes flickered past on the walls and on the shop fronts. The Vietnam flag flew everywhere. Across every open surface there were slogans chalked or painted or printed: ‘Doc lap hay chet!’ (‘Independence or death!’); ‘Nuoc Viet Nam sua nguh Viet Nam!’ (‘Vietnam to the Vietnamese!’); ‘Tha chet cou hon trelaine le!’ (‘Death rather than return to slavery!’).” (from Vietnam, 1965, Fawcett World Library) Was Harry Truman ignorant of the brutality of the French? Did he ever even consider the right of the Vietnamese to self-determination? After all, America would set the Philippines free in 1946. Why was this same consideration not given to Vietnam? Was it religious prejudice against non-Christians? Was it because the Philippines was a over ninety percent Christian and Vietnam was over ninety percent Buddhist? Was it racial prejudice against funny-looking yellow people in black pajamas? Nothing in my research has turned up any satisfactory answers, and none of the biographies of Truman or the writings of his close associates touches on it. America would have had enormous leverage to influence the fledgling government of Vietnam if we had allowed it to survive. While it is true that the Viet Minh’s top leadership said that they were communists, they took great pains to recruit people from all segments of society into the movement, which was first and foremost dedicated to achieving Liberty from their French and Japanese masters. McNamara writes that perhaps Ho would have turned out to be another Tito, a communist but wholly independent of control from Moscow. But he leaves it at that, justifying the decision made by Truman under the delusional fears of the international communist conspiracy foisted on America by conservatives. McNamara, like so many others, ignores the wisdom of America’s heritage of always supporting Liberty. I would go much further than saying Ho might have been another Tito, and argue that, given the two thousand years of hatred between Vietnam and China, that if Truman had recognized Vietnam’s sovereignty in 1945 in succeeding years Vietnam would have acted in its own self-interest, and would have done all it could to prevent the unification of China under any strong government, communist or otherwise. Witness the border war fought between Vietnam and China in 1979. Imagine what the world could be like today if Trainee Truman had not made that fatal misstep and insisted that America live up to our heritage of Liberty. But in 1946 the French did come back on a ‘temporary’ basis under an agreement reached with Ho and the Viet Minh which was supposed to lead to recognized Vietnamese nationhood under something called the French Union, under which Vietnam was supposed to be an equal partner. The Vietnamese were not as afraid of the French as they were of the huge and ancient enemy, China. Chiang Kai Shek’s enormous Chinese army, which had been authorized to disarm the Japanese in the north under the Potsdam agreement, posed a great threat to Vietnam’s independence. The two countries had been at odds for two thousand years, and China had occupied and controlled Vietnam off and on for hundreds of years at a stretch. The Chinese army was looting everything in sight, and showed all the signs of becoming an permanent occupying force. When given the choice by the Americans of which of the occupying forces they would deal with, France or China, Ho Chi Minh is reported to have argued to his compatriots, “You fools!. I would rather eat French shit for five years than eat Chinese shit for a hundred years.” So the Chinese left and the French returned. Negotiations on the nature of the new French Union began, but it soon became apparent to the Vietnamese that the French had no intention of giving up their colonial prerogatives, and when negotiations bogged down the French navy snuck some warships off of Haiphong and bombarded the city, killing six thousand civilians. The war had begun. The war of French colonial aggression was made possible thanks to the American weapons and financial support and the constant repetition of Big Lie Number One by the Truman and Eisenhower administrations to cover it up. By the time of the battle of Dien Bien Phu, in May of 1954, America was directly subsidizing eighty percent of the cost of the invasion, and the once-moderate Viet Minh had been brutalized and radicalized by eight years of bloody warfare. They felt betrayed by the hypocrisy of what they saw as a forked-tongued America, and they had suffered hundreds of thousands of dead at the hands of the French invaders. With the fall of Dien Bien Phu the Viet Minh found themselves on the verge of total victory. The French army was near collapse, its morale was nonexistent, and the continuation of the war threatened the stability of the French government. In the last year or so I have read many reports, mostly in veterans magazines, that the Viet Minh did not have the resources to continue the war, which is why they agreed to the terms of the Geneva Accords in the first place. I would like to relate a personal anecdote which I think should refute this notion once and for all. I have a friend, a naturalized American of Jewish descent who was born and raised in Algiers when it was occupied by the French. He is a successful businessman and has been in this country for over thirty years, and he turned sixty the same week McNamara’s book came out. Several years ago the subject of Vietnam came up in a casual conversation, and he told me the story of how he heard about the fall of Dien Bien Phu. At the time he was a nineteen year old conscript in the French army, and had just finished his basic training. He and his unit were stationed in a barracks outside of Paris, waiting for orders to go to Indochina, when the news of the fort’s fall came over the radio. I said, “Gee, that must have been tough.” He looked at me like I was nuts and said, “Oh, no! Everyone went crazy, dancing around and singing and whooping for joy!” I was dumbfounded and asked, “But how could you be so happy over the defeat of your own army?” He looked me in the eye and said, “Because we thought it meant the end of the war, and none of us wanted to die for Michelin Rubber.” I would argue that his unit was typical of the state of the French army, and any suggestion that the Viet Minh, fueled by the cause of freedom, could not have prevailed against such a demoralized force of colonial aggressors is sheer idiocy. My friend was lucky he wasn’t just a little older, or he might well have been one of the 90,000 French who actually did die for Michelin Rubber. At the Geneva Peace conference the French agreed to bug out. Laos and Cambodia were to become independent, and the French and Viet Minh military forces were to disengage and regroup behind what the Accords specifically and categorically stated were to be temporary boundaries, and national elections were to be held two years. Based upon the military situation, the Viet Minh could have held out for much better terms or pushed on for total victory, but China’s Chou En Lai, who was supposedly on their side, sandbagged Ho by supporting the softer terms proposed by the French. The Chinese were playing a double game: they wanted the white colonials out of Asia, but at the same time they wanted a weak and disunited Vietnam on their southern border. The American delegation, under Secretary of State John Foster Dulles, wanted the French to hang on, and Dulles was not pleased with the idea of free elections in Vietnam. In America it was the heyday of Joe McCarthy, at the height of our national anti-communist paranoia, and all the old “China hands” at the State Department had been purged from the government because the right-wing zealots wrongly blamed them, rather than the incompetence and corruption of the darling of the American right, Generalissimo Chiang Kai Shek, for the “loss” of China (the American who knew Chiang best, General “Vinegar Joe” Stilwell, commander of the Allied forces in the China theater of operations, so thoroughly despised the corrupt Generalissimo that he publicly called him “Peanut” as much for his preening vanity as for his resemblance to the Mr. Peanut logo on the Planter’s jars). As a result of the right-wing purges by men like Joe McCarthy and Richard Nixon American foreign policy in the Far East was flying blind, and Dulles, assuming he was telling the truth, was wrongly convinced that the Soviets gave the Viet Minh orders from the Kremlin. The fact of the matter was that the Soviets, as well as the Chinese communists, were arm-twisting Ho into accepting terms which did not reflect the Viet Minh’s superior position on the battlefield. The Soviets did not want to be viewed as supporting the Vietnamese, and despite what the Americans believed, they had given the Viet Minh only token material support. This was because the communist party was strong in France, and regularly drew thirty percent of the vote. The Soviet communists fully expected the French communists to take over the government of France via the ballot box, actually winning a national election, and they did not want to do anything which could be construed as opposing the interests of France and thereby offend the French electorate. To the Soviets Vietnam was an unimportant backwater, whereas a communist-dominated France could have been a stake in the heart of the western democracies. It is here that we begin to slide from Truman’s rather simple and straightforward Big Lie Number One into the far more complex Big Lie Number Two, the lie that South Vietnam was a legitimate sovereign nation and we were there to defend democracy from ‘foreign invaders’ from the north. The Viet Minh were pressured by China and the Soviets to lay down their arms and go along with the Geneva Peace Accords, and they finally agreed only because of the ironclad guarantee that nationwide elections would be held. The agreement called for a halt to the fighting and for each side to withdraw its forces, the Viet Minh to the north of the seventeenth parallel, the French to the south. The French agreed to a complete withdrawal after national elections were held, which were scheduled for July 1956, two years away. The accords were very precise in stating that the seventeenth parallel was not in any way to be considered a border, and that Vietnam remained a single nation. The Liberty which Harry Truman had denied to Vietnam was now within its grasp, and the composition of the government was to be determined by the principle of Democracy, which, after Liberty, was America’s second greatest heritage. Or so believed the Viet Minh. The official US State Department ‘white paper’ which was served up as the justification for our aid to South Vietnam dismissed the promised elections as a communist ‘trick,’ and in his memoirs President Eisenhower stated that if he had permitted the elections to proceed Ho Washington and the Viet Minh would have won at least eighty percent of the vote. When I was doing my research all those many years ago, trying with all my heart to prove that America was in the right, nothing I found disgusted me more than the blatant, bald-faced lies which were told to justify the establishment of the government of South Vietnam. It was plainly obvious to this patriotic, conservative Republican college sophomore that South Vietnam was a complete and utter fabrication, with no geographic, cultural or historical justification whatsoever for its existence. I could find no plausible legal justification for it, not in the Accords, not in history, not under any concept of international law except the law of power. All I was able to find were the self-righteous proclamations and undocumented allegations and assertions of the State Department. In 1954 America’s leading Catholic, Cardinal Spellman of New York, became the champion of a wealthy Vietnamese Catholic, Ngo Dinh Diem. Diem, who had been out of Vietnam for years and had played no part in the war to evict the French invaders. Diem had been washing dishes in a New Jersey seminary under Spellman’s control when they met. Diem, who was for a time been a government functionary under the deposed Emperor, had impressed the Cardinal with the zeal of his anti-communism. Spellman was concerned that the elite and privileged status which the Catholics in Vietnam, who comprised less than ten percent of the population, had enjoyed under the French, would be threatened in a sovereign Vietnam. Based upon Spellman’s recommendations, the Secretary of State and his brother, Allen Dulles, head of the CIA, for lack of any other alternative due to the absence of any other advice because of the McCarthy purges, flew Diem back to Saigon and proclaimed him President of South Vietnam. They followed up by sending in CIA teams to sabotage the implementation of the Accords. The newly-chartered CIA sabotaged railroads, buses, power stations, and were very effective at spreading terror through rumors of anti-Catholic atrocities. The CIA campaign to convince northern Catholics that “the Virgin Mary has gone south” panicked the Catholic population of the North into fleeing, and thereby gave Diem his only support base in the south–the carpetbaggers from the north which he soon appointed to all the village mayoral and administrative posts over the Buddhists which had held them even under the French. The French, who had promised to stay on until the elections were held, pulled out a year early. They did so against the strong objections of the Viet Minh, who felt that it was with the French that they had signed their agreement, not with Diem and his American supporters. (Indeed, the United States was not a signatory of the Accords. Instead, we merely ‘noted’ that others had signed them.) Diem and the Dulles brothers made excuses that the Viet Minh were violating the Accords and refused to even talk about the subject of elections as required by the Accords. In effect, they said that the guaranteed national elections, which had been the only thing which induced the Viet Minh to agree to their provisions in the first place, rather than pursuing victory on the battlefield, were out of the question. An obviously rigged election, in which the Viet Minh were not allowed to participate, was held in the South in order to make it seem like we were defending Democracy. To add international weight to the budding Big Lie, and to give our actions the veneer of legality, John Foster Dulles cooked up SEATO, the Southeast Asia Treaty Organization, and signed formal ‘treaties’ with his new ‘nation’ of South Vietnam. When Abbie Hoffman proclaimed the existence of “The Woodstock Nation” he had the support of millions of a generation, but there was no legal justification for any such ‘nation’–it was merely a state of mind. It was the exact same thing in the case of South Vietnam, except that there it was Americans John Foster Dulles, Vice President Richard Nixon, Senator Joe McCarthy and Cardinal Spellman doing a Republican war dance in lieu of the Vietnamese. They sprinkled some pixie dust over Spellman’s megalomaniac buddy Diem without bothering to ask the Vietnamese what they thought about their newly imposed ‘leader.’ Voila, they proclaimed a brand new ‘nation.’ With the refusal to hold true national elections, and the attempt to justify the dismembering of a two thousand year old nation, so began what was known at the time as ‘Cardinal Spellman’s War.’ Due largely to the Cardinal’s militant preaching justifying the illegal actions which had been taken, the phrase ‘Kill A Commie For Christ’ became part of American political discourse. Our nation, which had been the beacon to oppressed peoples since its inception, had thrown its support behind what soon became a brutal, corrupt, Mafioso-like thug regime, and we had done it in the name of defending Democracy. My research in 1968 was limited to the historical and legal origins of the war and did not get into the actual prosecution of the war. At the time the truth about Big Lie Number Three, the Texas-sized whopper told by President Lyndon Johnson when he announced that North Vietnam had attacked American ships, was not known except to those like McNamara who were in Johnson’s inner circle. Big Lie Number Three led to the Gulf Of Tonkin Resolution, which Johnson used as his legal justification to send combat troops to Vietnam, and the war’s nickname changed to “Mr. McNamara’s war.” Forty thousand Americans came back in body bags while Johnson was in office, and he later admitted that the radar blips which he had told the nation were North Vietnamese patrol boats which he led us to believe had been stupid enough to attack the far superior American destroyers. Years after the fact, Johnson admitted to close associates that the radar blip that got him the war powers he needed had been nothing more than schools of flying fish. Richard Nixon gets credit for Big Lie Number Four, his “Secret Plan To End The War” on which he based his 1968 election campaign. In reality his ‘plan’ was to split the protesters up by introducing a draft lottery and dupe the silent majority by extending the war for four unnecessary years in order to insure his own reelection. For two decades he claimed it was mere ‘coincidence’ that just two weeks before the election Henry Kissinger announced ‘peace is at hand’ on the same terms he could have had back in 1968. Nixon’s ‘secret plan’ had killed another eighteen thousand guys like me for his personal political glorification. I only wish the Trickster had lived long enough to have seen the publication of The Haldeman Diaries so that he would have known the pain of having his evil revealed and the knowledge that, rather than having been ‘rehabilitated’ as he thought, that the name of Richard Milhous Nixon would forever live in ignominy. I give Nixon and Kissinger co-credit for Big Lie Number Five, the lie that Cambodia asked for American military help. The leader of Cambodia, Prince Sihanouk, had been able to keep his people out of the war while keeping the Khmer Rouge insurgents in his own country at bay. For this he was seen by Nixon and Kissinger as an enemy because he would not send his tiny army to cut off North Vietnamese supply routes. So they engineered a coup and installed a compliant Cambodian general, one Lon Nol, whose claim to fame was that his name spelled the same forward and backward. Nixon’s subsequent massive bombing of Cambodia failed to achieve its military objective, the Khmer Rouge were strengthened because Lon Nol was seen as being an American puppet, and at least a million Cambodians were massacred when the Khmer Rouge took over because of what was sent in motion by the lies of Nixon and Kissinger. I am grateful for the truths which were revealed by Haldeman and McNamara, but disappointed they did not go further. I hope that this article may help some understand the Vietnam legacy better. For too long right-wing ideologues have used the war to divide America for their own political gain, but with the revelations of the lies perhaps we can finally begin to put an end to the foul squawks of the chickenhawks. I have found that when arguing with people over Vietnam that using the nomenclature of The Five Big Lies drives supporters of the war right up the wall. For example, when someone argues, “Richard Nixon ended the war,” I now respond with, “No, that’s part of Big Lie Number Four. Nixon did not end the war, rather, he extended the war to insure his reelection and as a result killed 18,000 guys just like me in the process.” Or when someone argues “we were there to defend democracy,” I say “No, that’s part of Big Lie Number Two, because in 1956 we refused to let Vietnam have an election. You can’t very well be defending democracy when you won’t let a country have an election.” Perhaps the final debate has begun. Most Democrats have long-ago accepted the fact that Vietnam was a mistake, and have laid the blame on Lyndon Johnson while letting Harry Truman and John Kennedy off the hook. Republicans have never accepted the notion that we did anything wrong in Vietnam, instead casting the blame on liberals for not pursuing the war vigorously enough and the media for publicizing it. I have never seen any Republican of national stature, not one, admit that our involvement was anything other than righteous and noble. In the last presidential election they lambasted Clinton on the ‘character issue’ for opposing the war. Because of Haldeman’s and McNamara’s revelations maybe the character issue can now be turned on its head. Perhaps we shall finally see how much character Republicans truly have–will they be able to look the truth in the face and admit that they were wrong and apologize for all the damage the lies have caused to the fabric of America? Don’t hold your breath. -30-
