Dear friends,

As an American, I feel a sense of responsibility for the actions of my government in Vietnam. Though I was a child during the American war in Vietnam, I am an adult in the country that still has not apologized for it's actions there.

I have read much about the war. I am not incapable of understanding the ideas of some of my country people who thought they were doing a good thing, incipiantly, by going to Vietnam. But I am not blinded by the sham of apologetics which has been used as a defense of American attrocities in Vietnam, which are not in any way excused by the attrocities committed by the Vietmin.

The American war in Vietnam was to defend the American made government there, in the South of a country, eighty percent of which was in favor of Ho Chi Min, for leadership.

All one has to do is consult the Pentagon papers on this.

The Vietnamese, right or wrong in American eyes, it does not matter, were entitled to throw off the shackles of colonialism, and they did, after long years of warfare against the cruelty of the French government.

We rewarded Ho Chi Min's assistance of America during America's fight with the Japanese--and his obvious love of our constitution--with financial support to the French war effort. They tried to retake their colony. Eisenhower sent money, fearful of world opinion were we to send planes with American markings.

I became aware of the Vietnam war through personal investigation after learning of the criminal behaviour inherent in my government's history beginning with my political awakening during the Reagan years and after reading about our involvement in Latin America. I moved on to Ron Kovic's story and then I went on to O'Brien, Karnow, Herr, etc.

I thank American soldiers when I meet them, for going. For many of them, as I read in 'Nam' by Mark Baker, were bamboozeled into thinking they were going to be involved in a sensible defense of freedom and came home broken. They were put in situations that would make the best of us, good natured and all, obscene killers. They were victims too.

I wanted to go to Vietnam and teach English and to take pictures of what I thought must be a wonderful, yet sad nation. I felt a sense of reverence for the people who like the grasshopper in one of thir folk expressions, tripped up the elephant.

Life took me to Korea instead and I fell in love with that country 'too.'

I am an American who has lived in a different country because my society now reacts differently, in some ways, because of that war. We are lucky to have lost that war. It taught us a lesson I hope; that the ferocious and terrible cannot conquer the spirit of the free and determined; the simple and the native; the forever vigilent of all of us conquering types.

I am sorry for what our people have done. May we not do this kind of thing again. And may we learn our histories before tampering with them.

A final note about a great man; a gift to the movement of peace: I am thankful for the teachings of Thich Naht Han. I hope that the Vietnamese government will welcome him home soon. He is a living Buddha of compassion.

I wish peace to the people of Vietnam and I hope our two nations can be more than diplomatic, and truly friends.
