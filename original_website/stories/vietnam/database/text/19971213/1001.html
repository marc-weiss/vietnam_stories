Vietnam and the Marine Corps changed my life forever. The Marine Corps changed me for the better; Vietnam for the worse. Twelve of us went in from my neighborhood group. Six were killed and four wounded. I didn't think about the war much after I returned in 1968. My mother said I was cold. I went to a friend's funeral who was killed after I returned home. His mother blamed me for being alive. I felt guilty about surviving. I started having real problems about ten years later. I was 31, and all I had was a gun and nine cents when I returned to Cincinnati after years of roaming. My parents were dead, and at their funerals I felt nothing. I didn't trust anyone. I couldn't hold a job. I wondered why I was alive when so many others had died. I slept in two hour shifts with the lights on and a gun next to me. I hated crowds. Loud noises put me on the ground. I couldn't drink any alcohol because I was afraid I would get violent and hurt someone. My relationships didn't last because they didn't like me sleeping with a gun. Finally in 1978, I walked into a Vet Center and discovered I was suffering from PTSD. It shocked me. Since that time, I have learned to cope, and I began helping other vets with their problems. I have written booklets on PTSD and the stressor letter,( which are being used in some VA programs). I have helped over 300 combat veterans become service-connected by doing things right in the complicated system. I also publish a bi-monthly newsletter called The S-2 Report. Working with other combat vets helps me to cope. The war never goes away, but I have beaten it and become a survivor.

Dennis Latham 1st Battalion 3rd Marines 1967-68
