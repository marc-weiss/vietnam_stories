A FATHERS LETTER TO HIS DAUGHTER...

This is a letter-story my Father wrote to me in December 1968 as a result of a Vision he experienced while out on flight training exercises, on a cloudy day in Fort Rucker, Alabama. He saw the "Apocalypse" and knew the would not be returning home after the war. This was written while he was recovering from his injuries in Japan before going back to Vietnam. I was 3 months old. He died two months after he wrote this, K.I.A. March 1, 1969, he was only 21. His name was: Ray Rene Davis, W3 160 727, WO, USAR, AVN, 174TH AVN CO, 14TH AVN BN, 16TH AVN GP, AMERICAL DIV, VIETNAM. His home town was Oakland, CA. I am sharing this with whomever chooses to read it in the hopes that it will heal someones pain, help the ignorant see more clearly and help other gain some prospective on a suituation different than their own.

There are many ways to get to the National Cemetery. You can go by automobile, through the many beautiful parkways of San Francisco, or by freeway, bus or taxi. There are many ways to get to the National Cemetery, but during this week there is no way that is not crowed with people. "Why should this be so?" you ask, for in the full flush of life there is something frightening about going to a cemetery--except at certain times. But this week, the week before the Holy Days, is one of these times. For this is the week that the Lord God calls His angels about Him and opens before them the Book of Life. And your name is inscribed on one of these pages. Written on that page will be your fate for the coming year.

For these six days the book will remain open and you will have the opportunity to prove that you are deserving of His kindness. During these six days you devote yourself to acts of charity and devotion. One of these acts is the annual visit to the dead.

And to make sure that your visit to the departed will be noted and the proper credit given, you will pick up a small stone from the earth beneath your feet and place it on the monument so that the Recording Angel will see it when he comes through the cemetery each night.

You meet at the time appointed under an archway of white stone. The words NATIONAL CEMETERY are etched into the stone over your head. There are six of you. You look awkardly at one another and words come stiffly to your lips. You are all here. As if by secret agreement, without a word, you all begin to move at once and pass beneath the archway.

On your right is the caretaker's building; on your left, the record office, listed by plot number and burial society, are the present addresses of many people who have walked this earth with you and many who have walked this earth before your time. You do not stop to think of this for to you, all except me belong to yesterday.

You walk up a long road searching for a certain path. At last you see its white numbers on a black disk. You turn up the path, your eyes reading the names of the burial societies over each plot section. The name you have been looking for is now visible to you, polished black lettering on grey stone. You enter the plot.

A small old man with a tabacco-stained mustache hurries forward to meet you. He smiles tentatively, while his fingers toy with a small badge on his lapel. It is the prayer reader for the burial society. He will say your prayers for you.

You murmer a name. He nods his head in a birdlike acquiescence; he knows the grave you seek. He turns, and you follow him, stepping carefully over other graves, for space is at a premium here. He stops and points an old shaking hand. You nod your head, it is the grave you seek, and he steps back.

An airplane drones overhead, going to a landing at a nearby airport, but you do not look up. You are reading the words on the monument. Peace and quiet come over you. The tensions of the day fall from your body. You raise your eyes and nod slightly to the prayer-reader. He steps forward again and stands in front of you. He asks your names, so that he may include them in his prayer. One by one you answer him.

My mother.

My father.

My sister.

My brother.

My wife.

My daughter.

His prayer is a singsong, unintelligible gibberish of words that echos monotonously amoung the graves. But you are not listening to him. You are filled with memories of me, and to each of you I am a different person.

At last the prayer is done, the prayer-reader paid and gone to seek his duty elsewhere. You look around on the ground beneath you for some small stone. Carefully you hold it in your hand, like the others, one at a time step forward toward the monument.

Though the cold and wet of winter and the sun and rain of summer have been close to me since last you were here together, your thoughts are again as they were then. I am strong to each of your memories, except one.

To my mother I am a frightened child,huddling close to her bosom, seeking safety in her arms.

To my father I am a difficult son, whose love was hard to meet, yet strong as mine for him.

To my sister I am the bright older brother, whose daring was cause of love and frea.

To my brother I am the friend who shared the common hope of glory.

To my wife I am the lover, who, beside her in the night, worshipped with her at the shrine of passion and joined her in a child.

To my daughter--to my daughter I know not what I am, for she knew me not.

There are five stones lying on my grave and still, my daughter, you stand there wondering. To all the others I am real, but not to you. Then why must you stand here and mourn someone you never knew?

In your heart there is a tiny small hard core of a child's resentment. For I have failed you. You have never made those boasts that children are known to make: "My daddy is the strongest," or the smartest, or the kindest, or the most loving. You have listened in bitter silence, with a growing frustration, while others have said these things to you.

Do not resent me nor condemn me, my daughter. Withold your judgement, if you can, and hear the story of your father. I was human, hence fallible and weak. And though in my lifetime I made mistakes and failed many people, I would not willingly fail you. We who have been of one flesh, of one blood, and of one heart are now come toghther in one memory.

You place the stone quickly on the monument and stand there gravely, your blue eyes wide. Within you there is a small but creeping doubt. Your father.

I have no shape, no rounded image, in your memory. I am nothing but a word, a picture on the mantelpiece, a sound on other people's lips. For you have never seen me and I had seen you only through dreams.

Then how can I reach you, my daughter, how can I make you hear me when even my voice is an unfamiliar echo in your ears? I weep, my daughter, I weep for all the life I gave you that I will not share. The joys, the sorrows, I will not know with you as my father has known them with me.

For though I gave you life, you have given me even more. I learned many things. I learned again to love my father, to understand his feelings, his happiness, his inadequacies. For all the things I meant to him, you have meant to me.

I never held you in my arms and pressed you close to my heart and yet I feel these things. When you are hurt, I feel your pain; when you sorrow, I share your tears, and when you laugh there is joy in me. Alll the things you are were once part of me--your blood, your bones, your flesh.

YOu are part of the dream I was that still remains. You are the proof that I moved and walked the earth. you are my legacy to the world, the most precious that I could bestow. All the values are as naught when compared to you.

In your time there will be many wonders. The distant corners of the earth will be a moment's journey; the deepest ocean, the highest mountain, perhaps even the stars themselves will be within the reach of your fingers. And yet all these miracles will be as nothing when compared to the miracle of you.

For you are the miracle of my continuing flesh. You are the link that joins me with tomorow, the ik in the chain that spreads from the begining of time to never ending.

And still, there is a strangeness in it all. For you who stem from the rearing passions of my blood and strength and join me with tomorrow, know nothing of me.

We shared dreams together. "What are you like, my father?" you ask in the silenceof your little heart. Close your eyes, my daughter, and I will try to tell you. Shut the bright green world from you eyes for just a moment and try to hear me.

Now you are still. Your eyes are closed, your face is pale and you are listening. The sound of my voice is the sound of a stranger in your ears, and yet, deep within you, you know who I am..

The lines of my face will never be distinct in your memory, yet you will remember. For some day, in some time, you will speek about me. And in your voice will be sorrow that we have never known each other. And in that sorrow there will also be a contentment. A contentment that will come from the knowledge that all the things you are stem from me. The things that you will give your daughter began with me, and what my father passed on to me, and his father behind him.

Listen to me, my daughter, and know your father.

Though the memory of man is a temporary thing because his life is but a fleeting moment, there is a quality of immortality in him that is as permanent as the stars.

For I am with you and you are with me, and the man that began with Adam will live foreber on this earth. As I once lived.

Once I breathed the air you breath and felt the soft give of earth beneath my feet. Once your passions raced in my viens and your sorrows wept through my eyes.

There are papers lying in some hidden valt with my signature scrawled upon them in now browning ink; a social-security number burried in the mass statistics in a governmanet file.

These things I once had, my daughter. And for this and for many reasons other than this, my name will not be forgotten. For in these mere written records alone there is evidence of my immortality.

I was not a great man whose history has been recorded for children to study in school. No bells will ring for me, no flags decend upon thier mast.

For I was an ordinary man, my daughter, one of many, with ordinary hopes and ordinary dreams and ordinary fears.

I, too, dreamed of wealth and riches, health and strenght. I, too, feared hunger and poverty, war and weakness.

I was a soldier shaking with fear; the man berating the umpire at the ball game; the citizen in the privacy of the voting booth,happily electing the worthless candidate.

I was the man who lived a thousand times and died a thousand times in all mans six thousand years of record. I was the man eho sailed with Noah in his ark, who was the multitude that crossed the sea that Moses held apart, I was the man who hung from the cross next to Christ.

I was the ordinary man about whom songs are never written, stories are never told, legends are never remembered.

But I am the man who will live forever in the thousands of years yet to come. For I am the man eho will live forever in the thousands of years yet to come. For I am the man who will reap the few benefits and pay for the many errors that are created by the great.

And the great are but my servants, for my numbers are legion. For the great lie lonely in their graves beneath their mighty monuments because they are not remembered for them selves but for what they made.

But for me, all who weep for their loved, also weep for me. And every time someone mourns, he also mourns for me.

You open your eyes in wonder and gaze upppon the six stones lying on my grave. Now you know, my daughter. This was your father. Your mother's arms enfold you, but still you stare at the stones. Your fingers point to the words written behind them oin the monument. Her lips move gently as she reads them to you.

Listen carefully to them, my daughter. Are they not true?

TO LIVE IN THE HEARTS WE LEAVE BEHIND IS NOT TO DIE.

*Note: In 1972 my mother was remarried to a wonderful, kind and loving man who was to become my "father", the only father I ever knew. His name is Norman Winsor Hendershot, III (A.K.A. Joe), served from 1968-1970,US Army RA TC, SGT (P), Specialist Five, 67N40 Instructor, Door Gunner, USAR CON GP (REINF)USAAC, 174TH Aviation Company (Air Mobile Light), 14TH CAB. Both my natural-father & step-father Joe served in the same unit, but at different times, just missing eachother. Life is full of coincidences. This is one that I find some strange kind of comfort in. I believe in my heart that my father knew of his approaching fate...death. I also strongly believe that God sent my mother and I Joe to be her husband and my father. No other man could have ever been a better father to me. I love him for being the kind, gentel, loving, forgiving and supportive father he has been always. I am just another innocent casualty of war, orphaned by the Vietman War, he is scared by the horrors of the war he experienced. We have a bond that can not be broken. It is simply unexplainable. I am one very fortuniate girl, indead. All of us hurt, but we all were able to heal and put what we could behind us and move forward in this life. Don't misunderstand me though, we all carry some sorrow with us always for what happened there. But, we all have to move on experience new & wonderful things, live... Joe is my father and I love him very much. As for the father I never knew, I have feelings of loss, sorrow and many, many questions which can not be answered untill I met my maker, Lord of Lords, King of Kings, Creator of the Universe. Until that day, I will live life to the fullest, honor the memory of those who gave their lives for our country, and enjoy the great gift of life and enjoy the wonders of a loving family. Thank you to all those who have touched my life, my parents, brother, sister, grand-parents, great-grandparents, aunts, uncles & cousins.

Peace be with you.
