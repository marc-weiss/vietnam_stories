Vietnam POW/MIA/BNR

These informal notes are offered for readers to understand that there are
many more dimensions to the over-all situation than meet the eye. Please
bear with me, because it will take a little time to explain.
I am a former Royal Navy pilot and have written extensively about military
aviation in the U.S. In the process of this work, I have had the pleasure
and privilege of meeting many U.S. military pilots and flight crew, a
remarkable body of men and women. On the last day of Vietnam, one such man,
Harley H. Hall, CDR USN, was shot down at Qang Tri, captured but never heard
from again. I dedicated a novel to him (SIERRA SIERRA, published by Wm
Morrow in 1979).
In 1976 Viktor Belenko flew a MiG-25 from his air base near Vladivostok to
the Hakodate civilian airfield on the northern Japanese island of Hokkaido.
Becuase of my writing background, the San Francisco CHRONICLE asked me to
review his book "MiG Pilot." I did so, and was contacted by Belenko. We
became close friends.
In 1994 Viktor and I teamed up to write a fact-based novel "A Full
Accounting," which describes how a Navy F-4 pilot is shot down, captured, and
taken to Hanoi. There his death is faked by the North Vietnamese, because
his skills are needed in the USSR--to train Soviet fighter pilots in air
combat, since they had only used GCI (Ground Controlled Intercept) until
then. Through clever `maskirovka' (the centuries old Russian tradition of
trickery, deceit and deception) they convince the American to betray his
country. (In writing this particular piece of the book, I was assured by
every U.S. military pilot I knew that no loyal American would betray his
country, but when I sent them the description of the `maskirovka' thay
admitted that they would be deceived.)
In 1997 a Russian test pilot and former fighter pilot on an exchange visit to
the U.S. has as his guide and liaison a U.S. Navy fighter pilot, a woman.
She happens to be the sister of the lost F-4 pilot. When she shows her
billfold to the Russian, she points to a photo of her brother and says:
"KIA/BNR in a Hanoi prison camp." "Not so," says the Russian. "He taught
me air combat . . . and saved my life."
Together the pair goes into the FSU and extracts our man from th gulag.
Here is the real point: the Soviets took 22,000 Allied POWs after WWII, in
the record. After Korea, in the newly declassified Eisenhower papers, they
took 2,000 (after denying this known fact for 40 years and calling the Czech
defector who told them ` a liar and a psychotic.') The Soviet SOP was to
steal brains to save billions in development, so they stole every expert they
could.
Recall that no airmen were returned from Cambodia and Laos, even though many
were downed there? Good. Add them to thre `maskirovka' and you get an
estimated 614 U.S. aircrew kept by the Soviets and taken back to the USSR.
So I ask you this: why has no journalist studied this problem? And I ask
further: since it is known that the above story is 100% fact based and not
fiction, why erect the cynical smokescreen of `archeology' for remains of the
dead in Vietnam, when it is clear that the living were taken away.
Isn't it time that someone asked these questions in public.
