Preface to: Remembering Heaven's Face Simon & Schuster copyright 1992

Nearly twenty years ago in Hue, I glimpsed an odd event that has lingered with me: A Vietnamese friend was trying to help me understand the notion of "mat troi" conventionally "the sun," but literally "face of heaven," which he said meant the countenance of Ong Troi, Mr. Sky, also known as Ong Xanh, or Mr. Blue, who watches our affairs and constantly reassesses our fates. The face of heaven, he said, can be seen in those mirrors, engulfed by ceramic flames, that top royal buildings in the imperial city, but, he added, heaven also appears in the humblest of homes as the mirror placed on family altars. On the mirror's face, Vietnamese paint the names of their ancestors, then cover the mirror with a red cloth.

I was also curious about the red cloth, and so, several days later, my friend took me to a balcony that looked down into the courtyard of a nearby home. "Be very careful, Mr. John," he said, as we crouched behind the thick colonial Balustrades. "If they see us, we have big trouble." There below, beneath the red-tiled eaves of an old house whose entrance gate still displayed a mandarin's insignia on the portal, some old men were standing in the dusty courtyard. One of them, with thinning white hair, began to play a wooden flute. Huddled on the balcony, we watched as, one by one, each took a red cloth, draped it over his face, leaned back to tilt his face skyward, and then began to dance in slow whirls, arms extended, palms up, wheeling in glides that seemed filled with rapture.

That mysterious dance holds elements of all of my memories of Vietnam. In always varying proportions, they contain a secret witnessing of a strange event, some bewilderment at what I saw, and some threat of violence- all tinged with a sense of the human spirit reaching toward heaven, of that spirit being watched by heaven, where, in the Taoist- Buddhist continuum, our moral efforts are judged and weighed.

I volunteered to go to Vietnam, but as a conscientious objector. Like millions of other young men, I came home filled with memories that sometimes seem more real than my present life. But as a civilian engaged in alternative service, traveling alone on public buses or on riverboats, or hitchhikinh on choppers and planes, I often saw a Vietnam that other Americans missed. "Di ra mot ngay, ve mot sang khon" is one of the proverbs I learned from peasants in the Mekong Delta: "Go out one day and come back with a basket full of wisdom." Leave the safe, unsurprising village, the proverb says, take the road, discover something new. Bring it home.

In starting to write this account, I found myself on that bright, perilous road once again. Opening up notebooks that have been taped shut for twenty years, I found myself going out again, once again traveling under a brilliant blue sky that is the face of heaven.

John Balaban
