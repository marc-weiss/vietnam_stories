In a very real sense, the Vietnam War is my life. I was drawn to Vietnam and the war like many in my generation. The war was all around us in a small, working-class town in upstate New York. The sorrow of war touched my family like it touched thousands of others. Teaching about this conflict was something that I wanted to do as long as I can remember. In the eighth grade, we had to write an essay about our future plans. I vividly remember writing that I would be a college professor and teach about Vietnam in the hopes that tragedies like this one could be avoided in the future. Despite the innocence and naivet of that statement, it has remained my beacon over the past twenty five years.

For the past fifteen years, I have fulfilled my prophecy, teaching courses related to the war to undergraduate students, many of whom were born after the fall of Saigon. Understanding the complexities of this tragedy has remained fascinating, exciting, and heartbreaking. Today's students, fortunately, are not trapped by the ideological baggage of yesterday and seem ready to embrace new interpretations, new methodological approaches, and new questions on the war. This scholarly detachment does not erase the passion of the era, however, as they are quick to understand that the Vietnam War remains the watershed event of their parents' lives.

Teaching undergraduates about a war that is in many ways very personal for me provides interesting and daily challenges. I find my own views constantly under attack from critical and quick-witted students. This forces me to rely more and more on historiographical arguments and textual analysis of primary evidence, making my teaching more nuanced and sophisticated. In addition, many of my students are learning Vietnamese and join me in reading Communist Party documents in class. We have also toured Vietnam together, visiting key places connected to the war and learning a bit about the Viets themselves.

The Vietnam War remains the focus of my academic life and has informed my private life in powerful ways. My interest in the Vietnam War has not diminished, but intensified over the last fifteen years primarily because there is still much to learn about the conflict and new students to teach.

Robert K. Brigham Assistant Prof. of History Vassar College
