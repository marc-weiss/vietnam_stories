Souvenirs

X-rays were a mystery at first,

but I learned to think in negatives, to read shapes of whole or broken bones, the gray nimbus of my flesh.

Through clear specks light shone like stars in a planetarium, different angles showed new constellations. "Shrapnel," I was told, "too small for concern."

"Nothing to worry about, unless it nicks a muscle or strapes against a bone. Shrapnel migrates, you know, burrows blind as worms, travels quite slow."

I relearned to stand, step, walk.

I conquered pain, relapsing only in winter when I woke from dreams of burning ore to the ache of knitted bone.

Years after the wound, a sliver of steel rose to the surface. Blind drunk on rum, with an exacto knife and needlenose pliers, I cut it free.

Regular as Easter, I change a blade and add to my collection. The only souvenirs I brought home from war: tiny corkscrew twists of metal.
