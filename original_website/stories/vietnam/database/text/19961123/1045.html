One subject that is hard to talk about, although it's getting a little easier each time, is about the day that I got shot. There are a lot of things that I didn''t know about it, even until recently. One of the things that I found out recently was that the guy that shot me was only 15 feet away from me, but with the jungle the way it was and everything, it was really hard to see. I didn't see him. I was walking in the point -- this was at the time I had the M79 grenade launcher; I'd had it for about two weeks -- and we were working along in Tay Ninh province along the Cambodian border and sometimes, outside the Tay Ninh province in Cambodia; we were working back and forth in this area and catching Charlie as he was coming in off the Ho Chi Mihn Trail, ambushing him, and we were working this one area for so long that apparently Charlie got wise to what we were doing and set up in one of our old positions and waited on us to come back. And I walked right into it and they had these clumps of bamboo on top of a mound -- we called them ant hills -- I forgot that I had quick release snaps on my rucksack and I'm sitting there trying to get my right arm out -- laying on the ground trying to get my right arm out of the rucksack so I could get it off my back, so everything had to be in the ground and I couldn't move, and next thing I knew, I woke up and laying on top of it, I saw this red fountain coming out of my arm and everybody was gone. And I remember hearing a faint voice but to be perfectly honest with you, I thought I was dead and I thought it was an angel yelling down at me and to this day I'm not real sure but it turned out to be my Medic and he had -- I don't know whether he was stupid or just brave or what -- even though the company had pulled back he still came up and was just out there wandering around by himself. He finds me, he starts yelling and he draws fire away from me. And he gave me a chance to get myself together and crawl back to him, and he was behind this log and when I got back there he started working on me, leaning over me, and I kept trying to tell him, "------Doc, get down!" I could still feel the wind of the bullets passing my head and he was over top of me and anyway, he patched me up and carried me back, or rather drugged me back for awhile because I couldn't get up. And I remember as we was passing my company commander a way back in the rear -- I got these bandages all over me, my arm, and I got shot in my hip, too -- and instead of saying anything else, the first thing out of his mouth was, "Where's your weapon, soldier?" And I wanted to smack him; I felt like telling him he was lucky right then that I didn't have it with that kind of a question. And the Medic told him well, a few things that I don't think you probably want to hear. But it got me back; they called in a Medivac chopper and there was, I forget how many of us, but there was a bunch of us that got hit that day, including my lieutenant. In fact, the lieutenant was over a ways from us, over to my right, where we got ambushed, and he tried to shoot the guys that got me, that's how I found our recently, I just recently found the lieutenant and he's the one that told me that the guy was 15 feet in front of me, and he shot the guy that got him, but he couldn't get the one that got me. And to this day, one of the things that has the most impact on me to this day, and it could be verified by most of the guys who know me around here, that I can't handle fire crackers or fireworks. If I hear fire crackers then I just go absolutely nuts, because every time I do, it puts me back in that ambush. That Medic was killed two months later doing the same thing. That's about it.

Mike Roy One of the things I remember that had a profound impact on me, I remember when we were processing through camp headquarters in Nam Khe, I'd went to the mess hall and, they had these trash cans outside, and after we'd got done eating we'd take our trays out and dump our leftovers out in these trash cans, cigarette butts and all, paper, everything else. And as we were dumping our stuff in, these Vietnamese women and children were dipping in there with their hands and filling up these sandbags with what we were throwing away and I thought, well maybe they were gonna -- you know, heck, I grew up on a farm, I thought maybe they were going to slop the hogs, turns out, I asked what they were going to do with that, and they were taking it home to eat, what was our scraps, they were taking home to eat. I didn't grow up in a real wealthy family or anything like that and I thought we had it hard growing up till I saw that, and it made me appreciate everything that I have got today. I worked hard for everything that I've got today. I've got a wife who's probably one of the toughest ladies I've ever met -- she had to be to put up with all the hell I put her through and she stuck with me. We've been married for 28 years and I consider myself one of the luckiest guys in the world for that reason.

When I came home I came home to -- when I got shot they sent me home. I went to Ft. Knox and spent -- after I got out of the hospital -- I spent 13 months down there and was assigned to an infantry unit in the 194 Armored Brigade down in Ft. Knox so I had time to do some healing before I went out into the civilian world and it's a good thing that I did because the attitude that I had, when I came home, especially running into some of the hippies and stuff, had I not had that time to cool off a little I'd probably be in jail right now. I know right after I got out of the army while I was waiting for my clearance to come through at Monsanto, I was working midnights at Woody's Super Market, and a guy came in -- I was working back in the deli -- a guy came in and started tapping the case and that just set me off and I started over the case after him. It wasn't nothing that serious, it's just that it was just one of those things I couldn't handle. I couldn't handle the fact that friends of my mom and dad's at the VFW and the Legions told me that I didn't belong there because I didn't fight in a real war, so it was real tough to handle that, but despite that, and despite what I had been through, I was determined that I was going to make something of myself and be somebody and I think I have succeeded.

For years I tried to hide, like Steve said, I tried to hide the fact that I was a Vietnam vet; I didn't want anybody even to know, after a couple bad experiences that I had when I first came home, when I first got out of the service, I decided I would just go and try to forget the whole thing and it didn't work. It seemed as though the harder I tried to forget the worse it got and then I ran into -- one of the guys that I worked with was a member of Chapter 97 and he kept after me to come down, check it out, what have you, and I went down to the Vietnam Veterans Memorial Park in Dayton for the dedication in 1986, on Memorial Day in 1986, and I had just retired from Monsanto on disability, after having a heart attack and what have you, so I really needed something to help me out; it seemed as through my whole life was starting to fall apart again. And I saw the color guard down there marching and dropping the wreaths in the river and all the things that was said at the park that day, what have you, that, to me, was when I started coming out of my shell and I decided then that I needed some help. I mean, before I just tried to hide it and tried to pass it off and forget about it. And I got talking with some of the guys but still wouldn't join anything, I didn't want to join anything; I didn't want to commit to anything, but eventually I came around, I think it was somewhat about 1988 or something like that, 1989, I don't remember, and I joined the Chapter and started getting involved and I found that there was other people just like me that was experiencing the same problems that I was experiencing, with the same kind of attitude that I had, and I hated the government, I hated politicians, anything like that, and I hated any kind of authority and yet I had been a police officer; after I got laid off at Monsanto for a while there I worked as a police officer and I was in Security up there so you know, I spent most of my life, it seemed like, in some kind of authority position, yet I hated it, and it was a position of responsibility. Had it not been for my meeting with the people that I did in Chapter 97. They tell me that there are over 58,000 names on the Wall in D.C. and I understand that there are twice that number or three times that number, I think, something like that, I don't know exactly, of guys that have died by their own hands since Vietnam and I know for a fact that I would have been one of those statistics had it not been for the Chapter. So, it's been a tough row but I have had a lot of good support between my wife, the Chapter, one of the doctors out at the V.A., that has worked with me for five years, and as much as I have hated politicians, I've been very active politically the last three or four years, including working on George Bush's campaign. That's a big change for me, I'll tell ya, because I didn't think that anything like that would ever happen to me. Especially with all the things that has happened and the attitudes that I have had, I would never would have thought that at this point that I would be involved in something like this Chapter or anything like it, let alone be president. So I have to thank a lot of people including two or these young guys sitting here with us, they've helped me come a long way.

Like I said, that was the last time he was ever at my house.

I just wanted to say, so many of our family members and friends and what have you, that don't know what we've experienced or don't understand what we've experienced as Vietnam veterans, so many times will say, "well, the war was 30 years ago, whatever, you ought to be over it by now." What we've learned to do, though, is put it into some sort of perspective, go on with our lives and try to build on the things that we've learned out of our war experiences And that is something, I hope, that I can convey to everyone I come in contact with, that I'm a combat wounded veteran that just want people to understand.
