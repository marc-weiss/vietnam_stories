Twenty years ago, I sat in a hearing room very similar to this one we are in today. I was there to testify about the continuing bloodshed In Vietnam. Among the other veterans who were sitting at the table with me was an angry but eloquent young man, dressed like the rest of us, in field jacket and jeans. I don't remember all of what he said but one sentence has remained with me.

"We are here to ask," he said, "and we are here to ask vehemently where are the leaders of our country? Where is the leadership?"

The veteran who asked those questions, who spoke so compellingly of the cost of war, is one of our leaders today. He is John Kerry, the Senator from Massachusetts. His voice has been one of those asking for restraint, speaking for moderation and voting to allow the sanctions time to work before rushing into what could be a costly and disastrous war. I commend John for this and I would like to add my own voice in a more personal way.

I was the military advisor for Trang Bang District, Tay Ninh Province, Vietnam, in late 1968 and early 1969. Trang Bang is located an the parrot's beak area of the Cambodian border. It was a very active sector for the NVA and the South Vietnamese militia that I advised suffered very heavy casualties. After weeks and months I became very familiar with death and dying but, feeling as invincible as any 23 year old, my own mortality remained a distant matter.

Then, early in the morning of April 26th, I had an incredibly close encounter with my own death. Approaching the village of Tan An,, my two point men were knocked to the ground from the blast of an 82MM mortar. After a few minutes, I moved forward cautiously to check the extent of their injuries and administer whatever aid I could. The Vietnamese did not have their own medic. About 40 feet from them I paused to take a real good look around. At the moment I stopped, I heard the click of a detonator less than a meter from where I stood. An often happens in those situations, I never heard the explosion of the chicom mortar round. My next recollection was of being rather high in the air as the exquisitely blue sky spun around me. When I returned to earth, I landed on my back. For a moment I thought that I had only had the breath knocked out of me but I seemed to be having a big problem turning over to reach for my weapon. it was still there but its plastic stock was gone and only the barrel and receiver group remained. Being a fast thinker, I realized that the situation was not good. I started to take a close look at my body. In a way, I wished I had not. Without going into greater detail I would have to describe the lower part of my person as shredded. In addition my right arm was bleeding from several places and I had blood in my right eye. My thoughts remained amazingly clear- I needed a radio to call a chopper and get to a hospital very quickly.

Somehow, I'm not really sure of those details, this was accomplished. Waiting for the med-evac, I realized I was dying. I can tell you this angered me a great deal. I was not frightened, I was not sad or contrite. I was very, very angry. And it was not anger directed at whomever had perpetrated this crafty ambush,(l had pulled the trigger too many times and directed too many airstrikes to see this as anything other than payback time), I was angry with a world that was slipping away from me, at a world where these things happened, at a world where war was commonplace and you could be a strong young man one moment and very dead the next.

The only thing that intruded an this thought was an incredibly intense pain. Somehow, through the pain and the anger I heard the whumpa-whumpa of the chopper. About 35 minutes after I was wounded I was at the 12th Evac Hospital in Cu Chi. By the time they got me to triage I was conscious but my lungs were shutting down and tunnel vision was setting in- death is not subtle. Unbelievably, I thought at the time, the nursed wanted to know my home address and telephone number. My last conscious words were an awful obscenity about the intelligence of the US Army in general. Ironically, it was addressed at one of the people who saved my life. I still feel the same way about the Army but I sincerely apologize to that wonderful, patient woman who really knew how to get that last breath out of an American soldier. I am sure that there are nurses like her in the Gulf now. My fear is that men wounded as I was, are supposedly to be evacuated by land rather than air. I can tell you with great certainty that I would not be here today if there hadn't been air-evac 22 years ago. I hope this consideration is taken into account by our leaders today.

I'd like to digress for a moment to make a point. several weeks ago there was a story in the New York Times from their white House correspondent. It was about the crisis in the Gulf of course, and my eye was drawn to one of the sections which was titled, "Nightmare Scenario". I expected to read about an American casualty estimate or, possibly, civilian casualties. I was sadly mistaken. A senior Administration official was commenting on the problems the Administration would face if Saddam were to stage a partial withdrawal from Kuwait. What would be their response, the story went, to such a diplomatic "preemptory strike"?

I ask you is this a nightmare scenario...? I'll give you a nightmare scenario- Try waking up in a hospital four days after you are wounded. You think you might be in Japan but you are not sure. Half the time you are not I even sure you are alive - you have only the pain to say you are. The war has been going on for five years but there aren't enough beds, medics, doctors or nurses. To get one x-ray can take 4 hours. You wait 18 hours without liquids or medication for surgery that might, or might not happen. Your family doesn't know where you are or even it you are still alive because the Army sends then one telegram to say you are critically wounded, then forgets about it for 3 weeks.

Now folks, I know a nightmare and that's a nightmare. Will this be the story in the Gulf, in a war of higher intensity and with less time to prepare? We are assured by our leaders that it won't but the evidence says otherwise.

And I would like to remind you if you are not already reminded by our presence in this room today that this war, this War for the Liberation of Kuwait, which so many people seem so anxious to fight, will not be over in a week, no matter what the Armed Services Committee experts have to say. Nor will it be over in 10 weeks or 10 years. For those men and women of this nightmare to come, and their families, and the families of those who become names on a wall, it will go on and on and on. And you, our leaders, will be gone, the newspaper and television people gone, everybody having moved on to new matters. But they will remain - alone with their pain and their loss.

To bring this to a close, I would like to tell you something that I consider very personal, - something that I thought I would never talk about in public. It addresses the reason why I am here today.

About 22 years ago, I woke up in that hospital ward in Takoda, Japan. I was one of many young men. Most, like me, would go home to families and loved ones devastated by the horror of what they would have to confront; a few had only hours to live, their families to be devastated by that loss. I can't begin to tell you of the pain to which I awoke. It was utterly overwhelming- pain within me,, despair all around me. If my words today could somehow in the future, take one moment of such pain away from one soldier, I would feel like I had accomplished a great deal.

In my final words I would like to remind our leaders that wars do not end. They live on in the people that are touched by them. A war in the Gulf would touch millions of people and make no take about it, this will be your war.

Ed Miles Vietnam Veterans Association of America

Ed Miles
