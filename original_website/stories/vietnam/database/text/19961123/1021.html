I grew up in Saigon, in a two-story villa on Phan din Phung, in a house with bars on its windows. My youngest sister was born there; Saigon was our childhood. When families were evacuated from Vietnam, my father sent us to Bangkok, where I eventually graduated from high school. My father was in Vietnam throughout most of the war; my sister is a Vietnam- era vet; my husband a former Vietnam Marine. That explains much of my interest in Vietnam, but my work with women's writing on Vietnam, specifically, is due to the absence of poetry available to me when I wanted to write my sister's story, my father's story, my husband's stories, through the eyes of a sister, of a daughter, of a wife. But I could find no such stories. The anthology is my attempt to make sure that others can find--and write--their stories.

A beggar at the gates of privilege-- For lost Saigon that became Ho Chi Minh City

In my dreams the city stands. The pastel shadows of its Gallic architecture bleed like Indian prints into the damp afternoon, and I watch the almond eyes of Brahmin oxen watching nothing. Their wattles sway together, the gee ox and the haw ox dancing ponderously, in perfect measure.

In my dreams the city blossoms. Fragile starfish flowers veil the white walls of our villa, while tiny geckos weave their jeweled patterns among banana leaves. We count the times they tell their names-- gecko. gecko. gecko. You can watch their dark hearts beating.

In my dreams the city calls my name. Melodies are torn from ragged voices by the fingers of the monsoon wind: I hear the clatter of the street man's bamboo sticks. He sells his steaming noodle soup from a cart outside the iron gate, the shape and sound of hunger.

In my dreams the city falls around me. Bullets pock the streets like hail while I count tanks rolling past, ancient beasts of dark green evil. Square heads squat on thick bodies. Each long gunsnout sniffs the wind. They smell their way home.

In my dreams the city is a black hole, lurking just beneath the mask of memory. Insistent that it has not put away its dead, it prowls the dark horizon of the morning.

We grieve together, night-caught, fleet and wicked, beggars at the gates of privilege.

by Britton Gildersleeve
