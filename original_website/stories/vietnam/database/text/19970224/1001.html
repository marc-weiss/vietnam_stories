Deep inside the Vietnam Wall of sorrows
A letter from Millfield
Lady Borton

It was years before I could visit the Wall.
In the early 80's, I often read about the Vietnam Wall. Our evening
paper fave its design extra coverage because the architect had grown up
nearby. I would read by my living room stove, surrounded by photographs
I had taken during the ear. The photos were faces of Vietnamese
civilians - mostly children - who had lost legs or arms.

Surrounded by those photos, I felt angry about the Wall.

We've forgotten now, but there was a ruckus years ago about the Wall's
design. Here was a national monument you could walk past yet not
notice. This monument didn't rise in glory, demanding honor. Instead it
invited you inside.

But I didn't want to enter.

In 1985, I attended a conference for Vietnam writers. I felt out of
place in the suave society of famous literati. But I forgot my shyness
when I met Sandie Fourlal. Her passion was pure. Unlike us
writer-dogmatists, Sandie had not lost a close relative or friend in
Vietnam. Yet single-handed, she had raised money for the monument.

I asked Sandie to take me into the Wall.

We went early on a summer morning in 1985. Washington was bustling, but
there was a gathered quiet among that day's first visitors to the
monument. The light was soft and gray. Rain grazed the Wall's granite.
When such a rain accompanies a funeral in Vietnam, the Vietnamese say it
falls like tears.

Sandie took me first to check the directory, which lists the name, home
town, rank and wall panel for each of those men who, if living , would
be my age. I felt disoriented. I didn't know where to look. What name
could I search for?

Years before, a former student had replaced me in Vietnam. He died
there. I knew our talks after my return had influenced his decision to
serve. But I couldn't look for Rick. We had been civilians. Rick's name
is not on the Wall.

I thought of the photographs in my living room at home in Ohio. Some of
those friends were later killed. But they were Vietnamese. Their names
are not on the Wall. I knew hundreds who had died in Vietnam. But I did
not know a single name on the Wall. Embarrassed, I looked up my family
name and wrote down the panel address of a relative I had never known
existed.

Sandie and I turned from the directory and entered the Wall. Its
descending "V" lured me as relentlessly as Vietnam had engaged my
generation. I felt like I was entering a wound that slashed into the
earth.

Sandie pointed to names from a skirmish. She knew the details of death
because a survivor had written to her. She showed me another skirmish,
then an extended battle. By now, we were deep into the Wall, and I could
recognize its patterns. There was no escape. Lists of names reached
over my head. They reached back to my right, back to my left.

I traced my fingers over a firefight: a platoon of names from a single
day. I watched the rain drops slide down the image of my own face
reflected in those names. Why did those men die? Whom had they loved?
Who still loved them?

Some men in that platoon were listed as missing: a frailty of hope. I
thought of a friend in Vietnam. Like American families, she searches
still for her missing husband. His buddies can't find were he died. With
no grave to tend, she mourns her husband's destiny as a "wandering
soul."

Thinking of that friend as I stood deep in the Wall, I felt like I was
inside the tip of a granite auger boring into the earth. It seemed to me
that stories behind the names etched into that granite must someday
press through the earth to Vietnam itself. Perhaps only then, when we
reach through with our own wall of sorrow to theirs, can we all be
healed.
