My brother, Keith Wesley Allen, was killed October 13, 1967, in Viet Nam. I was 16, my younger brother 14. I watched the PBS special tonight on Maya Lin; I sobbed when the memorial was shown as well as when it was talked about.

I vaguely remember the controversy surrounding its origins: that was during the score of my denial. During that time I resented my mother reminding me what October 13th meant if I happened to call home.

In the late 80's I had cause to be in Washington on another Friday the 13th week-end; as the old cliqued "fate would have it" I visited the memorial on the Sunday the 15th that my brother died from "a frontal head wound of such severity as to cause concern" that we had been informed of the previous Friday.

To say the experience was gut-wrenching would be a pro- found understatement. Even after decades of grief and denial, my hand shook as I took the receipt from the volunteer which had printed out on it the panel and posi- tion of his name.

My companion, a fellow worker, said, "Oh, I didn't know you knew anyone who was killed over there." "My brother," I managed to squeeze out before I erupted in what I can only describe as a fit of bottled up anguish that would not be denied further.

Once we found his name on the panel, I virtually collapsed in sobs and inexplicably longed for an eraser! God, how I wanted to take his name off that wall! I had so hoped that I wouldn't find it there.

Since we hadn't been able to look at him when his body was sent home, remember that "frontal head wound?", I had hoped that I could report back home that, indeed, the sixteen year old baby sister had been right! It was a mistake! We could all breathe easy; the dreams were true, instead. He was lost somewhere in the Pacific Northwest and couldn't remember who he was, living in the forests and fearing society -- but salvage- able to his family if we could only find him and gently flush him out!

But the volunteers had no erasers for the granite. And all these 29 years of dreams continue. How did the Vietname era affect me? I can only guess how it would have been had Keith lived or had the war not been. But in a trite attempt to answer briefly, the bubble was burst. Until Keith died, I believed in the wisdom of my elders, in the justness of our government and our way of life. After he died, all of the illusions were shattered and I still moarn for the promises that died in that far away land.

In 1986 I wrote a poem about my feeling on the subject:

Fifty thousand Jesuses were crucified on altars of fear. And nineteen years after all those nineteen-year-old champions were borne home on the shoulders of their peers we find we are wandering still:

those of us who waved adieu, obsentiously avowing the 'parture not a good-bye, willing the days to fly 'til hugs and kisses could rain on our parade.

But October is a chilly month no matter how many Octobers pass, for each drills home a fresh piece of news:

adieu really is good-bye.
