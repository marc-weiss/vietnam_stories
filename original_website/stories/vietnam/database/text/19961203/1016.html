You have to learn those lessons somewhere and if you don’t, you aren’t going to have those feelings. And everybody didn’t grow up. Things—where things changed for me—that’s where I became my version of an adult. I don’t think that without the organized opposition to the war, and how it affected me, that I would have necessarily learned those lessons. I had opportunities, they were there. I went to a public high school that was 95% black and I could see what was happening to people who were not from a middle class white family. And I still sleep walked through high school and I was protected by the school authorities who wanted to at least get me out and they were already giving up on lots of kids (and it didn’t start happening in the Dayton school system until the 90’s) and it had been happening in Chicago before but I didn’t get that until afterwards. The war opened my eyes, it opened my eyes to the fact that the events that change history and affect history are the result of choices or participation of individuals and in some ways, justice— peace and justice people keep losing. But we still keep learning lessons, we still keep being provided with a vision of something different by looking at the Vietnamese who really did struggle to build something else and by the Nicaraguans who were out there giving inoculations and teaching people to read, and those were ideas about how you build justice step by step and how you act, like when you do those visions that were shared and developed ,and those movements were gifts we lost and they shaped me absolutely.

>>>>> I was on the City Council in Ann Arbor when we were being lobbied to create a memorial to Vietnam vets and we eventually did and I had an enormously difficult time with that and wanted to see—I don’t feel that my own behavior was particularly honorable or required memorializing in any way but there were a lot of opponents to the war who did what they considered to be their patriotic duty, in the service of the most fundamental and important human values and I always felt absolutely that the idea that they should be blamed for the suffering of Vietnam vets was so wrongheaded that the only way to correct that was to create a memorial to the people who fought in or against that war and who struggled too and that any other kind of memorials were inappropriate and divides us and that our movements, we most often lose, not because we’re bad in the way we go about doing what we do and what we are always thinking about... but at least because they’ve always been a mechanism out there to oppose the goals of our movement and when we divide Vietnam vets against protester, it is no accident that we are divided, and the anger those people feel and the regret that those people feel is systematic and institutionalized by the U.S. government all these years. So be it.

Jeff Epton

Dayon, OH
