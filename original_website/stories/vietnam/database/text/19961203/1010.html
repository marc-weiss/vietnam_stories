Personal Reflections On the Vietnam Era

From 1967-1970 I was attending Western Washington State College (now Western Washington University). My wife and our three son's moved to Bellingham, Washington where I began college as a Freshman. I was 32 years old and working for an education degree. Each day on campus and each evening on the TV news we were reminded of the effects of war. I saw young men on a daily basis who's life revolved around "going to college" to stay out of war and in the evening young men "going to hell" to be in war. There were the usual campus protests, downtown marches, and letters to the editor. I participated in more than one "march" through the town carrying my U.N. flag.

Graduating in 1970, I obtained employment in a small town school district and spent 25 year working with 5th & 6th graders. The idea of world citizenship was woven into each day's activities. Reconciliation, harmony of the races, elimination of prejudice, and reverence for nature were also a part of every interaction with students . For many years I would show my students the egg stained flag as a symbol of my "involvement" in history. I urged students to settle differences through "negotiation" rather than violence.

Now retired, I have the time to become physically involved in projects of merit. Last Earthday, April 21, we attended a benefit concert given by our son Eric Tingstad and his playing partner Nancy Rumbel for the Earthstewards Network. My wife and I wanted to be a part of an effort to bring healing to a place of great suffering. It was at that concert that so many years of verbal work for International understanding found expression in personal involvement. I applied to be one of forty people from around the world to be a part of an undertaking called PeaceTrees Vietnam.

I chose to get involved because it is an opportunity for me to DO something for reconciliation among people rather than just talk about it or throw money at it. For years I have contributed money and energy to various causes promoting peace and understanding. This is an opportunity to put some personal action with the money. For over four decades I have stressed, while working with the children and adults in my life, the need to believe and work for the oneness of humanity, to eliminate all forms of prejudice, and to start building of a new era of worldwide cooperation.

The project was conceived a year ago under the direction of the Earthstewards Network, which is headquartered in Bainbridge Island, Washington. Last June an international team of landmine detection and removal experts went to the site. They assisted a team of Vietnamese militia in the removal of 360 pieces of live explosives, including 47 landmines, from an eighteen acre area. A variety of trees will be planted in their place. The land has been quadruple checked, plowed, and prepared for the seedlings. Now it is time for fruit and shade trees to be placed where the landmines used to be.

Later in November, I will be traveling with forty other participants from Western countries to join forty Vietnamese in a project called PeaceTrees Vietnam. The group, consisting of doctors, nurses, lawyers, construction workers, students, therapists, teachers, software developers, and social workers, will travel to Dong Ha, a town in Quang Tri Province, at the old DMZ, in Central Vietnam to work in the reforestation phase of a Landmine Awareness and Removal effort.

In addition to the work of tree planting, the forty participants from the West will also help equip a sorely lacking hospital and work with the children who were unlucky enough to find leftover landmines before the detection teams could.

Upon my return I will share my experience with dozens of students during visits to classrooms throughout the Northwest. I know young people are always interested in life in far off places and in this shrinking world, these far off places are getting closer every day.

I want to bring home a sense of accomplishing my goal of furthering the cause of unity between East and West. This will be a first hand opportunity for me to live and work with people I have come to highly respect having been enamored with Buddhist teachings for forty years. In recent years the writings of Thick Nhat Hanh, an exiled Vietnamese monk now living in France, have been a source of inspiration and solace. This venture will serve to give me first hand knowledge of the lifestyle of Buddhist peoples that I will incorporate in sharing the dearly held values of unity, cooperation and interbeing.

I am leaving on a 10:45 Air Canada flight to connect with Cathay Air this Friday. Upon my return I will add some impressions to round out this communique.

Sincerely, Jack Tingstad
