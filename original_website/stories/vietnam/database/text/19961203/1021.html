I was born July 23, 1970, during two wars that did not happen.

The first war -- the Cold War -- was expected to culminate in mutual assured destruction. But that destruction never came...the war was a sham.

The other war happened, but for whatever reason, no one wanted to discuss it. It was like a dirty word that wasn't to be mentioned in good company. In my solidly mid-western Catholic family the "V" word become almost as taboo as the "F" word. It was evoked only to express disdain of politicians and bureaucrats, and over time it became associated with everything that was wrong with this country. I don't believe this was deliberate. I suspect that after years of protests, nightly news coverage, body counts, and dead brothers and neighbors, America -- and my family -- just wanted to forget.

As I grew older, my quest to find out exactly what was so terrible about Vietnam led me to my school library. At the small Catholic School I attended, we had plenty of books on World War II--but not a single volume on Vietnam. The year was probably 1980. I had a great uncle that served under Patton in Europe and I devoured the World War II books (most of them written during the 1950s), admiring the brave soldiers with names like Dolittle, MacArthur and Montgomery. I even entertained joining the Army when I grew up. Looking back, I now realize that I was reading the same books that more than two decades earlier inspired numerous young boys to dream of becoming great war heroes --boys who later in their lives would fight and die in the jungles and deltas of Vietnam.

One day, while in the public library, I finally found a book on Vietnam. It was about the massacre at My Lai. As I read the story, my conception of America changed drastically. In fact, my entire conception of humanity changed. I had been told by all of the W.W.II books I read that U.S. servicemen were the good guys -- the men in the white hats rolling into Paris to liberate the French. The Nazis murdered people, not the Americans. Our soldiers were supposed to free villages, not torch them.

But all that disappeared as I read the story of My Lai. My own personal transformation from patriot to disillusioned cynic began with that book -- it began, unbelievably, when I was ten. My own loss of faith in the inherent goodness of America that resulted from that book must have paralleled, in some ways, what America experienced as they watched Vietnam unfold. I finally understood why no one wanted to talk about it. I now understood why some people treated it as four-letter-word.

For me, it took more than a decade for the war that never happened to finally happen. For me Vietnam began and ended in between the covers of that book. In the years since, I have read a great deal more on the Vietnam War, and I have come to understand that My Lai was just one terrible episode in a long and terrible war. There were no winners in Vietnam.

At 26, I am actually relieved to hear the word "Vietnam." It means that as a nation we've matured and we are finally willing to let some of the bitterness go. I think it began with Maya's solemn granite memorial--it reminded us that 57,000 people cannot simply be blotted out of our collective memory because they make us feel uncomfortable. It's about time we all started talking. The silence has lasted far too long.

Matthew Cardwell
