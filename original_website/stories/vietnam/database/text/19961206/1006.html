First let me say that I found the POV on Maya Lin to be very moving. I have visited the Wall twice, and I am always moved by it.

I served in Vietnam twice. First tour was June 1968 to June 1969. During this tour I served in the Interrogation section of the MACV J-2. This assignment took to me every section of the South Vietnam. On one occasion I went to the Hue area to locate a North Vietnamese private who had been captured in the DMZ during a cease fire period. This trip, which was to take me no more than two days, lasted a week as local units, both U.S. and ARVN kept this prisoner from going to Saigon for interrogation. I never did locate the prisoner; but, saw the ARVN aircraft take off from the Phu Bai field which carried the prisoner back to ARVN headquarters in Saigon.

There were many incidents as these in which both South Vietnamese military and our own units worked against each other. During this tour we experienced the Tet Offensive of 1969. This was offensive was not as intense as the Tet Offensive of 1968, but several installations were attacked by the V.C. I was on a vehicle that was attacked early on the morning of February 12, 1969. I was scheduled to meet my wife on February 14 in Honolulu for R&R. After that near miss, I decided to lay low until I could board a plane for Hawaii. I did not want my wife to travel to Honolulu only to learn that I had been killed driving down a street in the Tan Son Nhut area. If that were to happen, I wanted it to be as I was doing something important.

Between tours I was responsible for training Naval Intelligence Liaison Officers, called NILOs. I was an Army officer assigned to the Naval Amphibious School in Coronado, California, where these special Naval personnel were being trained. These men were assigned to Riverine, coastal, and SEAL operations. In the two years at this work I trained 55 Naval officers; I am especially proud of the fact that these men were trained very effectively. My primary hope for them was that they would survive their year. Among the 55 I trained, there was only one casualty.

My second tour was from September 1971 to September 1972. During this tour I was assigned to the Combined Intelligence Center, Vietnam. During this tour I came to know and like the Vietnamese people very much. This Center was combined in that both ARVN and U.S. personnel shared the same space. We occasionally worked together on developing common intelligence reports. One event that stands out in my memory was when were burning classified materials in a larger incinerator. The smoke became very thick. It was then we learned that our facility was neighbors to a Buddhist monastery. We believed we were secure, but suddenly through the smoke stood two Buddhist monks in the saffron robes complaining in Vietnamese that our smoke was interrupting their prayers.

When I left for the tour in 1969, I left behind my wife, six year old daughter, and five-month old son. My wife managed the family during both tours. I tried to stay in touch by regular letter writing, and an occasional telephone call using the MARS system and in my second tour and newly installed satellite telephone system. Once, when talking to my daughter, who was by the middle of my second tour, was nearly eleven, she announced on the satellite link, that she was wearing a training bra. I was pleased for her, but suggested that this is something she might want to tell me in a letter.

My experiences in Vietnam put me in touch with much misery. Especially in my work with prisoners. I thought often about what I found to be truly of value. I saw the V.C., and the North Vietnamese, committed to their cause; but, this commitment did not bring them a full and rewarding life. In fact, I saw as a result of their commitment many innocent people killed. For example, on All Saints' Day, 1968 (November 1), we took incoming rockets and mortars, one rocket hit a church filled with people observing the holy day at an early morning Mass. Many were killed,all wer injured. On our side I saw many officers serving their own career needs, and many of our fine young people being physically and emotionally injured. The result of my introspection was that I wanted a cause I could truly give myself to. This search has led me to find a life in the priesthood of the Episcopal Church. I find that Vietnam opened for me a thought process that took me away from the military career I had expected for myself and to a more rewarding life. My wife and I survived the war with our marriage in tact and with many memories -- some of them good, but many painful.
