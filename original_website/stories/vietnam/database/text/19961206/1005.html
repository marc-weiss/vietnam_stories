I escorted the body of a friend back to the U.S. from Viet Nam--from Nha Trang to Japan to California to Dover, Delaware to Washington, D.C.--by truck by plane by hearst. I hadn't seen him for about a year. When they opened the casket--there he was, silent under glass. I stood by him when friends and family came to the funeral. One person threw up on the floor by the casket. Everybody cried. His father asked me how he died. I could not tell. He had white gloves on his hands. I went back to Viet Nam.

Sometimes I miss an appointment. Sometimes I forget my wife's birthday. Sometimes I start for the kitchen, get half way there and then can't remember why I'm going there. I'll never forget the loading dock--the silver gray casket or the last time I heard my friend's voice on my radio. That was about 20 years ago--that was about some unforgetable mystery. I still don't understand it, but I can't forget it, and the feeling just won't go away.

I'm a philosopher. If I feel this feeling--maybe the feeling understands, but there will always be this gap. There will always be this gap. This space. This failure of time. This loss. This silver gray casket. This loading dock. This sheet of glass. This Viet Nam . . .
