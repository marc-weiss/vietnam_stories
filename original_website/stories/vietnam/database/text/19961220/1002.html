To the creators of this web site—you have offered the public a valuable service and I am most grateful for the chance to visit it both as a reader and contributor.

I am the thirty-year old daughter of Captain Roland C. Labonte, who was killed in action in Vietnam in April 1969. While only two-and-a-half at the time of his death, the Vietnam conflict has had a profound effect on my life. Not a day goes by that I wonder what it would have been like to grow up with a father.

My mother, a woman of great strength and integrity, raised my brother and I alone, while returning to college to earn her degree. She sacrificed her whole life for us and was always determined to ensure that we would grow up with the same opportunities as other children.

The pain that is endured by the families of those killed or missing in action is simply beyond description—their sense of loss is unfathomable.

Recently, the university for which I work sponsored a public lecture by Robert S. McNamara. Prior to the lecture, I had the opportunity to participate in a private gathering with McNamara of researchers and faculty at the institute where I work. McNamara was offering his "vision" of global security in the 21st century. He seemed overly-confident, almost arrogant, when questioned about his sudden conversion regarding the domino theory and its influence on the Johnson Administration's policies toward Vietnam.

Later that evening during the public forum, I sat dumbfounded as McNamara avoided questions on Vietnam and abruptly concluded the question-and-answer period just as two Vietnam veterans approached the microphone.

Melissa (Labonte) Phillips
1966
Providence, RI
