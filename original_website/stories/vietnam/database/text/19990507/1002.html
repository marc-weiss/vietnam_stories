I am not a veteran of the Vietman War, I am a product of it. I was born in April, 1975 when the war ended. Now that I am grown up I realize that my life has always evolved around the war, and I consider myself lucky. My dad enlisted in 1968 at the age of 18 with the notion that if he volunteered he wouldn't have to go to Nam. Following boot camp he was shipped out.

When dad got home he was still a boy, 3 years younger than I am now. Growing up I always looked up to my dad. I was daddy's little girl and we always had a very special relationship. But things were tough, and in time dad changed. He got angry and withdrawn, and I strived harder and harder to reach him. By the age of 12 I had seen every documentary, read every book and talked to him for hours on end about Vietnam. I asked about his experiences, and I now think that he told me too much. I know that my dad saw the enemy face-to-face, and killed as many as possible. I lived my childhood experiencing the war, over and over again. It got to the point that my father would only discuss the war with me, I became his confidant. This became a very heavy weight for me to bare.

To this day my dad is very important to me, probably the most important person in my life. I, now that I am grown up and have learned to deal with the things I know men are sometimes forced to do, understand the man I call dad.

This is a personal experience, but I wonder how many other kids of Vietnam Vets had the same. We are a sort of lost generation, we are special and strong.. I have never been to war, and being female I probably will never. But if I had the opportunity I would be one of the first to enlist. I am a product of the Vietnam Era, and I am proud. I would really like to hear from other kids who are like me, it's funny but growing up I never knew any. We are not all affected by the war, just as some vets are okay. My dad is one of the special ones, he didn't give up two years for his country. He gave up his life and part of mine.

Jodi
