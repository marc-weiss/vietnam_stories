I am honored to be here today and to have been asked, to speak at this special Memorial Day Dedication Service. I must admit though, Neil Block asked me if I would speak at the Vietnam, Dedication Ceremony, I had panicked In fact, my immediate response was, "Oh, no, I don't think I could do that." That was not the response of a person who has been a public speaker for 30 years, that was not the response of a minister who has preached for 18 years- it was the response of a Vietnam Veteran who has not spoken in public about the war since after his return to the states in 1968.

As I thought about the invitation, which by the way, means a lot to me coming from the Veterans of Foreign Wars. I decided perhaps it was time for me to talk about the War and My experience, and the impact it had on our country and my generation.

So, while much of the country today is recognizing the tremendous efforts and sacrifice of those who landed on the beach of Normandy on D day and began the liberation of Europe, I will focus this short talk on the Vietnam War. And I am dedicating it to Neil and to all the other Vietnam Veterans in the audience.

My father and I were not terribly close. While we did find ways to move closer in the last ten years of his life, there were very few things we had in common. One of them was golf. And the other, was that we both served in the Navy during a war. He, in World War II in the Pacific, and me in Vietnam, in the South Pacific. But we never really talked about our experiences until one day, when we were on our way to play golf, we stopped to have breakfast. All of a sudden the conversation became very serious. I told him something I had felt for a long time but had never said out loud. I said, "You are the lucky generation." He looked at me in kind of a very strange way. I went on to tell him that his was the last generation that grew up believing there was a man in the moon - I grew up knowing there had been a man on the moon; His was the last generation that grew up in rural communities untouched by modern technology, unknowing of all that is going on in the world. I grew up in the information age, knowing everything, seeing everything, watching history unfold in front of my well-worn TV eyes; his was the last generation that grew up having the full American dream intact; mine was the generation who saw the American dream tarnished by Watergate and other political corruption. Finally, and this was when it got very serious, I said his was a generation who was able to come home after fighting in a war not only knowing what you did was necessary but everyone around you knew it too. My generation fought in a war we did not understand and when we came home, we were demonstrated against, spit on, yelled at, shunned, and left alone. We had no justification, no heroes, no protection of the heart, no treatment for the wound no one could see. We were left on our own to figure it out by ourselves, and many of us, unable to deal with the reality of what we did and what we saw, did the logical thing to survive - the only thing we could do to survive - we buried it. We buried it in the deep place inside where no one could see, no one could touch, no one could hurt. Some buried it so deep they withdrew from the world around us and have never been able to reenter.

It was one of the most interesting conversations I ever had with my father. He argued with me on every point except the last - and when we got to that point, he looked at me and said, quietly, and simply - "You're right." And there was a very long pause after he said that - neither one of us said anything, and then we both had tears in our eyes. My father, because his son had been hurt and there was nothing he could do about it. And me, I had tears in my eyes because it was the first time I had admitted out loud to someone I had been wounded in 'Nam. It caught us both off guard. It was one of the few times I felt close to my father - I am sure it was because we had shared a deep and significantly personal moment. We ever talked about that conversation again, but I cherish that sharing only veterans can have, with one another. Especially when the veteran was my father.

That is one of the marks of our generation - the hidden wound. It is there, and it is still eating away at us, even today, but we just don't talk about it, because we can't talk about it. Our families are still divided. Our communities are still suffering both losses - the loss of young lives and the loss of innocence. The Gulf War did open up some opportunity for healing, but you can't just undo over 25 years of hurt and pain-it just doesn't go away that easy. Besides, it wasn't there when we really needed it. Most of us don't trust it now - it is too easy for everyone - it isn't about us, it is about a nation that can't own up to what it did. We never had our parade. We never will have our parade.

That is why I am so proud to stand here today to be the voice for those of us who served in this war and haven't been able to put expression to our experience. I will say this, and it is absolutely essential you hear what I say - though some of us may have a hard time discussing our participation in the war, and perhaps even struggle with the purpose and meaning of the war, that in no way lessens our pride in having served our country. We are United States Veterans and we have earned the respect of our fellow citizens - and it is long overdue. Look how long it has taken to acknowledge it publicly in this small community of Huntsville. Think of how many other places along the rural roads of our nation where there are still communities and families torn apart by their losses and families still broken by the causalities of this war - not just physical causalities, but the emotional, spiritual brokenness that goes to the deepest part of who we are as people. This war defined my generation - regardless if you were in the war or against the war - it doesn't matter - it is part of each and every one of us and it will not go away. It is time for healing. It is time to put voice to the pain. It is time to go beyond the wall.

That is why what we are doing today is so important. It gives us the chance to be together as a community - for a common purpose - to dedicate a memorial . It is important to note that this is not a monument - monuments celebrate something - memorials serve as a reminder - an invitation to remember, perhaps, more importantly, not to forget.

I served in the Gulf of Tonkin - I was on staff, Commander Seventh Fleet. While I was not involved in the Vietnam conflict for a lengthy period of time, my experience while I was there left an indelible mark on me. I was a communications yeoman and part of my job was to monitor all communications from the battlefield and re-code it for transmission to the United States. I spent hours at the teletype, reading causality reports - giving graphic descriptions of the wounds or manner of death of U.S. soldiers, and then transmitting them to the states with the address of the family to be notified. I remember those addresses. I always thought about the families, and what it would do to them when they got the telegram. It was a very personal and agonizing way to spend my time. Every day, every shift, it was the same. It seems I spent a good portion of my time on that ship dealing with death.

My second experience was when we entered Danang Harbor early one morning to provide ground support fire. We had a captain of the flag ship who believed we should be a fighting flag ship so we would leave the Gulf of Tonkin, head toward the harbor, and arrive before dawn. I was told that first night when I was on watch that I should go outside a watch if I could. We had five and six inch mounts on the front of the cruiser (USS Providence) and we provided ground support fire for troop cover and advancement. And so I did just that, I got off watch, put on my flak jacket, and I went out on the top deck of the ship, right behind the six inch mounts; It was a powerful experience, an overwhelming sight. I stood there absolutely mesmerized, caught in the recoil burst of flame - the July Fourth envy of any place but there. But then, after a long time of being hypnotized by the power of that powder blast, I happened to be looking at the hillside when one of the shells exploded. I don't know how many died. I don't know who I killed, but I know. I stood there dumbfounded - sick to my stomach, sick at heart. There are very few people who can point to one moment in time and say, this is where I lost my innocence, this is where I was changed. I can - it is indelibly marked for me - burned on the fabric of my soul. It was that moment I saw the shell explode. I believe it probably is for every single person who has ever fought in a war and participate in killing some other human being. There is a kind of "unreality" that marks the spot, and the time, forever. I remember it now as vividly as I did when it happened. I do not think I will ever be able to forget it. That is part of my wound.

My third experience was for two months prior to return to the States. I was temporarily stationed at the US Naval Hospital in Yokuska, Japan, where they brought the air-vac (the battle wounded) from 'Nam for hospital care before return to the states. My job was to interview those people, when they first came to the hospital and get the necessary information for preparation to send them home. I will never forget the devastation I saw - the physical destruction and damage done to vital young men who would never be the same. Eighteen and nineteen year olds who had lost their arms, or legs, or half their face to a mine. Pieces of human beings, some hardly enough intact to make it, let alone live a normal or productive life. Of all the things that happened, I think that has left the greatest mark of all, the deepest wound. And as difficult as all of that was, I was spared the horror and the trauma of the face to face battle in the fields and marshes. And I know that horror is among the greatest horrors of all - and for those of you who had to face that horror, I pray for the healing of the wounds you must be carrying around inside of you.


My final experience was what happened when I returned home to the States. It was early 1968 and the world had been turned upside down while I was gone. The war had become even more unpopular than it was before I left. When I first came back, I still wore my uniform for a short while. When I was in public, people would look at me funny. I couldn't figure out what it was. When I started college and the other students found out I was a Vietnam veteran, it changed the way they related to me - suddenly I was a bad guy. In their eyes, I was part of the war machine. It was, to say the least, a very confusing and difficult time. Not knowing other Vietnam vets with whom I could share my feelings, made it even more difficult. Not having been in land combat also made it easy for me to think I had not been affected that much by my experience in 'Nam. This made it all the more easy for me to let this part of my life become isolated and separated from the rest of who I was. And, to be honest, this is the first time s ince I started college I have spoken in public about my experience. I thank you for the invitation to change this in my life and to reconnect with a very important part of who I am as an individual. By asking me to speak here today on behalf of all veterans, you have made it possible for me to begin to remember what I had worked so hard to forget.
I want to close my speech with a poem I wrote specifically for this dedication. It is a poem that has been stored up in me for years. I started thinking about it ever since I saw the Vietnam Memorial in Washington. I had been to Washington and to the monument twice before, but I could not bring myself to go. Once, I actually went to the Memorial site but I could not bring myself to take that descending walk down into the pit. Finally, a few years later I visited the memorial site again. I want to share my thoughts of this experience. Since this dedication is the result of the Vietnam Memorial Moving Wall, I think it is an appropriate way to conclude this dedication speech and to draw, full circle, our shared experiences as veterans.



The Healing Wall

I

I ignored the Wall -
for a long time
I had managed to keep out unwanted reminders
of the memories of what I saw and did and felt
and the Wall threatened to violate ills, self-truce.
For a while, I refused to go to the Wall.
I came close, but could not bring myself to go down
into that black hole
I stood alone and watched it from a distance -
more than once
the large descending block of black, cold stone,
words carved on the sides, blurred by distance, running together
from my vantage point on the hill -
I saw it but I was not willing to descend.
So I stood from a distance, in the autumn shadows,
hands pocketed, and I turned my back and walked away, speaking
so low even I couldn't hear what I was saying,
"Not today, I cannot do this today."



I visited the Wall.
One evening in the Fall of the year,
when the cool autumn winds blew across the mall and the
early evening sun was crisp, I went to the Wall,
and I stood where I had stood before but refused to go,
And without ever deciding,
I found myself moving toward it,.
pulled by some force I could not name,
drawn by memories I could no longer, hide.
I began the slow descent into the black hole,
not wanting to
but needing to go back into what I had spent
twenty years trying
to forget.

At first tentative, I stood next to the first point of the wall -
looking down the long descent of widening black granite, wanting to turn back,
but I had continued, that time I needed to go.
I walked down the path, looking down,
afraid to look at the Wall -
afraid I would see a name, recognize a name, any name.
Perhaps a name I had seen before, a death I knew before
his family knew. Perhaps a friend, someone who died and
I didn't know.
I couldn't look but I could feel its presence - As I descended, it cast a silent,
growing shadow on me.
As the sun went down, the darkness deepened.
I stopped where the
walk meets in the middle, joining the two parts of the Wall together,
the deepest part of the Memorial
and my eyes began their climb up the wall. Grove by grove, name by name.


I saw what I knew would be there -
names - hundreds and thousands of names
carved into the cold hard flesh of that stone - carved by the trauma
and devastation of the bombs and the mines
and the sniper fire - and the yells and the screams
of young men dying and not knowing why. I heard the haunting,
hollow sound of the death of all of those soldiers whose
names I had seen and passed on to those places back
home who would receive the telegram "We regret to inform you. . .
I saw the names, blurred as they were,
I saw them and I could not move.




I touched the wall - down in the deep
hole where I stood,
I moved forward - not wanting to but needing to
feel it - to touch it,
to trace the edges of the letters of the names -
not to remember, but to forget.

I felt the smooth stone, gingerly at first, with one finger touching the
stone, feeling the contrast between that and the rough place where the
stone was violated by the names carved into it,
like the families and the towns that were violated by the
loss that caused the name to be listed as missing or dead.
And in the stillness of that night
(I remember the stillness, particularly the stillness),

I was alone, as I had been for years.

and then I did what all who go there must do,

I put my whole hand, palm down, against the stone -
first one hand and then the other. softly first.
Then pressing my palm harder against it until
the full weight of my body leaned against it.
Braced by the stone, held up by its quiet, dignified, strength
I became connected to the Wall,
connected into everything that happened, everything I had felt,
everything I had avoided for over twenty five years.

I was no longer leaning against the Wall,
I was becoming part of the Wall, or the Wall part of me.
The more I tried to pull away, the more I couldn't.
It began to pull out of me, emotions I had not felt since I was there,
since that first moment I saw and knew I had been part of someone's death,
reliving the moment of lost innocence, remembering the emptiness,
Feeling the sickness in my heart I had numbed for years.
That is when the first tear came, and then a second, followed by more -
Slow tears, wary tears from deep inside
I became prisoner to its truth, captive of its silent, vigilant, roll call of death.

Then I began to move my hands over the Wall - over names I did not know,
slow at first, and then faster, almost frantically -
at first not knowing why -
but then knowing -
I was looking for one name,
I was looking for the one groove my hands would know the best,
the one that would confirm what I always knew
to be true but was afraid to admit,
a name that wasn't there but should have been-
mine.
It was that realization, that surprise,

when it all rushed in, the horribleness too horrible to remember -
too awful to forget -
When it all came back in on me - overwhelming me,
forcing me to face what I could not accept
The source of my guilt, my one great sin -
I had lived, I had survived. I came back.

I was no more deserving than any one of these names, but I had lived.
My hands connected to the Wall,
I gave it back - and the wall took it, all -
the hurt, the pain, the grief, the guilt, the shame.
Both hands connected, leaning against the wall,
bearing full strength the weight of who I was,
for the very first time since coming home - I cried about the War.
These were not slow tears, they were fast.
I cried for me and I cried for every single name and family
and town that name touched - I leaned against that Wall and
it held me up
and I let it go.

When I was empty, out of tears
the Wall let go of me and I pulled away.
I looked around and saw others who had done or
were doing the same as I had done.
Each of us in our own way, letting go.
We looked at each other - we didn't speak, but we knew
and shared, in silence,
It was there, beyond the wall, where I learned I can stand being alone
as long as I have company.
It was there I learned the power of art to heal.

v

I left the Wall.
I ascended out of that deep, dark hole a different person.
Tired, emotionally exhausted,
I stood there looking back where I had been.
I knew my pain had not magically left me -
I carry it with me today - but I carry it, it no longer carries me.
This was the healing I could not find before -
The Wall told me my name was not there
and said go live your life, you do not belong here.
And so I do, live my life now, beyond the Wall.



As I turned to walk away,
I overheard a small boy, unaware of war and all its tragedy,
ask his father - "Why are there so many names on the wall, Daddy?"
and his Father's soft reply echoes in my heart, even now -
because I knew he too knew the Wall the way I knew the Wall -
he replied with the only answer any of us who have
been there have to give -
"I do not know. I do not know."



Epilogue

To this day I do not know why we have carved so many names on so many walls, but we
have. But what I also know is there are many causalities of war we never see carved on
walls - deaths not recorded by hammered chisel to stone but kept secret, wound unknown
even to those of us who are wounded. So, as we gather together at this and all the other
walls, of war, let us also honor those of us who lived with a silent, vigilant, prayer - a prayer ever on our lips and in our hearts...

No more walls please, no more walls.

Patrick Overton, Ph.D.

Columbia, Missouri
