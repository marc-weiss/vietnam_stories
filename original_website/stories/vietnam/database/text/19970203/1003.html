A Tribute to my Father, Henry Baker


I was born in 1959, the fourth daughter of a World War II veteran who
had served in Burma, among other places, repairing helicopter engines.
I learned later that many of those of his generation that most
vehemently opposed the war in Vietnam knew the terrain and the people of
the Indochina region well enough to understand that our involvement
there could only be disastrous for ourselves, as well as the native
people.

My father was a journalist through the 1960's and 1970's who wrote for
Connecticut newspapers such as the Hartford Courant, the New Haven
Register, and the Middletown Press. I grew up in Old Lyme, Connecticut,
which has become a tiny shoreline town. At the time we lived there,
however, it was a mix of the old established and well-to-do, and middle
and working class families. We qualified for both camps being
economically hard up, but hailing from respected WASP roots. Housing
developments were beginning to spring up full of young families like
ourselves with many children. However, most of our neighbors joined the
more conservative elite of the town and voted Republican, supporting the
war effort, or at least seeming not to oppose it. My father resolutely
began to ask people at the cocktail parties my parents were invited to
what their position on the war was, and if they did not oppose it,
declare an end to all conversation (much to my mother's embarrassment).

My clearest memory of those times was making up a sandwich board for my
father to wear at the time of the Christmas bombings of Cambodia which
read "Stop the Killing' on one side, and "End the Bombing of Cambodia"
on the other. It was my idea to write "Stop the Killing" because one of
a group of singers who had been invited to the White House to sing for
the Nixons had raised a sign saying just that in mid-performance and we
had seen her on the news. My dad spent a rainy cold day or two at the
A&P handing out literature from the American Friends Service Committee
who he was actively working with at the time. The image of him alone,
at the center of a small town which for the most part didn't care, and
didn't want to know, will stay with me always.

I am a mother of three children now living in Westchester County in a
town much like what the one I grew up in has become. Any contribution I
have made toward a better world, derives from his example. I am
currently a member of the Westchester Green Party and look forward to
doing what I can in my community both large and small toward advancing
the causes of social justice, peace, and a clean and safe environment
for my children and other children everywhere.

Heather Baker-Sullivan
