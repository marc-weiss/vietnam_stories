At the time of the killings at Kent State, I was already teaching in the high school where I still teach. A group of students staged a sit-in in the commons area. A number of them were cutting classes in the process. But they were clearly in earnest.

This was at the time when the "hawks" were utilizing the American flag as "their" flag that, so they seemed to want to claim, meant that the owner or displayer of the flag neccessarily was a supporter of the government's Vietnam policy. But I was purposely wearing an American flag lapel pin. (The brother of a friend of mine had his car stopped by the police and had been arrested for having a decal on his car which showed the flag interwoven with a dove.)

While observing the students who were carrying on the sit down, a substitute teacher came over to me and I clearly remember her eyes focused for a moment on my flag pin, said to me "You teachers ought to get the football team out here to push these bums out of the commons." I stunned her with my disagreement, saying these kids are carrying out a peaceful demonstration. Why would you want to start something that's likely to end in someone getting hurt?" She walked away in disgust and - so I imagined cursed me out for "deceiving her" by wearing the pin.

I also remember, probably not long after the son of a school custodian was killed in Vietnam that at a faculty meeting, one or two boys spoke to our faculty about a proposed Vietnam memorial. One of the two speakers was my neighbor. I felt that in the heat of the war, it was inappropriate to put up a memorial. I didn't want to see a partisan one go up and that was what at that moment seemed likely. The faculty was asked to give a vote of confidence on the idea of erecting the memorial. There were only two dissenters among us, a teacher of Spanish and me. Afterwards, my neighbor asked me why I voted against the proposal and I told him. I don't recall his response. But I think his mother who was very hawkish and with whom I remember having a number of Vietnam related arguments, spoke to me later, expressing her "disappointment" in my vote.

The flag issue, above, taught me the power of symbols. I remember that as a Boy Scout I had been proud to carry the flag in a Fourth of July parade. But in later years, as a teacher in New York state, I'm fed up with the phony requirement that every day students must stand up and salute the flag. If you want to take meaning out of something, making people do it daily is a good way.

The second incident remains something I continue to be proud of. Standing against the crowd is not easy to do. But I had a gut feeling that the memorial proposal was wrong at that time. In late years I have taken great satisfaction if that is the word, at the thought of the Vietnam Memorial in Washington, D.D. - which I have only so far seen in pictures or on TV. It's a healing memorial, not one that could continue to divide people.

From Howard Blue -Age 55 Huntington, NY
