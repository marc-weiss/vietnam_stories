Gotten over it

I am 63 years of age, married, two grown children, one granddaughter. I went to Vietnam twice. My first trip was not a very pleasant experience. I can still remember the piles of bags and the stench of their contents. I can still remember the rain and blood mingling and swirling, looking like some art form we used to do at the YMCA when we would put colored oils on water to see what pictures could be made. I can still remember unpleasant smells, pictures and experiences.

I can remember my second trip and the awful realization that the people of Vietnam did not give a damn about democracy and freedom and justice .All they cared about was getting through the day at hand and for the war to end. When it was time for the afternoon siestas, it was time to do nothing. Absolutely nothing. Who cared what the extra effort could have produced. I can remember the ROK troops hunting stray dogs for the stew pot. I can remember Vietnamese officers stealing our supplies, our mail from home, stealing the water pumps we installed for their running water, and on and on. I can remember the Vietnamese officers who spirited their families away to God knows where in stolen T-41s which we supplied to them.

I can remember the plane loads of furniture and supplies brought in just before the Secretary of the Air Force came in to visit the Vietnamese Tech Training Center and how all those supplies disappeared right after his visit.

I can remember that Air Force non-coms were threatened with court-martial for taking pictures of Vietnamese troops building villas for the local generals and colonels, on government time using United States furnished military supplies and equipment. Picture taking was a good way to get sent out of the country early.

And on, and on it went. American soldiers being killed for freedom. American soldiers being killed for what?

But the most important thing is that I can remember and I can also choose not to remember. I can choose to keep it out of my mind and get on with my life. I do not dwell on the past just as most of the veterans do not.

I am repulsed by the people I see walking our streets and sleeping in our parks wearing their "combat gear" and blaming society for the great wrongs inflicted on them. Many "veterans" are not even old enough to have been in the war. For them, it is a charade, preying on the guilt of people. Some are genuine veterans, some of them never having served in Vietnam. Some are veterans of Vietnam and still live in the past, why, I do not know. Perhaps they are happiest where they are, obtaining help and pity from a society they scorn, from people they detest.

My hope is that these people that have suffered from the war will come to the realization that the war is over, that there are organizations and people out there to help them back on their feet if they wish to stand again and that they should stop remembering.

Roland Rocheleau
