Once in that tropical country at the southeast end of Asia, my father had wore it regally, a warrior of a civil war he bravely fought and lost. Once, as a child, I had looked up to a man who had seemed more like a deity, a war hero -- Hadn't I imagined myself as an adult walking in his soldierly footsteps? A particular night emerges from my memories: The cool wind blows through a certain villa, where distant B-52 bomb explosions resound, melding with the monsoon rain to form that strangely soothing rhythm of my childhood lullaby. I am 9-years-old, an army brat living with my family in the imperial city of Hue, near the DMZ. Somewhere in the garden the dogs are barking; my father is coming home. A few army jeeps screech to a halt on the courtyard outside and my father emerges, an entourage of soldiers with M-16s and officers with maps and, army radios trailing behind him. He enters the living room, lifts me up for a warm embrace. His rough uniform emits the smell of sweat, mud, cigar and gunpowder -- what I will always associate with the odor of battlefield. "We won!" he says, laughing. "We won!" But that was a long time ago, of course, and only a battle, and in the aftermath of a defeated war, much has changed. When we set foot on the American shore, history was already against us. Vietnam goes on without us; America goes on without acknowledging us. In America, then, there are no territories for father and son to defend, no civil war to fight. In America the Vietnamese refugee's first self assessment is instead to his own helplessness. It is characterized by blushing, by looking down at one's feet, by avoiding eye contact, and by waiting: on the way for food in the refugee camp; in line for welfare and food stamps, for the free clinic exam, for jackets donated by charity, for green cards. As for the Vietnamese child, he will soon learn that he must face the outside world alone and that to grow up Vietnamese in America is to grow up with the Iegacy of belonging to the loser's side and to endure all,that entails. One morning in the locker room of my junior high, blond and blue-eyed Johnny M., standing gloriously naked, asked, "Refugee boy, which side were you on? The winner's or the loser's?", English still an unpendable language on my tongue, I answered, "Me: Loser's side!" And the room immediately erupted into laughter and I felt Johnny"s wet towel on my face -- the white flag, I supposed, to surrender with. In geography class, Mrs. Collier brought out the new map of Indochina. Vietnam, that country coiling in the voluptuous shape of an S, was no longer mine. It was repainted now in a uniform color - red -- the South flooded with blood. Mrs. Collier didn't know why, exactly, that strange Oriental kid, so quiet, suddenly buried his face in his arms and wept. It was my father's passion I was feeling. At dinner time, after a drink or two he would talk of our homeland and relive the battles that he had fought and won. Napalm fire, helicopters exploding, paratroopers landing, bombs fell from a drunken warrior's fragmented sentences, transforming the dilapidated apartment into a battleground. Did I not hear the wailing voices of Vietcong under fire, see a helicopter burst into flame, smell burnt flesh? What my father does not talk readily about, however, is the last time he wore his uniform. A day after Saigon fell to communist hands my father had commandeered a navy ship full of army officers to the Philippines. Nearing destination, he put away his uniform, changed into a pair of jeans and a T-shirt and threw his Colt 45 into the Pacific Ocean and; along with a hundred or so officers, asked the U.S. authorities for asylum. That image, perhaps more than any other, had haunted me. It spelled the end of my childhood mythology. At 12, I too had already become a bitter veteran of a war I never actually fought. Indeed, I wear no army uniform today; I carry a lap-top and a press card instead. There are several things to notice here. Firstly, you are standing virtually inside it, and all the emotional energy comes from you. And secondly, the names are carved deeply into the polished black stone so that during the day your clear reflection stands behind them; among them. Perhaps that is what the first, early, vocal critics of Maya Lin's design the likes of James Webb and Tom Carhart could not fathom. Or perhaps they were afraid of their own reflections, or the blunt simplicity. At night footlights set along the base throw up a rounded, scalloped glow, as soft as a night light, and the effect is that your reflection appears in front of the names. Which I take to be pretty spooky. The very day the memorial was dedicated another quality was discovered. People began to touch it. It was a physical, spiritual compulsion. Some touch it with their fingers tips. Some lean into it with their fists. Some sweep their hands across it, as if stroking a horse's neck. Some visitors seem to embrace it. Between the panels is space enough to press the stems of flowers, thickly folded notes and cards, dog tags--all manner of memorabilia and remembrances. The custom began the summer of 1982 when a man came to the construction site while the concrete was being poured and dropped his brothers Purple Heart into the mix; his brother a name on the,wall. Enigmatic or obvious.
