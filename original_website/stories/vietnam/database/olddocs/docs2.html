In 1963 I toured S-E Asia on my own after one and a half years in South Korea with the D.O.A. as a Recreation Specialist. The U.S. 'Military Advisors' in South Vietnam numbered 13,000 at that time. The people in all of S-E Asia thirty five years ago were the poorest I had ever seen. The escalation of the war in Vietnam never seemed justifiable. In the sixties my younger brother and I immigrated to Canada in protest of that war. Our family was divided. Scars remain. Some years ago a small replica of the Vietnam memorial was exhibited in Vancouver. I asked an attending U.S.war amputee to check a few names from his list. In deference I remained silent but wanted to ask him about his loss of both limbs. To what avail? I wanted to ask him. To what avail would my intrusion have been? I think of courage when the Vietnam issue is reopened. Courage in terms of the fragile trust held by those vets even years later when they cling to the glory days of their military service. I reflect on the courage, or endurance, of the Vietnamese women, children and elders. I think they are the real heroes of that war.

Stuart Franklin, a photojournalist captured the epitomy of courage in the June 4, 1989 photo of a Chinese civilian standing in front of four or five moving tanks in Tienneman Square. Point of View The Wall is a frozen whisper of names that will forever echo throughout our history. Thanks to Maya Lin, that wall will stand as a monument to the men and women who lost their lives in Vietnam.

Etched in black granite are not only the names of white Americans, but names also of those of Asian, Hispanic, Filipino, Native American, African, and other non-European ancestries. They were all Americans, each life a thread cut short, snipped from the colorful tapestry that was and is America.

As a former Marine of the Vietnam era, I have tried to capture the story of an Asian American combat veteran and the great sin he committed in Vietnam.

"I killed because I was Asian fighting in an Asian war. How else could I prove I was American?" David Wong, protagonist of An American Sin, asks.

The novel explores the issues an Asian American must face when he is asked to kill Asians and how someone of that ancestry may go too far in his desire to be American. Toward the end of the novel, it is at the Vietnam Veterans Memorial where Wong achieves a catharsis.
