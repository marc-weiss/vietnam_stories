A TIME TO HEAL By Jan C. Scruggs, Esq. "...let us strive on to finish the work we are in ... to bind up the nation's wounds." ABRAHAM LINCOLN, Second Inaugural Address. No event with the exception of the War Between The States so divided America as did Vietnam. Massive protests and a bitter national debate raged during America's longest military engagement. The very unity that preserves us as a free nation was torn asunder as that war dragged on. The entire affair ended ingloriously in 1975 as the world watched U.S. Marines rescue Americans from atop the Saigon Embassy. The war's tragic consequences are well known. Over 58,000 Americans died and 300,000 were injured. No yellow ribbons greeted the young soldiers who fought the unpopular war in Vietnam. I began the effort to create The Wall in 1979. Dedication was in November 1982. "The Wall" is now the most visited Memorial in Washington D.C. and is internationally recognized as an American architectural triumph thanks to the efforts of Maya Lin, an Asian American woman who designed the memorial while a college student. The graceful wall of names has helped the nation remember and reflect upon the difficult years of Vietnam. Those who fought the war and those who fought against the war have a Memorial that has helped to bridge a wide gulf. Few who went to Vietnam came back the same and, I suspect that for most veterans, the changes have been useful. Vietnam gave young people an opportunity to experience life and death and to see the best and the worst of human condition. While the war hurt my faith in the 1970's leadership, I have a better understanding of the strength of its people. Living in bunkers and shedding blood with people of all racial and ethnic groups is something that only those who go to war can have, especially in this increasingly balkanized and segregated society. I, for one, feel richer as a human being for the experience. Some of those who missed the experience of fighting for a society which they publicly proclaim is the world's best include Bill Clinton and Newt Gingrich. Had they gone through the ordeal of Vietnam would they be better leaders? You bet! Jan C. Scruggs, Esq. led the effort to create the Vietnam Veterans Memorial -------------------------------------------- Joe Galloway At The Wall this past Veterans Day weekend, at Panel 3-East, I had the great honor and privilege of lifting up Thomas Alexander Rudel, age 4 and 1/2, and introducing him to his grandfather, Capt. Thomas C. Metsker, who was killed on 14 November 1965 on Landing Zone X-Ray, the la Drang Valley, while trying to help another man wounded more badly than himself. Over my shoulder I could see the face of Tom's daughter and young Lex's mother, Karen. She has Tom's eyes and reflected in them was pride in her son and an overwhelming pain for the father who died when she was only 17 months old. Not one of us left Vietnam unchanged; no one left exactly the same as he was on the day he arrived. The war scarred bodies. memories, hearts. Some it made stronger; others it weakened and is still destroying, just as forgotten and corroded old landmines in Indochina still claim the lives of children not born when the mines were planted. Vietnam is indelibly stamped on us. On the second day of the ground war in the Persian Gulf I sat with an Army brigadier general, riding out a desert dust storm and waiting for the next phase of the offensive. He had been a lieutenant in Vietnam. I asked him what Vietnam meant to him. He looked startled for a moment, then replied: "It meant EVERYTHING. It was one of two defining moments in my life. It is the filter through which I see the world; by which I judge people and situations. Isn't it the same for you?" I nodded yes, then almost as an afterthought I asked what that second defining moment was in his life. "Raising my second daughter, who is a Downs Syndrome child," he answered. "I'm a military man and everything we do is a search for perfection. But that child taught me there are other ways to judge and appreciate perfection.'" How fortunate we are, as a nation, are to be served by men such as this, by men whose character was formed and forced in the fires of that terrible war. Joe Galloway is Senior Writer for U.S. News and World Report and co-author of two books: "We Were Soldiers Once...and Young" and "Triumph Without Victory" --------------------- Ann Mills Griffiths, Executive Director November 19, 1996 The Vietnam war altered my life forever when the devastating news was received that my brother, Lt. James B. Mills, USNR, was missing-in-Action. That traumatic event led to near-total immersion in a 30-year quest for answers about America's POW/MIA's that would take me to Vietnam Laos, Cambodia, France and Thailand. Official talks at private dinners with the highest leadership level of Vietnam in New York, Washington and Hanoi became part of my life. This quest would also mean nearly two decades of dialogue at the highest levels of the U.S. Government, meetings with countless Members of Congress and testimony before their respective committees, a SECRET clearance and participation in the official interagency policy process, appearances on all major networks, media interviews and public speaking and awareness events throughout our country. All of this, though totally unexpected, was necessary; I inherited a mantle from my father who preceded me as Executive Director of the National League of POW/MIA Families. Before Jimmy became MIA, I had monitored news about the war since first one brother, then the other, and my brother-in-law were serving in the U.S. Navy. In 1966, during the 7th mission of his second tour, Jimmy disappeared while on an F4B mission over North Vietnam, flying off the USS Coral Sea. He had flown 100+ missions over North Vietnam during his first tour in 1965, flying off the USS MIdway. My brother volunteered to go back; he and his single friends, even knowing the danger, recognizing that others had even more to lose, especially those with young children. The next few years were hard. Was Jimmy alive? Captured and being tortured? Would he survive to come home? Or was he already dead? As the war dragged on and so many lives were lost, I recalled vividly Jimmy's concern about friends and colleagues downed by AAA and SANS, targets that Washington had designated as off-limits during that period. My experience with this real aftermath of the Vietnam War has convinced me that uncertainty is devastating, whether over health, job security, personal relationships or any other important aspect of life. I have seen POW/MIA families' faith in our government shattered by political inattention, deceit, distortions and incompetence. I have also seen their faith renewed by the seriousness and dedication of President Ronald Reagan and others. Conspiracy and cover-up theories are a myth; there are too many good people involved. Besides, the U.S. Government is totally incapable of an EFFECTIVE cover-up. I've endured self-deluded RAMBO activists and con-artists that have preyed upon some families and the public. Other forms of exploitation have occurred and continue. The real manipulation, however has come from Hanoi -- Vietnam's leadership, not the Vietnamese people. Before it was politically correct, the POW/MIA families supported humanitarian aid to the people of Vietnam, Laos and Cambodia, assistance to the disabled, school construction and other projects. We still do. We also support a rational policy to meet Vietnam's political and economic objectives -- as they meet ours in terms of accounting for our missing -- not payment in advance in the naive hope that Vietnam will respond in good faith. Hanoi's record over the years proves my point to objective observers. Vietnam's leadership has enjoyed total continuity through seven U.S. administrations, and they have not been reluctant to exploit those changes, alternating between surges of cooperation and stonewalling. This pattern continues. Ending uncertainty and bringing facts to waiting families and our nation has been my mission for nearly three decades, over 18 years of which were as Executive Director of the National League of POW/MIA Families. We simply want answers that, according to senior U.S. Government officials over many years, could readily be provided on hundreds of missing Americans if the Vietnamese leadership makes the decision to cooperate seriously. To date, Hanoi has not demonstrated good faith, much less met President Clinton's certification to Congress that they are "cooperating in full faith." One of my greatest frustrations has comes from uninformed people who, in the name of "healing" and "putting the past behind," seem to believe that facts, principles and justice for those who serve can be ignored. It appears that in the current environment, commitments can be summarily dismissed to purse economic and political objectives, even when answers are being deliberately withheld as is the case with Vietnam. And, despite the smaller number of Vietnam War missing compared to other wars -- it is very different -- the U.S. was not the "victor," had no access to the battlefield and faced a national Vietnamese policy of exploiting the issue for their political and economic objectives. We waited impatiently as president Carter granted amnesty to those who avoided the draft or deserted, asking anyone who would listen, "What about us? What about unreturned US POWs and other still missing who were last known alive?" Answers didn't come. Oh, there were passive excuses for policy developed by then Assistant Secretary of State Richard Holbrooke. His solution? He told us, "Accounting for America's POW/MIA's is the "hoped-for by-product" of normalization of relations with Vietnam." That naive assumption failed in the late 1970's, and it isn't working now. My experiences related to the Vietnam War were not sought, but were instructive, educational and strengthening. Much of what I have learned has been eye-opening and not necessarily positive, but my appreciation for those who serve our nation has also increased, and there are many ways to meet that obligation. End Ann Mills Griffiths is Executive Director of the NATIONAL LEAGUE OF FAMILIES OF AMERICAN PRISONERS AND MISSING IN SOUTHEAST ASIA
