Sam

Sam liked to walk point. Sam was as black as a starless jungle night. Sam hated the Vietnamese. Sam was good at sniffing out caches of enemy supplies. But most specially, Sam was loyal to his friends.

A Co., 2nd Battalion, 35th Infantry had been moving through mountainous triple-canopy jungle for 2 or 3 days, using a P.O.W. as a guide to a V.C. base camp. On the 2nd day, he escaped. On the 3rd, we stumbled into a battalion size V.C. base camp, which had just been vacated. the food was still warm. Third platoon was in the lead with Sam at point as usual. As they were pacing through the trees, Sam gave an alert and the 3rd hit the ground. Almost simultaneously, a V.C. machine gun opened fire. Very quickly, I was moving forward to answer the call for a medic. Suppressing fire caused the machine gunner to vanish into the jungle and I was able to treat our two casualties - Sam and the 3rd Platoon leader. Both wounds were in the shoulder area towards the back of the neck. Both were medivaced to the nearest hospital and were able to return to their units in less than three weeks. Without Sam's alertness and warning, the number of casualties would have been close to thirty with a majority of the 3rd Platoon lifted as KIA.

Sam was killed later on at enemy hands, but his name is not on the Wall. Although when I look, I think I shall see it. Sam was medium sized black dog who was found half starved in an abandoned bunker by a member of the 3rd. He made himself a member of the 3rd - loyal unto death.

Dogs don't get their names put on walls.

H & I

Every night at L.Z. Dragon, the artillery fired H. & I. (harassment and interdiction). 155 mm howirzers were fired at irregular intervals throughout the night at pre-selected map coordinates. Likely spots were trail junctions, river crossings, or anywhere someone thought there might be a V.C. concentration. So the 155s would fire, the ground would shake and the troops would feel safe.

Then one morning a patrol carried in a seven year old boy. He only had one leg left. The other side was a bloody shattered stump. He didn't cry. He just stared. I'm sure he received excellent care (for awhile).

Today it would be called collateral damage.


Just A Thought

The power felt by a soldier in Viet Nam is truly awesome. Strolling through the villas and jungle, he was a king among kings. He would be carrying an M-16, an efficient and effective killing machine. On his belt and web gear would be grenades and beau coup ammo. He would be surrounded by a number of similarly armed strong young men, some of whom would be carrying machine guns, others grenade launchers and the most powerful weapon of all the PRC-25 - the radio. With it , he could call upon the full power of mighty America and rain death and destruction from the sky at his direction and discretion. Unbelievably powerful, yet infinitely vulnerable.

1/9/68

On 1/9/68, I was sitting in a Huey with a Lt. I didn't know - a pale young officer wearing the traditional G.I. issued black plastic frame glasses, like I was. Over the prop noise and the wind, he told me about life in California, his wife and two little daughters - one of whom he had never seen. They were little angels in the picture.

Then we landed. A bare hilltop. Incoming mortars. Incoming rockets. A little while later, I grabbed my aid bag and was heading toward a man laying in the dust next to a burning chopper. Besides being covered with hundreds of tiny shrapnel wounds, his left leg was a shredded pulp. But more importantly, there was a small hole in his chest spouting bright red life out of his body like a geyser. Three of us tried, but death won. It wasn't till after what was left in the body bag was added to a growing pile that I realized that it was the same California Lt., who would never see his little girl.

1/10/68

We were set down on a newly formed base which had been under fire for some time. Sandbags continued to be added to bunkers till total darkness stopped everything but incoming mortars and rockets. There were two of us in the bunker. I was the medic. The night was blacker than hell, highlighted by the glare of incoming rockets. Around two A.M. the front wall of the bunker exploded into nothingness. Instantaneously, I became what felt like pure awareness. I was very aware of floating over my body in the darkness. I told some one I wasn't ready to die. Then there was pain - severe, intense, unbearable pain. I was awake and lying in a hole full of muddy, bloody water. I felt for were my right leg used to be. My fingers pushed through a slimy mass of chopped meat and splintered bone. Then I realized the other man was in a similar situation - both of his legs were gone below the knee. I ripped open some bandages and put tourniquets on both of us. Then gave him a shot of morphine. I was scared shitless of not waking up if I took one. We both screamed for help till small arms fire started hitting close. I can remember somebody behind us - putting out covering fire with a blooper. Then I can remember feeling really pissed off - I could hear several calls for "medic" and "Doc" coming from the darkness and I couldn't do anything about it. About a million years later, some one pulled us from the hole. I was immediately consumed with a totally intense white hot blaze of pain. Then Nothing. Some infinite time later, I was in another bunker. There was some awareness of bandaging and getting a shot. I remember asking if it was OK to pass out which I immediately did

Then there were dream images of being carried, the whump-whump of helicopter blades, cold-sweating fear, and strange lights.

Three days later, I awoke with some awareness of having received Last Rites. I awoke to a very strange sensation - the feel of crisp, cool, clean sheets. I enjoyed this for some time and then finally opened my eyes. At the other end of the Quonset hut was a real, round-eye American woman (busy at some task). I just lay quietly and watched. Eventually, I could feel pain intensifying in my right leg. I knew it couldn't be there, but I could feel it. It was still too soon, I began to survey my other injuries - two sets of stitches in my right arm, two sets of stitches in my left arm, stitches in my chin and eyebrow, my hands and face peppered with bits of dirt, sand and steel. I lay and watched for awhile longer. Enjoyed feeling clean. Enjoyed watching a real woman.

Finally, it was time . I reached under the sheet.

I was four weeks in Tachikawa hospital in Japan - generally stoned out of my skull on morphine and Demerol, a unique and remarkable drug. It made the pain tiny and far away. Time would be distorted and the mind could travel some unusual roads.

Listening to Hoss and Little Joe talking in Japanese on TV was unforgettable.

There was a very long distance phone call home to my dad but between the sleeping pill and the Demerol I don't remember a thing I said.

One morning about 0200 hours, I was in a lot of pain and the corpsman tried to give me a Darvon instead of my pain shot. I tried to explain that I had been having trouble swallowing the large capsules, but he was insistent. So I tried and threw up on him. I rather enjoyed that, and I did get my pain shot.

Another painful, yet fulfilling moment occurred later in my stay. Eventually, I was able to disconnect my skin traction long enough to work my way into a wheelchair and sit by the bed while the sheets were changed. Of course, that still left 23 hours and 37 minutes of every day in bed with iron weights pulling on my stump. The urinals and bed pans were becoming a major downer, when that bright a glorious day came. I stayed in the wheelchair and was wheeled across the ward to the latrine. Using the rails and walls, I wore myself out maneuvering from one seat to another and it hurt like hell. However, it was the bowel movement of a lifetime on a real porcelain toilet that I could really flush.

It was the end of a long hot search and destroy mission. Almost time to call the choppers and fly home. The Lt. and I found an unexploded 500 pound while the LZ was being secured. We called it in on the PRC-25 and the Lt. decided to blow it himself. The chopper couldn't come in till the bomb was destroyed. So he set the charge and we hauled ass. Nothing happened. Ten minutes, nothing happened. And the choppers wouldn't come till it was blown. Still nothing happened and a night in the jungle was looming as a deadly possibility. A demolition's man came up to fix things. Of course some one {good old "Doc"} had to show him the way to the 500 pound rigged with plastic explosives and ready to blow. The Lt. had forgotten to crimp the fuse and it had fallen out. The choppers were able to extract us before dark, but no thanks to Lt. Asshole.

It was 24 hours I still have trouble with. The basic facts are simple. We had been on a company sized search and destroy mission in mountainous, triple-canopy jungle. The previous day, we discovered a large, recently used base camp and a waiting ambush discovered us. Movement was much slower and difficult than anyone expected. The point man had to be changed every 10 to 15 minutes - when his arm could no longer swing a machete through the thick walls of undergrowth. The heat, as usual, was stultifying and our lungs were seared. Of course, the battalion exec flying overhead in his freshly starched and pressed fatigues couldn't understand our lack of forward progress. At one point, the only way we could make any progress at all was to us the roots and rocks to push and pull our way up a very, steep, rocky stream bed. After a mere eternity of sweat, lung-searing heat, aching muscles, and a creeping numbness in each brain, it eventually became clear to everyone including the exec in his chopper that we weren't going to reach our objective before dark. So we finally set about cutting a night defensive perimeter out of the jungle. We were soon engulfed in clouds and blackness, and the jungle was still much too close. There was not nearly enough space for a good defensive position with adequate fields of fire. Our location was not exactly a secret, if anyone had been listening. The fear closed in with the blackness and clouds. There was a lot of prayer that night from men who weren't sure of morning. If there had been an attack, we would have been a bunch of sad, dead motherfuckers. A re supply of ammo and food was attempted, but the weather and a total lack of visibility were too much for the pilots who had to turn their birds around. Except for one who was beau coup brave and beau coup dinky-dau. He tried to come in with our badly needed supplies and flew into the side of the mountain. The explosion lit up the night for quite awhile. So we ate what food we had been hoarding. And when it was time I slept - despite the fear, despite the hunger but mostly despite the charred remains of courageous men that lay so close. It wasn't time to think then. That would come much later.

The next morning, we came back to life stiff, sore and wet. The leaves were dripping and there was still a cool dampness in the air that would soon become a smothering heat. The air still smelled fresh and sweet. A squad was detailed the remains of the chopper while we settled down to wait for a Shithook (flying crane) to haul it away.

A short while later , they captured "Charlie", a real live Viet Cong. Our prisoner turned out to be a 14 year o;d kid who had been conscripted by the V.C. when the passed through his village some several weeks before. Our prisoner had apparently not been given a weapon , but was used as mule to carry rice and other supplies. When he came down with a fever, he was left in the jungle. Several days later, he stumbled into our hands. Of course, this wasn't nearly enough information for our military.

I had been checking the physical condition of my platoon while I had the chance after spending several days in the jungle. When I looked around, our prisoner was being held to the ground by 3 or 4 large American G.I.s. Next to him, on one knee was our C.O. with a cocked .45 pressed to the kid's head. " What the fuck do I do now! I'm a medic. I'm supposed to save lives. " These guys were ready and willing to blow this kid's brains out. " but we're the good guys, Americans don't do things like that." While I was trying to convince myself it was a bluff, a chopper appeared to take our prisoner to a formal interrogation by the ARVN. I didn't know then that meant he would be tortured and/or killed, or converted into a proper V.C. guerrilla.

I spent many days and many more nights trying to figure out what happened and convince myself that the .45 would not have been fired.

Some years later while watching the news, I was reminded of that day in Quang Ngai Province in 1967. That was several months before Lt. Calley paid his visit to the village of My Lai in Quang Ngai Province.

Reflecting, now, I wonder how much that day in 1967 has affected the past 20+ years of my life.


PTSD

Postraumatic stress disorder consists of a set of characteristic symptoms following a psychologically distressing event that is outside the range of usual human experience.

Outside the range of usual human experience -- watching friends get blown apart; watching kids have parts of their bodies blown off and shattered; picking up bits and pieces for a body bag; living for months on intimate terms with death -- your enemies', your buddies', your own; having your own body shattered and blown apart; trying to hold together blood, guts and life. Every man or woman who has been in combat or worked to clean up the results has suffered from PTSD. That includes the World War II vets, Korean War vets ,Vietnamese survivors, and the Viet Nam vet. The onset can be anytime from weeks to years and it lasts a lifetime. For the Viet Nam vet, it is compounded and exists on a continuum. The casualties of Viet Nam begin with 58,000+ names on a black Wall in Washington, 10,000 graves at Trung Son Cemetary, the amputees still at Thuan Thanh Institute, 120,000+ suicides by Viet Nam vets, 67,000 Viet Nam vets in jail but don't end with the many, many apparently well-adjusted "normal" vets making a continuing contribution to society.

There are numerous vets, myself included, whose friends, family, co-workers, etc. would probably be amazed to know that their vet was having any symptoms of PTSD. With the exception of perhaps 2 or 3 people, I doubt if anyone realized I was having any problems. Myself included.

It took several years and a gradually dawning realization to make me aware. The 6 months in the hospital and the first few months at home were kind of a limbo time. I suppose I was too involved with physical recovery. Then I can remember crying myself to sleep. Frequently, for no obvious reason. More often, it was lying in the dark re-experiencing death and trying to figure out what else I could have done. Healthy young men shouldn't just lay there and die. Then there were the dreams. For the first few years, they were recreations of a variety of bloody and deadly scenes. Then later they began to change. They became episodes of being sent back to Nam, back to the boonies, back to combat. But, now it was with my wooden leg. There were uncounted nights of trying to pull that wooden and metal contraption through the sucking muck of endless rice paddies or climbing the steep trails of mountainous jungles. But the worst part was hearing the sound of gunfire and the call for "medic" or "Doc", and not being able to do anything because I only had one leg left.

Then some nights there was simply blackness -- no dreams, no memories. This could be the worst. On nights like this, I would awake in cold sweating terror -- really indescribable. Heart and pulses would be pounding; adrenaline pumping palpably through every artery; a real need to run, but nowhere to run, nothing to run from, and no ability left to run. Total absolute terror with nothing to point at and be afraid of. These were the nights I sat up trying to read, trying to watch TV until I was too exhausted not to sleep.

Then there were other nights after my kids were born. These were the nights I moved around in the dark checking security. I would lay awake planning defenses. Reviewing probable avenues of approach to my property, planning fields of fire, determining the best defensive positions. I was maintaining the security of my perimeter. And for many of those years, there was a revolver under my mattress. I still don't feel comfortable sleeping without a weapon close at hand.

But I got up every morning, went to work and took care of business. So, no problem, right?

Then there was the night of 4 July 1991. I went to a Pops Concert, mainly to listen to the 1812 Overture with cannon. It was the first of July celebration I can remember attending since before 1978. On the drive to the concert, I was building up a lot of anger and had a strong urge to destroy something. When I arrived amidst all the trappings, flags and banners, I became very anxious and nervous. From my seat, I could look out past the stage across a green river valley at a series of interlocking hills. Towards the middle of one hill was a small dark area which I couldn't identify from my position. It looked as though it could be the mouth of a small cave and it made me very nervous. In 1967 near a town called Duc Pho was a hill known as L.Z. Dragon. From that hill, I once looked out across a valley at a series of interlocking hills. One day near the middle of of one of the hills, we realized that a small dark area had appeared. It couldn't be readily identified from our position, but it made everyone nervous because it wasn't supposed to be there. On that day, an artillery barrage was called in to relieve everyone's nervousness. Meanwhile in 1991, I could only sit looking across a valley with my anxiety growing; with my right hand remembering the feel of the handset of a PRC - 25; with my throat trying to form the words, "This is Alpha Mike. We have a suspected enemy position........"

The most well known symptoms of PTSD are drug and alcohol abuse. Of course, I didn't mess with illegal drugs, drink beer or have more than a few social drinks. I didn't need any of that. I left Valley Forge Hospital with an open-ended prescription for Darvon Compound #65 - a large pink and gray capsule - the wonder drug of Viet Nam. Just tell old "Doc" you've got a headache or a pulled muscle, and collect a few of the magic capsules for your stash. Then when the need arises, wash a few down with a couple of beers and get stoned off your ass. When I first came home the pain was real and considerable. Even now, it is still there at times. And that bottle of pills stayed in my pocket for several years. I took several per day for I don't know how many years. The good old V.A. kept me supplied - every month they mailed me a new bottle. Then for full effect, I would have a glass of wine in the evening to relax - very civilized. Did I mention my wine glass was 8 oz. And sometimes, I even slept.

Finally, after I don't remember how many years, I decided not to refill the prescription. Still not sure how or why I did it. The wine lasted longer, but eventually it was stopped, too. Now, I take blood pressure pills daily and follow a low salt, low fat diet. However, if there a bottle of Darvon 65 or Demerol sitting in front of me, even now, I would very quickly find some very good reasons to pop a few.

But I still got up every morning, went to work, took care of my kids, and generally took care of business. So, no problem, right?

Wrong. There were anniversary reactions. These would normally include depression, anxiety, fear and some sort of re-creation or re-living of traumatic events - flashbacks, dreams etc. My anniversary starts on Christmas eve and runs through the middle of January. The first 2 or 3 years, I don't remember much of a reaction. Since then, its never gone away. The anxiety and depression would start early Christmas week because I knew what would be coming. Each night I would lay awake and re-play in my mind what happened on those days and nights in 1967 and 1968. Christmas eve was getting drunk and firing illumination rounds. Christmas Day was mass ,an Irish priest, and Irish whiskey. The day after Christmas was doing CPR on a blond boy full of bullet holes and losing the fight for his life!.......1/8/68 was watching an ear being packed in salt in a baggie. 1/9/68 was a major move and losing again. 1/10/68 was getting my fucking leg blown off, but I did save the guy who was with me. Frequently, these nights would require getting up at 1:00 or 2:00 in the A.M. Later, after becoming a social worker, I learned about anniversary reactions and eventually created my own plan for dealing with this recurring haunting. First, I set aside a specific time each day to "remember" that days events. Then on one day around January 10 I would "celebrate my anniversary". Usually, it was going out for a special dinner or concert, etc. Once, it was to see "Born ON the 4th of July". So, for the past 15 years or so, I have been celebrating my anniversary with either one or both of 2 people who love me deeply and whom I trust implicitly. They are my closest friends. I hope that someday they will both understand what they have done for me. My son is also beginning to be involved. He placed a copy of these pages at the Wall on his freshman trip to Washington.

Post-traumatic Stress Disorder is what you do to survive the, the events, the insanity. When that "event that is outside the range of human experience" is happening, it must be subdued, chained and locked away deep inside so you can survive and continue to function. Then later, it has to be dealt with in some way every day for the rest of your life.
