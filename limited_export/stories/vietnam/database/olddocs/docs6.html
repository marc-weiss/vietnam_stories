Twenty years ago, I sat in a hearing room very similar to this one we are in today. I was there to testify about the continuing bloodshed In Vietnam. Among the other veterans who were sitting at the table with me was an angry but eloquent young man, dressed like the rest of us, in field jacket and jeans. I don't remember all of what he said but one sentence has remained with me.

"We are here to ask," he said, "and we are here to ask vehemently where are the leaders of our country? Where is the leadership?"

The veteran who asked those questions, who spoke so compellingly of the cost of war, is one of our leaders today. He is John Kerry, the Senator from Massachusetts. His voice has been one of those asking for restraint, speaking for moderation and voting to allow the sanctions time to work before rushing into what could be a costly and disastrous war. I commend John for this and I would like to add my own voice in a more personal way.

I was the military advisor for Trang Bang District, Tay Ninh Province, Vietnam, in late 1968 and early 1969. Trang Bang is located an the parrot's beak area of the Cambodian border. It was a very active sector for the NVA and the South Vietnamese militia that I advised suffered very heavy casualties. After weeks and months I became very familiar with death and dying but, feeling as invincible as any 23 year old, my own mortality remained a distant matter.

Then, early in the morning of April 26th, I had an incredibly close encounter with my own death. Approaching the village of Tan An,, my two point men were knocked to the ground from the blast of an 82MM mortar. After a few minutes, I moved forward cautiously to check the extent of their injuries and administer whatever aid I could. The Vietnamese did not have their own medic. About 40 feet from them I paused to take a real good look around. At the moment I stopped, I heard the click of a detonator less than a meter from where I stood. An often happens in those situations, I never heard the explosion of the chicom mortar round. My next recollection was of being rather high in the air as the exquisitely blue sky spun around me. When I returned to earth, I landed on my back. For a moment I thought that I had only had the breath knocked out of me but I seemed to be having a big problem turning over to reach for my weapon. it was still there but its plastic stock was gone and only the barrel and receiver group remained. Being a fast thinker, I realized that the situation was not good. I started to take a close look at my body. In a way, I wished I had not. Without going into greater detail I would have to describe the lower part of my person as shredded. In addition my right arm was bleeding from several places and I had blood in my right eye. My thoughts remained amazingly clear- I needed a radio to call a chopper and get to a hospital very quickly.

Somehow, I'm not really sure of those details, this was accomplished. Waiting for the med-evac, I realized I was dying. I can tell you this angered me a great deal. I was not frightened, I was not sad or contrite. I was very, very angry. And it was not anger directed at whomever had perpetrated this crafty ambush,(l had pulled the trigger too many times and directed too many airstrikes to see this as anything other than payback time), I was angry with a world that was slipping away from me, at a world where these things happened, at a world where war was commonplace and you could be a strong young man one moment and very dead the next.

The only thing that intruded an this thought was an incredibly intense pain. Somehow, through the pain and the anger I heard the whumpa-whumpa of the chopper. About 35 minutes after I was wounded I was at the 12th Evac Hospital in Cu Chi. By the time they got me to triage I was conscious but my lungs were shutting down and tunnel vision was setting in- death is not subtle. Unbelievably, I thought at the time, the nursed wanted to know my home address and telephone number. My last conscious words were an awful obscenity about the intelligence of the US Army in general. Ironically, it was addressed at one of the people who saved my life. I still feel the same way about the Army but I sincerely apologize to that wonderful, patient woman who really knew how to get that last breath out of an American soldier. I am sure that there are nurses like her in the Gulf now. My fear is that men wounded as I was, are supposedly to be evacuated by land rather than air. I can tell you with great certainty that I would not be here today if there hadn't been air-evac 22 years ago. I hope this consideration is taken into account by our leaders today.

I'd like to digress for a moment to make a point. several weeks ago there was a story in the New York Times from their white House correspondent. It was about the crisis in the Gulf of course, and my eye was drawn to one of the sections which was titled, "Nightmare Scenario". I expected to read about an American casualty estimate or, possibly, civilian casualties. I was sadly mistaken. A senior Administration official was commenting on the problems the Administration would face if Saddam were to stage a partial withdrawal from Kuwait. What would be their response, the story went, to such a diplomatic "preemptory strike"?

I ask you is this a nightmare scenario...? I'll give you a nightmare scenario- Try waking up in a hospital four days after you are wounded. You think you might be in Japan but you are not sure. Half the time you are not I even sure you are alive - you have only the pain to say you are. The war has been going on for five years but there aren't enough beds, medics, doctors or nurses. To get one x-ray can take 4 hours. You wait 18 hours without liquids or medication for surgery that might, or might not happen. Your family doesn't know where you are or even it you are still alive because the Army sends then one telegram to say you are critically wounded, then forgets about it for 3 weeks.

Now folks, I know a nightmare and that's a nightmare. Will this be the story in the Gulf, in a war of higher intensity and with less time to prepare? We are assured by our leaders that it won't but the evidence says otherwise.

And I would like to remind you if you are not already reminded by our presence in this room today that this war, this War for the Liberation of Kuwait, which so many people seem so anxious to fight, will not be over in a week, no matter what the Armed Services Committee experts have to say. Nor will it be over in 10 weeks or 10 years. For those men and women of this nightmare to come, and their families, and the families of those who become names on a wall, it will go on and on and on. And you, our leaders, will be gone, the newspaper and television people gone, everybody having moved on to new matters. But they will remain - alone with their pain and their loss.

To bring this to a close, I would like to tell you something that I consider very personal, - something that I thought I would never talk about in public. It addresses the reason why I am here today.

About 22 years ago, I woke up in that hospital ward in Takoda, Japan. I was one of many young men. Most, like me, would go home to families and loved ones devastated by the horror of what they would have to confront; a few had only hours to live, their families to be devastated by that loss. I can't begin to tell you of the pain to which I awoke. It was utterly overwhelming- pain within me,, despair all around me. If my words today could somehow in the future, take one moment of such pain away from one soldier, I would feel like I had accomplished a great deal.

In my final words I would like to remind our leaders that wars do not end. They live on in the people that are touched by them. A war in the Gulf would touch millions of people and make no take about it, this will be your war.

In December of 1994 I went back to Vietnam; among my hosts there was the Vietnamese novelist Le Minh Khue, a woman who had been in the North Vietnamese Army during the war. The kids in her unit (like us, they were all teenagers) repaired bomb damage to the Ho Chi Minh Trail, filling in craters, defusing or exploding unexploded bombs. She did that work for four years, often under attack from American aircraft. As a helicopter crewman, I had sometimes flown over the area where she was, and sometimes fired down into the jungle that canopied the Trail. We had both become writers after the war, and friends in 1993, and we worked together editing an anthology, The Other Side of Heaven, containing stories by Vietnamese and American writers about the aftereffects of the war. In October of 1995, Khue came to the states with another writer, Ho Anh Thai, to help launch the book. We crisscrossed the country, an ex-marine traveling with an ex-NVA, trying to cut new trails away from the tangled myths of our pasts, just as we had when Khue walked me through her streets in Hanoi. In Washington, we went to the Vietnam Memorial. I took Khue and Thai down to the two panels where members of my squadron are listed and pointed out a name I always visit, Jim Childers, a boy who'd died in my place when we'd switched missions during the last week of our tour. Khue seemed moved, but she remained silent. I wondered, looking at her, if the names meant more to her now that she'd met us, or if she was still thinking of her own names, the kids who died next to her on the Trail when we were all kids, locked together in hatred. I wondered if she was thinking of the millions of dead and the hundreds of thousands of missing the Vietnamese still have, and how unequal the numbers are, and I thought how in the end it didn't matter, there was enough pain on both sides to allow connection and forgiveness. "We've all suffered," Khue said, reading my mind.

Look who I brought to you, I thought, as I touched my friend's name on the Wall. And I thought: Rest in peace, Jim.

Always, when I write, no matter what the subject, I go back in time to the little town in south Arkansas where I was raised, or I go to Vietnam. From those two places I take my compass reading, get my bearings, look into life. Here is a story I first told in an essay called "Leadership." At the time of the story, Al Lesniac was a young captain, a field artillery battery commander somewhere around Tay Ninh. I was the battalion S-3.

On a hot day long before the night attack, I was greeted by Lesniac's chief of firing battery. "Sir-, let me show you something.," he said as we walked away from the chopper. He led me over to one of his gun sections and took me below ground into a full-sized room. The gun chief and his men had dug the hole with the help of a front loader, lined it with the wood they'd salvaged from ammunition boxes, and inside the lined walls, they'd built book cases and stocked them with paperbacks sent by the special-service folks. The soldiers had also built a writing desk. There in Vietnam, in the middle of nowhere, in a hole in the ground, they had created for themselves a place fit to live in, knowing full well that the next morning they might have to tear it all apart, burn the wood, and move to a new position. They wanted to live every minute they could, and they wanted to last. Lesniac had given them reason to believe they could. It came as no surprise to me that at that firebase during a subsequent ground attack, those men did something remarkable, did it willingly, did it almost automatically, as if they were merely the instruments of their own salvation.

Lesniac had been a soldier before he had been an officer, and he never forgot what it felt like to receive orders, Yet when it came time to give orders, he never forgot that he had become an officer. Unlike many of our seniors in Vietnam, Lesniac knew his job, and he knew his men.

***

Vietnam is what happens when human beings--men and women, American soldiers--become as expendable as bullets, when those in charge lose sight of young men in the middle of a rice paddy in a faraway land sitting around an underground section room, reminding themselves of home. What happens when managers--at the highest level of government, or in the Pentagon, or even in the corporate boardrooms--lose sight of people is quite simple. They no longer see the far-reaching consequences of their actions. Having severed their ties with the human spirit, they no longer know how to "endure danger and ingratitude and answer a lie with the truth." They no longer know what it is to work with others and feel within their own breast a corresponding spirit of respect . They have forgotten the sacred lessons of leadership... or, perhaps, they never learned those lessons in the first place. Perhaps they were too busy memorizing the maxims, too busy climbing the ladder. What is worse, they seem to know nothing of the legacies they leave behind, out of touch as they are with the world of the spirit.

I remember one nine-year-old boy, all alone, whom the surgeons wanted to know more about before they put him under. "You're okay here, " I said in Vietnamese. "'The doctors will help you." But the boy-naked, held down by our hands, thrashing in his pee on the steel table, eyes wild with fear-kept crying,, "Dau! -Dau!"' ("Hurt! Hurt!"). His tiny hip had been slashed by shrapnel. He gripped my hand fiercely and kept screaming no matter what I said. I continued to try and calm him until the anesthesiologist said, "Forget it. His ears are probably blown. "

When the napalm cases started coming in, one of, the surgeons-all of whom had labored for hours in the intense heat without a break-sat down for a minute as his knees went wobbly. I remember one napalmed woman wrapped mummylike in yellow gauze, her face swollen horribly, thrashing on the floor next to her beautiful, chubby, naked, tanned infant daughter with gold earrings who was per- fectly whole except for one arm burned black. The child was lying quietly on an issue of the military's Stars and Stripes newspaper whose R&R ad said, under the ruined arm, "Expand your ego. Visit the exotic East."

There was a teenaged boy, a suspected Viet Cong, who gripped my forearm harder and harder as a nurse and I dressed his hopelessly gaping wounds and his burst bladder leaked through the fist-size hole in his right groin. There was the wife of Mr. Long, my village neighbor from three houses down, now deaf and crazy with slivers of shrapnel in her head; she had looked out during a lull in the mortar barrage just as one exploded in her front yard. Mr. Long, who had worked at the hospital years before, now begged me to get morphine and a catheter for his wife, saying he would apply them himself, just please help because I was an American and could.... There was an elderly cou- ple- the man with a wispy white goatee; his wife with white, yellowing hair tied up in a neat bun-whom I wheeled up to the operating room together on one gurney. She went in first; he was next. Although he had a hole through his hand the size of a Ping-Pong ball, he asked for local anesthesia so he could tend his wife and guard their handkerchief stash of savings. There was a wounded mother and her two wounded children; despite her own pain, she comforted her children continually and told them not to cry as I dressed their ghastly wounds. The littler toddler couldn't help it and shrieked, "May bay" ("Air- plane") again and again until I was ashamed to be alive, to be human let alone to be an American, to, be one of those who had brought the planes to this sad little country ten thousand miles away.

***

What collective karma, what shared fate, could Ong Troi have been weighing to have judged these innocent people so harshly? An elderly intellectual once told me that the war was the Vietnamese punishment for their destruc- tion of the Khmer and Champa kingdoms centuries before. But for me, after Tet, no philosophy or ideology - least of all, crusading American democracy - could justify or even remotely explain the slaughter of those civilians. From that day on, I had no facts or beliefs except for what had been done to those people.

At evening when I had a break, I recorded these events in my notebook as if it were a duty, as if having an account of the horrors could somehow mitigate them, keep them from consuming me, and bear them witness. But for twenty years now, I have kept these notebooks sealed shut and it is clear why: their contents are unbearable, and no testimony will ever help those victims. Now, forty-five years old, as maple seeds spiral past my attic window, I weep as I write this. Weep for Mr. Long, whose young wife went blind and crazy before she died a slow death with a head full of slivers, weep for the deafened boy who suffered alone without his parents, weep for the beautiful little girl who would lose her burned arm, weep for her napalmed mother, who would probably die or survive as a monster.

Twenty years ago, during Tet Mau Than, such tears would have seemed to me an obscene personal luxury in the presence of those wretched people. Tears would have rendered me useless: if I had begun weeping then, I could not have stopped.

The New York Times Op-Ed - Friday April 30, 1993

SAN FRANCISCO
Flipping through my United States passport as if it were a comic book, the customs man at the Noi Bai Airport, near Hanoi, appeared curious. "Brother, when did you leave Vietnam?" "One day before National Defeat Day," I said without thinking. It was an exile's expression, not his. "God! When did that happen?" "The 30th of April, 1975." "But, brother, don't you mean National Liberation Day?"

If this conversation had occurred a decade earlier, the difference would have created a dangerous gap between the Vietnamese and the returning Vietnamese-American. But this happened in 1992, when the walls were down, and as I studied the smiling young official, it occurred to me that there was something about this moment, an epiphany. "Yes, brother, I suppose I do mean liberation day." Not everyone remembers the date with humor. It marked the Vietnamese diaspora, boat people, refugees.

On April 29, 1975, my family and I escaped from Saigon in a crowded C-130. We arrived in Guam the next day, to hear the BBC's tragic account of Saigon's demise: U.S. helicopters flying over the chaotic city, Vietcong tanks rolling in, Vietnamese climbing over the gate into the U.S. Embassy, boats fleeing down the Saigon River toward the South China Sea.

In time, April 30 became the birth date of an exile's culture built on defeatism and a sense of tragic ending. For a while, many Vietnamese in America talked of revenge, of blood debts, of the exile's anguish. Their songs had nostalgic titles: "The Day When I Return" and "Oh, Mother Vietnam, We Are Still Here,"

April 30, 1976: A child of 12 with nationalistic fervor,, I stood in front of San Francisco City Hall with other refugees. I waved the gold flag with three horizontal red stripes. I shouted (to no one in particular): "Give us back South Vietnam! "

April 30, 1979: An uncle told me there was an American plan to retake our homeland by force: "The way Douglas MacArthur did for the South Koreans in the 50's." My 17-year-old brother declared that he would join the anti-Communist guerrilla movement in Vietnam. My father sighed.

April 30, 1983: 1 stayed awake all night with Vietnamese classmates from Berkeley to listen to monotonous speeches by angry old men. "National defeat must be avenged by sweat and blood!" one vowed.

But through the years, April 30 has come to symbolize something entirely different to me. Although I sometimes mourn the loss of home and land, it's the American landscape and what it offers that solidify my hyphenated identity. This date of tragic ending, from an optimist's point of view, is also an American rebirth, something close to the Fourth of July.

I remember whispering to a young countryman during one of those monotonous April 30 rallies in the mid-1980's: "Even as the old man speaks of patriotic repatriation we've already become Americans. Assimilation, education the English language, the American "I" - these have carried me and many others further from that beloved tropical country than the C-130 ever could. Each optimistic step the young Vietnamese takes toward America is tempered with a little betrayal of Little Saigon's parochialism, its sentimentalities and the old man's outdated passion

When did this happen? Who knows? One night, America quietly seeps in and takes hold of one's mind and body, and the Vietnamese soul of sorrows slowly fades away. In the morning, the Vietnamese American speaks a new language of materialism: his vocabulary includes terms like career choices, down payment, escrow, overtime.

My brother never made it to the lndochinese jungle. The would-be guerrilla fighter became instead a civil engineer. My talk of endless possibilities is punctuated with favorite verbs - transcend, redefine, become. "I want to become a writer," I declared to my parents one morning. My mother gasped.

April 30,1975: defeat or liberation? "It was a day of joyous victory," said a retired Communist official in Hanoi. "We fought and realized Uncle Ho's dream of national independence." Then he asked for Marlboro cigarettes and a few precious dollars. Nhon Nguyen, a real estate salesman in San Jose, a former South Vietnamese naval officer, said: "I could never forget the date. So many people died. So much blood. I could never tolerate Communism, you know."

Mai Hunng, a young Vietnamese woman in Saigon, said that, of course, it was National Liberation Day. "But it's the South," she said with a wink, "that liberated the North." Indeed, conservative Uncle Ho has slowly admitted defeat to entrepreneurial and cosmopolitan Miss Saigon. She has taken her meaning from a different uncle, you know, Uncle Sam.

"April 30, 1975?" said Bobby To, my 22-year-old cousin in San Francisco. "I don't know that date. I don't remember Vietnam at all-" April 29, 1992, is more meaningful to him, Bobby said. "It's when the race riots broke out all over our country. To me it's more realistic to worry about what's going on over here then there."

Sighing, the customs man, who offered me a ride into Hanoi, said: "In truth, (here are no liberators. We are all defeatedhere." There is no job, no future, no direction, only a sense of collective malaise, something akin to the death of the national soul. He added: "You're lucky, brother. You left Vietnam and became an American."

April 30, 1993: My friends and I plan to watch "Gone with the Wind" for the umpteenth time and look for a scene of our unrequited romantic longings: Scarlett, teary-eyed with wind-blown hair, returning to forlorn Tara. We no longer can. Children of defeat, self-liberating adults, we promise to hug instead and recount to each other our own stories of flight.
