Coming Home

I clearly remember the day I came home from the war in Vietnam:

* The taxi ride to my parents' new house, and the driver who warmly welcomed me back from the war -- the first of only 7 people in my world to ever do so -- and the way he suddenly refused to take my money for that ride, even though I had his money ready in my hand.

* And the strange little tan house, that squatted in the middle of a postage-stamp-sized yard that I'd never seen before, because my folks had moved from Eagle River, Alaska to Tacoma, Washington while I was away to the war.

* And the way my Mom was the first person out the door -- and the first to see that I, her only son, was a newly-created stranger in their midst. And how her rush of steps faltered as she sensed some of the unwelcome and unwanted differences between her son who left for war and the tanned young man who stood in her driveway.

And the way I could see she was feeling the depths of her loss even before she wrapped her arms around my neck and pulled me to her -- giddy as a young girl and sad as an empty and weary old woman all at the same moment. And the way she squeezed my neck so hard it hurt, trying to wring her son back from the man in her arms.

* And how dinner that first night was more tentative than any I can remember, either before or since, as my sisters began to feel the differences in me and a new pecking order was established -- with me on the outside looking in. Desperately wanting in.

* And the way everybody would suddenly try to talk at the same time -- blurting words, any words, to fill the awkward, stretched-too-thin silences that sprouted and grew between us. Words that were immediately swallowed by the embarrassed silence that followed.

* And the quiet after-dinner cigarette I had with my dad, out in the shaded back yard. Quiet and subtly uncomfortable as two men, father and son, veterans, alike but so very different, sniffed at each other like a pair of strange dogs. He was the only one in my family, in my world, who actually expected to see the change -- including me.Ā

But then, he'd come home changed in much the same way by another generation's war just 25 years before. Of course, expecting the change and dealing with it in his own son were two entirely different things.

He still needed time to get hold of this new version of his son. Another 25 years would do it -- almost.

* And then I remember, even more clearly, another warm evening in early June, just 6 days later, when I first discovered that the world I'd left behind had changed even more than I had...

Because that was the first time I was spit upon and called a baby-killer. I traveled the short path from raw stunned disbelief -- to white-hot anger -- and then on to a cold, towering rage that sprawled from horizon to horizon and blocked out the setting sun -- all in an instant much too short to register and far too quick to stop.

I erupted in a sudden flurry of violence that was as much a surprise to me as it was to him.

I learned to fear and hate that violence I carried with me then, and carry with me still.

I know now that I was not his enemy...

I was merely a symbol of what he saw as wrong with our society, with our country.

And he was not my enemy...

He was just a symbol of the way my world had changed while I was in Nam.

I left him where he'd found me. He was alive, but thoroughly bruised before I could regain control of my temper.

Hopefully, he was wiser for the encounter -- others might not have stopped when I did.

But then, others might not have lost their temper in the first place.

I set off to find my next lesson in the world I'd come back to.

It didn't take long. No more than 15 minutes.

I stepped through the doors of a tavern, looking for a cold glass of beer to ease the shake from my hands.

No such luck.

I was still more than 9 months too young to buy a beer in the State of Washington.

The bartender was a Nam Vet himself, and he was very apologetic, but he'd searched hard to find his job and couldn't take the chance of losing it...

Though he did set a brown paper bag (with 2 bottles of Rainier beer in it) outside the back door for me.

I was old enough to go to war, but not old enough to cast a vote for those who sent me. Or even buy myself a beer.

Has the Vietnam War changed me?

Oh yes.

Beyond any doubt.

Before the war, I didn't know I had a temper to lose.

I've never made it back into the "inside" of my family.

I have no trust for my government, and continue to feel like it governs me against my will.

I've been unable, until my current marriage, to sustain any relationship for any length of time.

I'm alienated from one of my two children.

I've never been able to hold down a job for any longer than 18 months.

I'm disabled by Fibromyalgia that is almost certainly caused by the distress of coming home from that war.

And, with my wife, I've written a book, "SCARS," that should never have been necessary - and that probably saved my life.

Yet I still find life to be an exciting exploration of the possible, and warm with the deep bond of love I share with my wife (3rd and last) of 18 years -- the first person to listen, to hear my cry for help, and to love me as much for my weaknesses as my strengths.

She was just 15 when the war ended, and she's still only the 7th major player in my life to welcome me home.

Together, we're working to welcome more of the millions scarred by that war to the only place they wanted to be -- home.

Steve
1950
Mosier, OR USA
