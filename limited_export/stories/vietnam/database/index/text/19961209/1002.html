The piercing scream of the police whistle cut through the thick soup of dust, heat, and the din of scores of scurrying people and monstrous machines. The traffic cop -- actually an ARVN soldier -- was trying to bring order to the chaos of this intersection of Highway 1, Vietnam's sole highway, and a cross street in the city of Da Nang, He wore the regular olive green ARVN uniform but it was starched and pressed. He was dressed out in white arm band, distinctive white service cap, and, surprisingly, white gloves. An incongruous sight in the heat and filth of Nam and war. He blew the whistle again. Loudly.

Were this a real city and not the artificial suburb created by the US military on the outskirts of the true Da Nang City in an area disparagingly nicknamed the Dogpatch, people might have heeded. Were these people familiar with the rules of the city and not peasants, they might have known what to do. Were there not a war on, the drivers of the numerous trucks, minivans, bicycles, and jeeps might have remembered there were rules other than the one cardinal rule of war -- survive. The whistle had little effect except to grab my rapt attention and prepare me for another Vietnam scene.

The impotent shriek of the whistle was replaced by the scream of an old woman. Her voice rose in cacophony with the nerve-jarring grate of metal being twisted and crushed. Not just metal. I heard the sound of bones being crushed. Slowly. About 30 yards away, a huge Marine "6-by" truck was slowly turning left directly towards me, making its way through the crowds of pedestrians and cyclists still trying to rush across the intersection in defiance of the traffic cop. The truck must have struck an old man on a bicycle and knocked him to the ground. The driver appeared to be unaware of the old man's plight because he continued his turn without hesitation. The right front truck tire had crawled over the bicycle's front wheel and, under it, the right leg of the old man. The old mama san nearby screamed again but was drowned out in the continuing din of traffic.

The old man's conical hat careened off his head and rolled on an arcing escape route in the dust. His bike and his leg had raised the tight side of the truck by about 10 inches signaling to the driver that something was wrong and that he should heed the traffic cop's orders -- even though the cop was ARVN. The truck driver finally stopped before the upturned hands of the traffic cop. The massive weight bore down mixing bicycle parts with human leg parts. The traffic cop frantically motioned the driver forward. The driver complied, let off the brake and the truck completed the joumey over the metal and limb. I remember associating the sound with that of brittle chicken bones breaking. I could hear it as clear as I could taste the dust. I stood transfixed.

The old man did not cry out or move. I remember thinking: "What an idiot! Move now. Three feet and you'll be out of the truck's way." He didn't move.

Now the traffic cop waved his hands authoritatively for the driver to stop again to allow the old man to escape. Instead, the driver began to grind forward again, A mournful groan escaped the old man's scrunched up mouth when the double tires on the second truck axle began their climb up onto the rear wheel of the bicycle no doubt sending new shards of pain through the old man's broken right leg. I saw him turn. He just stared at the giant tires on their course towards his bicycle seat, left leg and his tiny midsection. No movement. "Too late," I thought. "You're going to die. You should have gotten out of the way when you had the chance."

For a second time, the bike and crushed bones lifted the truck. Higher this time because the outer of the two wheels had added the man's left leg. Finally, the obstinate driver decided to heed the now frantic arm waving and whistle blows of the traffic cop. He broke the forward motion of the truck in unison with a low moan from the old man and then backed off the bike's rear wheel. The old man cried out in agony.

Now free, several people grabbed his feeble, broken body, whisked him and the mangled bike back into the dust cloud on the other side of the street, and vanished in seconds. Normal time was restarted by the sound of the whistle once more and the big truck completed its turn through the intersection. Seemingly oblivious, the driver gunned the engine as he pulled past me. The episode had slowed him down by a minute or so,

Then it hit me. I had felt nothing for the old man. He was a "Gook." Nam was changing me and I didn't like what I saw. Thinking back on that time, a disease like Alzheimer's comes to mind as an analogy of my transformation. In its early onset stages, the Alzheimer's patient is aware of the effects of the disease and, can comprehend the change that is happening. Later, the disease causes such profound change that the patient can no longer realize they have it. The, abnormal becomes normal. The mind reshapes its thinking processes and rationales as necessary to cope with its new limitations. I was able to see the early effects of the disease of war on me. For many Nam combat vets, their life experiences before the service provided an inadequate foundation to wether the psychic onslaughts of war's brutality, and their post-war experiences lacked the balm of acceptance and forgiveness.
