This is what the world knows of my friend Doug. I want them to know more. Doug was different than the typical high schooler. He cared about others and was confident about himself. Add to this he was good looking. All of this made him vary popular with the girls. Thinking back now, that is what made him with the guys too. We graduated in June of 65. We partied and had a great first few weeks of summer as we had had enjoyed for so many summers before. We had agreed that the thing to do was to join the Marines at the end of the summer. I went to an Uncles house in the city to work in the new and yet unnamed Silicon Valley. I don't know what Doug did. I would like to know, but I'm sure that it was more giving of himself to others. September 1965 found me working at Litton Industries in San Carlos and a draft notice in my hand. I moved back home for the duration. My town was not far from the town in American Graffiti, in fact we would go there en mass to 'drag to strip'. When I returned home most of the 'old gang' was gone. Including Doug. Time went fast, report date loomed close. I wanted to get something out of my military service and honor a family tradition. I enlisted in the Navy and was placed in the C.B.'s. I went to NSA, Da Nang in October 1966. I got a letter from home that Doug had been assigned to an area not too far from me. Hill 55 as I remember. Some time passed but I was able to draw a 4x4 from the motor pool and headed out to hill 55. Along the way I picked 4 'Jar heads' going the same way. I could use the company. About half way there we got rounds close, very close. Close enough to hear the snap of rounds within inches. I looked in the back of the 4x4 and the grunts were OK, but hugging the bed. We got to the HQ in another 45 minutes and the Marines just jumped out and dis-appeared. I found my way to the 1st. Sgt.'s tent. I told him who I was and who I was there to see. He took a long breath and said that Doug had died on the USS Repose about a week before. He was the platoon leader on a patrol when they got into some mines. He described how when Doug was wounded with one of the first mines. Disregarding his wounds, Doug continued rendering first aid to his platoon wounded. While moving from one to another he suffered a fatal wound. He had been recommended for a Silver Star and credited with the survival of many under his command. That was Doug. I knew that he would, no COULD, do no less. That was Doug. Honor was a concept that was in his soul. Love for others was not second nature, it was his first nature. I miss you Doug. I will live my life thinking of how you would handle a situation that I'm in. When I do, the toughest things seen so obviously trivial. Thank you.

Mike Carrington
