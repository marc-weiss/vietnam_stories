Basically, if you were out in a uniform you were kind of shunned and seemed like a pariah, so I had about another year of service time back in the States when I came back so I already had my decompression whereas a lot of you guys came back and jumped back into civilian life, I was still in the military for a little bit. But when we left post, we never wore our uniforms. We always cleared out and got as far away from the post as we could, and then after I got out of the service in 1971 -- I'd got married from a 'girl down in Georgia and got me a job -- worked down there a couple of years and got me a job up here at Wright Pat and I've been here ever since. I've been through two wives, and this is my third wife -- I don't know whether it was me -- I started questioning myself, because we pushed everything aside, you know, about Vietnam, just packed it away, didn't even think about it and got on with our lives, basically working and doing that, and I guess after about 1982 or 1983 when they dedicated the Wall, I think that had an impact on a lot of people, I know it did on me because I didn't know anything about it; I didn't pay attention to Vietnam or anything, I stuffed it and when that started happening, I kind of broke down. It was kind of surprising, a lot of these emotions started coming up when they were showing that on t.v. and then I got to wondering, what's going on. There was a special on t.v. that week, I think public broadcasting had something on it down at the Vet Center, and I forget what it was about, but it was about Vietnam vets, though, and I was watching that and it started to show the symptoms of post-traumatic stress syndrome and stuff like that and, you know, I started questioning, well maybe that's what's wrong with me, that's why I can't hang onto a wife. So I think I went out to the Vet Center and tried to search that out and tried to find where my head was at and where I was going, because I don't feel I saw a lot of traumatic stuff over there, as far as I know, and I was probably luckier than a lot of the guys that dealt with some of that stuff. But I think since that time, since getting involved in the organization and stuff like this, that I have come attuned to becoming more politically active to some degree and not take stuff for granted -- second guess anybody in authority that tells you something, particularly politicians. I think one of the things is that, one of the things I've come to realize, is that a lot of people in this country does take things for granted a lot and like Jim was saying, for what you've seen over there and what they've got. We went over there, I think -- my indication was that we were going over to fight for their freedom so that they could live, choose life like they want to and the people in our country don't really care. People died for the stuff over there and people hardly even want to go vote, so you know, it makes it -- I think it has had a big impact, a big impact on my life and right now I think I just live, eat and sleep that because I'm collecting anything about Vietnam, just trying to find a history of some of the stuff that we did over there that I wasn't aware of, about my units and stuff like that because we were never aware of it when we were over there, and basically what I am doing now is searching out my buddies and trying to find guys and trying to read the history of the units because a lot of the history isn't out there for the younger people to read anymore. And that is basically what I am trying to do, to collect as much history about the stuff as I can. And the wife would tell you right now that my whole basement is full of Vietnam stuff on bookshelves and every book on Vietnam you can think of and any memorabilia, video tapes, I'm just a junkie on it right now, I'm just getting historical information. So I think why this project [the Dayton Stories Project] is pretty good, too, so it puts our perspective in the local area. I guess that's it.
