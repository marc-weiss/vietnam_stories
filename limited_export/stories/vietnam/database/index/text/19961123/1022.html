The Anti-War Movement Revisited

I attended my first Protest against the war in Vietnam in New York in 1964 and my last in San Francisco in 1973. I marched, picketed, sat-in, counseled draft resisters, met with members of the Vietnamese Provisional Revolutionary Government in Europe and North Africa, and planned anti-war demonstrations. I stood outside the Pentagon in October 1967, burned my draft card at Columbia University in April 1968, and encouraged others to do the same. I was in a rage against the war in Vietnam.

I called myself a member of the anti-war movement, but I wasn't against all war. I had friends who were pacifists but pacifism didn't make sense to me. It seemed to me that sooner or later any colonized people who wanted their own sovereignty would have to take up arms and wage war. I never liked the idea of idealists in rich, powerful countries like France or the United States telling people in poor and relatively powerless countries like Algeria or Vietnam that war is bad.

It wasn't until I visited Vietnam in the winter of 1995, two decades after the end of the war, that I began to consider moral power of pacifism. I had seen the ways that the war - the bombing. the killing, the official lying - had warped American society and twisted our values, but I assumed that the Vietnamese had somehow been ennobled by the war. In Hanoi, where I met writers, teachers, students and ex- soldiers, I realized how much I had romanticized the Vietnamese, and I discovered how much they, too, had suffered from the war. I came to the conclusion - one that is perhaps obvious to many other people - that war is horrible, that it's essential to make every effort to resist war, and that while one can survive a war with dignity, no one really wins a war.

I don't know if I would do anything differently if I could relive the anti-war movement. I would still protest, still march on the Pentagon, still burn my draft card, and urge others not to join the army. I don't think I'd be a pacifist the second time around, but I would be a lot more respectful of pacifists and the pacifist movement. I would talk and write much more than I did then about the horrors of war, and far less about the heroism of soldiers no matter what uniform they wore. I would meditate on peace, pray for peace, dream of peace. Even in the midst of war I would do everything in my power to live and to act peacefully.

Jonah Raskin
