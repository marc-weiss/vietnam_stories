In November 1987, I took a job with the Veterans Administration Day Treatment Center in Boston as a Staff Psychiatrist, intending to do as little psychiatry as possible, and to restart an interrupted laboratory career of research in the nervous system. In December, Dr. Lillian Rodriguez--may her name be remembered with honor!--one of the founders of a treatment program for Vietnam combat veterans with severe PTSD, went on vacation to her native Argentina, and I was asked to "cover" her patients. Everyone, staff and patients alike, knew that she had had cancer surgery years before, but did not know that she had suffered a recurrence. Her vacation was extended with news of her illness and further surgery. Then we learned of her death.

Between the time Dr. Rodriguez left and we heard that she had died, a curious thing happened that I didn't understand at the time. One by one, the veterans in the program, and the members of the treatment team came to my office, apparently to talk about specific "coverage" matters, renewing this veteran's prescriptions, helping to get that veteran into the hospital. I now realize that I was being interviewed. One such interview is still fresh in my mind--with a man who had just been paroled from what used to be called the prison for the criminally insane--both because the man was so intimidating, and because the same man is still my patient. He jokes with other veterans about "what an asshole I was," and I joke about "shitting my pants" when I first met him.

The veterans saw something in me that I did not see in myself. When my chief asked if I would take over half of Dr. Rodriguez's patients in the Vietnam combat veterans group--he was expecting me to say, "No!"--I gulped and said, "I'll take all of them." My boss had trouble concealing his relief; I had trouble concealing my anxiety. What was I getting myself into?

The rest is, as they say, history. Within a few months of working with Vietnam combat veterans with severe post-traumatic stress disorder and personality changes I noticed the parallels between the story that Homer tells of Achilles in the Iliad and the stories that combat veterans (Korea and WW II also!) told of their war experiences. I thought this was something that everyone who had studied Homer's epics already knew, but that it would be a neat teaching piece for mental health professionals on how to take a combat history: remember the story of Achilles and you'll touch all the bases. This little paper appeared in The Journal of Traumatic Stress. My ever-resourceful middle daughter, Tamar, who was then a first year student at Harvard College showed it to her professor in a course on epic, to Gregory Nagy, one of the world's leading Homer scholars. He asked me to expand the paper into a book for one of the series of books that he edits. He said that what I had written--that the Iliad is about soldiers in war--had never been said in the 25 centuries of published Iliad criticism! I was stunned and delighted.

And, yes, my ego did get involved, because if you write something worth hearing about the Iliad, people will read your book for centuries, literally. This was my one shot at immortality. I rented a studio, wound up my research, cut out the general psychiatry part of my practice at the VA and went to work. I see from my computer that I had a complete rough draft of Achilles in Vietnam: Combat Trauma and the Undoing of Character in November, 1992. The voices of the veterans I have worked with are heard in the book. It is their book as much as it is mine.

It's now coming up on nine years since I started this work. My life has been completely redirected from its former course. Among the men I work with are a number of remarkable human beings whom it is a privilege to know. I have enjoyed profound satisfaction and pleasure, what Aristotle called *eudaimonma*, from the clinical work, from the creative and scholarly work of writing Achilles in Vietnam, and from public advocacy. The struggle to assist recovery from psychological injury has lit a fire in my belly for prevention of psychological injury in war. To my astonishment and the astonishment of the veterans, the message of prevention has been well received by many active duty military officers. While I did make specific policy recommendations about preventing psychological injury, I assumed that these would never be heard by military leaders. I now speak several times a year on prevention to audiences of active duty military officers--which gives the veterans who have taught me considerable satisfaction. I now work one-quarter time for the VA and spend the rest of my time, mostly unpaid, on writing and advocacy on behalf of prevention.

Of course, definitive prevention of combat trauma is the elimination of war--and we can do this through the creation of trustworthy structures of collective security. If we can abolish slavery (and that job is still unfinished!), we can abolish war. Few people are as ferocious in their desire to abolish war as the combat veterans that I work with--even though most of them felt during the Persian Gulf War, "Take me! I'm good at that. Take me, so some young kid won't have to." It will take centuries to end the social practice of war, like it did with slavery, but we must keep at it and pass this new abolitionism on to our children. Until this is achieved, we must do everything in our power to protect from preventable physical and psychological injury all those we send to fight on our behalf.

Jonathan Shay, M.D., Ph.D.
