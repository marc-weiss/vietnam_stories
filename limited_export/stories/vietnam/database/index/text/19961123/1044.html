Probably one of the less amusing incidents -- where we were at, Pleiku, we didnít get a lot of ground fire, we got a lot of rocket attacks and the area was fairly well built up and most of the rockets would have to come in pretty much from one direction because that was about the only unsecured area within maybe five miles or so. And where we were at on the hill, we were fairly protected because the rockets would have to take a pretty large trajectory to get to us. But on this day they didnít come in from that area, they came in -- there was a lake pretty close to there -- and they figured out later that they came across the lake and over into our area. The one rocket landed right in the mess tent while the cooks were cooking. And they were frying something and it overturned one of the big cooking pots they had, it was full of oil; of course, it ignited and a guy that I knew happened to be right there and got coated with this oil and immediately burst into flames. That mess tent -- I was in personnel -- but you donít stay there very long when you hear rockets come in so everybody was running out and this guy, what was left of the mess tent, he came out the back of it and he was just a ball of fire and some of his other guys, his friends and other cooks covered him up with -- I canít remember -- it was a tarp, I think, that was laying there, but he was fairly well crisp by then. And I think that wasnít the first person I saw got killed but that was the first person that I actually knew who died right there in front of me so I guess it brought to me that life is really a fragile thing. Probably makes you appreciate your time here on earth a little better.

It's really hard to come up with ways in which youíre different because most of us were kids, everybody over there was a kid, and we all grew up. You went over there pretty naive about life in general and the world and you get over there and you see people killed and you see, like you were saying, the conditions of the people that live over there in tar paper shacks that you used to play in when you was a kid; youíd throw them up in ten minutes, and that is what those people lived in, you know. But that is an awful lot...Like I said before, it makes you appreciate the time you have in this world and it also makes you appreciate being an American and that there are an awful lot of people in the world that get by on what we throw away every day. I think that it made me appreciate where I came from, the family that I had, the fact that we had food all the time, people in this country donít appreciate the fact that they donít go hungry, you know, they donít have to beg for a meal, theyíre not in any mortal danger when they wake up in the morning and, you know, guys that have been in Vietnam know that thatís not the case. There are places in the world where things arenít really safe and here people take it for granted and it really galls me when people at gatherings, you know, they talk, they badmouth everything in the United States, just petty things, you know, the price of gasoline or anything. It just gets to me. I just want to say, "Hey, youíve got all this stuff; you should appreciate this stuff." That's about it.

Jim Little

Dayton, Ohio
