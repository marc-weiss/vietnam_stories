In December of 1994 I went back to Vietnam; among my hosts there was the Vietnamese novelist Le Minh Khue, a woman who had been in the North Vietnamese Army during the war. The kids in her unit (like us, they were all teenagers) repaired bomb damage to the Ho Chi Minh Trail, filling in craters, defusing or exploding unexploded bombs. She did that work for four years, often under attack from American aircraft. As a helicopter crewman, I had sometimes flown over the area where she was, and sometimes fired down into the jungle that canopied the Trail. We had both become writers after the war, and friends in 1993, and we worked together editing an anthology, The Other Side of Heaven, containing stories by Vietnamese and American writers about the aftereffects of the war. In October of 1995, Khue came to the states with another writer, Ho Anh Thai, to help launch the book. We crisscrossed the country, an ex-marine traveling with an ex-NVA, trying to cut new trails away from the tangled myths of our pasts, just as we had when Khue walked me through her streets in Hanoi. In Washington, we went to the Vietnam Memorial. I took Khue and Thai down to the two panels where members of my squadron are listed and pointed out a name I always visit, Jim Childers, a boy who'd died in my place when we'd switched missions during the last week of our tour. Khue seemed moved, but she remained silent. I wondered, looking at her, if the names meant more to her now that she'd met us, or if she was still thinking of her own names, the kids who died next to her on the Trail when we were all kids, locked together in hatred. I wondered if she was thinking of the millions of dead and the hundreds of thousands of missing the Vietnamese still have, and how unequal the numbers are, and I thought how in the end it didn't matter, there was enough pain on both sides to allow connection and forgiveness. "We've all suffered," Khue said, reading my mind.

Look who I brought to you, I thought, as I touched my friend's name on the Wall. And I thought: Rest in peace, Jim.

Wayne Karlin

St. Marks City, MD
