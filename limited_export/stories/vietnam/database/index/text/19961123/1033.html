The Vietnam Veterans Memorial

The Vietnam veterans Memorial commemorates a military epoch, a benchmark of American history if there ever was one. The names of the 58,000 men and women killed are listed day by day, alphabetically.

You pass the first panel, a single line of four men killed in May, 1968. Guys from Colorado, New York, Texas, and Virginia. The second panel, three lines with fourteen names. Six lines and twenty-nine names. Eight lines of thirty-nine names. As you walk from one panel to the next the lists come thick and fast, the names a blur. Soon the panels are waist high, head high, then higher than you can reach.

There are several things to notice here.

Firstly, you are standing virtually inside it, and all the emotional energy comes from you.

And secondly, the names are carved deeply into the polished black stone so that during the day your clear reflection stands behind them; among them. Perhaps that is what the first, early, vocal critics of Maya Lin's design the likes of James Webb and Tom Carhart could not fathom.

Or perhaps they were afraid of their own reflections, or the blunt simplicity.

At night footlights set along the base throw up a rounded, scalloped glow, as soft as a night light, and the effect is that your reflection appears in front of the names. Which I take to be pretty spooky.

The very day the memorial was dedicated another quality was discovered. People began to touch it. It was a physical, spiritual compulsion. Some touch it with their fingers tips. Some lean into it with their fists. Some sweep their hands across it, as if stroking a horse's neck. Some visitors seem to embrace it.

Between the panels is space enough to press the stems of flowers, thickly folded notes and cards, dog tags--all manner of memorabilia and remembrances. The custom began the summer of 1982 when a man came to the construction site while the concrete was being poured and dropped his brothers Purple Heart into the mix; his brother a name on the,wall. This matter of left objects is another wonder unique to the memorial. Strictly personal, puerile and embarrassingly sentimental, or sage. Seriously heart-felt, ironic, or obscure. Enigmatic or obvious. People leave keepsakes, funeral-folded flags, teddy bears, personal letters, booze and cigarettes, unit patches, rosaries, Purple Hearts, arranged flowers, apologies and regrets. Hats and boots, Weapons and knives, marijuana joints and handmade Christmas cards, and money owed.

These offerings left at the wall are collected daily and kept in an archive. What a record it will make in a hundred years.

One more thing: soon after the memorial was built a wonderful rumor began to circulate. The story went that Maya Lin, the undergraduate Yale designer, got the inspiration for the memorial when her Vietnamese grand-mother came to her in a dream, spread her arms--thus--and explained what her grand daughter was to do. The story is untrue, of course, Ms. Lin is of Chinese ancestry, but what a powerful connection the story makes, still, of our relationship with the Vietnamese. Human beings have always consulted the spirit-world of dreams for answers, and powerful things happen when you invoke the dead and call to them by name.
