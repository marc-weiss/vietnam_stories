I was four when Mikey went to Vietnam. I didn't understand what that meant, only that he was a soldier in the Army now, and my father, a WWII Marine Corps veteran of the South Pacific theater, spoke with pride for his son heading into his own war now. I didn't understand war, either, only that Mikey was gone and I got his old bedroom and saw photos he took of the jungle where he lived now. I thought of it as just that-- the place where my brother lived now. In the Army.

He was my hero. My friend. The fun brother who took me for speedy rides in an old MG that he'd fixed up from junk and took me for ice cream at the DQ. The first night I slept in his old bedroom, I stayed awake, overcome by awe, as though I'd found myself in the home of a celebrity. I worshipped my brother.

I learned about the war, as much as I could grasp it, from my sister. Two years younger than Mikey and a student at the local community college. We were blue collar through and through. She was the first of any one of my family to go to college, even as a commuter. The college itself didn't even have a building but borrowed space at a prestigious Quaker college famous (or infamous, depending on your politics) for its pacifism. While our hawkish father spoke to the newspaper in a "man on the corner" interview, she began attending peace rallies on campus.

Concerning the question of Vietnam, my father's snippet said, "We should just whomp 'em good." My sister took to carrying a cardboard sign with a large red peace symbol on it. We rode an old tandem bike to the campus for rallies where people her age would look at me with compassionate eyes as if to say, "We're doing this for you."

Mikey's letters home went straight into my mother's hands. Her afternoon soaps held no interest in her when a thin envelope from Mikey arrived in the post. She showed no emotion, but I could feel her anxiety. I only saw her cry once, and that was over a package of his clothing that had arrived along with a photo of Mikey that my mother would not let me see. I found it in a shoebox years later.

I remember most of all the letter from the government, which my father read aloud to all of us. Odd, I thought, how informal it was. The letter spoke of how my brother might jump out into the pouring rain to take showers and mentioned assorted other bizarre behaviors we might expect him to display. The end said that we should be glad, though, because "your boy is coming home!"

I honestly don't remember when he did finally come back from Vietnam. If he came back from Vietnam. I remember my sister crying once. She said, "It's not him. It's just not him now." My mother's face was a mask of stoic weariness. My father watched television-- sit coms, not the news. Never the news-- and I saw Mikey next to never.

When I did see my brother, it was on a brief excursion home to pick up more of his belongings from his room-- now my room-- where he chastised me for "messing with his stuff" and only scowled at me when I asked him questions about where he had been. No more rides in his car, as he now rode a motorcycle. No more trips out for ice cream. Only silence and tension, but most of all absence. I saw less and less of him and soon after his return from the war, he wasn't in our lives at all.

New kids moved into the neighborhood, and in our perfunctory family headcounts, I often forgot to mention that I had a brother at all. Once every few months he might stop in to pick something up, borrow a tool from my father, eat my mother's lasagne. But he seemed to avoid us as much as possible and spoke through a face of stone in a voice devoid of expression.

He, too, went off to college, off to Ball State University on his G.I. Bill. Dated around. Graduated. Went to work in an office. Married. Had two children. Sent Christmas cards to our family in his wife's handwriting. Grew older and more silent. Stayed in the Army, though, and I understand he retired from the reserves.

I haven't spoken to my brother in years. I hear of him through my sister, who gets her news from mutual friends. He has cancer, unknown in my family. The chemo is working. He's hanging in there.

When first my mother and then my father died, Mikey showed up, executor of my father's will. I had lived with my parents for the past seven years, moving back home to help care for them in their failing health. Mike, in his monotone voice, told me that he'd give me three days to find a place and move out. He fished through the house for items of value and took them with him. He gave my father's car to his daughter to use. Told an auctioneer to take the rest. He allowed me to take an old wallet of my father's, a book of my mothers. On everything else, he saw a price tag.

"It changed him," my sister said. "He wasn't like this before Vietnam. He went over there and just got mean." While I understood my sister's view that the war had ruined many people, I personally thought that too many years had passed for the war to be the cause of his recent cruelty. As I spent the first night alone in the house, I pored over photographs in an old shoebox. Under Brownie camera shots of us as children, of my mother making Christmas dinner, of my sister pretending to drive my brother's car, I found a single photo of Mikey from the war. Eyes closed and body reclined in his fatigues on an Army cot, the picture showed something red oozed and spread across his chest. The photo my mother had refused to let me see as a child.

"Ketchup," my sister told me later. "When he sent home his stuff to mom, he sent this photo first. He wanted to make her think that he'd been shot and that his gear was coming home because he was dead."

She didn't need to remind me, but said once more, "It changed him. It made him mean and cold and cruel."

Not all the dead came home in bodybags.

Tony Beltrisi
1965
