I was born in 1965 and I have no direct memories of the Vietnam War since it "ended" prior to the point when I became aware of events on a national and international level, but the War has affected myself and my generation in many subtle ways.

Once, in an undergraduate political science class, I argued this point with the professor. He was delivering a lecture on the protests during this era and how he joined the Free Speech Movement at UC Berkeley. He stated that people in my generation missed the affects of the war since we were so young. I disagreed with him vehemently and listed the friends I had grown up with who were fatherless, because daddy had been blown up by a mine (two of my best friends from childhood had this happen). I told that professor that you can’t get much more “real” an affect than that.

My own father was in the California National Guard to avoid the draft and was gone for periods of time while call-up duty. He was gone when my younger sister was born because he was called up. This was the summer of 1967 and my mother feared rumors that said the California Guard was going to be shipped over to supplement troops in Vietnam. He was much more fortunate than my friends’ fathers, because that never occurred. He did have his brush with “battle”. When I was 3 months old, he was called up to help “restore order” during the Watts Riot. Not the same as being sent over, but this event had its effects on my family as I grew up.

I think that for my generation, Vietnam has left holes in the fabric of our lives that many are not aware of, like moth-holes on the back of a wool sweater that are sometimes not seen by the wearer, but are there nonetheless.

Alice Mercer
1965
Oakland, CA, USA
