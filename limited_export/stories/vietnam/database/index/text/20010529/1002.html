Question 1.: I joined the Navy in 1968 during the height of the Vietnam war protests. I remember thinking the war was wrong, but also thinking that I wasn't wise enough to join the many who protested the war. The day I entered the Navy, there was a symbolic protest. This was a distinct phase of the protest movement in which symbols instead of words were used. While I was being processed into the military in the "old federal building" in downtown Minneapolis, the protest occurred outside. A young woman (probably a student) undressed, stabbed some kind of melon; then, redressed. I didn't see any of this as we were kept busy going from room to room. The only vivid memory of the day was the swearing in of the enlistees who were entering the service from this area. I didn't know anything about the protest until my mother sent a clipping of it to me when I was in boot camp. If I remember correctly, she wasn't arrested for her nudity as no one complained! After basic training and cook's school (or Commisarymen's "A" School as it was officially named) in San Diego, I was posted to Da Nang. I did see a little of Saigon as my bags were held up when I went to San Bernadino for my flight to Vietnam. (I flew a commercial flight to Saigon.) I was stationed at the Naval Hospital near China Beach. Yes, just like the TV show except there was base after base in the area. I used to go to the China Beach PX where they had a USO entertainment center. Question 2: Most vivid memory? Hard to say as there were many vivid memories. I was in the rear; so, there wasn't much to worry about as far as getting hit. I do remember asking on my first tour of the base what all the little patches were on the Quonsot hut I would be living in. "Oh, those are from a mortar round. We were lucky as everyone was at the movie or in the shower when it hit." I went back the transient barracks at Camp Tien Sha and got as drunk as I could. It is amazing how you can get used to the noise and bombs and not think anything of it. This is what one thinks of when they read about current hot spots in the world. The people who live in Northern Ireland or in the Middle East are use to war even while they may hate or deplore the conflicts they are in. This was a lesson of Vietnam for me. The sound of helicopters and spending your time within the perimeter of barbed concertina wire are a vivid memory of daily existence. Other memories include a fellow cook from Indiana who played Chess with me and played basketball with some of the Marine guards. He was 5'4" tall; so, naturally, his nickname was Shorty and he played guard. Well, one day he was bawling his eyes out. One of his basketball compatriots had stepped on a landmine during a patrol the night before and had died. Of course, the great cliche of Vietnam which is seen in all movies and books is the friend dying in one's arms. I can only speak for myself, but I am sure there were others beside myself who spent a year in Vietnam and never saw anyone die. You still knew that death was omnipresent though. During my first couple of days in Saigon I heard that a civilian had died on a motorcycle; later, in Da Nang, I remember passing a USO entertainer with extremely large breasts (implants ala Carol Doda?) who was visiting the hospital I was stationed at. Less than a week later I remember hearing that she had been shot in the chest by a crazed enlisted man. Another vivid memory is of an X-ray. One night a man came into the hospital who had an unexploded granade in his head and he was still alive. Apparently a granade launcher sent this granade into his head and it hadn't exploded because (I was told) a granade from a launcher needs to revolve a certain number of times before it explodes.) Anyhow, a fellow cook who hung out near our hospital patient entrance got a copy of the X-ray taken of this man's head. He showed it to us some time later, but we were told not to tell anyone we had seen it. (I think he wasn't suppose to have a copy of it.) Anyhow, they had to wait for the Marine who had this granade in his head to die. Then, they set off the granade which was still in his head. Another myth of war is that there is continual gun fire and excitement. In actuality there are often great stretches of boredom. At our base this was alleviated by drinking, bringing whores on base (more about sex later); basketball, books; etc. At another base there was a bowling alley, but it was usually closed. The one time I remember going there; the pinsetters were Vietnamese woman who manually did the pinsetting. I heard it was often closed as bored bowlers often aimed at the women instead of the pins. I don't know if this is true. Creature comforts: Contrary to popular belief it wasn't always a sweat filled environement as us cooks on our base had air-conditioning. It is surprising what you can trade a case of steaks for. As a cook I could get my mail the night before by visiting the post office on base or I could get some new boots from the Marine supply post on base. (The Navy Hospital I was stationed at serviced wounded Marines as the Marine Corps doesn't have any medical service within their branch of the service. Also, medical enlisted personnel attached to Marine units are always Naval personnel.) We could go to the actual China Beach during our days off. It was the only beach in which lifeguards had to look both ways. I still remember there was an area where many of the men did their sunbathing in the nude. I can still remember our female nurses having a picnic on the beach near this nudist area. I noticed they were passing binoculars around one day as they checked the young nudists on the beach. After being stationed in Vietnam I asked for a tour of duty in the Mediterrean area as my brother (a Marine) had served on a Med cruise. I was stationed in the Eritrean province of Ethiopia. Even though this was hundreds of miles from the Mediterrean, it was considered part of this area as our base was a communications station for ships in the Mediterrean sea. It was a shift from tropical to a more temporate climate as our base was roughly 7,000 ft above sea level and the Eritrea area had a desert like climate. There was also a war going on as the Eritrea Liberation Front was trying to break away from Ethiopia to become a separate country. (It had been an Italian colony that was made a part of Ethiopia by the UN in order to provide the landlocked Ethiopians with a sea port on the Red Sea.) Still, there was a conflict in which US servicemembers lost their lives. Being only a few lives were lost; their service to their country was overshadowed by Vietnam. Also, after South Vietnam, the war in this African state (it was sporatic and we never saw or were particapants in any of the fighting) was dull. Question 3: My experience in Vietnam and Eritrea, Ethiopia taught me how blessed in riched our country is compared to what we call the "third world." Also, a plane trip across the Pacific ocean can be very instructional for a recent high school graduate. Like many of my era we also realize that war is often a poor method for resolving conflict. Question 4 and 5: How am I different today? I'll go back to before I entered the service in order to attempt to answer this question. (As I honestly can't say if I've appreciably changed.) I was and always have been physically attracted to my sex all my life. When I entered the service I remember that I had to lie in order to enter as there was a question regarding homosexuality on the form. While serving at my last duty station in Dam Neck, VA I came out to a superior that I respected. I had discovered gay bars, and met a very sexy man of Italian descent who visited Norfolk from Washington DC and drove a red convertible. I came back to the base with hickies and I was harassed by fellow workers. "Hey, who gave you those?" How could I tell them that it was a man? So, like I said, I came out to a superior, the first class (E6) Commissaryman who was my supervisor on my watch. He bumped this fact up the chain-of-command and I was discharged about two months shy of my four years. I came out in May and they didn't discharge me until the end of August. I worked at my normal job and had no problems getting along with my shipmates. So, I'd say that my service to my country has one enduring meaning in my life. This is my unalterable opposition to "Don't Ask, Don't Tell" and the idea that glbt (gays, lesbians, bisexuals, and transgendered) people cannot honorably serve thier country.

Unlike many of my Vietnam contemporaries, I wasn't involved in any heavy fighting. I did feel the stigma that surrounded Vietnam veterans. I was also double disillusioned in that I served a country that at that time and to this day doesn't appreciate the service of its glbt members. I got over the stigma of being a Vietnam Veteran when I attended a local reunion of all Vietnam veterans some years after returning. We were invited to march in a parade through downtown Minneapolis. The cheering crowds made me feel at home again. I still, however, feel the stigma that our country attached to my service as a gay male in its homophobic armed services. For years after leaving the service I would occasionally have a dream that I was back in the military in my old job as a cook and no one cared that I was gay. Recently (due to the internet) I have discovered organizations with members who feel as I do. I have joined the GLBVA (Gay, Lesbian, and Bisexual Veterans of America) and Post 448 of the American Legion which is a local of the American Legion in San Francisco (though I don't live there) whose members are predominately gay. Also, I have become an activist and support an organization that actively supports gay and lesbian servicemembers who are in trouble due to Don't Ask, Don't Tell. This organization is SLDN which is short for the Servicemembers Legal Defence Network. I would never have joined or supported this organizations had not I served our country in Vietnam and Ethiopia. To this extent you can say that my service still affects how I live and my politics. At the time of my service I remember leaving in constant fear that someone would figure out that I was gay. It is ironic that I was the one who ended outing myself. I just couldn't stand having to live a lie anymore. I still and always will admire those who come out while still in the service. Also, I don't think people realize the problem with "don't ask, don't tell" that the young gay men and lesbian women who want to enter today have. At least when I entered just about everyone was in the closet, but now young men and women are encouraged to come out of the closet while in high school or at college. Then, if they decide to enter the military, they are forced to reenter the closet. This is cruel. My only dream which many share with me is that some day in the near future openly gay men and lesiban women will be able to honorably serve their country without the additional worry of losing their jobs solely because of their sexual orientation. For anyone reading this I recommend Randy Shilt's book, Conduct Unbecoming or Allen Berubes book Coming Out Under Fire (about gays and lesbians during WWII) for great background material on this issue. My service has affected my thinking on other issues. The flag burning amendment that was banded about a few years ago. I thought this was a total waste of time. Since the supreme court rightly decided that this was political expression, no one burns the flag. Also, I'd hate to live in a country that forces its citizenry to respect its flag or face penalties. I have also found that as I get older I am getting more liberal unlike many in my generation who are getting more conservative. I have become active in a gay church and gay causes while earlier in life I was gay but chose not to be actively involved in changing laws and regulations that affected me.

Kenneth P. Sholes
1949
