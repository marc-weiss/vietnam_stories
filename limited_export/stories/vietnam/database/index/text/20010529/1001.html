Vietnam was like a hurricane.

It's central core was the sometimes quiet rice paddy's and jungle, but it extended out in all sorts of directions for thousands of miles, reeking havoc and destruction.

My small world was turned upside down in Chicago, those years of 1967 through 71, when I turned 18 and found that my draft number was too high to worry about, though I had been going on a preparation for CO status. The vets were coming back, many injured, some physically, many more emotionally, saying, "don't go. It's hell on earth". The marches in Chicago, the action in the churches of the suburbs. The end of innocence. It robbed me of some of my childhood, in a way that affected me then and now.

I look at my 16 year son now, and his life seems so normal. It's good. While he doesn't have to go through what my generation went through. It's bad though too. He isn't being forced to come to grips with what he really believes in. I'm sure someday he will have to.

I went to your wall, Maya, now our wall. I broke down and cried, not because I knew anyone there in stone, but because of what it did to me, our country and our innocence. It was overwhelming. Thanks for the greatest memorial I've ever seen.

AMB

Al Bergstein
1953
USA
