I wrote this essay for my high school American Studies class in April of 1996. I was a junior then and am a senior now. I found the project extremely moving and enlightening and learned so much in writing this paper. I just thought that I would like to share it. Thanks for taking the time to read it.

Maggie Hansen American Studies Assignment Vietnam

'An entity, a thing, a single, massive phenomenon that stretched from perhaps 1954 to 1974, a beast which did no good but much harm, a debacle without redeeming value. Or it is seen as a military fiasco, or a colonial endeavor, or a bad political dream." (Capps, p. 15), This is how Vietnam War Veteran Davidson Loehr described the Vietnam War in his testimony entitled "To Care Without Judging." Loehr served his l3-month time in Vietnam from July l966 to August l967. He believes that it is virtually impossible for anyone from the outside to understand what the Veterans of this war went through, what they actually experienced, and more importantly, how it has changed their lives. "To understand the experience of war you must be in war, Otherwise it isn't war that you understand, but merely the intellectual and esthetic concept of the Idea of war, And that's not what Veterans lived through at all." (Capps, p, 24). So, perhaps I am not the one who should be telling you about the Vietnam War, since I have only read and heard about it. I have never been in war, and I probably never will be, so I will never be able to truly understand what I read and hear. None of my close relatives or friends even served in Vietnam. However, I will do my best to tell you what I have learned about how the Vietnam War experience did affect the people who served there, along with the people they love.

Let me begin by telling you first about the Vietnam War itself. It was the longest war ever in which the United States took part, and the first foreign war that our country did not win. 58,000 Americans died in Vietnam and 365,000 were wounded. Excluding the countless numbers of Vietnamese civilians that were also killed in the war, about one million South Vietnamese and from 500,000 to one million North Vietnamese faced their deaths too. This war was at first a struggle between the South Vietnamese Army and the Viet Cong, a group of Communist revolutionaries who lived in South Vietnam. The Viet Cong were supported by North Vietnam and led by Ho Chi Minh, who had established the Communist government there in 1954.

Later in the war, most of the fighting was done by the United States and North Vietnam. The U.S. became involved because they were very concerned that the Communist activity in Southeast Asia was a threat to the security of the United States. In 1949, China was taken over by the Communists, and the US. feared a "domino effect" - they worried that if one nation in Southeast Asia fell to the Communists, all others would fall, too. The United States was afraid of the growth of Communism for many years. "We believe America has a divine mandate to evangelize the world to its own political and economic systems. War is a sacred instrument, the great cultic activity, whereby this mission is achieved." (Capps, p. 33). This is how William P. Mahedy, a former Augustinian monk and U.S. Army Chaplain who served in the Vietnam War, described the United States' knack for getting involved in foreign wars in his account, entitled "It Don't Mean Nothin -- The Vietnam Experience." President Eisenhower sent the first Americans to South Vietnam in 1955, although President Truman had actually sent troops to South Korea five years earlier to stop the Communist invasion by North Korea.

In March of 1965, President Johnson sent the U.S. Marines to South Vietnam, and they were the first American ground combat forces to enter the country. He did this after the Gulf of Tonkin incident, in which North Vietnam sunk two American destroyers in the Gulf of Tonkin, off the coast of North Vietnam. U.S. forces rose from 60,000 in mid-1965 to 543,000 in 1969. The United States relied on the bombing of North Vietnam and on 'search and destroy' ground missions in South Vietnam. They also used a plant killer called Agent Orange to destroy enemy food crops and to reveal hiding places. These three actions of the United States killed many innocent civilians and destroyed a huge portion of the Vietnamese countryside. In fact, more recent studies have shown that Agent Orange has brought about genetic disorders in some Vietnam Veterans and their children.

Peace talks in Paris in May of 1968 failed to produce agreement, and in June 1969, President Nixon called for stepped-up training forces for South Vietnamese forces and a gradual withdrawal of all U.S. forces from South Vietnam in his Vietnamization policy. In March of 1972, North Vietnam began a major invasion of South Vietnam. This led to more peace talks, and on January 27, 1973, the United States, South Vietnam, North Vietnam, and the Viet Cong signed a cease-fire agreement. The last U.S. ground forces left Vietnam on March 29,1973, but peace talks broke down and the war resumed. However, no American troops returned to fight. This war - a war that our country would never be able to win - was costing too much money, effort, and more importantly, lives. South Vietnam surrendered to North Vietnam on April 30,1975, when North Vietnam invaded Saigon.

Many, many people believe the United States should have never become involved in Vietnam. The doves were a group of war protesters who completely opposed involvement, and they believed that the nation was supporting a corrupt, undemocratic government in South Vietnam. They also did not believe that the growth of Communism in Southeast Asia was a threat to the security of the United States. They held mass protests. Increases in taxes due to the war also brought about a drop in public support, as did war scenes shown on television. This was the first time that the United States had brought a war home on TV. There was opposition to the draft, and many people burned their draft cards or fled to Canada, As Paul Sgroi said in 'To Vietnam and Back," his testimony of the Vietnam War, "We were mere pawns in a political and economic venture " (Capps, 29). Sgroi served in the First Cavalry Division Airmobile from October 1968 to October 1969 as a combat photographer in Vietnam.

I have given you a summary of the Vietnam War because I think it is necessary to know what happened there before you can even begin to understand how it affected the lives of so many Americans. That is what I had to do, as I went into this project knowing next to nothing about the Vietnam War.

I got a first-hand account of the war by interviewing my chemistry teacher, Mr., John Dragavon. As far as I am aware, he is the first person I have ever met who has served in Vietnam. I have known for some time that he was in the war, but I never thought much of it. It didn't change what I thought of him ... it didn't make him anymore than just my chemistry teacher ... until I interviewed him about his experience. Now, Mr. Dragavon is a different person to me. His story was very moving.

He served in Vietnam, in the region near the city of Da Nang, from 1970 to 1971. Lieutenant Dragavon was a Convoy Commander, and he drove a truck that picked up supplies from the seaports and ran them out to the troops in the field. Up to sixty trucks would go out in a convoy, often for three days at a time. Mr. Dragavon was 22 when he went to Vietnam, and had just graduated from Washington State University. He had enlisted in the Reserve so that he would not be drafted as a rifleman - he felt that he would have more control over his destiny as an officer than as a rifleman. He then enrolled in the ROTC at WSU. He trusted his country to do what was right, and that involvement in the Vietnam War was the right thing, along with sending hundreds of thousands at Americans to fight over there. So, he did not protest, although he did not even totally understand what the United Sates was doing in Vietnam.

Being 22 at the time, Mr. Dragavon was one of the oldest soldiers in Vietnam, except for his Company Commander, who was 26, and the Career Sergeants, who had been in the service for at least twenty years. The average age was that of a college sophomore, although many were younger. The closest Mr. Dragavon came to getting killed was by one of his own troops. There was a 17-year-old boy called Moon who had been sent to Vietnam in place of going to jail. He had stolen the Collection money from a church, and had been involved in a police shoot out. Moon wanted to drive, but Mr. Dragavon, who was definitely the senior officer, didn't want him to. For this, Mr. Dragavon almost lost his life. Fortunately, one of his other troops showed up then and stopped Moon, who was later sent to Tokyo.

Mr. Dragavon also noticed the race relations that existed between troops in Vietnam. African-Americans and Hispanics hated each other, and they fought each other with knives and guns. As Diana Seymour, the heroine in Terry Farish's Vietnam War novel "Flower Shadows" said, "People do awful things, Vietnamese against Vietnamese, man against woman, all the time, GI against GI. " (Farish, p 206). Hispanics didn't like the white Americans, either. Here they were, Caucasian-American, Hispanic-American, and African- American, and they were more concerned about fighting against each other than against the Viet Cong. I addition, most of the white soldiers had not grown up around, lived with, or worked with African-Americans, as the 1950s and 1960s were decades of continuing prejudice, and so they did not know how to relate to each other. Mr. Dragavon said it is also true that African-Americans and Hispanics together made up about half of the troops in Vietnam. However, they saw this, and knew for a fact that they were not half of the total American population. They were being used, to fight for a country that discriminated against them.

Race relations were not the only problems. White Americans even turned against other white Americans, usually because the war had driven them to insanity. As Paul Sgroi said, "Vietnam was 'beware of everything'... Beware of everything except your own sanity, which, by now, didn't exist." (Capps, p. 27). "Flower Shadows" is the story of Diana, a college girl from Texas who signs up to work for the Red Cross in Vietnam, doing programs to entertain the troops and to remind them that there was a life back home. She had no idea what she was getting into. She gets assigned to a unit with three other girls in Cu Chi, and one of the girls, Pearly becomes a very close friend of Diana. Diana falls in love with a grunt, who calls himself Big Foot. However, a GI breaks into the bunker that Diana's unit was staying in. Mad and crazy, he almost kills Diana, but when Pearly comes to the sound of Diana's screams, he turns on her, instead. Pearly is murdered, and this scars Diana forever. She doesn't understand why one American would turn against another American. She said, "I had never worried about the inside I had always worried about the outside. I had never worried about a GI ... My mind could not grasp, enemy. Now there was no distinction ... He could have been any GI. He could have been Big Foot.' (Farish, p, 204).

Mr. Dragavon told me this: "I was never so mad as when I came home, not because of the parades and all that [garbage] - we don't need that - but I went out the first day I was home, and I realized that nobody's life in my country, and in my state and in my town, nobody's life was affected one iota by the fact that I was gone. It was just like I went, stepped through a curtain, and walked to the other side, and it was just the same. They were doing the same thing, they had the same job, they had the same goals ... We weren't doing anything that affected the people here, except the people who were directly supporting the war .... So I wasted a whole year - just gave it away. I'll never forget that - I'll resent that as long as I live."

Paul Sgroi shared these feelings, concerning how Americans did not support the war, and also, how people took their negative feelings against Vietnam and threw them upon the Veterans themselves. "The day I arrived home a woman of my own age walked up to me with several of her friends ... and asked me if I had been in Vietnam. When I responded that this was my first day home she spat in my face and the group let loose with a barrage of insults. I don't think that she knew then that her spit would forever stain my face. It is not possible to wipe that spittle clean. It is on my face today. I have been able to partially clean it off, but it will be a long time before I am able to clean it all off. Welcome home." (Capps, p.29).

Davidson Loehr shared his feelings about coming home from Vietnam, too: "What was left was to come home - to a very poor GI bill, to a radical University of Michigan Campus with a categorical hatred for all Veterans - 'babykillers' we were called - and to a society that was beginning to find the scapegoat it so desperately needed in the Veteran themselves.' (Capps, p. 23).

From the Alain Boublil and Claude-Michel Schonberg musical "Miss Saigon," there are even words from the song "Why God Why' that express the ignorance of the American public toward the Vietnam War: "When I went home before, no one talked of the war. What they knew from TV didn't have a thing to do wit me." Chris, a GI, is in Saigon in April 1975, just before the fall of the city,

This was not fair to the Veterans themselves. Most of them did not choose to go, and none of them actually wanted to fight - to kill anybody or to put their own lives at stake. They went because their country sent them. Many of the troops were so young and naive that they did not even know why the United States was involved in a war twelve thousand miles away. In "Flower Shadows," Big Foot wrote to Diana in a letter: "I don't know what we're doing here. I think we're just out here for Charlie [ the Viet Cong] to shoot at,' (Farish, p.111). It was not their fault, and if they were drafted, they could not do anything about it. Some signed themselves up to go over because they knew that they would probably get drafted anyway, and sitting around and waiting for this time to come just made them too anxious. As Davidson Loehr said, "It was hard to know who was to blame. It was even harder to accept finally the fact that there was no one to blame, no adequate or effective scapegoat to take away the sins of the world, to make it all harmonize as it had once seemed to when life was so much simpler, so much less powerful." (Capps, p. 23).

Mr. Dragavon admitted having a very hard time adjusting to life when he came home. "And now, you were supposed to forget all that! Supposed to let it all go, right?" This is true of probably most Americans who served in Vietnam. He said, "I was a bit weird when I came home, as you might imagine." He had been married for two years before he left for the war, and when he came home, he found that his wife had gotten a job at a hospital, moved to Kenmore, and made new friends. He found that he was very out of touch with what was going on in the rest of the world, and even with the people he was closest to. Mr. Dragavon and his wife almost didn't make it. He described how difficult it was to adjust to normal life: "... And then being jerked out of a situation where you've been like that for a year, living under that kind of adrenaline rush for all that time, and then put back into a place where hey, commuter traffic is the biggest problem you got. With people you know, nobody you know. I don't know anybody! I know her, I think! We've been together three years, and I've been gone a year out of three."

Everyday events became ordeals for Vets. Mr. Dragavon mentioned that alarm clocks would scare him - it would go off, and he would run outside before he realized that he wasn't being shot at in Vietnam. He came home July 3, and the next day being Independence Day, the firecrackers simply drove him crazy. The noise was just too much for him to handle. In his testimony of the his experience in the war, Paul Sgroi wrote that he reacted to noise in exactly the same way. "Loud noises bother me. This is especially true of anything that sounds like a gun. I don't hit the floor anymore. There was a time when a car's backfire would push me to the floor, or to the street, within the blinking of an eye." (Capps, p. 31)

Veterans also experienced nightmares after the war - some probably still do. Paul Sgroi wrote that he had nightmares for the first seven years after the war, and would often wake up yelling or crying. Mr. Dragavon experienced flashbacks - one time he was driving at night, and suddenly, in his mind, it was daytime, and he was in Vietnam. He also said that his senses are still completely intact to the environment of Vietnam, "Even the smells and the colors are quite vivid in my dreams. I can smell things. That's how vivid things become when you're under adrenaline and tension and pressure all the time. You not only just flash back on the event, but you flash back on colors and wind blowing in your face, and the sound of a water buffalo, or sounds and smells and tastes and textures. I can smell the garbage dump in my dreams, I can smell it just as sure as I'm standing here."

Unfortunately, I learned that many vets turned to drinking when they came home, to help them cope with their memories of the war and the difficulties of starting over in America. Paul Sgroi wrote, "Alcohol became my best friend. I have survived by drinking. I drank from ten to twelve cans of beer per day in addition to whatever else I had." (Capps, p.31). Mr. Dragavon said, "So I did what a lot of people did. I started drinking, like crazy, for months." However, he met someone he knew from the military organizing graduate classes at the University at Washington. Mr. Dragavon decided to take up the offer, and he found that every single student in his class was a Vietnam Vet. That made things much easier for him, because he would not have fit into a regular college class. He was older than most college students, and the experience of being in the war separated him from them. He has since gone on with his life and learned to accept the fact that he can't change what happened in Vietnam. Paul Sgroi was not so lucky. He was married and divorced twice, and he ended up committing suicide in 1987. Other Vets turned to drugs and crime.

"Miss Saigon" tells the story of the GI Chris, and Kim, the Vietnamese bar girl he falls in love with. Chris notices her in a bar in Saigon in one of the last nights before the fall of Saigon. His friend John buys Kim for him - Kim is new at her job. Chris realizes from the beginning that she will change his life forever. He sings, 'Why God? Why this face? Why such beauty in this place? I liked my memories as they were, but now I'll leave remembering her." The two fall in love, but Chris is not able to take Kim with him when he flees Saigon as North Vietnam invades the city, although he desperately tries. He continues to try once he gets home to America, but he has no success.

He finally remarries, to a woman named Ellen. However, Ellen realizes that something from the war is bothering Chris. He has nightmares, too. She sings, "Last night I watched you sleeping. Once more the nightmare came. I heard you cry out something, a word that sounded like a name. And it hurts me more than I can bear, knowing part of you I'll never share, never know .... It's all over I'm here, there is nothing to fear. Chris what's haunting you? Won't you let me inside, what you so want to hide? I need you too!"

Little does Chris know that Kim became pregnant by him, and he has a son. He learns this through John, who works for an agency to help the survivors of war in Vietnam. He discovers that Kim is alive in Bangkok with a son. He has to break this news to Chris, who pleads, "Oh John, I never told her! My wife - how can I tell her? A child - What do I do? I shattered Kim, now Ellen too!" He finally tells Ellen about Kim, and they go to Bangkok to find her.

Meanwhile, Kim still believes that Chris will come save her someday and take their son, Tam, and her to America. She has no idea that her prince has remarried, although she soon discovers this. She meets Ellen and asks her if she and Chris will take Tam to America, where he can have a good life. Ellen refuses because she feels that it is not right to take a child from his mother. Ellen then turns all of her own anger and disappointment on Chris. "You can say what you want, but she's borne you a son. In your nightmares the name that you shouted was hers. You've been with me two years and not shared things you've done. Don't hold back - let me see. Is it her now, or me?" She is worried that Chris still loves Kim and will want to return to her, and is so unhappy that he never told her about this.

Chris responds, "Let me tell you the way it was, back when I was a different man, back when I didn't have a clue who I am. The feelings locked behind a dam, that kept me there in Vietnam! There in the shambles of a war, I found what I was looking for. Saigon was crazed, but she was real, and for one moment I could feel! ... So I wanted to save her, protect her. Christ, I am American! How could I fail to do good? All I made was a mess, just like everyone else, in a place full of mystery that I never once understood."

Chris and Ellen decide that they will support Kim and Tam in Bangkok from America, but this is not good enough for Kim. She wants Tam to live in America with his father. So, she takes her own life, realizing that it would force Chris to take his son back to America with him.

"Miss Saigon" is a very sad story, but one that many Americans found themselves a part of after the Vietnam War. Through the work of various agencies and organizations, many Vietnam Vets have discovered that they are the fathers of children in Vietnam. The GIs were very desperate for something to take their minds off the horrors of the war, which had already corrupted and destroyed most of their positive thoughts. These girls sold their bodies for money, as they had no other way to earn it. They had babies, and these babies became known as bui doi or "the dust of life". Chris's friend John sang about bui doi at a conference he gave for Vets: "They're called bui doi, the dust of life, conceived in hell, and born in strife. They are the living reminders of all the good we failed to do. We can't forget - must not forget - that they are all our children too." Some of these half breed children were taken in by their fathers in America, but usually their mothers in Vietnam could not support them, as was the case with Kim and Tam.

The Vietnam War was an event that has changed American society forever and affected the lives of countless people. I believe it was a terrible accident, and our nation paid and lost too much for nothing - except the lessons. It is so unfortunate that these lessons must be learned 'the hard way' - through experience. In "Flower Shadows", Hoa, a Vietnamese girl who lived with and helped Diana's unit at Cu Chi, said, "Bad luck. Bad Karma. That is what war is" (Farish,p.203) Diana sad, referring to Pearly's murder, "One of us had to die. Nobody got out of Vietnam without paying. If you came over as a cute college girl with nice skin and neat values, you wouldn't go home as one. This was war. Nobody got off," (Farish,p.210). In addition, war is not something you can forget. In "Miss Saigon," Chris's friend John said, "Like all survivors, I once thought, when I'm home I won't give a damn. But now I know I'm caught - I'll never leave Vietnam. War isn't over when it ends. Some pictures never leave your mind."

I feel that I have learned a great deal about the Vietnam War, and somehow, I feel more complete now. I didn't realize what I was missing before, but now I don't know how I could have ever considered it such a minor event. The Vietnam War was anything but minor. It was bad, and the badness of war will never change. It will never be a good thing. Although what I have learned is terribly sad, I am extremely thankful that I have had the opportunity to learn about Vietnam. I wish that every person in this country would take the time to learn about this war - it is part of being an American. This is one of those things that people have to know about so that it will never happen again. William P. Mahedy wrote in his testimony of the Vietnam War: "Those on both sides of the Vietnam conflict were exposed to and participated in consciousness-altering, irreversible, massive evil. Atrocity, hatred, wholesale slaughter and barbarous acts of all kinds are the stuff of war.' (Capps, p. 36).
