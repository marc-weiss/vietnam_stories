There is one group of people whose stand for or against the Vietnam war
evolved without going there, or necessarily having someone close to them go
there. Vietnam era vets who never got anywhere near Southeast Asia went
through the same process of being educated about the war as the American
public at large.

The difference was that we did it sooner because we were constantly
wondering if and when we would be sent to Vietnam. This caused us to think
about the issue in personal terms, not just react to the novelty of a
television war. Those of us who were stationed outside the U.S. saw very
little hard news. We ingested the military-controlled offerings, such as
the "Stars And Stripes", the unintentially absurd and universally ridiculed
Armed Forces newspaper, but we formed our opinions mainly by direct contact
with combat vets rather than through the distorted caricatures in the news
media.

We served with the Vietnam combat vets every day. We observed, at first
with horror, but later with morbid fascination, how the war had ravaged
their bodies and their psyches. Occasionally they talked about their
experiences. There was a desire to respect their privacy while being ready
to commiserate with them when they wanted to. We silently thanked God that
it had been been them, not us, who had been subjected to the ordeal. We
never said as much -- to do so would have been tasteless gloating or an
admission of cowardice. It was generally understood that the RVN vets
deserved respect; we struggled not to offend them.

It's interesting how most combat vets didn't talk much about their
experiences, but while drinking or smoking (usually marijuana), sometimes
the flood gates would open and they would tell as much as they could bear
to. The stories were never predictable, sometimes horrifying and
grotesque, sometimes banal, occasionally truly funny.

The important point is that their stories gave us snapshots of reality with
which to contrast media hype and government propaganda. Inexorably, the
truth soaked in that the Vietnamese were people, not cartoon villains.
After a while, we accepted as fact that economics and the struggle for
political power inside the U.S. were more important factors in starting and
prolonging the war. We realized the pointlessness of trying to prop up a
corrupt and ineffectual puppet government in the face a fierce desire for
national unity and the dream of ending a thousand years of foreign
domination. The warmongers' crusade to "contain communism" was revealed
for what it was, a massive and cruel hoax.

Most of this had been trumpeted for years by the domestic anti-war
movement. But ordinary people continued right up to the fall of Saigon
mouthing the war machine's platitudes about the need to "take a stand for
freedom" and the peurile domino analogy.

Public sentiment did begin to change when their sons, and some of their
daughters, came back in aluminum caskets, but grieving families often
rallied even more stanchly to what they thought was "the greater good". I
think what turned the tide was the surviving combat vets helping their
friends and families, as they helped non-combat vets, to see past the
hysteria and begin to think about the war in human terms.
