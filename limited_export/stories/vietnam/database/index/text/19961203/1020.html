I am a college professor at the University of California, Davis. I am 58 years old and a member of the Vietnam War generation. I served my country from 1962-1964, but did not serve in Viet Nam. Once I believed in the dominoe theory; once I believed in Viet Nam as a just war; once I believed in the need to stem Communism before this "ism" challenged and ruled the world. I changed 10 years ago.

Today, I coordinate the University of California, Davis - University of Ha Noi - Vietnamese Ministry of Agriculture Cooperative Program. Together, Americans and Vietnamese are working to build bridges into the 21st century. We have exchanged scholars, academic and research visits, embarked upon humanitarian efforts to reduce medical and nutritional problems in Viet Nam.

In Viet Nam the war is over; in America, the war continues to be fought.

There has been too much pain; nearly 60,000 American dead -- aproximately 2 million Vietnamese dead (both North and South).

Three years ago, I stood at the cemetery near Cu Chi; I stood with my Vietnamese friend at the grave of his father. Together we paid respects to the countless men, women, and children who died for their respective beliefs. Smoke from joss sticks swirled skyward. My friend took my hand and spoke -- Louis, my father would have liked you.

Professors, students, administrators at UC Davis and in Viet Nam together work towards a brighter future, one where former enemies can become colleagues and develop friendships based upon mutual respect.

Does not the same ocean that touches the shoreline of California -- create tides and waves that wash against the shoreline of Viet Nam?

Two nations -- once divided by war -- linked by the waters of our planet.

I would like to end by sharing three pieces written in 1995.....

First, written to the memory of American MIAs

GREEN, GREEN, GRASS OF HOME1

I buried the last of my comrades yesterday. I am the only pilot to survive prison in my jungle stockade. One by one we died... no news from outside, nothing. Days, weeks, months, and years passed us by.

Solitary confinement has been my fate for six days. Now they come. I am shackled, blindfolded, and transported by truck to this unknown place. I am led into a cage of lashed bamboo. The door is closed. Why have I been brought here?

My blindfold is removed by others also inside the cage. We are twelve. This day we share rain, jungle rot, insects, and rats. Crowded together, we twelve have no place to lie, no food, only dirty water. No place to shit or piss except in one corner of this hell-hole.

It doesn't matter for we are together.

Through the sleepless night we share each other's names. Passing time we speculate and talk of repatriation. We twelve are unbathed and stink, but we relish companionship. To speak is to share; to speak passes time.

Why are we penned this night like animals? Why have our jailers kept our arms and legs bound? Is this a temporary holding place? What will the morning and days following bring?

It doesn't matter for we are together.

Through the sleepless night we speak of our youth. James describes the time he pitched a no-hitter; Terry recalls that he usually struck out with the bases loaded. Through the light rain our experiences are felt and we are bound as one.

Through the sleepless night we speak of our coming of age. Charles describes his hangover the first time he got drunk; John recalls embarassment at buying condoms from a female pharmacist. Through the light rain our experiences are felt and we are bound as one.

Through the sleepless night we speak of our courtship. Thomas describes when he first held Laura's hand at a Kingston Trio concert; Mike recalls the restaurant setting where he proposed to Susan. Through the light rain our experiences are felt and we are bound as one.

Through the sleepless night we speak of our children. Edward describes how he taught his son to ride a bicycle; Mason recalls how beautiful his daughter looked the night of her prom. Through the light rain our experiences are felt and we are bound as one.

Through the sleepless night we speak of our parents. Frank describes how his dad worked two jobs to pay for his college tuition; Robert recalls his mother, how her kitchen smelled of freshly baked bead. Through the light rain our experiences are felt and we are bound as one.

Through the sleepless night we speak of honor. Samuel describes how his uncle died defending freedom at Iwo Jima; I recall with pride the names of men and women who served with me. Through the light rain our experiences are felt and we are bound as one.

The shafts of dawn's first light creep through the trees. Nameless, faceless, expressionless they come. Like in slow motion our bamboo cage door is opened. James, Charles, Thomas, Frank, Edward, and Samuel...they are taken.

Hands bound we cannot touch our comrades, we cannot. Led away across the forest clearing they disappear from view. We exchange glances; we hear volley fire..... Silence.

Dawn's first light has brought final recognition. Nameless, faceless, expressionless they return. Like in slow motion our bamboo cage door is opened. Robert, Mason, John, Mike, Terry, and I... we are taken.

Like in slow motion we are led across the forest clearing. I am flooded by memories, my senses are heightened. My time is near. I focus... hold the vision tightly:

Sky, bright, white clouds framed by azure. The sun at dawn, God's fire, warms and caresses my cheeks.

I focus... hold the memory tightly:

"The old home town looks the same, As I step down from the train. And there to meet me is my momma and poppa. Down the road I look and there runs Mary, Hair of gold and lips like cherries, it's good to touch, The green, green grass of home..."

Like in slow motion we are ordered to stop. I am flooded by memories, I am shrouded by a protective sense of calm. My time is near. I focus... hold the vision tightly:

Earth, cool from last night's rain; scent of spices in the air. Rhythmic sound of cicadas; visions of home.....

I focus... hold the memory tightly:

"The old house is still standing, Though the paint is cracked and dry. And there's that old oak tree that I used to play on. Down the lane I walk with my swe.......

The second, reflects the joy of family during recent years.....

FIGHTING CRABS

Hang Dao street at noon, north of Restored Sword Lake where vendors, old men and women converse, and children play. Smells and smoke from cooking fires fill the air, soft pastel light filters through the colored awnings that shade the broken tile sidewalk.

Two small boys engage in mock battle. They block the sidewalk where I attempt to pass. Each holds a fighting crab tethered by a thin nylon line. Words are spoken, threads jerked, and the crabs charge and feint in battle.

The brown-green prances, then advances with great pincers pawing the air. The smaller bluish-gray retreats amid the squeals of onlooking children and adults. Both crabs skittle sideways, the bluish-gray circles back and rushes the brown-green. Pincers click as street vendors and onlookers shout encouragement to their favorite combatant.

Little sister unaware of activities on the sidewalk outside father's shop slips through the door to join her brother in play. She runs down the cement steps, towards her brother, and reaches out to catch and hold onto his shirt for protection. She giggles and smiles at brother. He is annoyed by her distraction, jerks the line, and his crab retreats.

Quickly in one smooth motion, little brother turns, loops, and spins the long nylon thread around sister's body and binds her arms tightly.

Little sister thinking this a new game continues to giggle. Unable to move her arms she looks down between her legs and her smile turn to confusion. Unable to free her arms she looks upward into brother's face, then down again at the crab between her legs, which has now climbed onto her shoe.

Brother laughs. Little sister's confusion turns to tears and screams of fright.

Grandfather observes and speaks.

"Tran.... shame; release your sister quickly, now. "

Sheepishly, big brother unwinds the thread and frees little sister who runs to Grandfather's outstretched protective arms.

Grandfather strokes her cheek, then speaks softly:

"Tran... always be kind to little sister. She may become the mother of a nation. Crabs you find each day in the canal; little sisters are a national treasure."

Pincers upraised in defiance, Tran's crab remained in the center of the sidewalk looking to resume combat.

Onlookers drift off to other tasks.

The third and last, I wrote the day Viet Nam and the United States of America announced they would initiate diplomatic relations. I heard the news on the streets of Ha Noi.....

A NEW BEGINNING

Steel construction cranes rise high above the site that once caused men such pain and sorrow. Faceless ones now tell me that a new hotel, one that rises to a height of 15 stories and financed by Malaysia, will be erected on this site. Faceless ones now say that this hotel will be a new beginning.

Broken shards of jagged glass set along the tops of walls that once held men still glisten in the afternoon sun.

A wrecking ball smashes through one wall; trucks and men freely enter this place that once held men.

Soldiers drink their tea on the nearby sidewalk. They smile then ask me my mission as I pause to photograph this place. I explain. They turn to laugh and joke about new things, not of the past. They say that the hotel that rises here will be a new beginning.

I approach, then stand within the entrance gate to this place that once held men.

The driveway entrance to the site is soiled with mud and gravel, bits of garbage float here and there decorating potholes filled with last night's rain.

I dare not enter in this place that once held men. I cannot step inside, cannot. That action can be taken only by those who experienced the pain, who suffered the sorrow, who languished here. I cannot. I can only reach out and touch, feel the walls of brick and stone that once held men.

So it will be that from this place of darkness a hotel will rise. Will architects and engineers commemorate this place? Will they set forth within the lobby, upon the walls of polished granite-marble, a plaque with words for visitors to read, that on this place where now you freely stand, was a prison that once held men?

Another soldier approaches and asks my name, where from am I, and truthfully I reply. He offers both his hands, we shake, he senses sorrow, leaves me quietly within my thoughts.

My images still will not fade.

I see the men who languished here throughout seasons, throughout the years. I see their lost youth fading. I see their suffering, hear sounds when beatings, torture took their minds. I see the maggots, rotten food; the rats and lice; the cockroaches, mice. I see and smell the soiled toilets. I see the men shackled to their beds. I see the thinness of their ribs, their pale skin, their falling hair -- within these walls that once held men now torn down.

The pile driver pounds the earth and jars me back into the present.

I see cement poured into wooden frames; steel girders taking shape, a present vision soon to be replaced by sounds of tourist revelry.

Will tourists drinking in the hotel bar turn away from trivial conversation?

Will tourists at the registration desk care enough to ask the clerks or bellboys about this place?

Will tourists focus their thoughts back to the years when men were incarcerated here, within the place where now they clink glasses of scotch and say to their friends,

I say, old Chap," wasn't this once the Ha Noi Hilton?

I touch the former prison walls one last time, and recall the morning television broadcast from BBC....

"At the top of the news we have this announcement from Washington, D.C. Our BBC correspondent has learned from a high-ranking State Department Official that an announcement is expected at any time that diplomatic relations will be established between Viet Nam and the United States of America."

A new beginning.
