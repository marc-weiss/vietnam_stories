My name is Jana and I was born in 1947.

1. I was in college 1965-69 and grad school till 1974. 2. My most vivid memories of that era have to do with being involved in the anti war movement--at college with teach ins, working in new hampshire and mass in 1968 for McCarthy, participating in the march on the pentagon, but also spending a weekend with friends and learning later that one of the guys was killed in the war, after which his parents became very active in the antiwar movement. Probably my most searing memory is being with a friend at the pentagon march and having her freak out over the soldiers with fixed bayonets and she started screaming at the other demonstrators, I realized she needed help and really it seemed no one on either side could be very helpful--That really brought home to me that this was a terrible conflict that was tearing people up--not only the soldiers on both sides, but the people back home, whatever their positions on the war, it was a very sobering experience. 3. I think my involvement in the antiwar effort changed me in terms of the kind of career I sought. I went into college wanting to work in the foreign service, I changed to teaching in college. It made me much more cynical about state power. It also opened me to involvement in feminism. 4. How am I different today as a result? The war was the decisive event in my early adult life--I think it turned me toward a belief in grass-roots politics and trying to live my values, however partially I have done so. It also turned me inward--seeing all the anger and hate from both sides contributed me to wanting to heal myself. So the predictable years of therapy, which now in middle age has moved into more of a spiritual journey, finding direction in the writings of people like Henri Nouwen, Andrew de Mello, Thomas Keating. Also I think the political involvement and values that I took on in those years has helped me understand my teenage daughter's rebellion and anger today--both the healthy parts that are about fighting against injustice and hypocrisy and the unhealthy parts that are about fitting in with one's peers and demonizing one's "enemies."

I was really touched by the pov film on Maya Lin. Her work and being are inspirational.
