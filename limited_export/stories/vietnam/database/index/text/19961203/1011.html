After Kent State I just kind of felt—I was in college at that point—and I just remember thinking how unfair it was, so I got involved in AFSC and it was a real cultural thing, too, because I got involved with people and AFSC and started living in a commune which was great, people traveling through to speak about the war and going to demonstrations and it was just like my whole life for a long time, or I guess it was a fairly short time, considering. Went to Washington and I think one of my strongest memories— I have two really strong memories —and one was being arrested in Washington and being taken to jail. First they treated us to a tour of the city’s parkways in Washington—a tour, they took us in a bus and they took us around and pointed out the stuff, took us to jail, they had us fingerprinted and stuff and they took us to a jail and divided us up because there was so many of us.. And I remember they took people who were in the cells and put them all together. And it was the only time I had ever been arrested and I don’t think I’ll ever be arrested again because he had such a deep effect on me, what we were doing. It was just a real simple thing, we had overcrowded the jails and it just felt really wrong to me somehow. Whatever the memory.

Barbara Roberts

Dayton, OH

As told at the Dayton Story Circle
