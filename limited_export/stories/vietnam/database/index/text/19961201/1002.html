Subject: American invasion of S.E.Asia (1965-1973)

During the nine years of American invasion of southeast Asia, first in the former French colony of Vietnam, later in Laos and Cambodia, I worked as a physicist in industry and at a state college in New York. I was thirty-two years old in 1965 and was married with three small children. My family commitments and my work limited my participation in the anti-war movement, though I did make several trips to attend massive demonstrations in New York and in Washington, D.C. I should mention that during the Korean War I had been drafted and served in the U.S. Army. Fortunately, I was not in a combat zone. My chief frustration during the years of America's shame was the concentration of the media on the emotional opposition to the war rather than on the reasonable arguments, based on our own democratic principles and on the nationalistic character of the Vietnamese independence movement. One heard endless recitations of the "domino theory" by administration spokesmen, like Dean Rusk, McGeoge Bundy, Robert McNamara and by Lyndon Johnson himself, but the opportunities for voices like those of Tris Coffin, Doctor Spock, and others who could articulate the folly and disgrace of our government's actions were seldom heard on national media. Then, the assassination of Robert F. Kennedy left no effective presidential candidate on the Democratic side to oppose the war. We were left to choose between Lyndon Johnson's lackey, Humbert H. Humphrey, or Richard M. Nixon. Some choice! What we got was five more years of war! My chief frustration today is that we, Americans, have learned nothing from the terrible losses we suffered during those years of unforgiveable folly. The deaths of more than 58,000 American young men, the maiming of many more, the killing by bombardment, by napalm, by disease and starvation of two million Asians, the loss of more than $250 billion in national treasure, the ceding of our place in the markets of the world to Germany and Japan who overtook us during those precious, lost years of needless warring, all of these losses have had no consequences for those who were responsible for the war. Nixon and Johnson died leaving splendid memorial libraries paid for by the taxpayers. Nixon's funeral was attended by weeping dignitaries, including the current President. The Democratic and the Republican party are still the major political parties of the country. American militarism is alive and well: President Clinton is embarrased because he avoided (not evaded!) the draft. The "defense" budget is still, without a Soviet Union, of Cold War proportions. Only the American military have learned from the war in southeast Asia. They have learned to keep the media from reporting to us, the people, what is happening when American forces are committed to battle.
