The two most defining experiences of my life--and perhaps of my generation--were the Great Depression of the 1930's and World War II in the 1940's. Perhaps the third most compelling factor in my life has been the war in Vietnam and the Cold War struggle that provided the seed-bed of this tragically mistaken war.
As a new senator in 1963, I first spoke out against U.S. involvement in Vietnam in September of 1963. My concern over U.S. policy in Southeast Asia became an obsession by mid-1965. For the major part of a decade, the Vietnam War issue was the dominant factor in my Senate career. It was the effort to end that American involvement that led to my nomination as the Democratic candidate for President in 1972. The millions of people who flocked to my standard in 1972 did so in considerable part because they admired my vigorous and outspoken opposition to Washington's war in Vietnam.
That issue tore the Democratic Party and the nation asunder. The resulting division may have opened the way for me to win the Democratic presidential nomination, but it also contributed to my defeat in the general election.
If there is one effort in my long career in the U.S. Senate and as a presidential nominee that gives me the greatest sense of personal satisfaction, it is my long opposition to the war in Vietnam.

George McGovern
