Today, I am very different as a result of the "Vietnam Era". But first is the "Vietnam Era" over or still in process. And to whom is this question posed to? I am an American whose Viet Nam Era is still unfolding. I have been doing "business" in Viet Nam since October, 1968, when I arrived in Dong Ha, Quang Tri province via a USMC C-130 Hercules which kept rolling as we offloaded ourselves whilst NVA mortars peppered the ground around us. I was fresh out of the Vietnamese Language Short Course of 16 weeks, and had orders to report in at Ai Tu Airfield, later known as the Quang Tri Combat Base, over on the Provisional Marine Aircraft Group (ProvMAG-39). Soon the phrase "Quang Tri is Al Right" made macho meaning to me, when later I would fly back down to the southern most point I then knew in Viet Nam, Da Nang, Quang Nam province, or the Land of the Big PX. Viet Nam is and has been my part time home since 1993. I was one of the first Americans to ever return to the pre "doi moi" Viet Nam of 1985. That Viet Nam does no longer exist as the war-torn memory of wartime Viet Nam DOES NO LONGER EXIST. You see Viet Nam still affects my values, my relationships, my children, my politics, especially my livelihood,---- I am currently running a business there, BAI-VN, Ltd, a private consulting firm involved in business, tourism, transportation, culture, trade, education and such, that when I found this website, I felt as if I fell into like a Trojan Horse. My biggest contentiousness is with others either in America or throughout the world, for that matter, is for them to speak as if Viet Nam was an adjective, or adverb, and not a noun. For me Viet Nam is a proper noun. So as part one of what I, hopefully, can contribute is a continued narrative of what Viet Nam means for me TODAY and Tomorrow. About yesterday, I care little, because as the phrase "He has more yesterdays then tomorrows, little time remains for me to talk about yesterday, for yesterday is well-documented in the record. But TODAY is the blank screen here. So I will add but one recent story, found here below; and I welcome your feedback! Here it goes, in celebration of out tomorrows- FORMER FOES SMOKE PEACE PIPE IN VIET NAM DURING DEDICATION OF PEACE PARK (This article first appeared in Vietnamese in "Great Solidarity Newspaper" in 1996, in Ha Noi, Viet Nam) ( the bao Dai Doan Ket) by Joe Bangert*

Ha Noi, Viet Nam-In November,1995, on Veteran's Day, a caravan of Vietnamese and Americans, headed north, out of Ha Noi to dedicate "Veterans Peace Park" on a hillside in Song Mai village, Bac Giang town, in Ha Bac province, about forty miles north of Ha Noi. (Earlier in May,1995, I had the distinct pleasure of attending the ground-breaking ceremony with my then 10 year old son, Chris Bangert.) Included on one of the busses were twenty Vietnam veterans who hailed from such diverse locations as Texas, Hawaii, Florida , Wisconsin and Massachusetts. On this crisp autumn morning I was traveling with an old peace activist friend on mine from Tallahassee, Florida; Ed Deaton. ( Ed had flown into Ha Noi to help me open an American-style bar and restaurant "Cafe 1945" to coincide with Viet Nam's 50th Anniversary of Independence from France. But that is another story). As we arrived in Bac Giang town, in the distance, we could see the progress made since May, 1995. In the distance the outline of a giant white dove could be seen formed in the brownish red rice soil of the hillside. My heart throbbed with excited anticipation. That hilltop had been subjected to repeated American bombings during the late war.

This morning we saw about one hundred peasants gathering along with their children, bared footed, swarm around our approaching exotic shiny new vehicles. On such an occasion, work in the nearby rice paddy had stopped this morning. The children giggled upon seeing so many foreigners, in such unusual shapes and sizes, walking along the paddymoats and cresting the nearby hilltop. Along with us were our Vietnamese hosts, city slickers from Ha Noi, mostly "Veterans of the American war", or Vietnamese Vietnam veterans. Included was one man who brought the dream of Peace Park into fruition, a three-starred former Commanding General of the 308th Division of the People's Army of Viet Nam (PAVN).

The 308th Division was not only the victorious Viet Minh unit which captured the French Commanding General's bunker at Dien Bien Phu in 1954 during the first Indochina War, but the 308th (NVA) engaged in fierce fighting with U.S. Marines in Quang Tri province at a place called Khe Sanh, but later were also victorious in seizing Saigon in the Spring of 1975. We extended hands to shake and soon moved to hugs as we descended to the top of the hillside.

On the summit stood a wooden table, there were flowers and an offering of fruit, bottled spring water and a few bird cages covered with cloth. Soon the order rank out in staccato Vietnamese by my old friend Professor Nguyen Ngoc Hung, also a veteran-to start the plantings. Americans and Vietnamese rolled up their sleeves and tilled the soil and together, planted lovely shade trees and watered them very tenderly.

Upon completion of the planting a brief ceremony commenced David Giffey, a former U.S. Army photographer who had previously designed a similar Veterans Peace Park in Wisconsin, knelt and fumbled with matches and lit a smudging of fragrant herbs in a purification rite. The gray-haired veteran had once been assigned to a scout team operating in Tay Ninh and Song Be in 1965-1966 period, thirty years ago. Giffey then produced an aboriginal American peace pipe, and held it aloft-offering it to the four directions then gently touched the red earth of Bac Giang-lit it and passed it to his former enemies-gathered beside him, who in turn, passed the pipe in a circle of peace. Mike Boehm of Wisconsin then read a letter from the widow of Norman Morrison, a 31 year old Quaker who immolated himself in front of the office of then Secretary of Defense Robert S. Mac Namara in 1965 in Washington. Then my friend, Hung, asked me to read a very moving poem written by To Huu, a famous Viet poet, dedicated to Emily Morrison, Norman's five year old daughter. In the poem, also written in 1965, a rhetorical question is asked on behalf of a now fatherless child - "and MacNamara, where are you?" This line upon reading it aloud in English, moved me to immediate tears knowing full well that MacNamara, ironically, was also visiting Viet Nam, meeting and asking General Vo Nguyen Giap about the "truth" concerning the "Gulf of Tonkin" incident of 1964. MacNamara was not present on that now sanctified and purified hillside.

The closing ceremony of this dedication consisted of both Viet and U.S. veterans along with one civilian Viet lady freeing ten white doves. As these symbols of world peace flew on their way to freedom there was a humorous moment when one of the doves landed bullseye on the video camera lens belonging to a fellow former Marine, David Jones of Hawaii as he filmed the moving spectacle. As we were descending down the hillside together to the nearby commune of Song Mai village to share a simple lunch of green tea, riceballs and fruit and omnipresent Coca Cola - the Vietnamese lady who had freed the doves approached me. She complemented me on my Vietnamese and then tied a multi- colored string bracelet around both of my wrists. She told me in Vietnamese is was a custom of minority peoples in Viet Nam known as the "Black Thais" and signifies "good luck" and "long life". I noticed that her accent was distinctly "mien trung" or from central Viet Nam. During our communal luncheon Ed and I sat next to a wonderful woman named Betty Boardman, of Madison, Wisconsin. We quickly learned why she was with us. A peace heroine in her own right, Betty had braved high seas in a sailing vessel named the "Phoenix" in 1967 as part of a "Quaker Action Group" handelivering medical supplies to both north and south Vietnamese, and had personally witnessed and survived the wartime bombings of Haiphong and Hanoi. We veterans were all invited to spend the night with our former foes for an evening of celebration which included traditional "Cheo" folk opera and serious village partying. As Ed and I were heading back to Ha Noi Ed asked me "Joe- did you speak to the woman who was handing out the friendship bracelets?" I answered "Yeah just some small talk." He asked me if I could tell upon hearing her regional accent where she had come from. I told him central Viet Nam. Ed smiled and said "Joe- she came from Quang Ngai province, and is the only survivor left of the My Lai massacre!" I gently touched my two bracelets, their vibrant colors blurring from my tear-filled eyes of joy. It reminded me of a line from another poem, this one written by Ho Chi Minh while in a Chinese prison in 1944. "What could be more natural? After sorrow comes happiness!" I now deeply appreciated his sentiment.
