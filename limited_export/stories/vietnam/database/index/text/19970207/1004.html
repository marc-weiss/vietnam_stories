I was in Vietnam in 1967 to 1968. My duties were as an Army Medical Service Corps pilot to fly medical evacuation helicopters, call sign “DUSTOFF” and evacuate casualties to medical facilities. I saw and participated in the life and death struggle everyday with our mission to try to reduce the dying by rapid evacuation from the battle field. Here are some of my perspectives.

After completing my Army obligation, and finishing graduate school, I worked in hospital administration in Pensacola Florida. Because of my experiences, I took the mission to figure out how helicopters could be used in the civilian market effectively, cost justified and with patient care outcomes to improve mortality and morbidity. After several years of trial and error of system design, we successfully established a program, patterned after two other ventures in the United States, that was cost justified and saved lives. The key factor in our situation was that Pensacola was a small to medium sized market and when justified and proven in that community, the service then became viable to hundreds of cities nationwide. I was able to use the training and experience from my time in Vietnam to champion the cause with resulting programs that saved lives. This was a small return on the governments investment back to the civilian markets with direct results of saving peoples lives and reducing the suffering of thousands, because of the rapid response and transportation for medical treatment.

How I view life and the values can often relate to the circumstances at the time. Life and careers are not blueprints but are mosaics, modified with the facts at the time. In Vietnam, if I did not like the situation with enemy ground forces, I would change the situation with how I flew into the area, use of gun ship support, artillery support, etc. hopefully getting some of the odds in your favor. Assuming control of ones life and career and not expecting government or others ot take care of you, have similar parallels.

Another example was that myself and three other men entered the service on the same day, all four of us went through flight school together, were on the same plane to Vietnam, but then all assigned to different units. The results were: one was killed when he flew into the ground with night vertigo; one was seriously wounded flying into the DMZ and came back; the other disappeared in February 1968 and was never heard from although his aircraft was only one out of the four who came back on time but that generally represents the attrition rate of Dustoff pilots. Why I survived differently than the others may have been a matter of fate or managing the circumstances at the time.

Every generation seems to have a War to fight. We had Vietnam. Todays generation has the drug wars, urban violence and other serious challenges to their quality of life. I do not feel enough attention is given in the education systems, and understanding our national holidays, to recognize the many people who have served the country to preserve the freedoms we enjoy. The lives lost in all conflicts represents a gallant effort to step forward when our country has asked to be defended.
The cynicism of the federal government today, I believe, really started with President Johnson, micro managing the War with apparent little regard to winning. The political constraints seemed to be the overriding decision process rather than thinking of the person getting shot in the rice paddies and on mountain trails. If government officials/leaders have such little regard to their fellow citizen and the value of human life which is on the line, what else are they capable of? The ultimate seems to be the apparent abandonment of POWs. The only bright spots since Vietnam have been the Reagan and Bush years with their knowledge, experience and leadership that they would take care of their own combat soldier. President Clinton has no clue of what it is like to be shot at, in the ditch, or flying into heavy enemy fire, because of draft avoidance, etc.

In my opinion, contrary to the political efforts, Vietnam was a conflict that we won. The success of preserving HOPE and the opportunity of freedoms continues for so many people in Southeast Asia and other parts of the world because of the US forces. The communist world continues to crumble because of Hope for the future within a free society. We never really win wars but instead establish a new beginning for the future, based on the Hope for the freedom to live ones life without government interference.

Did the conflict affect my life? Most definitely yes. Does it have an enduring meaning today? Most definitely yes. I have seen, I have known, I have been there to be with the men and women dying, so that we in America can enjoy the freedoms in everyday life. Least we forget that legacy, we will have lost a bond of courage to face the difficulties in the future.



Vietnam Experience
Captain, US Army Medical Service Corps
57th Med Det. (HA)
Long Binh, Vietnam
1967-1968
