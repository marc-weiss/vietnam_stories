Peter, Paul and Mary sings a song called "Where have all the flowers gone". We were all just flowers, coming into bloom. Of one such flower, I would like to tell this story. He was like any young teen in the sixties. Athletic, young, full of piss and vinegar. He joined the Marines, because it was his thing to do. No one made him, he was just a patriot looking for a cause. He valued freedom above all else and was willing to go the extra mile for it. He left for Viet Nam in 1969 and eventually we met. i was point man and he normally walked behind me. everyone said not to make friends. But that was an absurd thing to say, since you cannot take a bunch of youth and throw them together under those circumstances and not expect to make friends. One truth about the Nam is that friendships did not last very long. After a couple months I was given the squad leaders job. That ended on April 30, 1969 when I was wounded and left. He stayed there. It was a short time later that I received a letter, telling me of the trajic accident he was involved in. One of those unexpected kind of things that only happens to someone else. The reports said that he was shot between the eyes with a pop flare. I never heard from him again. Life went on for me. I lived in Jacksonville, Florida and I knew he lived in Florida too, but did not know where. Along about 1976 I chose a career in law enforcement. I kind of moved around for a spell then settled back down in Florida. This time in Lakeland. In 1988 I thought I would try and look him up. I was a cop, I should be able to find anyone. I finally wrote the VA about him and anxiously waited to hear. Finally the letter came and very short and brief it stated, that he had died on May 5, 1972. I filed the letter away. I still did not know where in Florida he had lived. Just a few weeks ago after being on the net, I decided to start looking for a "why". I'm an investigator, its natural to want to know why. I ordered a copy of his death certificate. When it came in I found out that he had lived in Lakeland, where I live. I went to his old residence and even though all his next of kin are gone, I found someone who knew him. A friend from school. He told me a very sad tale about him. I figured this had to have made the local news. However it was twenty five years old. The library contained a mico-fish and I pulled up the newspaper article from May 5, 1972. It said that he was a college student wanting to study law, but something had happened to him. The flare had damaged his face and eyes. He had lost sight in one eye and was loosing sight in the other. There wasn't much help for blind people in 1972, especially young aspiring lawyers. He became depressed and on that fateful day in 1972 he ended what Viet Nam had started three years before. The article read "Veteran Found Dead". It told a sad tale of how a youn vibrant life had ended by hanging. I found his grave site and decided to attend it since it doesn't appear anyone else does. Am I different today? Oh yes. I've spent 21 years in law enforcement and I've seen a lot more death. But the faces of Nam I will never forget. I will never forget the ride in the chopper the day i was wounded. We had to ride with the KIA's to Danang. they were stacked like cord wood, young lifeless faces. Three hours before I was laughing and joking with these same faces. They were like little dolls with their fixed glass gazes into space. I wanted to touch them but they were not there. I wanted to touch my friend too, but he was not there. Nam had took him too.

Gary L. Bain Sr
1949
