When I am asked to speak at functions, Veterans meetings, traveling walls, I offer these words of what I hope are of encouragement and support. Meant with all the love a sister has for a brother, and a country. I have a plaque in my office - "The best Substitute for Brains is Silence". Wise words I take to heart.

I am his sister. I share that honor with four other lovely ladies from Texas. Barbara, Carlon, Bonnie and my little sister Lolli. My brother, Marine Lieutenant Allen Wesley Courtney Jr. was killed in hostile Action on May 27, 1968. He was a member of the Alpha/1/1. An only son, the best we had to offer. Our Hero.

How do you heal from losing someone that would have made such a major impact in your life? A Big brother would have come in very handy while I was struggling to finish school, and considering marrying the wrong man. I missed the sparkle in his eyes the day my children were born; a daughter and then, my own only son.

From the day two Marines came to our door, my family had a sense of loss nothing could fill. A loss so deep and so profound that my father died a year after we lost my brother, of a heart attack or was it a broken heart? My mother mourned the loss of her only son everyday till she joined my brother and father in the Houston National Veterans Cemetery. For twenty years his sisters accompanied her to this resting-place and stood above a grave of our Hero. My mother's most cherished possession was a small gold star.

Everyone loved my mother; she was the matriarch of our family line. The wisest, "Nanny". She lived to see her great grand children. My mother used to sing around the house, when she was sewing for us girls. Old gospel songs. I remember and can hear her voice so well, even now. Two of her favorites pretty well sum up how she taught her family to heal - "I will meet you in the Morning" and " A'int no grave gonna hold me down ". So well my mother knew these hymns, and so many times I know she was standing on the promise of this gospel. She taught me and my sisters and so many others who had the privilege of touching her life, these promises. And that is how we all heal. Standing on these same promises. That we will meet you in the morning because no grave can hold us down.

What legacy did the names on the Wall leave this nation? What did this conflict teach us as a civilization? What lessons do we carry through life because of this conflict? What do we want the names on the wall to say to a future nation of our children, grandchildren and all the other sons and daughters who will lead our country into the new millennium. How do we pass down the wisdom that we learned, the lessons of life, to those we have never met, and probably never will? How do you, as a citizen of the greatest country in the world, leave an impact on the future? What a wonderful age we live in! We are Free to voice our wisdom, to record it, to leave it as a legacy of our lives - I am speaking of the Internet. It is your voice to the world.

Our contribution is called FACES ON THE WALL. One of the largest Internet sites in the world with over 70,000 Pages dedicated to each name on that Wall. Our goal is to honor all of our heroes for future generations, by gathering photographs and biographies from you, the families and friends and the ones who were there. The firsthand accounts of the heroes we honor, not the politics or the pain.

We are trying to do our part to preserve the history. So that my grandson and yours will have a light that guides him through this life. The real stories of the heroic actions, the brave young men and women, the true history of this conflict. And by preserving these Faces and this true history that only we can preserve, we leave a legacy of learning, a legacy of dealing with loss, and a legacy of a country and of men so proud, so brave. That they gave their lives to uphold our freedom, and the freedom of many generations.

There is no ulterior motives, no political axes to grind, we don't solicit money, there will never be any charge to honor your hero on Viethero.com. We offer all the space you need. Take the pictures and memories and store them on this Internet site where they will never be lost, where old photographs of the young handsome Faces will never fade. It will stand for all time as the tool, which teaches the future what it really means to do the heroic action; to lay down your life if need be for your fellow man. A powerful legacy, an important lesson that will strengthen future generations, and teach them about the consequences of taking up a weapon. Maybe it will encourage a new breed of young minds that will first try to negotiate a peace, rather than answer in anger.

I was too young to care much about the politics of the Vietnam conflict. The political theories and reasons were an abstract thought that did not ease the feeling of loss. Though through the years I have read much about the conflict from many perspectives, I still do not have any opinions about the government's actions taken in so many political speeches. The only political statement I have ever made about this conflict was to a Senator from Texas. All I asked was that he visit FACES ON THE WALL, remember all these young heroes and then vote if we should put another generation in the same circumstances.

After 30 years, I still remember as if yesterday the early 1960's - my brother Allen in high school, wanting to be James Dean or maybe it was James Bond. A huge man of over 6'5", broad shoulders, a handsome smile, those big blue eyes. The jet-black hair he used to keep so neat with the comb he always had in his pocket. A football, basketball and baseball jock, lifting me on his shoulders as if I was a feather, believe me, I have never been as light as a feather. My little sister and I constantly hanging around, getting in the way, or being bribed to wash his car. It must have been so hard to be the only son of six children. Of course it seemed one of my older sisters was always feeding him, fussing at him, or him fussing at a girl ducking out of the one bathroom in our home. Can you just imagine living in a household of five sisters? His own personal fan club I assure you. He was kind to us little ones and very generous - all we had to do was ask and he seemed to have an extra nickel for candy - otherwise we would sit in the floor and throw a tantrum the likes of none you have ever witnessed - right in front of his latest girlfriend. He was always the game player, the instigator and the leader.

I was never worried about my brother adapting to the jungle or not exceeding at guerilla warfare. I had seen a mad band of girls chase him to the roof of our house - to await our parents return or whatever indiscretion to be forgiven. Or maybe a deal between he and his sisters struck, so that he could climb down to relative safety - he washed a lot of dishes in those days. Considering he used to finish his plate, my plate, my little sisters plate all the leftovers and still sneak down to the Orange Julius stand, I only worried about the men who were behind him in the chow line.

Allen was 9 years old when I was born; I was only 13 when he died. A brief span of happy times, wonderful love and acceptance, good memories and so many laughs. We remember our brother the same way you remember yours - chasing you around the house, all his girlfriends calling, all his great comic books. Because he was your brother or your son or your loved one, he was a part of you and so many good memories. Yes I remember the dances, the holidays, when he graduated from high school - so proud you thought your heart was going to burst. The day he folded up his large body and stuffed his processions in an old car and took off for college, to become a teacher or a football coach. The day you knew he had met the girl he was going to marry. Allen's bride had four sisters - something I will probably never understand. I know they adored him, because even though my brother had no children - all his girls named children after him - I know of at least 14 named either Allen or Wesley or Courtney, girls and boys - that's how I know he will never be forgotten. Through many generations my family has passed down these stories, memories, photographs, and a heritage of pride and courage; the tale of a reluctant warrior.

I don't want you to think Allen did not have brothers. He did. The young men used to flock to our front yard by the dozens. I am not sure if these boyhood friends hung around because he had beautiful sisters of dating age (referred to for all our years as the Courtney girls) or maybe it was because he loved them like brothers. I remember a day he returned from college - Why do you have to go? You are an only son; you do not have to go. I remember to this day my brother's answer - " I have to, all my friends are there. I am a Marine." My brother was exactly where he wanted to be, doing what he had to do. I remember well the last time I saw my brother, it was an early morning, he was home on leave to marry the girl he loved, and see his family. Trying to stay in condition, he was running around the block, and a little sister tried so desperately to keep up, him slowing down to run on the outside lane of the street, protecting me, I will always be his little sister.

He found all his brothers in Vietnam. I hear from them daily. After thirty long years of searching for the Courtney girls, thanks to the Internet and FACES ON THE WALL, they found us. They send me photos of my brother I had never seen, tell me all about his heroic actions and pretending he was John Wayne. They tell the real stories of the men who saved my brother's life more than once, they tell the stories of my brother saving their lives. They tell me that my brother is a part of our American History, a hero, like so many others of his time. They are Allen's brothers and they are my brothers too. Our bonds to close to ever let anyone forget. It was hard at first; hearing from the man that so many years ago was at my brother's side when he stepped on that land mine. So heartened to hear the reports that he had died also, were not true. Finding out one of Allen's commanding officers had named a son Steven Courtney Smith. A connection and bond with our lives that keeps healing an old wound, every time I get an email addressed to "Sis". Everytime I hear from another sister who lost a brother, everytime I meet a sweet lady wearing a gold star. Everytime I get a big hug from a Vietnam Vet, we heal.

I remember the first time I heard a twenty one-gun salute. And to this day it is hard for me to hear the taps play over a field of graves. People ask me why I have taken on such a huge impossible project as FACES ON THE WALL. There are 58,216 casualty records in my database. I always have the same answer. Let your voice be heard. Provide the memories and photos, we will preserve them, put them in a safe place for all in this world to learn from - another kind of flame that I hope will burn to eternity, so that you and I never have to stand over another dead soldiers grave. Because as a brother recently wrote me in an email - "I don't ever want to forget those that gave their lives for me and our country. And if we don't remind ourselves it is easy to forget."

We are at your Service,

Julia Lambert
