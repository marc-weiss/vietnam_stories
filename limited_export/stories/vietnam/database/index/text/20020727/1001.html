I enlisted in the Army,June 1968,as a,then believing, street-wise 18yr old from Brooklyn. Like many,I soon learned that my real education was about to begin. Arriving in Vietnam Dec.-68,I had allready started to question the beliefs guiding my life. I was to begin classes as a FNG,at a aviation co. based on the air field in PhuBai,RVN..My school mates,as was in basic and advanced training,were from different parts of the states,different colors,religions,enviorments.All of us infected with the bias,prejudice and streotyping instilled from others.They who daily claimed responsibility shaping our lives.As the year passed some minds,like mine,awakened to the realities and realizations our experiences were teaching us.Example: Talking with a fellow class mate,black and from the south,he kept using the common derogatory words, used to describe the people whose country was housing our school,as gooks,slopes,slopeheads.At that moment I turned to him and said: "Shut up nigger".It dawned on me,and happily on him, that the derogotory words we've learned to demean other humans becomes a part of a process.That which we make less than human,by the continual use of those words,terms etc.,can progress into a devauled respect for the lives of those humans.In the long run.No matter what the color of ones skin is.It all comes out red.As the school year was coming to a close I noticed some of my class mates were missing.I learned that a few of them got lost on one of the last field trips.Too bad. I know they would have aced the final exam.They sure picked up alot of learning on those trips.A few of the class are going to be left back. Oh its not because they did'nt learn anything.It appears that they don't want to continue the education at home.Somehow they feel more can be learned or achived by re-taking the course.Maybe get higher grades and help the FNG's along their way.Me and the others? Well we passed the finals.Some of us did better than others.I guess it all depended on how much research one got to do.Hopefully we will continue to embrace these hard learned lessons,the awakenings they evoked and instilled,and carry it truths as the guide in our continuing education thru life.I'm sorry if my ramblin' makes no sense to many. I sure if you were there you'd understand.Those of you who experienced one of those life altering episodes.(not being born again or some other religious manifestation) But where at that one moment in time all the pieces of the puzzle fit together and the picture is clear.More than an epiphany.A bond that manifests itself in that newly created part of your soul and its sensor alerts you to a kindred spirit.That is the hardest thing, for people without it,to truly understand what The Wall can evoke.So when you see one of my class mates,out there in the world,be kind,say thank you and accept the fact that he/we live and deal with a different set of truths.

Giacomo Liberatore
1950
Brooklyn,N.Y.
