This is a combination of hearsay and memories born of years of pride and years of regret. Do with it as you will. Raymond Dotson Dudley, NC 28333 THE CEASE FIRE AT QUON TRI Copyright (c) 1989 by Raymond Dotson

The Corporal picked up his Uzi and sat up, Buddha style, crossing his legs under him. "Goddam this filthy stinking place!" he said, just loud enough for his companions to hear.

There was no breeze... No air movement at all. The jungle humidity was so high that water continually dripped from pointed leaves of vines suspended from treetops high overhead. Sweat poured from the faces of three other young, bearded men lying quietly on their bellies in thick tangles of jungle growth. They were at the edge of a clearing, separated from each other by only a few feet. Each wore a mud stained khaki tee shirt and bag-pocket fatigue trousers in a random mottled pattern of olive drab, brown and black.

The Corporal tossed off his helmet and hooked sweat from his brow with a crooked forefinger. He looked at his hand, shook his head in disbelief, then slung the water from his fingers. He pulled a soggy khaki cloth from a hip pocket and tried to dry the salt from his burning eyes. He squinted as he held up grimy binoculars and swung them in a slow arc. There was no movement on the ground, only from a multicolored variety of birds flitting about in the trees. An occasional small monkey let out a raucous yell that mixed with the bird calls.

The other three soldiers held M-16 carbines and one had a large canvas bag, bulging at the sides with supplies. Grenades, looking somewhat like stunted pineapples, bristled from a webbed khaki belt clenched about the waist of each man. The stink of decaying vegetation, kept damp by the dripping foliage and heated by trapped tropic air, assaulted their nostrils.

"Henry, there ain't a damn person, Cong or other, in miles of here," said a soldier with a pockmarked face. "One could hear anybody moving through this crap a mile away."

"Bullshit!" said a soldier with tattooed arms. "Bullshit John. Ol' Cleve here could walk right past you and you wouldn't even see him, much less hear him. Ain't that right Cleve? Tell him, boy..."

"Right you are, buddy. I'm the best man-jack jungle sneaker in this man's army, by God. Hey, did I ever tell you about the time I snuck up on a meetin' of the Klan? Went right up to where they's meetin' and they didn't even..."

The Corporal stopped him in mid sentence. "Yes, Cleve. Only about a dozen times you told us."

"How about you?" the young black man asked, turning to his tattooed friend. "Did I tell you about that night?"

"Goddam it we've all heard how good you are. We all know how good you are, OK? Can you guys stow it for a while? We've got to decide what to do about that frappin' French Plantation house. Let me think, will you!"

The Corporal again raised the binoculars and studied a complex of buildings that lay at the edge of the open space, about three hundred yards away. Vines had grown over the doorways and windows of the main house but someone could have slithered through and could even now be inside watching the clearing.

Two small buildings lay further out in the clearing and another larger one, perhaps a storage house or servants quarters, sat just to the right of the main house. There was no sign of life anywhere. "We'd best wait a while and see if there's any movement in there," the Corporal said.

"Henry should open the package now," said the tattooed soldier. "That might be candy, in there, Henry. What if it's candy and you carrying it all over hell and back out here in the heat? Dammit, if that's candy and you let it ruin, we'll have your ass, by God."

"Yeah. What if it's candy, Henry?" echoed Cleve.

The Corporal looked down at the end of a gaily wrapped package protruding from his pocket. He smiled at their impatience. "It's not candy."

"How you know that? Did you sneak off and open it already? You wouldn't do that without telling us. You wouldn't, would you Henry?" asked the one with tattooed arms. They were all seated now, staring intently at the Corporal.

"I know it's not candy because it's not heavy enough. It's too light to be anything to eat, and besides, I can hear something tinkling inside when I shake it."

"So when you gonna open the damn thing? What are you waiting for?" this from the soldier with a pocked face.

"Do you guys know what day this is?" asked the Corporal.

They each looked around at the other inquiring faces. Finally Cleve flashed a toothy grin. "Hey, I know! I know what it is Henry! Hey you guys, it's Christmas. That's what day it is!"

"Christmas day," said the Corporal. "Today is December the twenty-fifth, Christmas day. Today is what I've been waiting for. Today we open the package. We'll just say it's for all of us."

"Can we open it now, Henry? Our package?"

"I was waiting until later. I'd hoped we could be inside the big house when we opened it. Hell, it's probably a pair of socks or..." he smiled, "or a damn necktie. It's from my Aunt Ethel back home on Cavanaugh Creek. My dad's Old Maid sister. It can't be much."

"Neckties and socks don't jingle for Christ sake. Come on, open it. Open it right damned now. We want our Christmas present now, don't we?" John looked to the others for support.

"Open the present!" they all chimed in.

Looking carefully at the decaying structures and still seeing no movement, the Corporal gingerly pulled the long thin box from his pocket. The corners had been broken down and the entire package was now limp from perspiration and jungle drippings. The paper wrapping was decorated with scenes from Currier and Ives, a snow covered country lane with a horse pulling a smiling, rosy cheeked young couple in an ornate sleigh. Each of the soldiers wanted to see the package so the Corporal passed it around before he carefully lifted the end flaps to exposed the contents.

"Balls!" said one.

"Decorations!" said another. "Goddam Christmas tree balls."

The Corporal smiled. "That's my Aunt Ethel for you. That's exactly the kind of thing she'd send."

"What in hell are we gonna do with damn Christmas balls in this stinking place?" asked John, the pock marked soldier. "Hang 'em on banana trees I guess? Or these palms, maybe? Shit! Throw the damn things away, I say."

"Some of them are broke. We'll give the broke ones to you, John." the Corporal said. "We'll throw yours away. Me, I'm going to hang mine on this palm. It'll be just as much Christmas tree as we want it to be. How about you guys? Want to hang them up here? We'll leave 'em here when we go on." Then, laughing, "Can't you just see Charlie some day come walking through here and find a palm tree growing red and silver balls?"

The Corporal took a fluorescent red ball and hung it near the top of a small palm with lacy fronds. He was reaching for another when a shot split the silence.

Instinctively, all four soldiers fell face down in the damp weeds. Hundreds of birds beat the air as they took flight, responding to the sharp bang of the rifle shot. Then all was quiet. Very still and quiet.

Without moving a muscle the Corporal asked, "Who took a hit?"

"Not me" Each of the three soldiers replied nervously. The Corporal very slowly raised his head and looked about. He had unconsciously grabbed the Uzi as he fell but he could find no target. Nothing moved but the confused, circling birds.

"Mer-ree Krees-mus, Joe." said a distant voice. The Corporal could not determine from where it came. They lay perfectly still.

"Mer-ree Krees-mus." said the voice again. "I make good shot, Joe?"

No response from the soldiers. This time, however, Henry the Corporal had caught the direction of the sound. Whoever was speaking was in one of the buildings across the clearing. The Corporal started sliding backward, trying to get deeper into the thick foliage. "Bastard's in the house," he said. "Get further back in the trees before he shoots your ass off."

"You no go, Joe" came the voice again. "This Krees-mus day. We have sis fire Krees-mus day? We no shoot on Krees-mus, OK Joe?"

"What the hell you know about Christmas, Charlie?"

"I know Krees-mus, Joe. I work this place for much years. I catch rub-ber for Damfrench. You know catch the rub-ber, Joe? Rub-ber for wheel?"

"The Goddam gook bastard wants to talk us to death." whispered Cleve.

"That's better than shooting us. Let him talk. Go on Henry, talk to the bastard," encouraged Phil.

"I don't know much about rubber plantations, Charlie," yelled the Corporal. "Why'd you shoot at us if you want a cease fire?"

"I no shoot at you, Joe. I shoot at you, you be dead GI. I shoot pretty red ball. You put new ball, I shoot new ball."

The soldiers all looked at the palm tree, now missing the one ornament it had sported.

"That's some damn good shooting, Charlie. But how do I know you won't shoot us instead of the balls?"

"Tomorrow I shoot you. Krees-mus I shoot balls. We have one day sis fire. OK Joe? I no shoot you. You no shoot me?"

"The little bastard might just mean what he says. I'm going to sit up. If he gets me, you guys burn his ass good. I'm sure he could get any one of us right now if he wanted to. The bastard sure can shoot." The Corporal slowly sat, as before, Buddha style. "My ass is puckered tight," he said. "I can feel the spot, just below my shoulder blades, where the son of a bitch is aiming right now. Lord, I hope he meant what he said."

Nothing happened. He waited for some further indication of Charlie's game. It's his rules, thought the Corporal. Let him make the next move.

"Put more ball in tree, Joe. I show you how I shoot good." The Corporal moved back to the tree and put a silver ball in the same place as before. Just as he pulled his hand away, he felt a puff of hot air and saw the ball disintegrate with a slight pop. It simply ceased to be.

He heard a rollicking belly laugh coming from one of the houses. "No sweat Joe. Is eees-eee. I no shoot good?"

"You shoot very good, Charlie. Probably the best I've ever seen." He put up another silver ball and, even though they must have been harder to see than the red ones, the cackling sure-shot plucked it, too, off the limb. Softer, to his men, "This frappin' little bastard could've gotten us all if he'd wanted. He's a real trick shot artist."

"Yeah, just think what a hell of a good time he'll have tomorrow," John said without humor.

"The son of a bitch will be picking us off tomorrow one by one." Cleve looked to Henry for approval. "Let's blow the bastard to hell and back right now. Let's not play his cat and mouse game now, knowing damn well he'll blow us away tomorrow."

"We ain't got much choice. If the bastard gets tired of shooting the balls off trees today, he can just as easy change the rules and have his cease fire another day. It's his Goddam rules we're playing by, and I don't like 'em!" The Corporal scratched his head in thought. "Tell you what; let's change the rules just a little bit more in our favor. Cleve, you're always telling us how you can sneak up on anyone at night. How are you in broad daylight?"

"What? Here? Here in the open? Hell, man, give me a chance, will you? I try to cut across there" he pointed to the clearing, "our potshot friend'll bang my can." He looked around at the thick foliage behind them. "Now back there, man, I could move through there with no sweat. Give me enough time to backtrack and circle 'round behind him, I could work my way down beside the house and blow him away. I'd need time though. Maybe a half hour."

Henry studied their situation at greater length. "We might be able to get one of us back into the thick of the trees if Charlie could still see the other three are staying put. It's worth a try." He shouted across the clearing, "Charlie. You got any water?"

The same cackling laugh came back. "No water, Joe. Got hooch. Find Damfrench hooch under house. Ver-ree good hooch. What you need water? Yank GI no can get water of big leaf? Big leaf have much water catch inside."

"Shit no, Charlie. We don't know how to live off the jungle like you guys. Our canteens are empty. Can we come and get water from your well? There must be a well there by the house."

"Have no water. No well at Damfrench house. Fall in long time now. But, ver-ree good hooch." More cackling.

"The son of a bitch is plastered!" Phil laughed. "Dumb ass drunk. On wine, probably."

"Yeah, but that ain't hurt his shooting none." Cleve retorted. "I can get him, Henry. I sure as hell can cream him if you can get him to let me go back in the jungle to get water. I'll cut around back there to the left and set a charge under him and he'll not ever know what hit him."

"OK, but it's your ass if he catches you. You best be good at it. One sound or one scared bird cuts loose and he'll get you for sure. Then it'll be us too. He's damn good, so you best be better than good." He watched Cleve for any sign of hesitation but found instead that the tall young Negro was strung out on adrenaline. He was psyched up on the thrill and danger of it, trembling with anticipation.

"I'll get him. I'll blow his jive ass off. Little gook son of a bitch. I'll get him, man. I'll cream him."

"You take a slab of the plastic dough. One slab ought to be enough to blow in the whole side of the house. Be careful though that you know where he is. We'll only get one go at him. Miss that and it's all over." Henry was planning the attack as he went along. "And Cleve, we got any more of the R.C. detonators?"

"Yeah, one, maybe two."

"Good. Set the charge with one of them, then get your ass back here but fast."

Cleve dug into the sack and pulled out a flat slab of gray oily substance much like children's play dough, sealed in a thin plastic bag. He hefted it for weight. "Sure this will be enough?"

"Joe!" from across the clearing. "Joe. You have red ball for tree? Put ball on tree, Joe. I shoot ball on sis fire day. No shoot Yank, today shoot ball."

"Get your stuff and get ready to go," the Corporal said under his breath. Then he shouted "We don't have any water Charlie. I have to send one man for water. Why don't you bring us some of your hooch? OK Charlie?"

"Hooch make Joe shitface. Damfrench hooch no make Charlie drunk." Then more cackling ending in coughing and sputtering. "You put ball on tree!" Very demanding now.

"OK Charlie," Henry replied. "Take it easy. We'll watch you shoot some more, but I don't have many balls. Can I put up paper? You can shoot paper can't you?"

Cleve stuffed the plastic and a tiny black box into his pocket. He made a big show of shaking a canteen, looking into the opening, then shaking it some more. He slung the strap over his bare shoulder and started backing slowly away from his friends.

"Where he go?" shouted the hidden enemy. "No go away. Stay where I see."

"He's just going for water Charlie. Damn it now, you take it easy. I told you we don't have no water. He'll be back when he gets us some water. You have the hooch and we don't have any. We got to have water. OK Charlie? OK we get some water so we have something to drink on this special cease fire day? We ain't got no hooch like you, Charlie." Then quietly, "Keep moving slow. Don't hurry. He'll let you go."

"Glad you so Goddam sure," Cleve retorted, his voice edgy.

Henry tore a small corner from the Currier and Ives print. "Look here Charlie. Here's a piece of pretty paper. I'll hang a little piece on the tree for you. There. How's that? Think you can hit that, Charlie?"

The only response was a loud crack from across the open space and a whisper as the paper disappeared.

"Very good Charlie. Very, very good. You're the damn best shot I ever saw." He sat tearing the paper into small pieces. Each time the unseen man demanded, he hung up another piece. Each time the paper disappeared in a puff of hot air. Henry was killing time with each small delay. "Where did you learn to shoot so good?"

"I work for Damfrench in big house for long time." He spoke more slowly, his voice more hoarse. "Damfrench show me to shoot gun. Damfrench show me to be Chreesh-tun. I know Jesus Christ, Joe. I am Chreesh-tun since be little boy." Henry looked at the others in turn. They had never heard of a Christian Vietnamese. After a short pause they hear the sound of a breaking bottle.

"Are you drinking up all the hooch Charlie?"

"Drink all... hooch pretty dam quick. One bottle more."

"Look there" whispered Phil, excitedly. "There by the big outhouse. Is that sneaky shit there already? Yes, by God, that's him squatting there by the side of the outhouse. He's doing it."

"Don't nobody use binoculars. Charlie's got to have a damn good scope on his sniper's rifle. He can see us very clear. Let's just keep him busy showing off so he won't start shooting us instead. We have to keep things going for a while longer." He hung more paper.

After a few more delays and a few more shots, they heard Cleve coming back into the clearing. He came strutting in, slinging the canteen by it's canvas strap. "Did you guys hear what he said? He's no damn Buddhist, man, he's a Christian. I didn't know they had any Christians gooks. You still gonna cream him, Henry?"

"What the hell do you think? Of course we'll cream him. We got no choice, as I see it. Only lets us let him have a little more fun and get a little drunker. The bastard hasn't missed a single shot yet. I never heard of anyone shooting that good." The Corporal smiled, a strange twist appeared at the corners of his mouth. "But let him miss just one ..."

He reached for the canteen and took a long draw at it's contents. Then, as he handed it back, he took a small black box from Cleve. It was about two by four inches and an inch thick with a toggle switch and a pushbutton. A small diameter wire pulled easily from one side and hung limp, almost to the ground. The Corporal was careful to keep the box at his side, opposite the houses.

"Tell me more about when the French were here," the Corporal asked. "Did you say you gathered rubber for them?"

"Damfrench more bad American. I no like. I no like American now. Why you no good, Joe?" His voice was thicker and he spoke slower and more carefully.

"Hell, Charlie, we're not bad people. I have a wife and a son. My son is named Henry too. We live in a small house and my wife works and I work... Well, I did work before this," he spread his arms to indicate the jungle.

Another shot, another missing piece of paper.

"Why you here? Why you come here to kill my people, Joe?" A slight pause, then "Why we have to kill other, you... me?"

"Because our government sends us out to do it, I suppose. Because we both have responsibilities. Do you understand that?"

"I not here for gover... gover-mint. Nixon crazy. You come kill my people... I come kill you. You go home to wife and boy, Joe. You go home now. Go away... So I no have to shoot you tomorrow like pretty paper. Like little red ball. I no have to kill you too."

"I can't do that, Charlie, you know that. Not now. Maybe tomorrow my people and your people will have a big cease fire for everybody. Nixon was just elected again for four more years. Maybe he'll do it now. We'll wait and see tomorrow."

"Tomorrow not come for too many my people. Too many my people die already. No hurt you, Joe. No burn you home, kill you wife, kill you boy." He paused, perhaps to have another drink. "What you name, Joe?"

"My name is Henry. This one over here is Phil and that one is John. Cleve here went to get the water."

"Name... Is like On-ree? Like Damfrench On-ree? Damfrench bad. You Damfrench, or you American?"

"I'm American. All of us here are American. My name is Henry. Hen--ree. Why don't you come out here, Charlie. Go with us back to our big camp. We can tell them you are our friend. We'll take good care of you."

"No can do, GI." he said. Then another shot. This time the paper still hung in the tree. They all looked at it, then slowly they turned to look at Henry, questioning.

"You missed! By God, Charlie, you finally missed one. You must be drunk Charlie. Too much hooch."

"Dam dam dam dam" they heard.

Henry looked toward the buildings across the clearing. "Sorry, Charlie" he said, and pressed the very tiny red button on the black box.

The entire side of the largest out-building buckled inward, then seemed to break violently into mortar dust and stones. The other walls blew out and the roof first raised a few inches into the air, then dropped into the remainder of the crumbling rubble. The entire explosion sequence seemed to happen in slow motion and in absolute quiet, but by the time the debris had started to settle back down, the shock wave hit them. It was a terribly loud sound, but there was almost no smoke.

Long after the sound wave had died away there remained a total quietness throughout their part of the jungle. Even the birds and monkeys who normally squawked protests at the slightest sound, were quiet. And there were no cheers from the American soldiers. Henry heard a muted "My God" and a quiet "Damn," but there was mostly a silent reverence. The jungle had been commanded to stillness.

And the stillness remained for some two or three minutes. By then the dust also had fallen back to earth since there was no wind to move it away.

Cleve saw Henry wink one eye as another shot broke the unnatural stillness of the dripping jungle. One side of Henry's head wasn't there any more. He squatted where he had stood. One second he was standing, the next his legs folded and he knelt as in prayer, sitting on his heels. Finally he toppled over on his side. There were only a few tremors as his muscles jerked involuntarily.

The three others, when they were finally able to move after the shock of seeing their Corporal downed, found the small brown man amidst the rubble of the collapsed building. He lay partially out into the greenness of his jungle, his upper body ripped apart. He still held the long barreled Russian sniper's rifle. When they prodded him with the muzzle of an M-16, his head rolled to one side and he looked into their angry eyes from a torn and bloody face.

"... Krees-mus?" he whispered.

Later, the soldiers loaded the stiffening body of their dead companion, Corporal Henry Casey from the far-away Kentucky backwoods village of Cavanaugh Creek, onto a rescue helicopter that had been called into the clearing.

Their original mission had been to make certain no one was hiding in the plantation houses. After fully satisfying those requirements, they boarded the helicopter and lifted away.

Soon the roar of the chopper engine and the whup-whup-whup of the whirling blades faded into the distance. After a short while, the multicolored flitting birds and the small scampering monkeys resumed their chatter.

On the lacy fronds of a small palm tree hung one silver and three red balls under a golden crucifix taken from the neck of a small dead enemy.
