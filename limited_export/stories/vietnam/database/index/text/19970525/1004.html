During the years of The Vietnam War I was a young fellow living in the Northeast of Brazil in a beautiful city called Recife (state of Pernambuco). Our country by then was under a very harsh military dictatorship, submitted to the policy of the US and its paranoid sense of persecution that would last through the era of the cold war. Our particular military dictatorship held firmly for 21 years, from 1964 to 1985. That is considered a very special period of history when uncle Sam was still addicted to the "big stick" diplomacy. Any method would comply with any excusable justification of the moment. So, we were under dictatorships not only in Brazil, but in Argentina, Uruguay, Chile, etc., and in the meantime the North American youths were dying in Vietnam. In the cited countries some leaders and the young people were dying as well: tortured and killed by their government, police and agents trained by CIA to defend the world against the danger of communism. At the same time, fortunes were being amassed by those who were in power and took the opportunity to simply loot and steal public property, while being hidden by statements of purity and decency. Recently we saw one of them being ousted, Mobutu Sese Sese Seko from Zaire. During The Vietnam War years I met many young soldiers who came in through the port of Recife in their long journey from the United States to their predicament. I talked to a lot of them, trying to understand their language, and listening to their stories and their conceptions and dreams about the world. For me it was a fascinating world. They all came in imposing big ships, wearing impressive white uniforms for a brief stay in our capital. I never thought of one of them being killed by a bomb, a shot or a mine, because I myself was inhabiting an abstract planet, absorbed by private ambitions and aspirations that occupies adolescents. That was more than 30 years ago! Now and then I remember a young Marine whom I once met in the Central Post Office of my city, and who was asking for help to buy a stamp in order to send a post card to his family back in the States. This image can clearly be recollected, because I had the company of a cousin who was a very promising pianist. After changing some words, my cousin found out that the young Marine once studied piano and that he knew most of the important classical composers. We became friends and had a very good time exchanging ideas and commentaries. A couple of days after leaving our city the young Marine send a post card to us, in which he wrote about his pleasing remembrances of his passage through Recife. Then, one day, in response to a note he had sent to the young fellow with some negatives and prints, my cousin received a document from the US government communicating that the young man had been killed. It was only in that precise moment that we learned everything about the meaning of Vietnam War.

Márcio Accioly
1948
Brasilia/DF -- Brazil
