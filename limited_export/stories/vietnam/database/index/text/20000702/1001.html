I was a career officer in the army. These thoughts are 30 years old and are the best I can remember. I arrived in Viet Nam and although I was a field artillery officer, I was recruited as an adjutant for an aviation battalion. After a couple of months I was able to wrangle a job with an artillery battalion. My first assignment was as a liason officer for Norm Scwartzkopf. This was, in retrospect, an assignment which changed the way I felt about life. Schwartzkoph had a very unique way of motivating people to do as he wanted, ( if you served with him you know what I mean, if not its indescribable) He instilled a fear of himself which was greater than a fear of the VC. To the untrained eye, he was a oaf and a brute who ruled with fear as his ally. Most of his company commanders an platoon leaders did not understand his rigid requirements for flak jackets and helmets in the field. As an outsider I realised he was trying to save lives. I didn't always agree with his philosophy, but later in the war, as a battery commander I mimmicked his behavior and drove my people to do the things that would protect them from enemy morter attacks and give them a place to hide if they needed it.

I don't know how many lives then LTC H. Norman Schwartzcoph saved in his infantry battalion by being unrelenting in his insistance on wearing helmets and flak jackets, But I know that in my artillery battery he saved several lives by the lessons he taught me. I WILL NEVER FORGET!!

Gary M. Biehl
1944
Kelso, WA USA
