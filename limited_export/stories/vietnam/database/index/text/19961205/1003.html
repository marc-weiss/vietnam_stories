I don't know how to add to the story I wrote the other night but I guess I'll just try to pick it up from there. As concerned as I might have been, or thought that I should have been, sleep came like a sledge. It seemed as though I had just closed my eyes when someone was yelling at me. Get up, wake the fuck up man. I was being shaken from my nappie. I opened up my eyes and there was Kraus. I said "Kraus, man what are you doing here, they said you were dead man." "Fuck no man, I ain't dead, they can't kill me." It was a cause for a celebration, though everything was a cause for a celebration. I was really glad that Kraus was still with us. Dickie had another day with us because the runway was torn up and no flights were going out. I started on a tremendous bender during which I assisted the seabees in tearing down what was left of our hootches with my bare hands. Joe was a little prone to excess in his younger days. We all kind of let off steam after the attack, our tour was nearing its end we had had a touch with war. We were airwing Marines in a fighter squadron, we didn't see any of the field that our less fortunate troops did, a fact that I try to make no apologies for. I went where they sent me and was pretty good at what I was asked to do. In the first part of our tour we had alot of problems with our planes, paticularly the yaw stab systems and generators. Because of my diminuitive stature I soon became the resident expert on installing yaw accelerometers. An electrician named Bishop and I also became the generator and CSD experts so even though I lacked discipline I contributed. In a short period of time we had the bugs worked out of the planes and they flew wonderfully. By the time we were ready to leave, our main maintenance problem was changing wing tip light bulbs. The F-8 was a great bird, but the wing tip bulbs had to be designed by someone with a brother=in-law in the bulb business. We all wrote letters home letting our folks know we were ok and anxiously awaited seeing newspaper accounts of what had occurred. They never came, we were small potatoes in the world news of that period. The Six-Day War had captured the news. There were rumors that we would be going there next, but there were always rumors. We also heard very little about the riots that were tearing our country apart. I had no idea how extensive the riots were until I returned home and got the stories from my friends. As it turns out the riots had actually started in a town just a few miles from where I lived in Plainfield, N.J. By a strange quirk I had a connection to part of the story. I had dated a girl named Joanne Silvestri from N. Plainfield before I left for Hawaii. Her uncle was a cop in Plainfield. The way I heard it was that he he was invesigating an accident in an area was nearly all black, it was very hot and very humid, that made everything worse than usual. An argument started and soon her uncle was attacked, he was "beaten and stomped to death with shopping carts". From there it was all downhill, one town after another all over the country blew up. I say in honesty that Ray Giuliano a buddy of mine in the National Guard saw more small arms fire in Newark than I did in Vietnam. I was amazed to hear that tanks were firing on the buildings along Springfield Ave. with 50mm. machine guns. Fifties are not to be used on military personnel in combat according to the Geneva conventions of war and they were being used on apartments in Newark. I don't know what it is like now in that area, but up until a few years ago I know that many of the buildings have been left vacant and still full of bullet holes as a reminder of that summer. In the beginning of this story I said that the summer of 1967, particularly July was a turning point for the nation. I consider the Six-Day war and the riots two key factors in my thesis. A third is the Sgt. Pepper album, and the accompanying changes in the lives of America's youth that accompanied it. The first time I heard the album was in a corporal tent. Our tents were segregated by rank overtly, and segregated to a lesser degree covertly. I was a sgt. and was in one of the truly integrated tents for that rank, and I am genuinely glad I was in such a tent. In retrospect I think the frienships that developed in our tent served as an example for anyone that cared to look at us. Our squadron had no racial problems during a period when in some other outfits people were getting clubbed with two by fours coming out of the shitter. That period of time time has served as my own example of how harmony can be achieved under difficult conditions, again I should add, we had it easy. I had reenlisted for four years while in country so I received my orders earlier than the others. I had asked for embassy duty in Scandinavia, floyd Bennett NAS, or Beaufort,SC. I had to put a majhor duty station as my third choice so naturally I got orders to Beaufort. I left nearly 3 weeks before the rest of the squadron because I was going home by ship. They were doing a cost cutting operation so I lucked out. I came home on the Gen, John Pope. We arrived in San Diego and the docks were lined with cheering crowds and young school children. Once again for some reason what I experienced was quite different from the reception most got when they arived in the world. It wasn't until a week later in San Francisco that I saw the type of reception most had, abuse. I flew back to Newark next to a hippie that wqs into a new religion, 2 parts Christianity,3 parts Judaism, and 1 part Hinduism. As far as I could tell it was 100 % bullshit, and the only thing with meaning was the money his Canadian girlfriend had that allowed him his intellectual freedom. I think the guy dosed me up, take this brother,may it serve you well became a phrase heard quite often back in those days. This becomes another key factor in the change that took place during that summer. The country I returned to was vastly different to the one I left and drugs were a big factor. We had very little drug use in our squadron until just before we rotated. I smnoked herb there for the first time in my life,and I really liked it. It was not until years later that I smoked it regularly.

Joe Byelick
1946
