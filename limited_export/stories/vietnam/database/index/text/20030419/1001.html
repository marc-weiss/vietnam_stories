The Fishermans Troubled Odyssey

I have three treasures. Guard and keep them: The first is deep love, The second is frugality, And the third is not to dare to be ahead of the world. Because of deep love, one is courageous. Because of frugality, one is generous. Because of not daring to be ahead of the world, one becomes the leader of the world. --Lao-tzu

Ever since I was a little boy, my father had always encouraged me to accompany him to watch the sun rise with him whenever he went fishing. I seldom went. With the increasing emergence of technology, I grew up indoors alongside the guidance of videogames, television, and computers. This is perhaps where we draw the changing point; the line where we can see the transformation of culture in the new-bred generation. Perhaps the last seedlings of a yellow jasmine flower. While given new life, being winded to a foreign location, and can no longer blossom in the same garden cultivated by the uniform surroundings of its prior generations. From there, its new roots developed in a new land.

I grew up in America, an interval of an entire ocean, thousands and thousands of miles away from my parentsĺ homeland in Vietnam. As the youngest of nine children, and the only one born in America, I have lived a life free from the wrenching grasps of poverty and hunger. There has always been food on the table and I had a proficient education, as opposed to my fatherĺs life in Vietnam. I had never stopped to think about how the abundance of opportunities that arose specifically for my siblings and I. One early morning, as the sun began to rise I found my father standing along the edge of the darkened horizon. From there, a story of undying hope and struggle began to blossom.

My father, Nam Van Tran, lived in a land of consistent turmoil. Vietnam has a history of almost constant wars: a thousand years under Chinese domination, a hundred years of French reign, and thirty years of brotherly fighting. Influenced by the war, the Vietnamese people have been anxious to live in peace. During the Vietnam War era in the late 60ĺs, my father served in the Vietnamese army on the battlefields as 1st lieutenant of his battalion, directly opposite to the communists. By 1975, the communists occupied South Vietnam. His participation in the war led to his arrest where he was a prisoner of war for almost 3 years. The struggle against the maltreatment of dictatorial regime continued. The communists, having no tolerance for anyone who did not obey their cruel laws, imprisoned and killed their opposition. It was then, where he decided to become a fisherman. That advantageous position allowed for an easy escape by boat. With nothing but a suitcase and deep love, my father guided them to a small fishing boat geared for freedom. The small boat, containing a total of 45 persons, left Vietnam on May 29th, 1981, adding to the 14,300 other persons that left that month alone. The boat drifted aimlessly into the open sea, with a depleted supply of food and water. They ran into heavy storms, typhoons, and ruthless pirates. On the brink of death, hope was all that my humble father had, embodied by the vigorous glowing sun. After 15 days and 15 nights, they finally arrived in the Philippines. Soon after, they fled to America, where they settled and where I was born.

There has always been an emptiness inside of me, longing for connection; for understanding; for knowledge; for ultimately finding my niche in the world. I now understand why my father had always encouraged me to watch the sun rise. For him, it was his source of hope and will. An undying hope to provide a life for his children shielded away from the ugliness of humankind; A will that guided him through escaping, and surviving through the storms so that he would be able to provide for his family. Itĺs rising sun, carrying with it rising opportunities, so that I am able to go to college and provide a life for his future grandchildren. Ultimately, each individual occurring sunrise, with the early morning radiance of 6 AM progressively climbing past the shadows, is a sign of a new beginning.

Today, as the convergence of technology and life continues, the survival of my own heritage provides a difficult journey. For me, the sun is a remembrance of where I came from beyond my technologically cultivated life. As the jasmine flower slowly withers, and as the sun sets, my father turns the age of 60, and his generation fades beneath the horizon. Regardless, there will always be a genial, golden warmth within the story of the fishermanĺs troubled odyssey.
