The war in Vietnam, as for so many fellow Americans, became the pivot around which my life turned in those years. It seemed an endless, furious round of demonstrations, teach-ins, traveling and speaking everywhere. I went to Japan in 1966 with fellow SNCC member, Ralph Featherstone (later blown up in his car), speaking in 13 cities in 14 days. I flew to North Vietnam with the poet-priest Daniel Berrigan during the Tet offensive of 1968, to bring back three fliers released by the North Vietnamese. I was arrested a number of times, and testified many times at the trials of other defendants.

Through it all, I was teaching my classes at Boston University. And a profoundly important lesson -- which I first learned while teaching in a black college in the South during the civil rights movement -- was reinforced for me: that I could not, must not, be simply a classroom teacher, while terrible events swirled through the world outside. I had to leave the classroom, bringing students with me (or following them) and constantly make connections between what was going on outside and what we were talking about in the classroom.

I wanted to tell my students again and again: to stand aside, or to bury myself in “scholarly” work, while bombs were falling in Vietnam and children were burning and body bags were coming home by the thousands, was to collaborate with what was happening. It was clear to me that my students learned more by how I acted than what I said, and if I wanted them to become active citizens, engaged in a constant struggle for peace and justice, I had to set an example, even in the small things I did.

I became acutely aware of what Rousseau once pointed out, in a time when France desperately needed change and the Old Regime seemed entrenched.

He said (to paraphrase him) that “we have doctors and lawyers and businesspeople and engineers; but where are the citizens among us?”

Howard Zinn
