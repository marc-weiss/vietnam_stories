When I dropped out of college in 1968, it was as if I had written my own death sentence. I was reclassified "1 A" for the draft and such news convinced my father that I was doomed. In spite of his participation in the Air Force in WWII, Vietnam was an anathema to him and his youngest son was to die there. A lawyer was found who guided me in the process of convincing the Army that I was too crazy to serve in the military. Three years later, I was reclassified "4 F" and pronounced too crazy to serve in the military. All that time I was exposed to Vets who came back from "Nam" who were clearly too crazy to survive life back in the States. They would sit next to me in college during my frequent reentries to academiae; these were former Green Berets who had killed the enemy with bare hands. These were shadows of former consciousness who would copy my exam answers and silently sit next to me at lunch, never discussing the past. Yes, we know of Agent Orange; and yes we know about Cambodia; and My Lai, and war protestors abusing our returning troops, and the failure of this country to take proper care of the crippled returnees. I didn't go. I didn't take refuge in Canada. I didn't have to wait for my country to give me amnesty. But I greave the fallen and the mamed. A generation of young men, my generation, torn to shreds by the platitudes of politicians and Generals "All we are saying, is give peace a chance."

Benjamin Sperber
1950
Los Angeles, CA.
