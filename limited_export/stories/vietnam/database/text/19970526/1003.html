In 1967 I was delivering newspapers in Framingham, Massachusetts. My memory is brief here. Every day I would open up the bundle of newspapers. On the front page, at the bottom was a box. Vietnam casualties. Americans Dead: Americans Wounded in Action: Viet Cong Dead: . I just remember watching the numbers day after day. Sometime they were very big numbers. Maybe 400 Americans dead in one day. I worried about getting into a car one day and being taken to the airport to be flown to vietnam to help fight the war. It seemed so likely a person would die if they went to vietnam. Around when I was 14 the war ended I guess. And a time came when there were no more Vietnam casualty numbers on the front page of the local paper.

I often think of the long happy summers our family had while the war carried on far away. The barbecues and happy times on the beeches of Cape Cod. All those things going on here, while simultaneously, many Americans were in the most unbearable situations in vietnam. Wounded in the jungle, maybe being starved and tortured while we all just carried on, really enjoyed our lives. It seemed like our world was oblivious to what other Americans were suffering for our sake supposedly, in Vietnam. Most adults didn't talk to kids much about what was going on. We really didn't get it. Life in Framingham, Massachesetts in the 1960s was so prosperous, safe and just ordinary. All the adults had good jobs. All the kids at school lived in nice homes, in nice neighborhoods.

What a contrast between our lives and the lives of the soldiers in vietnam. It doesn't seem right. I feel guilty somehow that we should all have been doing something for the men and women in vietnam. Like we all should have been more personally invested in the war, contributing, personally sacrificing. Those Americans over there must have felt so personally isolated from us Americans back here. Maybe they felt abandoned and forgotten about.

Rather than one select group of our society doing it all, suffering it all, sacrificing everything while the rest sacrificed nothing. I don't mean to downplay what families suffered when they lost a family member.

I feel a heartfelt connection to that time, to the men who suffered and died over there while I was busy growing up in such a great, safe world here. Those times in the 1960s had a flavor which only those who had been alive could have sensed. Books and movies couldn't convey it to you. Sometimes certain songs from that era bring back the flavor or feel of the 1960s. It's really fading for me now. But when I see pictures of American soldiers in Vietnam, and hear the music, it's like I'm back in that time all over again. There's something powerful and emotional about that time which make these times in the 1990s seem flat and irrelevant.

A CAPTIVATING DISCUSSION ON VIETNAM: I have discovered what was for me a very powerful discussion of the Vietnam War in Chapter 6 of a book by M.Scott Peck. The book is "People of the Lie. The Hope For Healing Human Evil" In chapter 6 there begins an examination of the Vietnam conflict, specifically Mylai. But really it covers the whole Vietnam involvement and without saying much more, I strongly urge people to read it. It looks at war, the military and group evil. This is my contribution. Small compared to the great involvements of guys who were born just 9 years before me.

-Rick Allen eugen, Oregon. May 1997
