Soldiers spend most of their time waiting. In the military patience is more than a virtue, it is a vital tool. In the fall of 1971 I sat idle at a fire base for several hours, kept company by a small Vietnamese boy. It was a completely desolate place, thoroughly defoliated and pockmarked with shell holes. On the flight in I was stunned to see for the first time how large the defoliated areas actually were and how completely devastating these powerful chemicals could be. There was simply not anything green remaining at all. What had been a lush tropical jungle looked from the air like a forest of huge tooth picks in a desert. The boy had wandered in from the surrounding waste land and casually approached our helicopter. Alone and dressed in nothing more than filthy rags it was obvious he was quite ill. I guessed his age to be about 12 years and already most of his teeth were gone and the rest rotting. He smelled awful, his stomach sticking out like a small balloon. We exchanged names and I showed him the picture of my wife I kept carefully wrapped in plastic in my pocket. He showed me a figure he had carved from a piece of soft wood using a sharpened spoke from a Honda motor bike wheel. It was the image of a woman and was quite nicely done. I would learn later that the name he had given his work was not a name but the Vietnamese word for grandmother. I lifted him up into the helicopter and set him next to me on the door gunners seat. We shared a C ration pound cake and then he curled up next to me with his head on my arm and went to sleep. I could see now how weak he was and it occurred to me that he would soon be dead. He clung to my arm while he slept, savoring a few moments of companionship in the shade. When it was time to go I set him down on the ground and offered another can of C rations. Without hesitation he offered his carving in return but was delighted and relieved when I handed it back. He stood near the spot where we met as the helicopter turned to fly away and I wondered where he had come from and where he would go. There was nothing but moon scape for many miles in any direction.

In the years since we met I have often thought of this small boy and his quiet dignity. Like so many others my experience as a combat soldier in Vietnam left me feeling bitter, frustrated, and more than a little confused. The little fellows courage has been an inspiration to me and it has been comforting to me, comforting out of all proportion to the actual event, that I knew him and was able for at least a moment to offer some simple human kindness. I have even imagined that some day I would go back there and find him, that some how he would still be there, waiting.

Chuck Sowards
