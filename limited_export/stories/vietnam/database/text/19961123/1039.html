REAL TIME AND THE REFUGEE

I know now why I cannot travel light. Twenty one years after my arrival to the United States, I have at last been acclimated to Fall and Winter in America. I have finally gotten used to my luggage.

Twenty one years ago, I left Saigon as a thirteen year old teenager with two small leather bags containing my best Sunday clothes, a pink tropical silk blouse, a pair of black bell bottoms, a pair of pink leather platform heels, a necklace of bright garnet beads and all the anticipation and excitement of a sheltered child who finally was given the chance to travel to the West. I spoke no English, and America in my mind was a metropolis filled with reflecting skyscrapers at twilight. I thought all Americans got dressed up for dinner, and had big cars with white leather steering wheels and pale cream leather interiors. Images of a prosperous postwar America were everywhere, in our Vietnamese schoolbooks, in the old movies we saw at the Rex Theater in Saigon. But in 1975, as a newly arrived refugee, I was already twenty years behind the post-modern time zone. I was already living in the past, with my up-to-the-minute bell bottoms and my pink platform heels, but with the outdated innocence and the anticipation of a child of the fifties. With my very limited understanding, I did not grasp the American malaise of post-Watergate and post-Vietnam. I did not understand why the young kids at my new school had long hair and went barefooted to school.

There were no skyscrapers in suburban Virginia.

In the years that followed, I struggled to escape my past and the past of my private America. A riddle, which past is a refugee's present, which past is a refugee's future ? My parents, working hard, poured into all of us their dreams of prosperity, their expectations of a glorious future. The future that was. The future that should have been part of a mythical America. That is why F. Scott Fitzgerald resonates so strongly with me. He spoke directly to my sixteen year old heart with that famous ending in The Great Gatsby:

As I sat there, brooding on the old unknown world, I thought of Gatsby's wonder when he first picked out the green light at the end of Daisy's dock. He had come a long way to this blue lawn and his dream must have been so close that he could hardly fail to grasp it. He did not know that it was already behind him, somewhere back in that vast obscurity beyond the city, where the dark fields of the republic rolled on under the night...."

Recently I dreamed that I had to leave the U.S. on short notice and all that I could bring with me was two small pieces of luggage. In my dream, everything I had accumulated in these twenty one years were gone, and I ended up with two small satchels filled with the gossamer things of summer. In the dream there were no platform shoes in my bags. There were no winter clothes.

I used to think of luggage as trappings and memories from the past that challenge and impede a refugee's progress. Examining my recent dream, I realize I was upset because I could not take my winter clothes with me back to Vietnam. After twenty one years, I have finally become acclimated to Fall and Winter in America--another type of baggage, another past--the past of my present existence, as opposed to the past that has now become a part of my imagination.

This morning, after a scanning of my closet--every morning I need to reinvent myself in my dressing rituals, coming up with a way to package myself for another bureaucratic subterfuge--what other people would refer to as the day-to-day work. I have tried to conform to prosperity and success, but have not been successful in the trying. So bright clothes and platform shoes have become my revenge and my subterfuge. Bright clothes in shades of papaya, saffron and ocean water--orange, bright yellow and royal blue--associations of summer that do not stifle you like the tropical heat. Fall makes me think of transformations, and not of death or winter. But summer, looking back, has begun to depress me.

This realization--that I have become a child of Fall and Winter--has surprised me with its simplicity and directness. Perhaps I have realized that the past no longer is disconnected from the present or the future. Perhaps the past never ceases to be an elusive layered thing. That now, on a daily basis, depending on the phone calls or whom I would see or where I would go for lunch--I would speak as much Vietnamese as English--that something I say may evoke understanding or recognition--and best, a smile--regardless of the linguistic choice; that Washington, D.C. is not that far away from Saigon... so it seems that la vie quotidienne has begun to take shape as the life I have always dreamed for myself.

(To be acclimated to Fall and Winter, is to realize at last, that one has a hard time traveling light. To be acclimated to Fall and Winter, is to realize that life is never safe. To be acclimated to Fall and Winter, in America, is to embrace the wonderful precariousness of one's refugee experience.)

A joke I like to evoke from time to time with my mother. When I was a child sometimes we would watch cai-luong or Southern Vietnamese opera together in the evening. The word "cai-luong" means renovation, but the opera was always about some past tragedy--some young people met, fell in love, became separated by some unforeseen tragic forces--it was usually their evil and close-minded families. But regardless of whatever plot, the ill-fated lovers would invariably meet again, some twenty years later. (For some reason, it was always twenty years in these cai-luong operas). "Roi Hai Muoi Nam Sau." "After Twenty Years." The joke had to do with our disbelief--my mother and I never suspended our disbelief. In these plots the lovers would always meet, twenty years later, as sextagenarians, or looking very much decrepit, with snow-white hair, deep lines on their faces, hoarse whispers.... A couple of tough decades. Perhaps they were shooting opium. My mother and I, we would laugh. Mortality and old age became an unintended joke. Yet beneath our disbelief there lurked an unexamined faith--now suddenly perceived: that the passage of time can be a merciful interval. Time does not ravage but beautifies and credits the face. Experience warms you up to Fall and Winter. Loss of innocence gains courage. Real time finally catches up with the refugee.

September, 1996

Thuy Dinh

Washington, D.C.
