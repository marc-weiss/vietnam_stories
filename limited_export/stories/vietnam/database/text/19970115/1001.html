VIETNAM WAR



The Vietnam War, a military conflict in the Indochina peninsula of Southeast Asia, originates in the late 1950’s as a guerrilla-type civil war in South Vietnam and broadening, especially from 1965 onward, into an international conflict involving both the United States and North Vietnam.
In the spring of 1954 the Geneva Conference was called to discuss the terms for ending the Indochina War, a conflict between Vietnamese nationalist and Communist forces including the French colonial government and its allays. The conference opened on May 7. Participating in the conference were France, Great Britain, the United States, China, Cambodia, Laos, the Bao Dai government of Vietnam, sponsored by the French, and the Vietminh government. On July 21 agreement was reached on an armistice providing for the withdrawal of French forces from Indochina.
The major clauses of the agreement relating to Vietnam were temporary partition of the country, a ban on any increase in armament in either part of the country, establishment of an International Control Commission composed of Indian, Canadian, and Polish delegates, and a nationwide election to choose a single government for Vietnam. The United States and the Boa Dai government refused to sign the agreement, though the United States declared it would not use force to prevent its implementations. The Boa Dai regime asserted that its interests had been betrayed by the Western nations, but it was generally acknowledged that the Vietminh, under Soviet and Chinese pressure, had conceded more than they had to, given their military victory.

After the withdrawal of the French, the Ho Chi Minh government quickly consolidated its hold over North Vietnam. In South Vietnam, French control was replaced by the support of the United States, which regarded South Vietnam as the as a link with the non-Communist security system in Southeast Asia. The U.S. view was that if South Vietnam became Communist, all the neighboring Southeast Asian countries would also fall under Communist control.
Ngo Dinh Diem became South Vietnam’s premier. Initially he was in a very weak position because of the nationalistic conflicts, because of the control exercised over some areas by religious political sects, and because of long standing antagonism among southerners, central Vietnamese, and the usually better educated and politically more experienced northerners. Diem consolidated his powers by dividing his enemies, militarily suppressing the sects and initiating public works projects and limited land reform program. He then held a referendum deposing Bao Dai and making himself the chief of state. Starting in the later 1950’s the Diem regime came to be regarded as increasingly stagnant, repressive, corrupt and discriminatory against Buddhist and southerners. The United States continued to offer strong support to the Diem government because it appeared to have united South Vietnam and to be in full control.
In 1956, Diem, refused to have elections concerning the reunification, on the grounds that free elections would be practically impossible in Vietnam at that time. Not able to achieve unification through peaceful means, Vietnamese nationalists and Communists began an counterattack among the countryside of South Vietnam.

As a result of this insurgency, war began and continued until late October 1972, following secret negotiations in Paris between Henry Kissinger and Le Duc Tho of North Vietnam. These secret negotiations resulted in a tentative agreement based on a nine-point peace program. When the firing ceased, Saigon was in possession of more land than it had held in 1967, which included all the major cities and the entire coastline. In April of 1973, the last of the U.S. troops left Vietnam, and in August of the same year, the Congress of the United States passed a legislation forbidding the use of any U.S. troops in Indochina.
After researching and knowing the history of this war, I called my mom and asked her if she knew anyone who went to Vietnam that I could interview. She thought for a while and suddenly remembered that she has a cousin who is a veteran of the Vietnam war. We hung up, and about 15 minutes later she called me back with a phone number. She encourage me to call him, but I toll her first that: “please, shouldn’t I now a little bit of information about this guy first? She says: “ Juanchi, I know this man since I was little. We were very close friends in high school. He was one of the greatest athletes of the school. He represent Puerto Rico in competitions against Cuba and Dominican Republic. You can not imagine how good and fast he was. Well, just so you can know, he was ranked number 20 in the country for being an outstanding track and field runners. But now, Juanchi, those things are only memories, memories, and more memories….”
At that moment I was confused, because I could not realize why if he was such a well recognize athlete, had to go to war… So I ask my mom, and she immeidiatly told me: “Don’t you dare ask him that question, because he will really get annoyed. Instead, I will answer it to you….Juanchi, it was all the contrary; his quickness was the very first reason that lead him to war. He was so fast that the USA army wanted him to be a minute man, using as excuse that he can be of great help. So, knowing this, I recommend you to be as smooth as possible in asking questions, because until I remembered, he is very sensitive in regard to this subject”. At that moment there were only four words that came into my mine… “how ironic is live”.
After hanging the phone, I immediately called him up and introduce myself. We talked for a while about what is going on in Puerto Rico and about the family in general. He was in a cheery mood at the beginning of the conversation, but as soon as I began to question him, his attitude changed completely. “What happened in Vietnam?” was my first inquiry. There was a moment of silence on the other line and instead of receiving a direct answer, I was told that he was not willing to answer that question. After 3 minutes of noiselessness, he quickly told me what he was willing to tell me about Vietnam. He basically said that he was drafted when he was 20 years old and that the 2 years he spent there are a part of his life he would rather forget. He stopped once again and I asked him if he was feeling all right. He said, “Juanchi, the problem is that you will never imagine, how much suffering and pain I saw in that place. There is not one book , article, or encyclopedia that can really describe the human disast er that took place in Vietnam. There is nothing worse in this world than killing a man who you know has a family; destroying their future. Sad, it is very sad, but it is the truth, and it turns more complex when you realize that you were part of that truth. I am going to tell you more, the death percentage of the Puerto Ricans was the highest of all. By that time, Puerto Rico was a total disaster: young kids without fathers, wives without husbands…It was horrible, you cannot imagine. Most of those who made it back, are insane or have no legs, like me, or no arms. When I arrived at Puerto Rico I was praised because of my bravery and other such things, but for me, that was and is pure shit, because that war decided my future, decided the future of my family. I, now, am just a veteran who never went to college, because some bastards decided that violence was the best way to solve things. The thing that bothers me the most is that the people who decided to fight where never there and will probably never know wha t it is like to kill a man, or feel pain and suffering from hunger and the absence of love. In war, every minute you are fearing for your life because the only thing you have in your mind is that if you don’t kill first you are going to get killed.”
After hearing this, I was astonished and left without words. I did not how to react. I was anxious to ask him about his legs, but I didn’t because of what my mom told me. Wow!, how much suffering was in that mans’ voice. He not only lost his future in sports, but now he had no legs. It is so ironic because as I see it, before him going to war, his legs were symbol of success as a great runner, and afterwards, that symbol changes drastically to one of frustration, sadness, and loneliness, due to the fact that where his legs (quickness) that put him in war.
Suddenly, he ask me how old I was. I told him I was 19. He ask me about my father and I said he was 48. “So, wait a minute,” he said, “why didn’t your father go to Vietnam?” I replied, “I really don’t know… But why are you asking?” He answered, “I was just wondering…” For a moment there was only silence between us, I was very anxious to know what had happened with my father. I thanked him for his help. “I really appreciate all that you have told me.” He said, “Don’t worry kid, at least now you have a sense of what war really is; remember that I have read a lot and there is not one book, article or encyclopedia which can describe the atmosphere and suffering that was felt in the Vietnam War.”
I hung up the phone and immediately called my father. I could not wait to ask him the reason for why he did not go to Vietnam. He answered the phone, and I immediately posed my question. He asked me why I wanted know. I implored him to tell me. He said, “All right Juanchi I will. When I was about your age, I was drafted to go to war. My father was very scared, because he knew that if I went to Vietnam I would never make it back. I would not have been able to have a family; in short, he knew that going to war would destroy my life for ever, even if I survived. So, Juanchi, you will never imagine how many things he did so I would not have to go to the war. He spoke with colonels and generals of the Puerto Rico Army in order to put me in a high ranked position, thus avoiding having me to go. And, finally, about one month before my departure, I was exempt of going only if I went to college and studied Law. Well son, know you now how I turn out to be a lawyer, because that’s the story…think about it becau se you should be proud of it.”
After hanging up I was perplexed, thinking about how lucky my father was; moreover, how proud I should feel of my grandfather, who rests in peace, for everything he did. He was a hero, a real hero, because he followed the ideas of the veteran who I interviewed. My grandfather tried to avoid violence, thus preserving the future of the international community; due to the fact, that if it was not for him, I, the future of that past, would probably not be writing this paper now.
