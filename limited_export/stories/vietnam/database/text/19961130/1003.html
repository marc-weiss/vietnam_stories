The eve of July 4,1967,Danang. It was the loudest noise I will ever hear. These days I consider 1967, and specifically a short period in July of that year as a turning point in the course of our country. I always say I had it easy in Viet Nam because by comparison to those who were in the bush I did. In July of 1967 there were four J.Byelick Marines in country, James S. and Joseph D., my cousins and next door neighbors, and Joseph M., a Marine I never met but was always just missing in various places during my seven year stint. My cousins were 0311 and 0381, a grunt and a mortar man. I was in the airwing, a radar/nav technician. A wingwiper. My story is not one of blood and gore, it's more of friendship, comraderie and family. It's as though I spent much of my youth in some charmed way and there was always a story going on behind the day to day activities. That July night, my worst experience of the whole thing I had been in the ville, Dogpatch, partying down with my best friend from my hometown, Stirling, N.J. He was going to Hong Kong the next day and his outfit cut him loose a day early so we could spend some time together. He and I had been classmates from kindergarten and although we fought nearly everyday for four or five years from the second grade, by the time we were in high school we were literally inseperable. I was the one who screwed up in high school and wound up working for my dad, a builder, and when I joined the Corps he was my replacement for the summer. I was surprised when he joined the Corps too and was happy when he wound up dating my ex-girl friend in Memphis where I had gone to school. Soon I got word that he was coming in country and when I found out he was a few miles away at Marble Mountain I was overjoyed. We got to see each other three times during the overlap of our tours but the most memorable was the night the VC hit the bomb dump 125 meters away. The people in the ville knew we were going to get hit, they would"t let us into the place we noemally would go because it was on the trajectory, a fact I put together later. Instead they took us into the bush on the other side of the road going up hill 327. That part of the story is a fill in the blanks, a task of great ease for any twenty year old there at the time. We got back to the hootch just before midnight, hit the showers, yes we had had showers by then, living conditions improved throughout my tour. We hit the rack and had almost enough time to fall asleep before the first rockets whooshed in. The tell-tale sound of those Russian rockets with the expanding fins. They walked the rockets right through our compound but none of our hootches got a direct hit. The target was the the bomb dump and the boys out in Happy Valley with their bamboo aiming sticks did their job. When the bomb dump blew it through 250 pound bombs three miles away and the secondary they caused cost us some of our planes, F-8e's. The new tin roofs we had just had installed were torn off as were the louvers of the side facing the dump. There was a big Air Force barracks even closer to the dump than us and it was seversly torn up. We had a few seriously wounded troops but no kias. When we first bailed out of the hootch My buddy went to the right and I went to the left, so we wound up in different bunkers. After some yelling he came into the bunker that I was in.When it was just rockets we hunkered down in the corner of the bunker, me over him, him being the guest and all. Then the dump let loose and we didn"t know what the hell was going on. It was this deafening noise that you didn"t so much hear as you did feel. The wind was like a hurricane blast tearing the hootches up and then the shrapnel started falling. Big chunks, little chunks, it just kept falling for what seemed like a half hour. Yod could hear crunching or just tinkling down on what was left of the tin roofs. I remember hunkering down over him and saying how great the story would be in The Mountain Echoes, our loval newspaper about these hometown best friends who get reunited and blown away on the fouth of July. The bunklers we had had had no roofs so the falling shrapnel was something you just had to hope didn"t land on you. Even the small pieces were bad because it was all still red hot by the time it landed. We spent several hours in the bunker while there were repeated secondaries in the bomb dump. Itwas probably five or so in the morning before we rentered the hootch. Word began to filter back that the flight line had also been hit by both rockets and secondaries and that we had several kias there. One of the dead was Don Kraus. Don had taken my duty that night so that I could party with Dickie, I had done the same for him when his brother had come up from down south. Every body liked Kraus and he and I had become tight from the first day we met in Hawaii. I remember the clerk at group headquarters asking me as soon as I checked in when the last time I had been in country was. I told him that I hadn't been yet and he said quickly,"Well you're going now." That was fine with me because I had decided a long time before that day if there waqs a war during the years of my service age days that I was going to be in it. My father had been a shipbuilder during WWII and so he was deferred, he was also borderline age-wise but I know that it was something that bothered him alot over the years. He actually did some activist work when the unions company he was was working for let materials needed for for the ships sit outside the shop while they were in contract negotiations. Letters to the editor and that kind of thing, ultimately it cost him his job and of course his job. It came at a time when things must have turned and he was never called. The clerk at group assigned to VMF(AW)232, an f-8 squadron that was preparing to transpac to Japan and on to Viet Nam in a few months. I toted my stuff to their squadbay and found a locker and began unpacking. I made alot of noise and was soon being screamed at by some guy sleeping a couple of bunks down. Finally he gave up trying to sleep and came over to my area. I was putting up pictures in my locker and as I put up one of my younger sister he blurted out, "Who's that?' I answered that it was my younger sister Cheryl. He said, "I'm going to meet her someday." I said "Yeah right" and he began to insist that he would meet her someday, I just let it drop after offering him her address and telling him he could write. He said that he didn"t want to write her, but continue to say that he would meet her. That night in July when we finally wound down down wnough to get an hour or so rest the thought that this guy, my friend was dead instead of me, and that he wasn"t going to meet my sister afterall.

Joe Byelick
1946
Isla Vista,CA.USA
