For the past 15 years at Old Dominion University I have been teaching a course called "Understanding Violence". The course covers everything from teenage suicide to the holocaust with child abuse, various forms of violence against women, hate violence, police and prison violence and war in between. We look for common experiences across forms of violence for those who do the violence and those who are the targets (victims, survivors). We often find that the perpetrators were themselves victims. It's an eye opening experience for the students. After the course, most can never look or experience violence in the same way again. Every year toward the end of the course, we deal with the Viet Nam War (in close conjunction with Robert J. Lifton's NAZI DOCTORS.

In the section on Viet Nam, we always read 1993 Op/Ed piece written by David Branco entitled "March 16, 1968: Reemember My Lai?". We always read and study Mark Baker's NAM. For that book students are required to read write down quotes from the book (3 from 3ach section) which caught their attention for whatever reason and to explain why they pick that quote. Additionally, we alsays watch the Frontline Video "Remember My Lai" . Finally students are required to write a paper in which the address the following questions about the video and their reactions:

1. Which events or incidents that occurred at My Lai are most significant to you and why? Why do these stand out? 2. Which reactions of the victims at My Lai (both American soldiers and Vietnamese civilians) are most significant to you and why? 3. How do you feel knowing that it was Americans, people like you, who committed these acts? 4. To what extent do the words and descriptions of the My Lai events reflect the analysis of the Viet Nam war policy makers described by Laren Baritz in his chapter "God's Country and American Know-How" (from BACKFIRE)? 5. How is this mindset similar ro and different from the mind set of teh Nazi Doctors described by Lifton? 6. In what ways do the "suvivors" (soldiers and civilians) reflect the reactions of survivors or Hiroshima and Nagasaki (Liftons, LIFE IN DEATH). 7. How do you think you would have reacted had you been given orders to shoot and kill people like those killed at My Lai? How do class discussions of institutional violence help you understand your reaction?

These papers are always incredibly thoughtful. Since many students have paernts who served in Viet Nam, these readings and assignments often lead to some important family discussions (at least this is what students report).

Almost all students (especially in the 90's) having vaguely heard something about all of this, but it's touched on very little in their high school classes. They feel they have learned from the experience.

I hope this give teachers some ideas.
