I'm haunted by images of VietNam. One image that is burned into my mind is following a ton and a half truck full of caskets draped in the South Vietnamese flag. The truck pulled into a Vietnamese house and I saw two little children come running out of the house and the one little girl burst out crying and starting stomping her feet and pulling at her hair while tears streamed down her face. The men in the truck, dressed in army uniforms, got out and unloaded a casket, at random it seemed, and then just drove off. I thought to myself, don't they call first and let the family know that their son or daughter or father has been killed? The South Vietnamese soldiers...they had families too. I wake up in the middle of the night, drenched in sweat, thinking I am still there. I walk to the bedroom window and look out over the peaceful neighborhood. So many young men will never see this scene, I am not afraid to die. I think often of these men, who never came back. I visit the Wall every chance I get. To pay my respects to the men and women who gave their life. Another image, I was standing guard in DaNang, rocket city, when a truck full of casualties rushed in. Something fell out, a body part, I stared at it for a long time. A stray dog trotted over and sniffed it, I threw up. I was nineteen you see, I had never seen a human body part. I wake up again, drenched in sweat, walk to the window, it's so peaceful. The moon casting her pale light on the houses below. Never forget them, runs through my mind. I'm not as much as a war hawk as I was before the war. My dad was career army, served two tours in VietNam. He never talks about what he saw or did. My older brother was career Air Force, he spent one tour. He doesn't talk about it either. I married my high school sweetheart, she says I came back a different man. Another image, the day I left for VietNam, we took a walk in the park, I held her tiny hand, the sun was shining, it was a gorgous day. Family's and children were playing everywhere, the park was filled with thier laughter. We stopped and she turned to me, she had big crocodile tears in her pretty blue eyes. She looked up at me and in a choking voice said, "Please come back to me." It tore my heart out and made me realize that war was not an adventure after all. We're still married, going on 25 years. AIC Arnulfo "Yogi" Ybarra, 366th Combat Support Group, DaNang AB, Republic of South Viet Nam. April 1972 - March 1973
