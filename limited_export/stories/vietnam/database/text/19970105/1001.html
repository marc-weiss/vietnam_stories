Did you ever hear the story of the bright little boy who grew up with total trust in his father. He was brilliant; he was strong; a paragon of religious morality with a first class education. After all, his father taught him everything he knows. His Dad even taught him to be the greatest chess player in the entire world. By the time he was 20, he was the world champion. Everyone realized no one could beat him. The son was living the life of Riley, cars, travel, respect, admiration, a lovely wife, two children and a father and mother who were, oh so proud. His future was unlimited, assured and respected by the entire world. Then one day as he was about to begin a series of chess matches, his father intervened as he sometimes did with advice and council; the father was always right; his authority was never questioned. The father told him he would have to play his next matches a very special way–quite unique in the universal game of chess. These next chess games would have to be played with new and creative moves, dazzling strategy and unique tactics; many of these moves would have to be the most brilliant ever conceived; his offensive moves had to be quick and stunning; his defenses had to ring true to his strongest financial sponsors and fans all over the world. The father also told him, you must be sure you do not check his King, that is, play the game as brilliant and creative as you know how; take all the risks to show your superior skill levels, keep your mind and body clear and prepared, but never cause the Check-Mate. The son finished over thirty matches using this technique with no wins of course, because he was told not to check the King of his opponents. His trusted father changed the rules. But, knowing his father always cares for his very best interests, and brought him to this level of the good life, he knew his Dad must have a reason for changing the rules at the last minute. The son never even thought of questioning his father’s authority; after all, “this is my Dad”. During this series of matches, he tied 27 matches and lost only 3. Actually, this was one of the few times he had lost, but thought nothing more of it because he was confident he could beat any one of his opponents even if he played them at the same time. His father, his fans and many of the media continued to peruse him to discuss his life and the many championships he had won in the past. He still carried the prestige and respect of everyone. His place in world history was assured. But something seemed senseless to the son; the father kept on insisting the son must not check the King in any of the matches; this went on for the next 7 tournaments; each tournament had many matches. The officials were giving him funny looks; some of his sponsors dropped their endorsements, a few of his friends stopped calling him. Then one day during the eighth tournament, he was in match number 14; he couldn’t resist, he check-mated his opponent and won the match. The crowds cheered; his old friends came back to congratulate him. But his father sanctioned him severely in front of his mother, and his friends for failing to follow instructions.. The son argued with Dad; his Dad slapped him. This process went on for some time. Eventually he lost his friends; they called him a loser; they said he had a mental breakdown; his blood pressure went to 230 over 125; he was all stressed out; he could not sleep; he began to drink to relieve the stress; then he smoked marijuana; his Dad and Mom began drinking also; they were also under great stress; he started to avoid matches by making all kinds of excuses; all of his sponsors dropped out. To avoid the stresses of the matches, of his father, of his mother, of his family and friends, he ended up taking drugs to relieve the pressure. He nearly destroyed himself. He was never invited to play in international matches again. His plight was over a game; try the game of ‘war’ for ten years for life and death and one just begins to understand what was happening in Vietnam and the subsequent destruction of authority in the value systems of all our institutions, our peoples and our government. The 60s, the drug epidemic, degradation of educational standards, nudity, immorality, draft dodging, desertions. Old value systems are discarded when the original core of established authority between the son and the father is fractured. The breach of trust dealing with life and death is an uncompromising sin. Oh the pain of it all; the son could have won all the matches in seven to 21 moves. But it is to late now; he may never recover from the trauma; he certainly will not trust again. Oh, by the way, he was divorced twice before entering drug rehabilitation.
