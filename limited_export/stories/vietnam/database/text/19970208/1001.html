The 707's landing gear came down, and the time had come for my re-entry into society. Fifteen months and 21 days in the "Nam" had given me a new perspective on life. Now, I was coming home, but to what? We had heard about the protests and marches against the war. Even my girlfriend was protesting at Ohio State. Alot to deal with.. but then, so was being a 19 year old infrantryman. I went through my DEROS,(ending time in active service) in Oakland,Ca turned in all my gear, and put on my dress greens...complete with the medals, ribbons, and spit shined boots. I must say I looked spiffy! (In order to fly military standby, I had to wear it) We had also heard that back in the "world", protesters were spitting on GI's, harrassing them, and in general, didn't appreciate what we went through...what we sacrificed for God and Country! I had a long wait at the airport, most all flights going east were booked. It did'nt matter, I hadn't called my folks to tell them I was on my way home...thought I would sur- prise them. I hadn't eaten since leaving RVN 22 hours earlier, I decided to treat myself to a delicious steak dinner, with all the trimings; and lots of fresh vegtables, and a cold beer. As I sat there eating, I looked up and noticed an elderly gentleman sitting a few tables away, would look at me then look away. He did this a few times, but I was too busy enjoying the T-bone, and reading the Army Times. I finished, and ask the waitress for the check, I didn't want to miss the next standy call. She returned say- ing my dinner had been taken care of. When I asked who/why, she handed me a note on a bar napkin... " I couldn't help notice how much you look like my son David. I can tell by you medals you were in Vietnam. My David never made it home, but he always talked about his "buddies". He would want me to say "Welcome home, we're proud of you!" .....I still have that napkin, and I got my welcome home...and so did David.
