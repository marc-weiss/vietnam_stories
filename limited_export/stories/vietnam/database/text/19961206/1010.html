I can't say that Vietnam was worse for me than for anyone else. It was certainly not as bad for me as it was for others. I didn't lose anyone I loved although I knew a lot of people who did.

Vietnam did for me what it has done for a lot of people - made me distrustful of authority, unwilling to accept most stories from politicians and others who put themselves in authority without a huge "grain of salt."

I do remember being horrified (and very naive) when politicians such as VP Agnew condemned those of us who thought the war was wrong as spoiled brats. I do know, however, that many people who claimed to be anti-war simply didn't want to be drafted. I'm not sure that was good for the country.

When my husband came home from Vietnam his experience with job interviews was that not only was his time in the Army treated as no experience but also with some degree of contempt. It was as if he was stupid for going. He had been in ROTC in college and believed in national service but after Vietnam that idealism was crushed. When the Viet Cong took over and the government in Saigon fell it was so obvious that his year there had been a waste of his time and ours.

We are paying dearly for our involvement in Vietnam. As a country we are turning inward and the temptation is to leave the world on its own as if our location between two oceans will make much of a difference in the future.

I believe that our country is becomeing increasingly polarized and Vietnam is one of the reasons. Nobody really trusts politicans, foreign service careerists and certainly the military to tell us the real truth. It is all too obvious that too much of our government is paid for by private sources who are pushing their own agenda irregardless of the far reaching consequences.

Will it take something really dreadful to bring us together as a nation? I'm afraid it might. Still - I'm at heart an optimist and feel strongly that things could change for the better. I hope so.
