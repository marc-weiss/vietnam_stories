In March of 1968, just weeks after the Tet offensive, I was flying down to Columbia, S.C. to visit a friend. My flight took me from Toronto to Detroit, to Kentucky, and on to Atlanta and Columbia. At the airport in Kentucky (just across the river from Ohio,; I can't remember the name), the plane half emptied, and a bunch of young men filled the plane. A fellow of 19 years of age sat beside me and we began to talk. He told me that he had just been inducted into the army that morning, and was on his way to Fort Jackson in Columbia. He also told me that he had been married for all of one month. When I asked him why he was going willingly, he replied; "Because my country asked me to." I though to myself; "You stupid fool!"

Here I was, a Canadian, who opposed the war, and was, of course, protected from it's direct consequences. I have felt guilty about that ever since, and when I see clips of the Memorial in Washington, I usually break into tears.

David L. Shanoff
1940
Toronto, ON CANADA
