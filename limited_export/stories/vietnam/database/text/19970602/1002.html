I'd like to relate a story that has been with me for a great many years and that I have not shared with many others. It concerns a man I met when we both had volunteered for a (formerly) classifed intelligence operation called MACSOG(CCN) [Military Assistance Command Special Operations Group, Command and Control - North] in November 1970.

We had both already served a tour in Vietnam with the 5th Special Forces Group) and had extended our Vietnam tours to participate in this special unit. The unit conducted ground recon in Laos and North Vietnam with small teams of mercenaries led by one or more Special Forces troopers. We became friendly when we we going through specialized recon training at a camp located in Long Ton in III Corps.

Once we completed training, we were assigned to separate teams. In March of 1971, I returned to the states on an 30-day R&R because I had volunteered for yet another extention of my Vietnam tour. When I returned, I found that my friend had been declared MIA while on an op in Laos. This is not exactly what it seems, however. In this unit (and the other SOG units, C&C Central and C&C South), all american personnel captured or killed, but not recovered, were officially listed as MIAs. [Non-assigned unit personnel, such as chopper crews, FACs or the indigenous mercenaries were not counted in the total casualties for the operations.] I later found out that when a recon team visited the site where the team had disappeared, they found most of the team dead, shot in the back of the head (as they had been wounded in the fight). No trace of my friend, however. For years prior to this incident, the NVA had a price on all of our heads for live capture, so the assumption was that he had been captured by the NVA. This was never proved, one way or another.

After I returned to the states and left the army, letters to my state's US Senator asking his help in getting an accounting for my friend brought no results. Our politicians were much more interested in ending our involvement than accounting for our missing. DoD simply re-stated he was an MIA and no other information was available.

Fast-forward to spring/summer of 1973. DoD declassified the all of the "SOG" projects and claimed that only 89 people were killed in these projects during the entire war. [BULLSHIT! More people than that were killed/captured in CCN alone during my tour when casualties were NOT the heaviest.] I challenged this DoD assertion in the NY Times and sparked a congressional investigation. Not because the number of casualties in whatever named columns were important, but to focus attention on our people that may still be alive at that point. If my friend was to get out, the time was then. We who worked with the Vietnamese knew that the North would keep some of our people back, to use as bargaining chips in later discussions. My friend was in a prime category to be withheld from the pending release of POWs. He had classified information, was never acknowledged as captured by our military and because Special Forces personnel (especially SOG) were seen as special enemies by the North.

A number of SOG personnel and I testified before Congress. Senator Jessie Helms and the Senate Armed Services Committee took no effective action to account for these people, but DoD did increse the number of "official" SOG project KIAs by a handful. I was young and ignorant of history back then.

Some of our POWs come home in late 1973, but not all... My friend was not among them. Sone years later, J. Carter declares all POWs/MIAs as "officially dead," sealing the caskets of any who may have still been alive. After all, dead men cast no votes and who wants dead "baby-killers" to come back anyway. Not an uncommon sentiment even today.

Well here we sit in 1997, with relations with the Vietnamese having been normalized. Where or what is the driving force to make an accounting of KIA/CIA/MIA/POWs happen. It would not be in the interests of Vietnam to acknowledge any "held-back" POW/MIAs, so what is the logical conclusion? They have been quietly buried in unmarked graves, never to see the light of day again. If you were in charge of the Vietnamese government (with nothing to gain, everything to lose and grudges to settle), wouldn't you do that too? Think about it...

If you are a student of history as I have become, you may recognize this as the normal course of events after an unpopular war (our portion of the Vietnam war being no exception).

Does it hurt? Yes...

Will it always be with us? Yes, again...

For a century-old example of this approach to peace, try watching the film "Breaker Morant."

It's still the same old story folks, soldiers in an unsuccessful and unpopular war always pay the final price for peace, whether they survive or not...

P.S. My friend was Captain Danny Day Entrican, USA, 5th SFG(ABN) On the wall in DC, he is listed as MIA with a small cross next to his name.

Sorry I couldn't do better Danny...

Tom Marzullo
