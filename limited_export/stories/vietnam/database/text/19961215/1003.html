I am now 20 years old, too young to remember Vietnam from anything but Oliver Stone movies and television documentaries. None of my family members served in the war. My father, born in 1941 and of the proper age of the time to face the draft, was blessed (as I see it) with a plethora of medical problems that made him unfit for military service. I am very lucky because of this, for if he had gone, perhaps I never would have been born. Of other family members, one uncle entered the Peace Corps and the other just missed out on the war by being too young to be drafted. There too I am very lucky, for when I vist the Wall I have no one that I must personally grieve for.

Yet still I wonder about Vietnam. I have visited the Wall twice now, and each time I wonder how I would feel if I had some personal connection to the war. Without this, I can only speculate from afar on Vietnam- unlike most of the other participants in this dialogue. Yet I am not unmoved by Vietnam. I cry even for those who I do not know. Especially I cry for the one man who went in my father's place. To him, whoever he is, I owe everything. I will never forget his sacrifice. Thank you for giving everything so that I, along with the rest of my generation, might enjoy a life of freedom. Your sacrifice has not been in vain.

- James Brian Hawken Born July 24th, 1976
