As an eight year old in 1967 I was horrified to hear and see the supposed wholesale slaughter of our servicemen in Vietnam as was portrayed by the media on the evening news stories. In the working class, agricultural community where I lived with my family it was unheard of to question the actions, let alone the intent, of those in authority. Yet, I had to know if my dear friend, next door neighbor, the young man that helped us haul hay, Keith, was among those mangled and bloody American bodies that the media presented to us nightly via the television screen as graphic proof that the American forces were losing the "conflict" badly. So, I got his address from his parents and wrote him a letter asking him how he was and how things were going.

Keith was a truck driver in the Army while he was in Vietnam and as such I guess he had some idea as to how things were going outside of his immediate area. You see he took water to the various field units in that army truck he drove. It was his opinion, as best as I can remember it, that it was very dangerous where he was at, the roads were mined, there was a constant threat from snipers, motar and rocket attacks were frequent, he was wounded once by sharpnel when the truck he was driving ran over a land mine, he regularly saw wounded men and dead bodies. But all of this was to be expected in a war, not some police action being waged by the big, bad Americans on a bunch of backward, hick farmers as portrayed by the nightly American television media. What was needed was to either allow our forces to fight a war against a very well trained, equipped, and dedicated enemy or to pull our forces out and let the Vietnamese government handle their internal struggle alone.

The "rules of engagement" that our forces were odered to follow was absurd. As such, the American politicians and the war profiteers needed to come to Vietnam in the active combat zones and fight the war that they had forced young men like him to fight for them. Upon his return home, Keith threatened my draft-age brother with physical harm if he allowed himself into being drafted and subsequently fighting a "rich man's war." Since the overwhelming majority of those people being drafted were from the less privliged segments of American society. Since I was eight years old, I have viewed the media, in general, as mere mouthpieces of mostly worthless ruling class propaganda. This view is in part due to the injustices that I saw during those war years. Hopefully, our society will never go through another "Vietnam."

Michael E. Collins
1959
Maynardville, TN, USA
