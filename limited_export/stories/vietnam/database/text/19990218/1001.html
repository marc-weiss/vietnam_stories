LOVE FORGED INTO STEEL: MEMORIES OF A VIETNAM VETERAN'S WIFE

The past 24 years have taken my husband and I on a journey we never intended to make - a journey of trial by fire, deep into the bowels of the furnace where love is either inciner- ated, or shaped into something much stronger - like iron being forged into steel. The trials of fire we've faced have been the remnants of the Vietnam War, and I share our story with the hope it may inspire other families to stay together and seek the help they need to begin healing the wounds of war. *****

Fred and I were married on Aug.4,1974, and at the very moment we stood on the altar taking our vows, 3 deafening claps of thunder shook the temple walls, and torrents of rain fell from the sky, portents of stormy times to come. Exactly one week later, I met "Killer Fred," and any illusions I may have had that the Vietnam War was over, were abruptly shattered. I awoke in the middle of the night to go to the bathroom, and when I tried to get back into bed, Fred sprang on me like a tiger pouncing on his prey, grabbed me by the throat, and snarled into my ear, "Don't move or I'll kill you!" His twisted facial features were out- lined by the moonlight streaming through our bedroom window, and I knew he could easily make good on his threat. Then, as suddenly as he had attacked, he released me, and fell into what appeared to be a sound sleep. I stood frozen in terror, afraid to move a muscle, afraid to even breathe. Minutes passed before I regained enough composure to back out of the room. Still trembling, I laid down on our living room couch. An hour later, I heard Fred get up, and my heart started pounding as I watched him walk across the living room and over toward the couch. He crouched down next to me, leaned over, gently ran his fingers through my hair, and whispered in his soft, familiar voice, "What are you doing all the way out here? Please come back to bed. I need you beside me. Do you want me to carry you in?" Confused, and still scared, I said, "Don't you remember what happened?" "No," he answered, his voice filled with sincerity, "What?" I recounted how he had attacked me, and he said, "I'm sorry, I should have warned you about this. Occasionally when I'm sleeping, I flashback to Vietnam, and if I hear any soft noises in the room, I think someone is trying to sneak up on me and kill me. If you make alot of noise and call out my name when you're getting into bed, I'm sure it won't happen again." I accepted his explanation, but in my heart, I wondered why I'd never met "Killer Fred," during our year long engagement. Had he kept him hidden somehow, afraid I wouldn't marry him if I knew of his existence? The Fred I knew and loved had told me many tales of war, but he'd been careful not to let me see this wounded dangerous side of himself. Now that we were married, the wounds began to surface again and again, becoming an integral part of our lives - sometimes drawing us close together, and some- times threatening to rip us apart. My heart would reach out to him at night, when I would be awakened by his crying, shaking, and sweating, as he lived through yet another night- mare in which he was buried alive and desperately trying to dig himself out of a grave filled with the bloody bodies of his dead buddies. "It's O.K. it's O.K., I'd say, as I rubbed his back. "You're here with me now. It's a nightmare. You're O.K." I learned that many of his flashbacks occurred during the day- time, triggered by everyday sights, sounds, and smell. Helicopters whirl- ing above, would become choppers coming to carry his wounded body back to the hospital. A car backfiring would have him sprawled down on the ground to avoid gunshots. Fourth of July fireworks would become a battle- field, with mortars and gunshots surrounding him. I became used to the fact that his gun was a constant companion which he would wear on a leg holster whenever he left the house, and keep in a night stand drawer when we slept. He would have preferred to sleep with it under his pillow, but I convinced him not to, afraid that he might shoot me during a night time flashback. I became used to being in corner tables at restaurants, so he could sit with his back against the wall, surveying the room, making sure no one had the chance to sneak up on him and try to kill him. One of the most terrifying remnants of the war surfaced when I was eight months pregnant with our child. I was watching the news with Fred one night, and saw a report which said that Vietnam Veterans who had been exposed to agent orange had an increased likelihood of having children born with severe birth defects. I was an obstetrical nurse at the time, and had never heard of this possible link. I clutched my belly, praying that our baby would be born healthy. Fred seemed less afraid than I was, but one month later, when our son was born, Fred was the first to check him over, looking for any signs of defects, and when he found none, he cried with relief and thanksgiving. An obstetrical nurse who worked with me, and was in the delivery room with us, told me that she'd never seen a man cry so much at the birth of his baby. Fred was teaching when we had our son, and for a few years, he did well on the job. But he never completed the required master's degree, and he started having difficulty with the school principal. He changed schools several times, but everywhere he taught, he would have difficulty taking orders from the principal, and his immediate super- viser. Periods of unemployment followed, and I became the main provider for our family. Around this time period, newspaper and magazine articles began to run stories about a psychiatric syndrome affecting many Vietnam combat veterans, called Post Traumatic Stress Disorder, (PTSD). I realized that Fred's job difficulties and other behavior fit the syndrome, and was causing a tremendous amount of emotional pain for both of us. In tears, and unable to tolerate any longer the way we were living, I begged him to get the psychiatric help he needed. He had always hated psychiatrists, and had vowed he'd never see a "quack shrink," so I was surprised, but tremendously relieved when he called the local VA Outreach Center and arranged to begin treatment with Dr. John LaMothe, a psychotherapist in Poughkeepsie, New York, who was familiar with the syndrome. Two years later, I entered therapy myself. The divorce rate for combat veterans is nearly 90%, but because of the counseling and support we have received, our marriage is one of the 10% that not only survived, but is currently thriving. Next year we will celebrate our 25th wedding anniversary and we're thinking of renewing our vows in a formal ceremony attended by close friends. This time we can appreciate more fully the meaning of the words, "for better or worse, richer or poorer, in sickness and in health." Having survived the worse, the poorer, and the sickness, we've finally come to a place where our love has grown strong, like iron forged into steel by fire.

Gail Sobotkin
