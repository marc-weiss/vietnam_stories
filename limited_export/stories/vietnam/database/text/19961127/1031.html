In fall 1969 they held the draft lottery and my number was 4. I was already in my freshman year of college so I felt relatively "safe". My Dad was a career military man and I was raised to go into the military. But I was more driven toward getting my college education than enlisting in the Navy.

Several months later, in spring 1970, I was enrolled in a religion course. The instructor was a campus radical that preached confrontation.

I'll never forget one of the evening classes. Just before it started, I found out that a man I had known five years before, a family man, had been killed in Viet Nam. The religion class was being held at an outdoor location and I remember laying on the grass and crying about his loss and mine.

I was never the same after that. The combination of my instructor's rantings and that poignant moment of loss set the course for my life. I basically dropped out of reality for the next several years. I stayed in school to avoid the draft, fought bitterly with my parents, and turned to a counter-culture life-style.

The problem was I was raised to love our country and respect the military. As a result, I've always been in a quandry about what to feel regarding Viet Nam. I basically shoved it to the back of my mind and avoided confronting it.

Then, several years later, the "mobile" Viet Nam memorial came to our town. I visited it, found the name of the man I knew who had been killed there, and wept. It was a release for me. It also returned me to my "roots".

I'm now a solid supporter of our Armed Forces with strong politics that supports our President's policy of enlargement and engagement.

In retrospect, the combination of my religion instructor and that event one evening side-tracked me from a career in the military. I know now my destiny was there. Only now am I fulfilling it.

Larry Sichter
1951
