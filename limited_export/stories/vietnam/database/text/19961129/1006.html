Well, I guess it's kind of strange to have a story from someone who wasn't alive during the war, but I know the impact Vietnam has had on my life is profound. My father, like most other fathers of my age group, served in Vietnam. He doesn't talk about it at all, and I've accepted that I'll never know why. When I was 5, in 1983, my family visited Washington D.C. and my most vivid memory is of the Wall. Now, I know that this was only 1 year after the memorial had gone into place. Then, I didn't know what was going on, but I knew there was something strange about the area I was standing in. There was my stalwort father, crying and there was I, a five year old, lulled into calmness by a structure that I couldn't begin to comprehend. At that young age, I felt the emotion that the Wall stands for. It was nearly like the grief of all those who had visited before was collected in that shape waiting to be shown to someone who can make a difference in this world. That day proved to me that I want a part in making this world a better place, a safer place, for all of us to exist in. There are better ways to solving problems than war. When has shooting someone who you do not know ever helped a situation? I hope that before I am through on this earth that somehow, I'll be able to convince at least one person that speaking and compromise is an infinately better alternative to killing innocent people. I don't know how I'll do it, but be assured that I will try. That is how Vietnam affected me. It molded a child born after it's death into a person who will work for the ideal of peace and compromise; yes, even with those who we now consider our enemies. Those enemies will soon be our friends, rest assured of that.

Rose
1978
Moosup,CT,USA
