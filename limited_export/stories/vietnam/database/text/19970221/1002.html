My father, Henry Leonard McNair, known as Bubba to all that knew and loved
him, was in Vietnam when I was born in October of 1967. I do not know my
father well. What I do know of him is the battle he still had to fight after
he came home.

I was in my early twenties when I learned what my father was going through
when he returned to the States. It is not a pretty story, but I feel I need
to tell it for my own sake if any is left to forsake at all.

My father died in a car accident three years after his return home. I was
four years old. The cause of the accident is unknown. He was in a van
traveling on a highway to a job. It would be his first day on the job at
some sort of manufacturing factory where he would be a manager of personnel
(all women, I believe). The authorities say my father's vehicle just went
off the road. He collided with a bridge and went to the emergency room for
head and chest injuries. My mother, Julia, arrived at the hospital and was
told after the initial diagnosis that he would be fine. The families urged
her to go on home to get some things because she wanted to stay there by his
side. I am sure she would not have left if she knew he would soon lapse into
a coma.

This story and similar stories were given to my memory. Only later, after
visiting my father's sister, Lynn, did I learn of other stories.

My father was arrested and put on probation not long after he came home from
the war. He was arrested for exposing himself to a minor. My mother stood
with him through the humiliation, not only for her, but for my father. My
mother told me that he did not understand himself and not to be ashamed. The
war affects men in a way we can not comprehend.

My aunt told me about a time when my father came to see her. It was after
the arrest. He was seeing a psychologist then. My aunt is the only one he
told about what happen at base camp. Of course, she emphasized that the
casualties of war are not all death, but some are of evil faces. He told her
that the guys all used to relieve themselves from stress, or whatever you
want to call it, by standing in front of a fence somewhere inconspicuous
along the perimeter where young girls would come to meet them. They would
stand and tease the men from the other side while the men proceeded to play
with themselves to the point of ejaculation.

I often ponder about my father. Of the war. I feel for him wherever he may
be. I am proud of my father, Bubba McNair. These are my memories....

I love you Daddy,
Miss Sonya
