EPILOGUE to my book in progress.

War spotlights the worst of human nature, but wartime challenges also bring out the 'better angels of our nature.' We seem to be fascinated by the passion of it all, even though no one in his right mind wants to be a victim.

In a utopian world, there would be no war; everyone would love his fellow human. There would be no meanness, no hostility, no vengeance; a wonderful scenario, yes, but unfortunately, not reality based.

During my post-war hippy days, my pacifist friends contended that world peace is a simple proposition. "Dismantle the weaponry, disarm the troops. Refuse to fight and there will be no war."

So simple but so difficult. The big question, of course, is, "Who goes first?" The United States has many foes who would love to see our defensive walls crumble. If we laid down our arms, do you think they would follow suit? Hell no. In the real world-like or not-it's the nicest guy with the biggest gun who keeps the peace.

I am grateful that young citizens of this country are still willing to risk their lives so that all of us (conscientious objectors included) can freely exercise our personal convictions. This marvelous liberty we often take for granted is not free, it 's been purchased and preserved for us by rivers of American blood.

Having delivered my patriotic screed, I must now admit that I had my doubts about serving in Viet Nam. Many of them were voiced in my diary while I was there. Our objectives in that little Southeast Asian nation seemed unclear. The American way of life was certainly not hanging in the balance. The domino theory of Communist expansion was not particularly convincing. Nevertheless, 29 years later, there's a certain pride in knowing I was there. I'm not sure why. Maybe its the passion of it all. And, I don't pretend to understand the odds - why some get pud duty in supply depots or aircraft carriers at sea, while others find themselves knee deep in rice paddies catching Viet Cong bullets. I don't think God spared me because he liked me better. Sitting on the catwalk at night, lulled by the sound of the ship's iron hullcutting through the surf, Richard Adams, Ernest Lewis and I would discuss the Cosmos, and how we fit into the scheme of things. Those questions remained with us in civilian life. They finally got the best of Ern in 1973.

I dedicate VIETNAM CRUISE, to his memory. I dedicate it to the wounded and dead of Viet Nam and all foreign actions. Than), you, guys (women, too) for giving your all
