From January 1964 through November 1968, I served in the United States Air Force. After completing pilot training, I served for 18 months as a troop carrier pilot, flying various kinds of missions in Southeast Asia, including dropping flares over North Vietnam and eastern Laos. For the next 21 months, I served as a forward air controller in South Vietnam. Because of this service, I have been denied all employment opportunity in the United States for the nearly 30 years since I received my honorable discharge. By flying for a short time as a civilian pilot in Southeast Asia for low wages, as I described in my book, The Phnom Penh Airlift (McFarland, North Carolina, 1990), I was able to earn enough money to support my graduate school education. During this time, I had the personal satisfaction of contributing to extending the lives of the 2,000,000 victims of the Khmer Rouge for about a year. I chose to attend graduate school because of my interest in biology and because my scores on the Graduate Record Examination placed me in the upper 1% of college graduates and graduating seniors seeking admission to study for advanced degrees. After receiving my M.S. in the United States with an average of A in 1973, I correctly perceived the chances of a veteran to make a career in an academic field in the United States to be minimal because of discriminatory practices by those who were protesting the war by keeping veterans from studying, finding jobs, obtaining support for research, and being given scholarship awards. For example, I was told not to apply to Cornell University in 1969 because I let the admissions advisor know that I had recently returned from Vietnam. At the time, Cornell was receiving more public funding than all other colleges and universities in New York State combined. Their staff may have cursed the government for the war, but the money didn´t stink! Since leaving the United States, I received my doctor of science degree in Germany and have been working there and in Brazil ever since. In 1988, I completed my habilitation in biology at the University of Hamburg, which gives me the right to work as a full professor. However, I have been trying to return to the United States and have been turned down for all the vacancies I applied for, both at universities and with the government. In 1986, my name appeared first on a list of eligible candidates for a research position with the U. S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), having been given a rating of 95. My name was followed by the names of four other Vietnam War veterans. My score was more than 5 points higher than that of the highest non-veteran on the list, so my 5 point preference made no difference in the rank. Faced with the choice of having to hire a veteran, the EPA cancelled the selection. At the time, that agency employed the lowest percentage of Vietnam Era veterans of all independent Federal agencies: 13 out of 1000 employees were veterans of that era. However, some of the departments had even worse records. Of the State Department employees, only 9 in 1000 were Vietnam Era veterans, and only 1 in 1000 Department of Education employees were eliglble veterans of the Vietnam Era. To me, it is not surprising that education in America has degenerated so catastrophically. The assorted Marxists and other politically correct educators, who refuse to work with veterans, have made such a mess of the American education system that it will take more than a generation to clean it up. In 1986, after being refused all employment for almost 18 years since my discharge, I filed a civil action in the United States District Court, Eastern District of New York, to force the Federal Government to enforce the Vietnam Veterans Readjustment Assistance Act. I also filed suit against the State University of New York for discrimination during the selection for a temporary research position with a salary of $18,000 per year. The case against the Federal Government was dismissed, while from the State University, I received $9500 and an admission that my rights to affirmative action as a Vietnam War veteran had been violated. Privately, the head of the search committee admitted that I had been the most qualified applicant, but I had been blackballed by a member of the search committee, who had objected to the fact that I identified myself as a veteran in my cover letter. This brought the legal status of veterans to my attention. My suit had been for age discrimination because the judge had ruled, based on ample precedents, that a veteran is not permitted to sue in his own behalf if he is discriminated against by a Federal contractor because of his war service. The Secretary of Labor is the only person permitted to take action, and he never does. As a result of this action, I obtained a report from the OFCCP saying that the State University of New York had not violated my rights as a Vietnam Era veteran and a signed statement from representatives of the university admitting that they had. In fact, for all other protected groups, the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission is responsible for investigating discrimination complaints, and if the complainants are not satisfied with the action taken, they may sue in Federal court. If a veteran is discriminated against because of his war service, he must complain to the Office of Federal Contract Compliance Programs of the Labor Department. Each time I am discriminated against by an American university, I must file two separate complaints, one with the EEOC for possible age discrimination and the other with the OFCCP as a veteran. I have therefore had many chances to compare the investigations conducted. While the EEOC obtains several pounds of documents, including applications filed by the successful candidates, the OFCCP obtains no records at all. That can be certified by the court because I sued to obtain the records of one investigation by the OFCCP, and my suit was dismissed on the grounds that no records existed. Recently, I have obtained records of selections conducted in 1994 and 1995. One university that rejected my application in the first cut without reviewing any of my qualifications hired a person who had had 6 years of postdoctoral experience compared to my 17, had authored 21 scientific papers compared to my 48 (37 during the same time period), and had spent the last years of the draft as a student. The OFCCP reported that the quality of the successful candidate´s research had been better, although records from the EEOC showed clearly that my application had been rejected before any of my publications had even been reviewed. At a small college, I was rejected in favor of a 29 or 30-year-old with less than three years of post-doctoral experience and only credited with co-authoring six publications totalling 36 pages. At the time, my scientific publications in books and journals totalled more than 1670 printed pages and covered the fields the college claimed to be seeking competence in. The OFCCP rejected my complaint on the grounds that I was not qualified to teach at a small liberal arts college. Why I should only be qualified to teach at a large college but not a small one was never explained. The action by the OFCCP is final, and judicial review is not permitted. The EEOC rejected my complaint on the grounds that I had only taught three courses completely independently. The investigator failed to notice that the successful candidate had never taught a single independent course. I had much more experience teaching as part of a group and instructing laboratory courses than the successful candidate, who had only taught in these ways. However, I have filed a civil action for age discrimination, although my status as a combat veteran probably plays a greater role in the discrimination than my age does. In any case, I am at a great disadvantage because I cannot afford a lawyer and must proceed pro se unless the court appoints a lawyer for me. From my experiences, I am convinced that the OFCCP should be abolished as a major impediment to veterans finding employment. It is no surprise to me that so many Vietnam War veterans are homeless, given that the hiring and firing is firmly in the hands of persons who should have served their country during the war (or at least gone to jail if they wished to protest by refusing) but found ways to duck out of their legal obligations. As three New York State employment specialists told me at various times, no university will hire me because every selection committee includes persons who dodged the draft during the Vietnam War and would feel uncomfortable working with a veteran. If the OFCCP were doing its job, no universities using "academic freedom" as an excuse for systematically preventing veterans from working or studying would be receiving any Federal funds. The EEOC has been established for investigating discrimination complaints, and it should investigate for veterans just as it does for everyone else. The veteran should also have the right to sue, just as everyone else does. It would save tens of millions of tax dollars to abolish the redundant OFCCP, which has a proven record of failure. This would also be a signal to those Federal contractors that have long been keeping veterans out of suitable employment that they will have to change their ways. It is not right for Congress to assume that persons who were spitting on veterans a few years ago are going to hire and promote them today. During the past two months, I have sent this information to Senator Arlen Specter of Pennsylvania, who is the new head of the Senate Veterans´ Affairs Committee. He has not bothered to answer. Perhaps someone from Pennsylvania could forward this message and demand his response. I have many more details that I could add, but in the interest of space, I will have to cut this letter short.

Sincerely yours

Charles W. Heckman Dr. Sci., habil.
