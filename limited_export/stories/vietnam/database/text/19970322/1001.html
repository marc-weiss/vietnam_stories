I went to Vietnam in January 1970. I was a tanker, if that means anything. Spent my year up on the DMZ. I have no real war stories to tell. As the guy in "Word of Honor" said, the best war story he ever hear was..."A bunch of us went up that hill, and some of came back. That's all except the details.

It lingers with me everyday in one form or another. I seem to be able to function rather normally....married, been working at the same place for 20 years, etc.

But I know it did something to me. I just can't put a finger on it. I remember my first initiation to shit. Lost 3 friends on May 5, 1970. The next day, I saw the infamous picture of Kent State in Stars & Stripes. I thought the entire wold was going nuts.

Anyway, when I came home, there weren't a lot of Vets around to talk with. Spent years with the events of May 5th rumbling around in the back of my mind. I'd read a book about Nam, but it just didn't ring true. I'd see a movie, and it just didn't ring true.

As time went by, I started to quetion my memory of that day back in May of 1970. Then I went to The Wall in 1986. It sucked the air right out of my lungs. It was like I was back with my old friends after 16 years. So many names etched into that black granite tombstone, I didn't know what to think.

Then one of the volunteers asked me if I needed some help finding a particular name. For a moment I was speechless. It had just occured to me that my memory was of their nicknames - we all had nicknames. But somehow a brain cell fired and we were able to find the names of my three buddies.

May 5 must have been a lousy day. They all died within minutes of each other, but are seperated on the wall by many others who also died on May 5.

But just seeing their names on the wall provided me with an much needed "external confirmation" of my memory. I didn't know it at the time, but I had spent years beginning to question the validity of my memory.

The Wall is a MUST SEE for every Nam Vet. As sad as it is that the Wall need to be built, it always leaves me feeling a bit saner when I visit it. I can't call it an enjoyable experience, but it certainly a meaningful one.

As much as we need to put the war behind us, we have a responsibility to remember it. I took a picture of the panel with my buddies' names on it. Somehow, I caught the reflection of the Washington Monument in the photo. It has been blown up and framed. It sits right in front of me at the desk I am sitting at. There are probably days I don't notice it, but they are far a few between.

The Wall. Thanks to all who put an effort into it's development.

Now if I can just figure out how to explain war to my two nephews who think their Uncle did something heroic before they were born. It's like they are envious. I remember feeling that way before I enlisted. How does one learn the misery of war without actually experiencing it? Question of the Ages, I suppose.

It's one I've been pondering for years. I can only chalk it up the power of "Old History's Whore."
