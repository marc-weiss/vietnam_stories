I think the 60’s in general and the Vietnam War which was probably was the main focus of what happened in the 60’s made me aware of the untruthfulness of our government and it also made me aware of the need to be concerned, not just for my own person but for others and I think, for me, I look at the 60’s as the time when I became, I looked at everything before and said, wait a minute, thousands and thousands of years we have been doing this way, we’ve been going to war, and here we are in 1960, whatever the year, and there’s racism, there’s war, we’re killing each other, all these pollutants that are killing the environment, it hasn’t worked, every way we have tried to change that hasn’t worked because it’s still going on. And I think maybe that’s why I became a hippy of sorts, because I saw that as a different lifestyle, we have to look at removing everything—government, economics, none of the system that are there work and we have to be concerned with each other and our place here together and so since then, and one thing that changed me was that it led me into education as a career as the way to affect the most people, to help bring peace, I became a teacher as a career goal, or choice, so that was a major factor and then ever since then I have tried to instill in my children, to question authority, not just accept war, that’s what I teach in my classes, question authority and you have to teach critical thinking and don’t accept what you’ve been told and work for a better life. The other aspect was that I’ve lived my life or tried to live my life as if it was the ideal. I don’t always succeed, I’m not perfect, but I try not to—I’m sure there is some racism in me and some bigotry and sexism in me but I try not to. I want a world where we’re all equal and so I try to live that lifestyle, so I think that is a legacy I learned from the 60’s, I think before then I was more self-centered—I’m still self-centered—I think the 60’s have made me become aware for myself the need to focus outward and that has carried on the rest of my life. Everything that I am I really attribute to what happened in the last five years of the 60’s and the first couple years of the 70’s.

David Hurwitz

Dayon, OH

As told at the Dayton Story Circle
