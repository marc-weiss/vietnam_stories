At the brash age of twenty four, I had seen much in the world and felt that I had wisdom to share from my childhood years of growing up in Asia . The unpopularity of my sharing kept food from my table one summer. In October 1969 I spoke the following:

A famous French novelist once remarked that: "In every century, every man, in his turn, reclimbs Calvary." The late American poet Robert Frost, even in the wisdom of his 80 years refused to see the challenges of life in the 20th century as more difficult to face than those in ages past. It would perhaps, in a philosophical sense, be possible to agree with such a point of view, but I think that, basically, the French novelist and the American poet were wrong.

(There is much in the middle that I spoke to my audience that I will leave out and give my final remarks)

Americans would do much better not to make war on Asians, but to seek new and improved ways in which to cope reasonably, realistically and humanely with the existing order of things in Asia, we must strive to meet the present challenges in Asia, without creating new and unnecessary ones. And, in particular, we must come to accept with openness and without prejudice the permanence of differences among people, governments and nations. We must taste to the lees the validity of the old maxim: "One man's meat is another man's poison." Uniformity and conformity, wherever they arise, are always regarded as the red- headed stepchildren of the universe. Ultimately, toleration, understanding and reason must come more cheaply to man, even if man has to pay more dearly to obtain them. If the peoples of the world learn nothing else by the end of this century, they must learn to live together - different from each other, but together - in peace. The primary goal of our generation must be to defeat war itself. For, if we do not, then who can say but that someday soon - perhaps even at this moment - someone, somewhere, may be calling for a moratorium on man.

Russell W. Godwin Moratorium Day Address October 15, 1969

Russell W. Godwin
