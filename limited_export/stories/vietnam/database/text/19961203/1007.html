I was drafted in December of 1968,arriving in Vietnam the following September.I had extensive training as an infantryman, but on my arrival in-country I was sent to an artillery battery, even though I had never even seen a howitzer. I spent a year in "I" corps, serving in the Americal division.Being the new man on 155 mm. howitzer, I was made the loader, lifting 98 pound shells to ram into the breach. I did this up to 350 times in one night. Today my back is shot, but I've never filed a claim. I got out of there in one piece so I was thankful for that.The duties of a "Gunbunny", as we were sometimes referred to, was brutally hard at times, and dangerous on occasion.Artillery batterys were favorite targets for enemy "sappers", who would slip through our defenses unseen and unheard, carrying satchels of explosives ,intending to take out any artillery pieces, ammo bunkers, and U.S. personnel that got in their way. We feared them greatly but we also respected their bravery, for most of the missions they conducted resulted in their deaths. Oddly enough, we held the South Vietnamese soldiers(ARVN'S) in contempt for their cowardousness and thievery.

We fired millions of dollars worth of shells, but rarely knew if we hit anyone or anything, unless our forces were in the area to do a body count.We once fired a mission to destroy a downed "Huey" in order to keep the radios on board out of enemy hands.

I witnessed feats of incredible incompetency by certain superiors that got people hurt and sapped away what little moral we might have had. To this day I cannot tolerate the actions of incompetent people placed in charge of others. I am proud to be a Vietnam veteran, even though we brought no trophies home. I learned to become a very self-sufficient person as a result of my tour there, and found that a person under those circumstances can be pushed to perform far beyond the limits of your imagination.

I run a small sign business today, after retiring from a career in the fire service. I'm the happiest I've ever been, because I call the shots . I want to end this story by making a comment on the anti-war movement of that time. We all knew we were in a hopeless situation, but it would have been nice to have gotten a little more support at home.How would you feel if you were playing football in a difficult game, and your own hometown fans were loudly booing all of your efforts?

James L. Polewchak
