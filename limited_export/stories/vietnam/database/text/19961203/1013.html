Listening to everyone so far, I had so many images, people making me think of so many different things that happened, it is really impossible to think of what was most memorable. I also was in college when I first became aware. University of Michigan in 1965 and that’s about all the time I spent in school, in 1965, but everything that happened for the next ten years felt like part of the Vietnam War to me, regardless of whether it is related in other people’s minds, it was all about the war, and we never did do enough to stop it and it really pissed me off when George Bush said that "It’s all behind us,” that we had finally put the Vietnam War behind us because we clearly did not. And that was a time when an enormous number of people spoke in really clear voices and it didn’t affect policy, and not only did that experience shape my life when I became 18 and 19, and you go through these really intense experiences, I just look back at that time when a lot of us learned not to believe that this democracy functions the way it was supposed to and that it was unmistakable that the war should end and that only elites were capable quite on their own for deciding that the war should go on. And I just think of so many things that affect me and changed how I perceive, and they were related to the war.

And the psychiatrist I got in Detroit thought I was absolutely right and all the things I said that were irritating and confusing to the people who just wanted to process me through were appropriate and I didn’t belong in the war but professionally it was clearly obvious that I was not crazy. I passed my pre-induction physical and then I went back to Ann Arbor and I wrote my draft board a bunch of other letters and I wrote conflicting things, not because I felt conflicting things but, you know, I said I wanted to reinstate my application for CO, no I don’t want to, and I sent my student deferment back long before I had quit school and eventually they drafted me and I went to Canada and wrote them from there and I wrote them that I had a change of heart, you know, Toronto was now my home and I was going to try to join the Royal Canadian Mounted Police and was going to be looking for things in Canadian democracy that I was no longer able to do with American democracy. They never answered me directly but eventually I did get a letter from the U.S. Attorney for that district and I got into all sorts of trouble in Toronto and after spending most of 1968—I was in Toronto when I heard that King was killed and that was—to me, King was a casualty of the Vietnam war, because that is how I understood the war at the time, and I still think King was a casualty of that and other things as well. And in Toronto, feeling like I had run away from the fight to stop this was—and King getting killed—was probably the worst possible. He was just so important. And the things that happened on television during that war, and what we talked about with Willie and other stuff, were so affecting. And I was also back in the country for a little while in ‘68 and I went to visit my parents in Chicago, happened to be during the Democratic convention and I was with them the first time of police violence, And we didn’t know it had happened and we were watching the news and it came on and they were really afraid of what I would do. I told them, I wouldn’t do anything— I can’t watch this. Of course I left there and went down to the convention. I didn’t get arrested but I was there and for three nights running.

Jeff Epton

Dayton, OH
