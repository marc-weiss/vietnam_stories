Tomorrow, my older brother is going back. He's actually going to board a jet and fly across the blue Pacific and land in Siagon. Help me spend these frequent-flyer miles and come with me. Let's see it again, he said.

He was USMC, in I Corps. I was USArmy, IV Corps and I told him there was nothing in the Mekong Delta worth seeing; unless you're some kind of mud-freak. He, for the first time, described some exotic and beautiful scenes between Da Nang and the DMZ.

I was briefly tempted. But I told him that I never want to live that scared again and, since I'd gotten this far away in thirty years, there's no way I could go back.

He was a grunt in, as he is apt to affectionately say, Uncle Sam's Moldy Crotch. An oft-time RTO for the FO (or whatever the hell the USMC calls it). Two-and-a-half years in country. We figured it up, once: calculations based on a thrice-daily bowel movement (which more than accounts for the "extras" of ubiquitous diarreha) and the number of days in-country, he had more time in the crapper in a combat zone than most of the WWII-types had in a combat zone. Sort of an unpleasant bit of trivia that, nonetheless, illustrates his longevity incountry.

He leaves tomorrow. Lately, we have reminisced and at length. Studies of maps set off strong emotions for him and he struggles for his voice.

I am afraid for him. He is my only brother and we made it out of that smell of hell and I'm not so sure that we have'nt used all our chances. Not that he'll do anything stupid or take any extraordinary risks.

Nope. It's only that Vietnam has been and, I suppose, will always be a place of death. And the only reason for my 51 years is the Grace of God.

I guess that'll also have to be sufficient for my older brother.

But I'm still afraid for him.

NEIL
1947
