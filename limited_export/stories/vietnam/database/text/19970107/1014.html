A True Story

Dear Mom and Dad, I would appreciate your saving this for my own future reference. It is not designed to be of literary value, but only to convey my thoughts and memories.

We had spent the night on Apache Hill. At 0600 we started down. We were going light, which meant leaving our packs behind. We carried our rifles, ammunition, hand grenades, and canteens. At the bottom of the hill was located the first of a series of villas. Two or three hooches togather surrounded by the little buildings used to keep the stock, pigs, water buffalo, chickens, etc. Perhaps 50 to 100 yards away there would be another series of buildings. This extended for several miles. We had been in the first Villa a number of times in the past month. We had spent a dat there, but usually it was only to get water from their well and then move on. When we went for water, we were never allowed to fill our own canteens

The babysons would rush to the well, draw the water, and insist on filling the canteens. Usually there were so many of them, all trying to be of service, that they actually hindered the process. The older ones, 9 or 10, were paid with cigarettes, the younger ones with candy or gum. On this particular day, however, we were not after water. This day we had a different mission. My squad was told to set up flank security as the Company commander and platoon ldeader along with their radio operators set up in the villa itself. At approximately 0800 four armored personnel carriers (APC's) approached the village and stationed themselves on line just outside. 15 minutes later the mission began. SEARCH AND DESTROY. My first such mission and I hope my last. The people in the villa undoubtedly knew what was coming. They began to move everything out of their hoodches. As the Company Commander moved out he turned to the rear security element, "Leave nothing standing." The grass roofs burn very quickly and very hot. The flames go so high that they burn the coconuts hanging high above the ground. I was glad I was on security in the first villa, because these people had been eager to help me. But my luck wasn't to hold out. The APC's moved out to set up flank security and we were told to move into the villas and search out the hooches and then burn them. In the first villa I moved into, there was momason busily moving things out of her hooch. Her two little babysons standing off crying. When I came up the momason moved off to join her babysons. They clung to her starring horrified at the GI's they had once befriended. I went through their hooch. Nothing. As I moved outside the platoon leader walked by, "Light it up." I turned and put a match to the grass roof. It burned quickly. When I turned around the momason and her two babies were crying and looking at me. Not fear, not hate, but disillusionment filled their eyes. I walked away, their crying followed me. As I walked passed one little stock shelter the lieutenant called out, "Don't leave that standing." I placed some loose hay on the roof and set it afire. When I had walked about 100 yards I heard a terrible squealing. I turned around and realized that there had been a pig in that shelter. He had no way out. It was too far back and too hot, anyway. I moved on. By noon I had begun to rationalize what I was doing. The colonel had said the V.C. had used these villas for resupply. After chow we coninued our mission. One place I came to looked like a place of worship. There was picture of Budda above what looked like an alter. I tried to burn it, but the match went out so I left. Somebody else probably had better luck with their matches. At one villa there were three old ladies living in one hooch. When I appeared one began yapping at me. I couldn't understand what she was saying, but I could tell she was pleading with me not to burn her house. She grabbed me by the arm. I shook her off and lit a match to her roof. She grabbed a pan of water and doused the flame. I lit another match, she grabbed another pan of water. I took the water away from her and poured it on the ground. She ran after more water, but by this time the fire was out of control. I walked away. The women were hurrying back and forth pouring water, helplessly, on the flame. Finally, our mission was completed. It was terribly hot and I was glad for the chance to rest. I thought of all those little babysons that would go to sleep without a roof over their heads, wondering why the GI's whose canteens they had so faithfully filled, had come and burned down their house and stole their mother's money. I hope it fulfilled some purpose.

This story was written two days after the mission. It was not my last search and destroy.

Viet Nam vet 1969-70 11B40
