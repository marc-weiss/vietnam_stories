A Matter of Perspective

Vietnam was the defining event of my life, Al Santoli once told me. A combat infantryman in Vietnam and author of two highly acclaimed oral histories of the war, he undoubtedly spoke for the vast majority of Vietnam veterans. But he did not speak for me, for I had "seen the elephant" 16 years earlier in Korea as an 18- year old rifle company squad leader with the 3rd Battalion, 21st Infantry in 1950-1951. Even though I was twice wounded there, by comparison my 1966-1967 Vietnam tour as the operations officer of the 1st Battalion, 2d Infantry was a walk in the woods.

Thus I had what few of my fellow Vietnam veterans possessed, an ability to put the war there in perspective. That perspective was heightened during my second tour in Vietnam in 1974-1975 as a negotiator with the North Vietnamese in Saigon and Hanoi on the POW/MIA issue. Being the second-to-the-last soldier off the roof of the American Embassy on April 30, 1975 as Saigon fell to a multi-division cross-border North Vietnamese Army (NVA) blitzkrieg was an experience that dramatically changed my perspective on war.

Another stroke of good fortune was that throughout the two decades that I served on active duty after my first tour in Vietnam I was surrounded by fellow officers who had also served in Vietnam, thus never experienced the alienation that plagued so many other veterans. For ten of those years I taught Vietnam War strategy at the Army Command and Staff College and War College.

Ironically, it was my second tour in Vietnam, not my earlier combat tour, that had the greatest effect on my life and on the life of my family. By 1974 the Viet Cong guerrillas had long since disappeared from the battlefield and the main adversary was the NVA regulars in the Laotian and Cambodian border areas. Saigon was so peaceful that my wife and younger son accompanied me to my new assignment and my older son, than a West Point cadet, spent his Christmas and Easter vacations there.

He left from his Easter vacation on March 30, 1975, the same day DaNang fell to the NVA, and my wife and younger son left a week later. He had earlier applied for a four-year college ROTC scholarship, and the fact that he was the only candidate from Vietnam threw him into a special category. Not only did he win the scholarship but also gained admission to Georgetown University's prestigious School of Foreign Service, also on the basis of being a "foreign" student. Thus the Vietnam War had a profound impact on his life. Evidently both sons were favorably impressed with the military under pressure in the closing days of the war, for both are now Army lieutenant colonels.

For my part, I was in Hanoi a week before the fall of Saigon negotiating the terms of the withdrawal of the Americans from South Vietnam. It was then I told my NVA counterpart, "You know you never beat us on the battlefield!" To which he replied, "That might be so, but it is also irrelevant." That remark would spark my later book on the war, "On Strategy: A Critical Analysis of the Vietnam War" (Presidio Press) which would examine precisely why it was irrelevant. Written at the behest of the Army Chief of Staff, it sought to explain what went wrong in Vietnam.

The book went on to become a minor classic, still in print 15 years after its first release. Used by the Army, Navy, Air Force and Marine Corps staff and war colleges, it is also included in the curriculum of many civilian universities. Since my retirement from active duty I have continued to lecture on the war within the military and also at civilian universities, most recently at the University of California, Berkeley.

"You are the only person I know who became famous on the basis of one book," said British military historian John Keegan. Be that as it may, that book and my subsequent "Vietnam War Almanac" (Facts on File) led to my 1988 appointment as founding editor of VIETNAM magazine. With the more than 400 articles published during my tenure, the magazine has helped make sense of the incredible complexities of the war. Thus my Vietnam service not only had a major effect on my own family and future, but influenced, to some degree, the views of the 100,000 readers of VIETNAM as well.

A combat veteran of the Korean and Vietnam wars and editor of VIETNAM magazine, Colonel Summers is the author of the award-winning On Strategy: A Critical Analysis of the Vietnam War (Presidio), the Vietnam War Almanac (Facts on File) and the 1995 Historical Atlas of the Vietnam War (Houghton Mifflin).

Colonel Harry G. Summers, Jr., USA (Retired)
