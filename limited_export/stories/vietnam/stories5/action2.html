The war in Vietnam, as for so many fellow Americans, became the pivot around which my life turned in those years. It seemed an endless, furious round of demonstrations, teach-ins, traveling and speaking everywhere.

Through it all, I was teaching my classes at Boston University. And a profoundly important lesson -- which I first learned while teaching in a black college in the South during the civil rights movement -- was reinforced for me: that I could not, must not, be simply a classroom teacher, while terrible events swirled through the world outside. I had to leave the classroom, bringing students with me (or following them) and constantly make connections between what was going on outside and what we were talking about in the classroom.

I wanted to tell my students again and again: to stand aside, or to bury myself in "scholarly" work, while bombs were falling in Vietnam and children were burning and body bags were coming home by the thousands. It was clear to me that my students learned more by how I acted than what I said, and if I wanted them to become active citizens, engaged in a constant struggle for peace and justice, I had to set an example, even in the small things I did.

Howard Zinn
Historian
