"I am going to tell you more, the death percentage of the Puerto Ricans was the highest of all. By that time, Puerto Rico was a total disaster: young kids without fathers, wives without husbands… It was horrible, you cannot imagine. Most of those who made it back are insane or have no legs, like me, or no arms.

When I returned to Puerto Rico I was praised because of my bravery and other such things, but for me, that was and is pure shit, because that war decided my future, decided the future of my family. I, now, am just a veteran who never went to college, because some bastards decided that violence was the best way to solve things. The thing that bothers me the most is that the people who decided to fight were never there and will probably never know what it is like to kill a man, or feel pain and suffering from hunger and the absence of love. In war, every minute you are fearing for your life because the only thing you have in your mind is that if you don't kill first you are going to get killed."

Suddenly, he asked me how old I was. I told him I was 19. He asked me about my father and I said he was 48. "So, wait a minute," he said, "why didn't your father go to Vietnam?" I replied, "I really don't know… But why are you asking?" He answered, "I was just wondering…" For a moment there was only silence between us, I was very anxious to know what had happened with my father.

I hung up the phone and immediately called my father and posed my question. He asked me why I wanted to know. I implored him to tell me. He said, "All right Juanchi I will. When I was about your age, I was drafted to go to war. My father was very scared, because he knew that if I went to Vietnam I would never make it back. I would not have been able to have a family. In short, he knew that going to war would destroy my life forever, even if I survived. So, Juanchi, you will never imagine how many things he did so I would not have to go to the war. Finally, about one month before my departure, I was exempt of going only if I went to college and studied Law. Well son, now you know how I turned out to be a lawyer.

After hanging up I was perplexed, thinking about how lucky my father was and how proud I should feel of my grandfather, who rests in peace, for everything he did. He was a hero, a real hero. If it was not for him, I, the future of that past, would probably not be writing this paper now.

Juan Casillas (excerpt from oral history)
