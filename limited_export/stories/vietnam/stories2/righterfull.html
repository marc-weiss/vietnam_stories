Willie Righter

(edited excerpts from "story circle" of former anti-war activists convened in Dayton, Ohio at the request of P.O.V.)


The most compelling memory that I have about the war was this time when we were in Philadelphia and my older daughter couldn't have been much more than three or four, and she woke up crying in the night because we had the television on and she had seen [a Vietnamese monk] douse himself with gasoline and go to the ultimate, and I remember her crying out, "He's burning, he's burning!" and of course we stopped having her watch the news at that point. But I think in those days at least the news used to confront us with what we were doing and what was happening and I guess I came away from that experience with a deep conviction that none of us had done enough to stop that war.

I think the question that plagued after Vietnam was, did we do enough, could we have done more, what would it take. In 1984 we met together because a lot of us were concerned about what the U.S. policy towards Central America was, at that time, particularly aimed at Nicaragua, and out of that concern had risen a movement across the country called the Pledge of Resistance: people promised to interfere with the workings of the U.S. government if we invaded Nicaragua, some to risk arrest and some to be supportive to those who were risking arrest. And to a person, as we sat around that table and talked about what our motivation was and why had we come together for that first meeting, every person there said it was Vietnam.

So a lot of us, spent a lot of the energy for the next ten years into opposing U.S. policy in Central America.

I guess my biggest concern is that I think people can get focused on outcome and say if we can't stop the war then it's not worth trying and I think in the intervening years between Vietnam and Central America, for me, a theologian named Bill Stringfellow was absolutely key because he framed our response not on the basis of success, and that is a paradox, because you really need to work like you're working for success, the quality of the work, the quality of the research, the quality of the commitment has to be just as high as if you were really going for success. But that the questions of resistance and advocacy are absolutely essential in a democracy, that if people do not resist what they know is wrong, if they do not advocate for what they know is right and they don't keep doing that, whether they are winning or not, then I think basically we are going to end up living in a fascist country. So that is what it did for me. It was a determination that I don't quit until I'm dead.
